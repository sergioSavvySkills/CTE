### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The phrase means that codes of ethics are operational standards with real consequences, not vague aspiration. They are typically structured into 5 to 9 numbered provisions with interpretive commentary that explains how each provision applies in real clinical situations. A motivational statement encourages general virtues; a code of ethics specifies expected conduct and provides a basis for professional discipline when a member falls short.

2. Any four of the seven: (a) Primacy of patient interest: the practitioner's decisions are guided by patient well-being, not personal convenience or financial gain. (b) Respect for autonomy, privacy, and confidentiality: patients have the right to make their own decisions and control their health information. (c) Competence and continuing education: practitioners must maintain the skills needed to do their jobs safely and recognize the limits of their own competence. (d) Integrity and truth-telling: includes error disclosure, meaning telling a patient when something went wrong. (e) Collaboration and accountability for delegated work: practitioners are responsible for what they supervise or delegate, not only what they perform themselves. (f) Advocacy and social responsibility: professions have a duty to the communities they serve. (g) Professional self-governance: each profession holds its own members accountable through discipline and peer review.

3. A code of ethics is developed by the professional organization through member participation, a review committee, a comment period, and a vote; it is enforced by the professional organization, employer, or licensing board. Scope of practice is grounded in state law, enacted by the legislature, and enforced by licensing boards and courts. A code violation is a professional matter; a scope violation is a legal matter, though both can result in discipline.

4. The four dimensions are scope breadth (what tasks are authorized and how much independent decision-making authority the practitioner holds), supervision requirements (whether the practitioner works independently, collaboratively, or under direct supervision), prohibited tasks (the hard limits that cannot be overridden by training or competence), and delegation authority (which roles can assign which tasks to whom). Example for prohibited tasks: a medical assistant in Texas may not perform IV push medications regardless of training or prior-state authorization.

5. Competence reflects what a practitioner is able to do; scope of practice defines what the practitioner is legally authorized to do in that state. Texas scope-of-practice rules, not the practitioner's training history, govern what she may perform. The controlling principle is that the more restrictive document controls, and Texas law does not authorize MAs to push IV medications.

6. Points of friction are areas where one profession's scope intrudes on or competes with another's. The textbook's specific example is full practice authority for nurse practitioners: in some states NPs may practice independently, but in Texas APRNs must have a collaborative agreement with a physician. It is a friction point rather than a simple scope violation because both NPs and physicians may follow their own rules while still disagreeing about whether the policy itself is appropriate and safe.

7. The principle means that a sincere desire to help a patient does not grant legal authority to perform a task that is outside one's defined scope. If a law prohibits an act, good intentions do not change the legal outcome. This matters for students because early clinical experience can create a sense of confidence that outpaces legal authorization, and acting outside scope exposes both the practitioner and the patient to harm and liability.

8. A code violation is handled by the professional organization, the employer, or the licensing board, not by a court. Consequences can include censure, suspension from the professional organization, or referral to a licensing board. A scope violation is a legal matter enforced by the licensing board and potentially the courts; consequences can include license suspension, revocation, and criminal charges in severe cases.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The ANA Code of Ethics, Provision 3, gives the nurse a duty to protect patients that extends beyond her immediate care relationship. That duty includes reporting unsafe or unethical practices even when the person engaging in them is not in her direct reporting chain. The nurse's obligation flows from her professional code, not from her organizational hierarchy. Being a subordinate does not eliminate the obligation; the code is her professional commitment, not her employer's org chart.

2. The physician violates AMA Principle III (honesty with patients and colleagues), because documenting exams that were not performed is a direct falsehood in the medical record. He also violates Principle IV (patient welfare), because falsified exam records can mislead the care team, delay diagnosis of real findings, or provide false reassurance, each of which harms patients.

3. Codes and scope do not always align. Example: a medical assistant sincerely believes that performing a task the AAMA Code of Ethics would endorse is the right thing to do for a patient; but if that task is outside Texas MA scope, the code points toward action while the law prohibits it. In that case the law wins because the controlling principle is that the more restrictive of the two documents controls.

4. The physician's behavior is both a scope violation and a code violation. A physician's scope includes performing and documenting history and physical exams; documenting exams not performed is an act within the general role but falsified, making it an unethical act within scope rather than a scope overstep. It violates the code because of the honesty and integrity provisions. It may also constitute fraud, a separate legal issue.

5. The common provision on collaboration and accountability for delegated work establishes that practitioners are accountable for what occurs within their professional sphere, including failures to act. A nurse who becomes aware of unsafe or unethical practice and does not report it may face professional discipline for abandoning her duty to protect patients. Depending on whether patient harm results, there could also be personal civil liability for failing to act on known information.
