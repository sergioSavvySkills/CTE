# Demonstrating Understanding, Adapting Practice for Diverse Patients

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Explain how a specific diversity or cultural factor shapes a healthcare encounter in plain language
- Propose concrete, behaviorally specific adaptations for patients who differ from you in language, culture, religion, ability, gender identity, or immigration status
- Distinguish cultural humility from cultural competence and explain why the distinction matters
- Identify when to escalate to an interpreter, chaplain, social worker, or ethics consultant rather than improvising alone

A student nurse is about to enter a patient's room for a postpartum assessment. The chart notes "Hmong-speaking, limited English." She pauses at the door and thinks: I need a qualified interpreter before I start. She calls the video interpreter service, waits three minutes for the connection, and begins the assessment in partnership with the interpreter. Later, her preceptor tells her that the previous shift had tried to use the patient's teenage daughter to interpret, and the patient had nodded along without understanding the discharge instructions. The student's three-minute pause prevented a medication error and a preventable readmission. That pause is what this chapter is about: knowing, in advance, what to do and why.

### From Identification to Action

Chapter c2P asked you to identify the cultural, linguistic, and socioeconomic influences that shape healthcare delivery. This chapter asks you to go further: demonstrate understanding by applying that knowledge, explaining what you would actually do in a specific encounter, and why.

The difference between knowing about a cultural influence and demonstrating understanding of it is the difference between recognizing that LEP patients are entitled to qualified interpretation and being able to say, in your own words: "Before I take a history from this patient, I will request a qualified video interpreter, not a family member, because family interpreters create privacy problems and because medical terminology requires professional training to translate accurately." That second formulation is demonstrating understanding.

**Cultural humility** is an ongoing posture of learning and self-examination, not a set of facts to memorize. The concept, introduced by Tervalon and Murray-Garcia in 1998, contrasts with **cultural competence**, which frames cultural knowledge as a finite body of information to be learned and then applied. Cultural humility recognizes that cultures are too internally varied and too dynamic to be fully mapped, and that the most important clinical skill is not knowing a set of cultural facts but approaching each patient with genuine curiosity about their specific context, values, and preferences. A provider who memorized "Jehovah's Witnesses refuse blood products" but does not think to ask the specific Jehovah's Witness patient in front of them about their personal beliefs and how far they extend those beliefs is practicing cultural competence without cultural humility.

### What Demonstrating Understanding Looks Like

Demonstrating cultural understanding has four observable components.

**First, explain the effect specifically.** Not "culture affects care" but "when a patient from a culture where family makes health decisions collectively receives a serious diagnosis, asking them to make an independent decision in a 10-minute appointment may produce a non-answer that looks like avoidance but is actually a request to involve the family first."

**Second, propose behaviorally specific adaptations.** Not "be culturally sensitive" but "I will ask the patient at the start of the visit whether they would like a family member present for the discussion, and if so, schedule a longer appointment so that person can be there." Generic responses like "be respectful" or "be culturally aware" are not adaptations. An adaptation names a specific behavior that changes the clinical encounter.

**Third, recognize limits and name the next step.** A student who encounters a patient whose religious beliefs about end-of-life care conflict with the treatment plan should not improvise a theological response. The appropriate next step is a chaplaincy or spiritual-care referral. A patient whose family is pressuring them to accept a treatment the patient does not want may need a social-work consult or an ethics committee consultation, not a well-meaning student working outside their scope.

**Fourth, examine yourself.** Every provider has cultural defaults, ways of communicating, assumptions about decision-making, and beliefs about what "good care" looks like, that feel neutral but are not. The US biomedical system has a culture: it values individual autonomy, directness, efficiency, and technology. These values are not universal. A provider who assumes that every patient shares them will misread patients regularly. Identifying your own defaults, and the behaviors you will use to check them, is part of the clinical skill.

### Applying the Framework: Four Patient Scenarios

The framework applies the same way across any encounter: name the cultural or diversity factor, identify the federal lever, propose at least two specific behavioral adaptations, name the right resource to involve, and note what you will check about yourself.

**LEP patient with chest pain (language, Title VI):** Request a **qualified medical interpreter** before taking any history. A family member is not qualified, even if bilingual: they may edit information, lack medical vocabulary, and their presence may prevent disclosure of sensitive concerns. Speak in short sentences, direct questions to the patient, and confirm understanding by asking the patient to restate the plan. Self-check: am I rushing because the interpreter service takes time?

**Deaf adult in the ED (disability, ADA):** Ask the patient's communication preference before assuming. ASL interpretation is not the same as written notes: ASL is a separate language from English. Request an ASL interpreter, face the patient when speaking, and address the patient directly rather than the interpreter. Self-check: am I assuming cognitive impairment because the patient cannot hear? Hearing and cognition are unrelated.

**Jehovah's Witness patient declining transfusion (religion, patient autonomy):** The patient's refusal is a protected decision, not a misunderstanding to correct. Explore their specific position (blood fractions vary in acceptability), plan the surgery using bloodless techniques, and document thoroughly. Escalate to social work or ethics consultation if there is any concern about coercion, not to change the patient's mind, but to verify the decision is autonomous.

**Transgender adolescent for routine well-care (gender identity):** Use correct name and pronouns from first contact, including in hallway conversations with colleagues. Use an **organ inventory** intake form that documents anatomy present rather than assumed anatomy. Do not require the patient to explain their gender identity as a condition of receiving care. Protect adolescent confidentiality: some patients are not out to parents, and inadvertent disclosure causes harm.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A student health worker is opening an encounter with a Hmong family whose grandfather has a serious diagnosis. The family has asked the medical team not to tell the grandfather directly. The student has been taught to prioritize individual patient autonomy, the biomedical default. But she also knows that in many cultures, including this family's, withholding a serious diagnosis from the patient and managing it within the family is not a violation of dignity, it is an expression of love and protection. Rather than making a unilateral decision, she uses the Kleinman explanatory model to open: "Can you help me understand how your family would like us to share information about your grandfather's health? What would feel right to him, and to you?" That question opens a conversation rather than closing one. The ethics consultation the team eventually arranges will navigate the legal and ethical complexity of disclosure and surrogate decision-making. Her job is not to resolve that tension alone, it is to ask the right question and bring in the right support.

### When to Escalate Rather Than Improvise

Part of demonstrating understanding is knowing the limits of what you can handle alone. The following situations call for escalation to appropriate resources.

A **chaplaincy or spiritual-care** referral is appropriate when a patient's religious beliefs are affecting their treatment decisions, when a patient expresses spiritual distress, or when end-of-life care involves religious rituals that the clinical team does not know how to accommodate. Chaplains are trained for these conversations. Students and most bedside nurses are not.

A social-work referral is appropriate when a patient's socioeconomic circumstances are creating barriers to following a care plan, when a patient may be in an abusive or coercive situation affecting their healthcare decisions, or when housing instability, food insecurity, or transportation barriers require community-resource navigation.

A **promotor/a de salud**, or community health worker, is a culturally tailored role particularly relevant in Texas Latinx communities. Promotoras provide health education, care navigation, and community connection in the patient's home community and language. They are a referral option in many Texas health systems and federally qualified health centers.

A **sanctuary clinic** policy should be known by every staff member in settings where patients include undocumented immigrants. Patients who fear that seeking care will trigger immigration enforcement may delay or avoid care for themselves and their children. Sanctuary policies, and the ability to communicate them clearly to patients, are part of the care environment.

### Key terms

**Cultural humility:** an ongoing posture of learning and self-examination in cross-cultural healthcare encounters; distinguished from cultural competence by Tervalon and Murray-Garcia (1998).

**Cultural competence:** a framework treating cultural knowledge as a finite set of facts to be learned; a weaker clinical posture than humility because cultures are too varied to fully memorize.

**CLAS Standards:** the HHS Office of Minority Health's National Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services Standards; the operational framework driving interpreter services, translated materials, and staff training requirements in accredited healthcare settings.

**Qualified medical interpreter:** a trained, vetted interpreter competent in medical terminology; required under Title VI; family members and children are not qualified interpreters for substantive clinical conversations.

**Organ inventory:** an affirming-care approach for transgender and gender-diverse patients that documents anatomy present rather than assuming anatomy from gender assignment at birth.

**Affirming care:** use of correct names, pronouns, and responsive language for LGBTQ+ patients; the standard of care described by the National LGBTQIA+ Health Education Center.

**Chaplaincy / spiritual care:** the hospital department providing culturally and religiously responsive support, including care during serious illness and end-of-life rituals; the appropriate escalation for religious decision-making conflicts.

**Promotor/a de salud:** a community health worker in Latinx communities who provides culturally tailored health education and care navigation; particularly relevant to Texas healthcare settings.

**Explanatory model (Kleinman):** the patient's own understanding of what is wrong, why it happened, and what should be done; a core tool of cultural humility that opens rather than closes clinical conversations.

**Sanctuary clinic:** a healthcare setting with internal policies limiting cooperation with immigration enforcement, creating conditions where patients can seek care without fear.

### Chapter summary

Demonstrating understanding of diversity and cultural practices means more than recognizing that these factors matter. It means explaining specific effects, proposing behaviorally specific adaptations, knowing when to escalate to qualified resources rather than improvising, and examining your own cultural defaults with honesty. Cultural humility, an ongoing posture of learning and curiosity, is a stronger clinical stance than cultural competence, which assumes a fixed body of knowledge can cover every patient encounter. The concrete adaptations in this chapter, requesting qualified interpreters, using organ-inventory intake forms, making chaplaincy referrals, communicating sanctuary-clinic policies, and using the Kleinman explanatory model to open clinical conversations, are skills that can be practiced and improved. They are not optional for healthcare professionals working in diverse Texas communities.

### Review questions

1. A preceptor tells a student: "You'll get the hang of cultural competence. Just learn a few key things about each group and you'll be fine." Using what you know about the distinction between cultural humility and cultural competence, explain what is limited about the preceptor's advice, and describe what a culturally humble response to the same patient population would look like.
2. A Deaf patient in the ED is being given discharge instructions by a nurse who is writing notes on a clipboard and handing them to the patient. The patient is nodding. Identify two specific problems with this approach, and describe three concrete adaptations the nurse should have used.
3. You are a health science student about to complete your first clinical rotation. Identify one aspect of your own background, language, culture, or personal experience, that may create distance between you and some patients you will encounter. Name two specific, concrete behaviors you will use to narrow that distance. Explain why naming a behavior is more useful than stating an intention like "I will be more open-minded."
