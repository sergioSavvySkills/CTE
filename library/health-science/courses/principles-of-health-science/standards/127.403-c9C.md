## Learning objective

Students will be able to discuss the ethical issues healthcare technology raises — across AI and algorithmic decision-making, genomics, telehealth, wearables and consumer health tech, and end-of-life technology — and apply the four ethical principles to evaluate tradeoffs in each.

## Content stub

- **Why technology concentrates ethical issues:** every new capability creates new versions of old tensions among autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice — and often creates them faster than rules, regulators, or professional codes can keep up
- **AI and algorithmic decision-making in clinical care:**
  - Diagnostic and predictive algorithms (sepsis prediction, imaging triage, readmission risk)
  - Risk of encoded bias — algorithms trained on historically biased data reproduce disparities (well-documented example: the Obermeyer et al. 2019 *Science* paper on a US healthcare algorithm that systematically under-identified Black patients' health needs)
  - Accountability when the algorithm is wrong — the clinician remains responsible; automation bias (over-trusting the machine) is a new failure mode
  - Patient autonomy and consent — does the patient know an AI is shaping their care?
- **Genomics and genetic testing:**
  - Who owns genetic data; who can see it (employer, insurer, law enforcement); what happens to samples
  - Incidental findings — the test reveals information the patient did not ask for (e.g., a BRCA result from a different panel)
  - Information about relatives who never consented (first-degree relatives share ~50% of variants)
  - Genetic discrimination — GINA protects in employment and health insurance but not in life, disability, or long-term-care insurance
- **Telehealth and remote monitoring:**
  - Access expansion for rural patients vs. the digital divide (broadband, devices, digital literacy)
  - Quality-of-exam limits (no hands-on assessment)
  - Licensure across state lines — which state's scope and standard apply
  - Continuous data streams from home monitoring and the privacy/alert-fatigue tradeoffs
- **Wearables and consumer health tech:**
  - Data accuracy and the "false reassurance or false alarm" problem (direct-to-consumer heart-rhythm detection as the canonical example)
  - Employer-wellness program access to employee data
  - Secondary use for research — most consumer-grade wearables are outside HIPAA
  - Consent when the device is a gift or a condition of employment
- **End-of-life technology:**
  - Mechanical ventilation, ECMO, artificial nutrition and hydration — when technology prolongs biological life beyond what the person would have wanted
  - Advance directives, medical power of attorney, POLST / MOLST forms
  - Cultural, religious, and family variation in end-of-life preferences (crosswalks with c2P)
- **Reproductive and fertility technology:** IVF, embryo selection, genetic screening of embryos, surrogacy, gamete donation — fast-moving legal landscape with meaningful state variation (especially Texas after *Dobbs*, 2022)
- **Discussing, not resolving:** the TEKS verb is *discuss* — the standard asks students to surface the tradeoffs honestly using the principles framework, not to claim a single correct answer

## Vocabulary

- **Algorithmic bias** — encoded bias in a clinical algorithm trained on historically skewed data (Obermeyer et al. 2019 documented this in a US healthcare algorithm).
- **Automation bias** — the failure mode of over-trusting a machine output; a new risk of AI in clinical decisions.
- **Incidental finding** — information revealed by a test that the patient did not ask about (e.g., a BRCA result from an unrelated panel).
- **Digital divide** — inequitable access to broadband, devices, and digital literacy; a justice issue in telehealth expansion.
- **Advance directive** — a patient's document (living will, directive to physicians, POLST/MOLST) directing end-of-life technology use. *(also in c10A)*
- **POLST / MOLST** — Physician/Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment; portable clinical orders that follow the patient across settings.
- **ECMO** — extracorporeal membrane oxygenation; end-of-life technology that can prolong biological life beyond a person's wishes.
- **GINA** — federal protection against genetic-information discrimination in employment and health insurance; does not cover life, disability, or long-term-care insurance. *(also in c9B)*
- **21st Century Cures Act** — federal law whose information-blocking rule intersects with patient access to emerging clinical-data technologies.
- **Hastings Center** — a leading US bioethics institute whose reports frame current technology ethics debates.

## Assessment stub

- Principle-mapping drill: given 5 technology-driven scenarios (an AI sepsis alert fires on an otherwise-stable patient; a direct-to-consumer genetic test reveals non-paternity; a telehealth visit uncovers elder abuse the clinician cannot investigate in-person; a patient's smartwatch flags an arrhythmia that their cardiologist cannot reproduce; a family asks for continued ventilation against a signed advance directive), identify which of the four principles are in tension for each and describe the tradeoff in 2–3 sentences
- News critique: find one current (within the past 12 months) news article about a healthcare-technology ethical issue (AI bias, genetic privacy, telehealth fraud, device failure); in a 1-page response, summarize the issue, identify the two most important ethical tensions using the principles framework, and propose one specific mitigation that named actors (regulator, clinician, vendor, patient) could implement
- Reflection: describe one piece of healthcare technology that has been used by you or a family member (patient portal, wearable, telehealth visit, advanced imaging, connected glucose meter); identify a privacy, consent, or accuracy concern you had not previously considered; name the principle it sits under
- Discussion leader: prepare three questions to lead a class discussion on one technology category from the list above; the questions should surface at least two of the four principles in tension

## Activities

- **Tech-Ethics Principle Mapper** — five tech scenarios rotate: an AI sepsis alert firing on a stable patient, a DTC genetic test revealing non-paternity, a telehealth visit uncovering elder abuse in another state, a smartwatch arrhythmia the cardiologist cannot reproduce, a family demanding continued ventilation against a signed advance directive. For each, student drops the four principles onto a two-axis tension field (high/low tension × who bears the risk) and writes a 2-3 sentence tradeoff statement. A model from Hastings Center commentary unlocks post-submit.
  → *ethical reasoning, analysis*

- **Algorithmic-Bias Detective** — student reviews a simulated sepsis-prediction dashboard that alerts on a patient population. The dashboard shows the algorithm's performance stratified by race: sensitivity 72% white vs. 51% Black. Student has to identify which of the four principles is most violated, propose one audit the Obermeyer et al. paper would suggest, and name what a clinician should do differently today when the tool is deployed as-is. A second round shows a "fixed" algorithm — student must judge whether the fix addresses root cause or just masks it.
  → *analysis, ethical reasoning*

- **End-of-Life Tech Tradeoff Wheel** — an interactive wheel with six positions: mechanical ventilation, ECMO, artificial nutrition, dialysis, CPR, palliative-only. Student spins per patient card (80-year-old with stage-4 lung cancer; 22-year-old with reversible opioid overdose; 65-year-old with dementia and aspiration pneumonia; 45-year-old with TBI and signed directive) and picks which technologies the patient's advance directive, POLST, or substituted-judgment standard would support. Wrong picks trigger a Texas HSC Chapter 166 callout about the specific directive language.
  → *decision under constraint, ethical reasoning*

## References

- **Obermeyer et al., "Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations," *Science* (2019)** — foundational empirical evidence of algorithmic bias in a US healthcare setting.
- **FDA Artificial Intelligence / Machine Learning-enabled medical-device guidance** — regulatory framework for AI in clinical tools.
- **Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 2008)** — federal protections for genetic information in employment and health insurance; scope and limits worth knowing.
- **21st Century Cures Act — Information Blocking rule (ONC, 2020/2022)** — patient-access provisions that intersect with emerging clinical-data technologies.
- **American Medical Association *Code of Medical Ethics* — Opinions on AI (5.2) and Telehealth (1.2.12)** — profession-level guidance on the technologies above.
- **Hastings Center reports and *American Journal of Bioethics*** — accessible peer-reviewed sources for current bioethics debates in emerging technology.
- **CMS Interoperability and Patient Access final rules** — federal rules shaping data access for patients and apps.
- **Beauchamp and Childress, *Principles of Biomedical Ethics*** — the four-principles framework for structuring the discussion.
