# Recognizing Equipment Malfunctions

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the warning signs of a potential equipment malfunction across common healthcare devices
- Distinguish device problems from user errors and from true patient emergencies
- Describe the immediate safety actions to take when a malfunction is suspected

A night-shift nursing student notices that the IV pump on her patient's pole is beeping and displaying an error code. The patient's arm looks fine and the line is running. She does not know what the code means, but she does know three things: she should not silence the alarm until she understands it, she needs to check the patient first, and she needs to get her preceptor immediately. Knowing those three things, before ever troubleshooting the pump itself, is exactly what this chapter is designed to teach.

### What counts as a malfunction?

A malfunction is any situation in which a device fails to perform as intended. Not all malfunctions announce themselves with dramatic alarms. Healthcare equipment can fail in at least seven distinct ways.

**Alarms and error codes:** The device is signaling a problem. Never silence an alarm you do not understand. Know the difference between alarm types: an informational alarm is advisory, a critical alarm requires an immediate response. Error codes must be looked up in the device's **Instructions for Use (IFU)**, the manufacturer document available in every facility for every device.

**Abnormal readings:** More dangerous because there is no alarm. A blood pressure of 60/40 in an awake, alert, comfortable patient does not fit the clinical picture. A pulse-ox of 78% on a patient who is talking calmly is almost certainly not a true saturation. The core question is: does the number match the patient?

**Physical damage:** A frayed cord, cracked housing, bent needle, or leaking reservoir means the device comes out of service immediately. Physical damage visible on the outside often signals an invisible safety risk.

**Unresponsive devices:** A frozen screen, a device that will not power on, or software that crashes mid-cycle cannot be trusted. Remove it from service.

**Calibration drift:** The device produces numbers and does not alarm, but the numbers are systematically wrong. A glucose meter reading 15 mg/dL high on every sample will not alert anyone. Calibration drift is caught only by quality control (QC) checks.

**Unexpected behavior during operation:** An infusion pump running at the wrong rate, a ventilator cycling out of sync with the patient, a defibrillator that fails to charge. These require immediate action because the device is actively in use.

**Battery and connectivity failures:** An AED with a dead battery fails at the one moment it is needed. A telemetry system that stops transmitting means a patient is no longer being monitored without anyone knowing.

### Common failure modes by device type

Some device categories have characteristic failure patterns worth knowing.

IV pumps alarm for **air-in-line**, **occlusion**, and **dose-error alerts**. Most occlusion alarms trace to a kinked tube or a patient repositioning, not a pump malfunction. Check the tubing and the patient's arm before assuming the pump itself is the problem.

Ventilators alarm for **disconnection**, **high pressure**, and **low tidal volume**. A high-pressure alarm on a patient who is coughing is usually the patient, not the machine, but you must still respond, assess the patient, and call your preceptor or respiratory therapist.

Defibrillators and AEDs display **pad connection errors**, **low battery** warnings, and **self-test failures**. AEDs perform daily automated self-tests. Checking the log during safety rounds is how failures get caught before an emergency.

EKG machines produce **artifact**, distorted tracings caused by **60-Hz electrical interference**, patient movement, or a loose lead. An apparent dangerous rhythm on a patient who is alert with a normal pulse is almost certainly artifact. Reattach the leads and repeat the tracing.

Sterilizers can produce a **biologic indicator (BI) positive**, meaning the QC test using live bacterial spores failed and instruments in that cycle may not be sterile. A positive BI is one of the most serious equipment failures in a healthcare facility because instruments may already have been used on patients.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A medical assisting student is running point-of-care glucose testing during a morning diabetes clinic. She gets a glucose reading of "HI" (over the meter's readable range, often above 600 mg/dL) on a patient who appears comfortable, is speaking normally, and tells her he ate a large breakfast. The student's first thought is to check whether the patient matches the reading: is this patient showing signs of extreme hyperglycemia? He is not. She checks whether the test strip lot number is current and whether the QC check was done this morning. The QC check was not done. She pulls the meter out of service, labels it "Do Not Use," notifies her preceptor, and documents what she found. The preceptor runs the QC check, finds the meter is reading significantly high, and sends it to biomed. The student never assumed the number was correct just because the device produced it.

### The core heuristic: patient first, then user error, then device

When you see an abnormal reading or an alarm, apply three steps in a fixed order.

First: is the patient in immediate danger? Assess airway, breathing, and responsiveness. If the patient is deteriorating, call for help now. The device question is secondary.

Second: is there a non-device explanation? A pulse-ox of 78% may be a probe that slipped off, nail polish interfering with the sensor, or poor peripheral circulation. A pump occlusion alarm may be a kinked tube. Check the obvious user and environment factors before pulling the device.

Third: if the patient is stable and user error is ruled out, suspect the device and take it out of service.

Skipping step one is the most dangerous error a student can make. A pulse-ox of 78% that is real hypoxia, treated as a sensor problem, can result in a patient's death.

### Immediate safety actions when you suspect a malfunction

The immediate safety action sequence is the same across nearly all device types.

**Ensure patient safety first.** Switch to a backup method if available: count the respiratory rate manually, use a manual blood pressure cuff. Call for clinical backup.

**Take the device out of service.** Physically remove it from the patient-care area so it cannot be accidentally reused.

**Label it "Do Not Use"** with the date, time, your initials, and a brief factual description of what you observed. This label is the first link in the evidence chain.

**Preserve everything.** Do not throw away IV tubing, blood glucose strips, or electrode pads. Biomed needs those items to investigate. A discarded tubing set makes a pump malfunction investigation impossible.

**Notify the right people:** your clinical supervisor and the biomed/clinical engineering department. If a patient was harmed, risk management must also be notified. The next chapter covers the formal reporting process.

**Document factually** in the patient record: what you observed, when, what the device displayed, what you did, and the patient's clinical status.

### Key terms

**Critical alarm:** a device signal requiring immediate response; never silenced without understanding the cause.

**Error code:** a device-generated code indicating a specific malfunction or required action; look it up in the IFU before acting.

**Calibration drift:** a device that is working but producing inaccurate values; often caught only by quality control checks, not by clinical intuition.

**Biologic indicator:** the sterilizer quality-control test using live bacterial spores; a positive result means instruments may not be sterile.

**Free-flow event:** unintended, uncontrolled IV infusion; rare on modern smart pumps but historically catastrophic.

**Artifact (imaging and EKG):** device-produced signal distortion, such as 60-Hz interference or motion artifact, that can mimic real pathology on a tracing or image.

**"Do Not Use" tag:** the physical label placed on a device removed from service, including date, time, initials, and a brief factual description of the problem.

**Take out of service:** the immediate safety action of removing a suspected-malfunctioning device from the patient area before investigation.

**Instructions for Use (IFU):** the manufacturer document that is the primary reference for any specific device; available in every facility for every device in use.

**Alarm fatigue:** desensitization to alarms caused by excessive or frequent false alarms; identified by The Joint Commission Sentinel Event Alert No. 50 as a major patient-safety issue.

**Clinical engineering / biomed:** the department responsible for investigating, repairing, and clearing devices after a malfunction report.

### Chapter summary

Equipment malfunctions fall into seven categories: alarms and error codes, abnormal readings, physical damage, unresponsive devices, calibration drift, unexpected behavior during operation, and battery or connectivity failures. The most critical clinical-reasoning skill is applying the correct decision order: assess the patient first, rule out user error and environmental factors second, then suspect the device. Common devices have characteristic failure patterns, from IV pump occlusion alarms to EKG artifact to sterilizer biologic-indicator positives, and recognizing those patterns prevents both under-reaction and over-reaction. When a malfunction is suspected, the immediate safety action sequence is always: ensure patient safety, remove the device from service, label it "Do Not Use" with complete information, preserve all associated items for biomed investigation, notify the supervisor and biomed, and document factually. Students are not expected to repair devices; they are expected to recognize the warning signs and escalate correctly.

### Review questions

1. A patient's pulse-ox is reading 78%. The patient is awake, talking comfortably, and shows no signs of respiratory distress. Walk through the three-step decision framework from this chapter: what do you assess first, what non-device explanations do you check second, and at what point do you suspect the device itself?
2. A sterilizer biologic-indicator test comes back positive in central sterile processing. Identify what this finding means for patient safety, name the system this department belongs to, and describe the immediate actions that must follow.
3. Explain why alarm fatigue is a patient-safety problem rather than just an annoyance. Use one specific example from the chapter, such as IV pump alarms or patient monitor alarms, to support your answer.
