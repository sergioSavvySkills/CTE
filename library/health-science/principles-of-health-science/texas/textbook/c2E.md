# Planning and Preparing Effective Oral Presentations

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Plan an oral presentation by identifying the audience, message, and time limit before building any slides
- Storyboard the message flow before selecting which cards become slides
- Apply slide discipline so the audience can listen without reading
- Rehearse aloud to surface timing and pacing problems before delivery

A student finishes a 12-slide presentation on infection control for a nursing in-service. She practiced it silently the night before and it felt solid. In front of the group, she runs 4 minutes over, loses her place twice, and realizes during the debrief that she used the word "um" to fill nearly every pause. None of these problems would have survived one rehearsal out loud.

### Plan before you build

The single most common mistake in oral presentation preparation is opening slide software before the message is clear. A deck of slides is not a plan. It is an output, and outputs built without a plan reflect the plan's absence.

Before any slides exist, answer four questions in writing. Who is the audience, and what do they already know? What is the single message you want them to carry out of the room? What is the time limit? What decision or action do you want them to take after the presentation? A presentation that cannot answer these four questions in two sentences each is not ready for slides.

For healthcare presentations specifically, the audience shapes everything. The same clinical case presented to a peer clinician, to the patient's family, and to a hospital board requires three completely different language registers, three different depths of technical detail, and three different calls to action. Matching the message to the audience is not just a communication courtesy. It is the baseline competency.

### Storyboard before slides

A **storyboard** is a sketch of the presentation's message flow, usually on paper or index cards, built before any slide software is opened. Each card represents one idea. The storyboard answers: does the message flow in a logical order? Does every card earn its place? Is there a card that is actually doing two jobs and should be split? Is there a card that could be cut entirely without the argument breaking down?

Only after the storyboard is complete should the decision be made about which cards become slides, which become spoken points, and which can be cut. This sequencing produces a presentation where the slides exist to support the speaker rather than to serve as the speaker's script.

The storyboard also reveals where transitions are weak. If moving from card 3 to card 4 requires a long explanation, the logical connection between those ideas is not as clear as it seemed while writing the outline. Fixing that problem on index cards takes 30 seconds. Fixing it after the deck is built takes much longer.

### Slide discipline

**Slide discipline** is the practice of limiting each slide to one idea, keeping text minimal, and using honest visuals that the audience can grasp in 3 to 5 seconds. The test for any slide is whether an audience member could read it while also listening to what the speaker is saying. If reading the slide requires more than a few seconds, the slide is competing with the speaker rather than supporting them.

Specific rules: one idea per slide. No more than 25 words of text per slide on most slides, with exceptions for charts or diagrams. Font size at or above 24 points so every seat in the room can read it. Visuals chosen to illustrate the point, not to decorate the slide.

The most common violation of slide discipline is the "wall of bullets" slide: eight or ten bullet points, each a full sentence, while the speaker reads every one aloud. The audience read faster than the speaker speaks, so they finish the slide text before the speaker does, and then they are waiting. The solution is to put only the key term or phrase on the slide and speak the explanation aloud.

### Clinical oral presentation formats

Healthcare workers give oral presentations in formats that have specific time budgets and conventions. Knowing the format before preparing is part of the planning step.

A **brief (TeamSTEPPS)** is a 3 to 5 minute start-of-shift or start-of-case presentation naming goals, anticipated issues, and team roles. It is structured, fast, and focused on the immediate work period. The audience needs specifics, not context.

A **huddle (TeamSTEPPS)** is a 2 to 3 minute mid-shift regroup used when conditions change. It is not a scheduled meeting. It happens because something has shifted and the team needs to realign. The speaker names what changed, what the new priority is, and what each person's next action is.

A **debrief (TeamSTEPPS)** is a 5 to 10 minute post-event structured review. It covers what went well, what did not, and what to change next time. The debrief speaker's job is to create space for honest answers, not to defend what happened.

**Grand rounds** is a formal hospital teaching presentation, typically 20 to 45 minutes, with slides, followed by discussion. The audience is clinicians who expect precise terminology, evidence citations, and a clear clinical argument.

An **in-service** is a brief staff-education presentation delivered to colleagues on a unit, typically 10 to 20 minutes. It is more conversational than grand rounds and usually focused on a single practical skill or policy change. The audience wants to know what to do differently on their next shift, not just what the research says.

A patient-education talk is delivered directly to patients or families, often in groups. Jargon is replaced entirely by plain language. The message is built around what the patient needs to do at home, not what the clinician finds clinically interesting. Teach-back is used at the end to confirm understanding.

### Rehearsing aloud

**Rehearsing aloud** means speaking the presentation out loud, not running through it silently or reading the slides. Silent review does not surface timing, stumble points, or pacing problems. Saying the words out loud does.

Rehearsal should happen at least twice: once with slides to check timing and transitions, and once without slides to confirm that the message holds up on its own. If the presentation falls apart without the slides, the slides are doing the work the speaker should be doing. The speaker should know the message cold enough that the slides are supporting evidence, not a script.

Specific things to listen for during aloud rehearsal: filler words ("um," "uh," "like," "so," "you know") that appear when the speaker is not sure what comes next; pauses that are too short (rushing) or too long (losing momentum); hedging phrases ("kind of," "sort of," "I think maybe") that undermine the speaker's authority; and transitions between sections that are not smooth.

Managing nerves is part of preparation. Scripting the first 30 seconds of the presentation and practicing it until the opening comes out cleanly reduces the anxiety spike at the moment the speaker stands up. Controlled breathing before starting and knowing the opening sentence cold give the body something to do besides panic.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A health science student has 5 minutes at the next staff meeting to present new hand-hygiene compliance data to the nursing staff on her unit. She starts by storyboarding: the message is that compliance dropped to 72% last month and the team needs to know why and what to do differently. She builds 4 slides: the compliance number, the comparison to last month, the two most common failure points from the audit, and the one change the team is being asked to make. She rehearses aloud twice, cutting her time from 7 minutes to 4 minutes 45 seconds on the second run. She also catches that she said "kind of concerning" to describe a 12-point drop and replaces it with "a 12-point drop that puts us below the national benchmark." She walks in with 4 clean slides and a 30-second opening she knows cold.

### Key terms

**Storyboard:** a paper or index-card sketch of the presentation's message flow, built before any slide is created, used to confirm logical order and identify what to cut.

**Slide discipline:** the practice of one idea per slide, minimal text, and honest visuals, so the audience can listen without reading.

**Rehearse aloud:** speaking the presentation out loud before delivery to surface timing, stumble points, filler words, and pacing problems that silent review does not catch.

**Brief (TeamSTEPPS):** a 3 to 5 minute start-of-shift presentation naming goals, roles, and anticipated issues.

**Huddle (TeamSTEPPS):** a 2 to 3 minute mid-shift regroup when conditions change and the team needs to realign.

**Debrief (TeamSTEPPS):** a 5 to 10 minute post-event review covering what went well, what did not, and what to change next time.

**Grand rounds:** a formal 20 to 45 minute hospital teaching presentation for a clinical audience, using precise terminology and evidence citations.

**In-service:** a brief staff-education presentation delivered to colleagues on a unit, focused on one practical skill or policy change.

### Chapter summary

Effective oral presentations in healthcare begin with planning, not slides. The planning phase answers four questions: audience, message, time limit, and desired action. Storyboarding maps the message flow on paper before any slides are built, which reveals logical gaps and unnecessary content early. Slide discipline limits each slide to one idea with minimal text and readable fonts so the audience can listen without reading. Healthcare workers encounter specific oral presentation formats, including TeamSTEPPS briefs, huddles, and debriefs, and formal contexts like grand rounds and in-service sessions, each with defined time budgets and conventions. Rehearsing aloud surfaces timing, filler words, and pacing problems that silent review misses every time.
