# How Government Shapes Healthcare, Local, State, and National Levers

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the distinct roles that local, state, and national governments play in the health science industry
- Match a specific healthcare policy or action to the level of government responsible and the lever being used
- Analyze how a single policy change affects patients, providers, and the healthcare workforce
- Explain what happens when government levels conflict or overlap in healthcare decisions

During the COVID-19 pandemic, a Texas school district received three different sets of guidance about mask policies: one from the local health department, one from the state governor's office, and one from the Centers for Disease Control. All three disagreed. Students, parents, and school nurses were caught in the middle. That moment of confusion was not a system failure, it was the normal friction of three levels of government with overlapping and sometimes conflicting authority over public health. Understanding that structure, who controls what and why, is essential knowledge for anyone entering the health science field.

## Part 1: Who Does What, and with Which Tools

### Three Levels, Three Distinct Roles

Government shapes healthcare at three distinct levels, and each level uses a different set of tools to do it.

**Local government** means county and city health departments, public hospitals, emergency medical services, school health programs, and the inspectors who check restaurant cleanliness and facility safety codes. Local agencies are often the first responders to a disease outbreak, the ones knocking on doors, tracing contacts, and distributing vaccines in a school gymnasium. They are also the level of government closest to individual communities, which means they can respond to local needs, but also that their capacity varies enormously from a well-funded urban health department to a rural county with a single health officer.

**State government** holds the **licensure** authority that determines who is legally allowed to deliver healthcare. Every physician, nurse, pharmacist, dental hygienist, and medical assistant in Texas holds a license issued by a state board. States also co-administer Medicaid with the federal government, establish public-health laws governing disease reporting, vaccination requirements, and quarantine authority, and fund or operate state psychiatric hospitals and public-health labs. The **scope of practice** that a nurse practitioner or physician assistant can exercise in Texas is set by state law, not federal law, and it differs in important ways from the scope in neighboring states.

**National government** operates at a different scale and uses different tools. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (**CMS**) writes the rules for Medicare and Medicaid, the programs that together pay for roughly 40 percent of US health spending. The **FDA** approves every drug, medical device, and biologic sold in the US. The CDC leads disease surveillance, publishes guidance during outbreaks, and funds state and local public-health departments. OSHA sets and enforces workplace safety rules binding on nearly all healthcare employers. The NIH funds the basic and clinical research that produces new treatments. HRSA funds safety-net clinics and trains the primary-care workforce. VA and TRICARE provide care to veterans and military families.

### The Five Levers Government Uses

It is useful to think of government not just as "making rules" but as using specific levers to shape what healthcare looks like on the ground. Five levers appear repeatedly.

**Coverage and payment** is the most powerful lever. What Medicare and Medicaid agree to pay for, at what rate, determines what services get delivered, where, and to whom. A decision by CMS to cover a new procedure or drug unlocks that treatment for tens of millions of patients overnight. A decision to cut the payment rate for a service can push hospitals to eliminate that service line entirely.

**Licensure and scope** determines who is allowed to deliver care, with what training, in what settings. The Texas Nurse Practice Act, for example, defines exactly what a registered nurse may do independently versus under physician supervision. When Texas expanded the scope of practice for nurse practitioners in 2022, more Texans gained access to a primary-care provider because NPs could open independent practices in underserved areas.

**Safety and quality regulation** sets how care must be delivered. **CLIA (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments)** establishes quality standards every clinical laboratory in the US must meet regardless of state. The CMS **Conditions of Participation** are the minimum standards a hospital must meet to receive Medicare and Medicaid payments, covering everything from infection control to patient rights. These are not suggestions. A hospital that fails its CMS survey faces loss of Medicare payment, which would force most hospitals to close within weeks.

**Public-health law** is the lever most visible during outbreaks. States have broad authority to require disease reporting, mandate vaccination for school enrollment, order quarantine, and restrict the sale of tobacco products. Local health departments can impose restaurant closures and site-specific health orders. Federal agencies like the CDC can issue guidance, but their enforcement authority depends on whether their rules fall within existing federal statutory authority.

**Research and workforce funding** is a slower lever but a powerful one. NIH funding priorities shape what diseases get studied. HRSA loan-repayment programs incentivize physicians and nurses to practice in underserved areas. State support for medical schools and nursing programs determines the size of the pipeline entering the workforce over the next decade.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A hospital in a rural Texas county is considering whether to add a telehealth service for mental health patients. The hospital's compliance team identifies the following: the CMS reimbursement rules for telehealth were expanded during the COVID-19 pandemic through emergency waivers, and many of those expansions became permanent through subsequent legislation. Texas state law governs which mental-health providers can practice via telehealth and from what physical locations. OSHA's bloodborne-pathogen standards and CMS Conditions of Participation apply to the telehealth workspace just as they do to a physical clinic. All three levels of government are present in a decision that looks, on its surface, like a simple technology question.

## Part 2: Conflict, Overlap, and Real-World Consequences

### When Levels Conflict

Government levels do not always agree, and when they conflict, the resolution follows established legal principles. **Federal preemption** means that a valid federal rule overrides a conflicting state rule within the scope of federal authority. The FDA's approval of a drug preempts any state law that would prohibit that drug's sale. A state cannot require a different safety label than the one FDA has mandated.

But preemption has limits. States have broad authority under the Tenth Amendment over health, safety, and welfare, which is why Medicaid eligibility varies by state, why scope-of-practice rules for nurses differ from Texas to California, and why vaccination-mandate rules differ across state lines. **Medicaid expansion**, the ACA provision that allowed states to extend Medicaid coverage to adults up to 138 percent of the federal poverty level, was upheld by the Supreme Court but made voluntary for states. Texas chose not to expand, meaning hundreds of thousands of Texans in the coverage gap between Medicaid eligibility and marketplace subsidies remain uninsured, while neighboring states that expanded cover similar populations. That single state-level choice has measurable effects on rural hospital finances, uncompensated care costs, and workforce demand.

The COVID-19 pandemic put these conflicts on public display. Emergency Use Authorizations for vaccines were a federal FDA lever. Workplace vaccination mandates were challenged under OSHA's authority. School mask requirements became a battleground between local school boards, state governors, and federal guidance. Quarantine authority sits primarily with states, but the CDC has statutory authority for disease control at US borders and ports. Knowing which level has authority over which piece of a public-health response is not academic, it determines what a health department can actually order and enforce.

### Analyzing Any Policy: A Six-Step Framework

One practical skill this chapter builds is the ability to analyze any healthcare policy using a consistent framework. For any policy or government action, ask these six questions:

1. Which level of government is acting?
2. Which lever is being used (coverage, licensure, safety/quality, public-health law, or research/workforce funding)?
3. What is the intended effect on patients, providers, or the workforce?
4. What is the observed or likely actual effect?
5. What is the effect on providers and the healthcare workforce specifically?
6. What unintended consequences occurred or might occur?

Apply this framework to **EMTALA**, the 1986 federal law requiring hospitals with emergency departments to screen and stabilize any patient who arrives, regardless of ability to pay. Level: national. Lever: coverage and payment (backed by the threat of Medicare exclusion). Intended effect: eliminate the practice of "patient dumping," transferring uninsured patients to public hospitals without stabilizing them first. Observed effect: emergency departments became the safety-net access point for tens of millions of uninsured Americans, contributing to ED crowding. Effect on workforce: ED nurses, physicians, and technicians carry an enormous share of the uncompensated-care burden. Unintended consequence: the ED became the primary-care provider of last resort for many uninsured patients, a role it was not designed or funded to fill efficiently.

### Policy Cases Worth Knowing

Several real-world policies illustrate the framework in action.

The **ACA (Affordable Care Act)** of 2010 used multiple levers simultaneously: coverage expansion through Medicaid and marketplace subsidies, value-based payment reform through CMS, preventive-care mandates requiring coverage without cost-sharing, and employer coverage requirements. Its observed effects on coverage were substantial, reducing the uninsured rate significantly in expansion states. Its effects on workforce demand were also real, adding covered patients created demand for more primary-care providers. Unintended consequences included administrative burden on small practices navigating the new reporting requirements.

HIPAA in 1996 used the safety-and-quality lever to establish patient privacy rights in an era when electronic health records were beginning to spread. Its intended effect was to protect patient information. Its observed effects included significant IT investment across the healthcare industry, changes to how information is shared between providers and family members, and occasional frustrations when care coordination is hindered by overly cautious privacy interpretations. HIPAA also created new roles in healthcare compliance and health information management, expanding that portion of the workforce.

State-level scope-of-practice expansions for nurse practitioners and physician assistants illustrate how the licensure lever affects access to care. States that allow NPs to practice independently, without a physician supervision agreement, show improved access to primary care in rural and underserved areas. States that require physician supervision create administrative overhead that limits where NPs can practice and drives up costs. Texas sits between these extremes, and the ongoing policy debate is directly relevant to any student in this course who is considering a nursing or advanced-practice career.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A student is assigned to analyze how the decision by Texas not to expand Medicaid affects rural hospitals. She applies the six-step framework. Level of government: state (the Medicaid expansion decision is made at the state level within the federal ACA framework). Lever: coverage and payment. Intended effect of non-expansion: limit state Medicaid budget growth. Observed effect on patients: hundreds of thousands of Texans in the coverage gap remain uninsured. Effect on rural hospitals: higher volumes of uncompensated care erode operating margins, accelerating rural hospital closures. Effect on workforce: lower hospital revenue limits hiring and salary competitiveness in rural areas. Unintended consequence: rural hospital closures reduce local emergency access, shifting the cost of uncompensated care to remaining providers and increasing avoidable emergency transport miles. The student notes in her analysis that these effects were documented in peer-reviewed literature and in KFF state data, and that they are ongoing, not historical.

### Key terms

**CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services):** the federal agency that writes Medicare and Medicaid rules and controls roughly 40 percent of US health spending through coverage and payment decisions.

**FDA (Food and Drug Administration):** the federal agency that regulates drugs, devices, and biologics; its approvals carry federal preemption over conflicting state drug rules.

**Licensure:** the state-level authority determining who may legally deliver healthcare, with what training, and in what settings.

**Scope of practice:** the state-defined tasks and decisions a licensed practitioner is authorized to perform; varies significantly across states for nurse practitioners and physician assistants.

**Medicaid expansion:** the ACA provision allowing states to extend Medicaid to adults up to 138 percent of the federal poverty level; adopted by most but not all states, including not Texas.

**EMTALA:** the 1986 federal law requiring hospital emergency departments to screen and stabilize any patient regardless of ability to pay; enforced through Medicare exclusion.

**CLIA (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments):** federal quality standards that apply to every US clinical laboratory; a safety-and-quality lever used by the national government.

**Conditions of Participation:** the CMS minimum standards hospitals must meet to receive Medicare and Medicaid payments; the primary federal quality lever over hospitals.

**Federal preemption:** the legal principle that a valid federal rule overrides a conflicting state rule within the scope of federal authority.

**Public-health law:** the lever used by states and localities to mandate disease reporting, vaccination, quarantine, tobacco restrictions, and clean-water standards; most visible during outbreaks.

### Chapter summary

Healthcare in the United States is shaped by three levels of government, each using distinct tools. Local health departments handle outbreak response, inspection, and EMS. State governments control licensure, scope of practice, and co-administer Medicaid. The federal government sets payment rules through CMS, approves drugs through the FDA, funds research through NIH, and enforces workplace safety through OSHA. When levels conflict, federal preemption resolves some disputes, while state authority over health and welfare resolves others. Major policies, from EMTALA to the ACA to Medicaid expansion, can be analyzed using a six-step framework that identifies the level, the lever, the intended effect, the observed effect, the workforce consequences, and the unintended outcomes. Every student entering health science will work inside a system shaped by these decisions and will need to understand why the rules are what they are.
