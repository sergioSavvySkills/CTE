# What Makes Communication Work, and What Breaks It

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Name the components that make clinical communication effective
- Identify the patterns and barriers that make communication non-effective
- Explain why communication failures are treated as patient-safety events, not personality problems

A nurse walks into a room to discharge a patient. She runs through her checklist, hands over the papers, and asks, "Do you have any questions?" The patient says no. Three hours later, the patient calls the emergency line because he does not understand how to take his medication. The checklist was complete. The communication was not.

### Communication is a patient-safety skill

The Joint Commission, which accredits hospitals across the United States, tracks the root causes of serious patient-harm events. Communication failure appears at the top of that list year after year. A **sentinel event** is a patient-harm event serious enough to require a full root-cause investigation, and when investigators trace back what went wrong, they find a broken communication chain far more often than they find a broken machine or a missing supply.

This reframes what communication means in a clinical setting. It is not a soft skill or a personality asset. It is a measurable, teachable safety practice, and failing at it carries the same consequences as failing to wash your hands.

### Components of effective communication

Researchers studying clinical communication have identified a consistent set of behaviors that produce better patient outcomes and fewer errors. The Calgary-Cambridge guide and the SEGUE framework are two widely used medical-education tools that map these components in detail. The most important ones are below.

**Active listening** is the foundation. It means making eye contact, keeping an open body posture, paraphrasing what the patient says, summarizing before moving on, and, critically, not interrupting. Observational research by Beckman and Frankel found that clinicians interrupted patients' opening statements within a median of 18 seconds. A follow-up study by Singh Ospina and colleagues found the median was 11 seconds. The patient rarely finished naming their concern before the clinician redirected the conversation. What was missed in those first 11 seconds often did not surface again.

**Open questions** invite a full answer. "How have you been feeling since we last met?" gives the patient space to set the agenda. **Closed questions** pull for yes-or-no answers. "Did you take your medication this morning?" is a closed question that has a place in a structured assessment, but using only closed questions throughout an encounter means the patient's actual concern may never surface.

**Empathy statements** name the emotion without judging it. "That sounds really hard" is an empathy statement. "I feel so bad for you" is sympathy, a different move that shifts attention from the patient to the clinician. Empathy keeps the focus on the patient's experience and signals that it is safe to say more.

**Productive silence** is the pause after a difficult question. Three to five seconds of quiet often surfaces the real concern. New clinicians are trained to fill silence. Experienced ones learn to hold it.

**Precise language** means specific verbs and measurable values rather than approximations. It reduces the chance that a message lands differently than it was sent.

**Closed-loop confirmation**, also called **check-back**, is the TeamSTEPPS technique in which the receiver reads back or paraphrases a message to confirm it was received accurately. The sender then confirms or corrects. This one step catches errors that survive every other safeguard.

### Components of non-effective communication

Non-effective communication is not simply the absence of the good components. It has its own active patterns, each of which causes specific harm.

Interrupting and talking over a patient cuts off the opening statement before the full concern is named. This does not save time. Patients who are not heard often raise the unresolved concern later, at a worse moment, or not at all.

Using **jargon** without translation puts the patient in a position where they cannot engage with their own care. The AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit documents how widespread low health literacy is across adult populations and why clinicians should assume it rather than screen for it. A patient who does not understand "myocardial infarction" but nods along anyway is not a difficult patient. They are a patient who has been failed by the communication.

Minimizing emotion shuts the door. "Don't worry about that" or "plenty of people have worse" signals to the patient that their concern is not welcome. Once the door is shut, it usually stays shut for the rest of the encounter.

Missing nonverbal signals means not noticing that a patient's expression does not match their words, that a family member in the corner is quietly crying, or that the patient has leaned back and crossed their arms since you mentioned the medication change.

Assumptions and stereotypes lead a clinician to decide what a patient needs before asking. Assuming an elderly patient cannot understand a complex explanation, or assuming a young patient is healthy, replaces the actual patient with a mental shortcut.

### Barriers that derail even good technique

A clinician can know every technique and still struggle when barriers are present. Language differences, hearing or vision impairment, cognitive conditions, acute emotional distress, cultural differences in eye contact and silence norms, time pressure, and screen distraction from the electronic health record all degrade communication quality even when both parties are trying. Naming these barriers is not an excuse. It is the first step toward addressing them, which is the work of the next chapter.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A medical assistant calls a patient to review lab results over the phone. The patient speaks English as a second language and the connection is poor. The assistant reads through the results quickly using clinical terms, asks "Is that clear?", and the patient says yes. The next day, the patient's family calls asking what "elevated creatinine" means and whether they should go to the emergency room. The assistant used no banned words and no rude tone. But jargon reached a patient who could not engage with it, there was no check-back, and the question "Is that clear?" is a closed question that rarely surfaces confusion. Every non-effective component was present, quietly.

### Key terms

**Active listening:** eye contact, open posture, paraphrasing, summarizing, and not interrupting; the foundation of effective clinical communication.

**Open question:** a question that invites a full answer and lets the patient set the agenda ("How have you been feeling?").

**Closed question:** a yes-or-no or short-answer question used deliberately when a specific answer is needed, not as a default.

**Empathy statement:** naming the patient's emotion without judging it ("That sounds really hard"); distinct from sympathy.

**Productive silence:** a three-to-five second pause after a hard question that often surfaces the patient's real concern.

**Closed-loop confirmation (check-back):** the TeamSTEPPS technique in which the receiver reads back or paraphrases a message so the sender can confirm or correct it.

**Jargon:** technical vocabulary used without a plain-language translation; a barrier to patient engagement and a component of non-effective communication.

**Sentinel event:** a Joint Commission–defined serious patient-harm event; root-cause analysis of sentinel events consistently names communication failure as a leading contributor.

**Calgary-Cambridge guide:** a standardized medical-education framework that maps effective patient-communication components used in training clinicians worldwide.

### Chapter summary

Effective communication in healthcare is built from named, learnable components: active listening, open questions, empathy statements, productive silence, precise language, and closed-loop confirmation. Non-effective communication has its own active patterns: interrupting, jargon without translation, minimizing emotion, missing nonverbal signals, and assumption-driven shortcuts. Barriers including language, cognitive load, cultural norms, and time pressure can undermine even skilled communicators, and naming them is the first step to addressing them. Communication failure is not a personality flaw. It is a clinical error with measurable consequences, and treating it as such is the standard in every accredited healthcare setting.

### Review questions

1. A clinician asks a patient, "You're not in any pain right now, are you?" Name the non-effective communication component this question represents and rewrite it as an effective alternative.
2. Explain the difference between an empathy statement and a minimizing response. Give one example of each using the same patient concern as your starting point.
3. A root-cause investigation finds that a medication error was partly caused by a clinician not performing a check-back on a verbal order. Which effective communication component was missing, and what would it have looked like in practice?
