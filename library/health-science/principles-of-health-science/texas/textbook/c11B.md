# Industry Safety Standards in Healthcare

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Apply standard precautions and transmission-based precautions correctly to patient scenarios
- Perform the correct donning and doffing sequence for PPE based on precaution level
- Execute the RACE fire-response sequence and the PASS extinguisher technique
- Identify common facility emergency codes and describe the appropriate student response to each

On her first day of clinical rotation in a hospital, a student watches a nurse stop outside a patient room, read the yellow contact-precaution sign, go to the supply cart, pull a gown and gloves, put them on in a specific order, and only then open the door. An hour later, an overhead announcement says "Code Red, fourth floor east." Every staff member on the unit moves immediately, each one doing a different thing. Both situations are governed by safety standards: one built from decades of infection-control science, the other from fire-safety codes. This chapter teaches you what those standards require and why they work the way they do.

## Part 1: Infection Control and Standard Precautions

### The chain of infection

Infection spreads through a predictable sequence called the **chain of infection**: an infectious agent leaves a reservoir through a portal of exit, travels by some mode of transmission, enters a new host through a portal of entry, and infects a susceptible host. Every one of those six links must be intact for transmission to succeed. Break any single link and the chain fails. That logic is the foundation of every infection-control practice in healthcare.

The modes of transmission relevant in clinical settings are contact (touching an infected surface or person directly or indirectly), droplet (large respiratory particles that travel roughly six feet before falling), airborne (small particles that remain suspended in room air for extended periods), vehicle (contaminated food, water, or instruments), and vector (insects). Most healthcare-associated infections spread by contact and droplet, which is why hand hygiene and PPE are the dominant interventions.

### Standard precautions: every patient, every time

**Standard precautions** are the CDC's minimum set of infection-prevention practices applied to every patient at every contact, regardless of diagnosis or suspected infection status. The logic is that any patient can harbor a pathogen not yet identified, so treating every contact as a potential exposure is safer than waiting for test results.

Standard precautions include five elements. First, hand hygiene: performed using the WHO Five Moments framework, before patient contact, before an aseptic task, after body-fluid exposure risk, after patient contact, and after touching the patient's surroundings. Second, PPE as indicated by the expected contact. Third, safe injection practices, meaning one needle and one syringe for one patient at one time. Fourth, respiratory hygiene and cough etiquette. Fifth, cleaning and disinfecting surfaces and shared equipment between patients.

**Alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR)** is the preferred method for hand hygiene in most situations. It is faster, more effective at killing most pathogens, and less damaging to skin than repeated soap-and-water washing. The two exceptions are important: when hands are visibly soiled with blood or body fluids, and when caring for patients with C. difficile or norovirus. Both of those pathogens produce spores that alcohol does not kill. For spore-formers, soap and water with physical scrubbing is required.

### Transmission-based precautions

When standard precautions alone are not sufficient, CDC guidelines call for adding **transmission-based precautions**, layered on top of standard precautions, chosen based on how a specific pathogen travels.

Contact precautions, used for pathogens spread by touching (C. diff, MRSA, VRE), require a gown and gloves before entering the patient's room. Droplet precautions, used for pathogens spread by large respiratory particles (influenza, pertussis, meningococcal disease), add a surgical mask and eye protection. Airborne precautions, used for pathogens spread by small particles that stay suspended in room air (tuberculosis, measles, varicella), require an N95 respirator, eye protection, and placement of the patient in an **Airborne Infection Isolation Room (AIIR)**: a negative-pressure room that keeps contaminated air from flowing into the hallway.

### Donning and doffing sequences

**Donning and doffing** refer to the ordered process of putting on and removing PPE. The sequence is not arbitrary: it is designed so that clean items are always put on before contaminated items are touched, and contaminated items are always removed in a way that prevents touching your skin or face with contaminated surfaces.

Donning order: gown first, then mask or respirator, then goggles or face shield, then gloves last. The gown is tied before putting on anything that goes on the face, because tying the gown involves touching the back of the neck.

Doffing order: gloves first (the most contaminated item), then goggles or face shield, then gown, then mask or respirator last. Hand hygiene is performed immediately after removing gloves and again after the entire sequence is complete. Removing the mask last protects the face and mucous membranes from any contaminated particles that drifted during earlier steps.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A nursing student is assigned to care for a patient under airborne precautions for suspected tuberculosis. Before entering the room, she confirms the AIIR door is closed and the negative-pressure indicator is lit. She puts on her gown, then fits her N95 respirator and performs a seal check, then puts on eye protection, then pulls on gloves. Inside the room, she completes vital signs and a brief assessment. Exiting, she removes her gloves at the door, removes her goggles in the hallway, removes her gown by rolling it inward so the contaminated outer surface folds on itself, then removes her N95 by the straps without touching the front. She performs hand hygiene before and after the mask removal. No self-contamination occurred because she followed the sequence without shortcuts.

### Healthcare-associated infections

Standard precautions exist in part because healthcare settings create unique infection risks. **Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs)** are infections patients acquire during the course of receiving care for other conditions. The major HAI categories include central-line associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI), catheter-associated urinary tract infections (CAUTI), surgical site infections (SSI), ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP), and C. difficile infections. These are largely preventable. Standard precautions form the foundational layer of every HAI prevention bundle.

## Part 2: Fire Safety and Emergency Response

### Fire prevention in healthcare

Healthcare facilities present fire hazards that most other buildings do not. Oxygen is used routinely for patient care, and while oxygen itself does not burn, it accelerates combustion dramatically: a small spark in an oxygen-enriched environment can produce a fire far larger and faster than the same spark in normal air. Other hazards include electrical equipment concentrated throughout the building, flammable cleaning and disinfecting agents, and the presence of patients who cannot self-evacuate.

Prevention focuses on controlling ignition sources near oxygen, enforcing no-smoking policies throughout the campus, storing flammable materials away from heat sources, inspecting and maintaining electrical equipment, and keeping fire-stop materials intact in walls and ceilings. Zone valves that shut off medical gas to sections of the building are a key feature that students should know how to identify.

### RACE: the fire-response sequence

When a fire is discovered in a healthcare setting, the response follows the **RACE** sequence: Rescue, Alarm, Contain, Extinguish or Evacuate.

**Rescue** means moving anyone in immediate danger away from the fire. In a hospital, this may mean a patient who cannot move on their own. Rescue comes first because no other step matters if patients are burning.

**Alarm** means activating the fire alarm system, pulling the nearest pull-station, and calling the facility's fire code (often "Code Red"). Alarm before fighting the fire, because if the fire grows beyond your control, others need to know.

**Contain** means closing doors and windows to limit the spread of smoke and fire. In healthcare facilities with fire-rated doors, this is especially important. Smoke kills more people in healthcare fires than flames do.

**Extinguish or Evacuate** is a conditional step. A small fire, no larger than a trash can, that you can identify, in a room with a clear exit behind you, with a trained responder present, may be extinguished. All other situations call for evacuation.

### PASS: operating a fire extinguisher

When extinguishing is appropriate, the technique is **PASS**: Pull the pin from the extinguisher handle. Aim the nozzle at the base of the fire, not the flames. Squeeze the handle to discharge. Sweep the nozzle side to side across the base.

Four conditions indicate you should not attempt extinguishment: the fire is larger than a trash can, you do not know what is burning (because extinguisher class must match the fuel), smoke is blocking your exit path, or you have not been trained on extinguisher use. In all of those cases, contain and evacuate.

### Evacuating patients

Patient evacuation in healthcare follows a priority order: ambulatory patients who can walk are moved first, then patients in wheelchairs, then patients who are bedbound and require staff assistance. Horizontal evacuation, moving patients to a different smoke compartment on the same floor through fire doors, is preferred over vertical evacuation. Stairs are used when horizontal evacuation is not possible. Elevators are never used during a fire: a loss of power traps occupants inside.

### Emergency codes

Healthcare facilities use color-coded overhead announcements, called emergency codes, to communicate the type and location of emergencies without creating panic among patients and visitors. The exact codes used vary by facility, but Texas hospitals have moved toward standardized patterns through the Texas Hospital Association.

**Code Red** signals a fire. **Code Blue** signals a cardiac or respiratory arrest and summons the resuscitation team. **Code Gray** signals a combative person. **Code Pink** signals an infant abduction. **Code Silver** signals an active shooter or armed person. **Code Black** signals a bomb threat at facilities that use this code; this designation varies more than others.

The **Rapid Response Team (RRT)** is not technically an emergency code but is one of the most important response systems students will encounter. When a patient shows signs of clinical deterioration before reaching full cardiac arrest, any staff member can call the RRT. Early intervention catches deterioration before it becomes a cardiac arrest, which dramatically improves outcomes.

Students on clinical rotation are not expected to lead any of these responses. The appropriate student role is to call for help immediately, stay with the patient if safe to do so, assist the responding team as directed, and not leave until formally relieved.

### Other emergency situations

Chemical spills require evacuation of the immediate area, containment if possible without personal risk, and immediate consultation of the **SDS** for the substance involved. The SDS contains the hazard information and recommended emergency response for the specific chemical. All chemical spills are reported to a supervisor.

Workplace violence situations call for de-escalation if the student has training, otherwise the priority is creating distance, alerting security, and documenting the event per facility policy. Students should never attempt to physically intervene in a violent situation.

Severe weather and natural disasters trigger facility shelter-in-place or evacuation plans, depending on the hazard. Mass-casualty events activate the facility's incident command system, and all staff report to their assigned staging areas per the disaster plan. The student's job is to follow direction from facility leadership, not to improvise.

**Incident reporting** is the final safety standard covered here and one of the most important habits for students to develop. Every adverse event, near-miss, and unsafe condition is reported: factually, objectively, and without delay. The purpose of incident reporting is system improvement, not individual blame. A student who observes an unsafe condition and does not report it is not protecting anyone.

### Key terms

**Standard precautions:** the CDC's minimum infection-prevention practices applied to every patient at every contact, including hand hygiene, PPE as indicated, safe injection practices, respiratory etiquette, and surface cleaning.

**Chain of infection:** the six-link sequence of agent, reservoir, portal of exit, transmission, portal of entry, and susceptible host; breaking any one link stops spread.

**Transmission-based precautions:** contact, droplet, and airborne precautions added to standard precautions for specific pathogens.

**Airborne Infection Isolation Room (AIIR):** a negative-pressure patient room required for airborne-precaution patients such as those with tuberculosis or measles.

**Alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR):** the preferred hand-hygiene agent for most clinical situations; not effective against spore-forming organisms such as C. difficile and norovirus.

**Donning and doffing:** the specified ordered sequence for putting on and removing PPE to prevent self-contamination.

**RACE:** the fire-response sequence: Rescue, Alarm, Contain, Extinguish or Evacuate.

**PASS:** the fire-extinguisher technique: Pull the pin, Aim at the base, Squeeze the handle, Sweep side to side.

**Code Red / Blue / Gray / Pink / Silver / Black:** facility emergency codes for fire, cardiac arrest, combative person, infant abduction, active shooter, and bomb threat respectively.

**Rapid Response Team (RRT):** the clinical team called when a patient shows signs of deterioration before reaching cardiac arrest.

**SDS:** Safety Data Sheet; the OSHA and GHS 16-section chemical-hazard document consulted during chemical spills and exposures.

### Chapter summary

Industry safety standards in healthcare fall into two broad families. The first is infection control: standard precautions applied to every patient every time, with transmission-based precautions layered on for specific pathogens. The PPE selection and donning and doffing sequences are not preferences, they are evidence-based procedures with a specific rationale at each step. The second family covers fire and emergency response. RACE organizes the fire-response steps in priority order, PASS organizes extinguisher use, and the emergency code system gives all staff a common language for rapid communication across a large facility. Healthcare-associated infections, fire injuries, and emergency harms are largely preventable when these standards are followed consistently. Students who know the standards before their first rotation arrive ready to protect their patients.

### Review questions

1. A nurse is caring for a patient on contact precautions for MRSA and then immediately goes to see a patient on airborne precautions for suspected tuberculosis. List, in order, every PPE-related step the nurse must take between leaving the first patient's room and entering the second. Explain what would happen if the nurse skipped the doffing step and went straight to donning the next PPE set.
2. A fire is discovered in a storage closet just down the hallway from two patient rooms. One patient is ambulatory, one is bedbound. Walk through the RACE steps for this situation, making the decision at the Extinguish-or-Evacuate step and explaining your reasoning. In what order do you move the patients, and why?
3. An overhead announcement says "Code Silver, main lobby." You are a student in a patient room on the third floor. Describe what Code Silver means, identify your appropriate role as a student (lead, assist, stay out of the way, or evacuate), and explain why a student should not improvise a response to this type of emergency.
