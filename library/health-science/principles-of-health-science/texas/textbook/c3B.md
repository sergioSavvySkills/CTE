# Communicating Across Difference, Putting Skills into Practice

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Demonstrate effective communication skills through observed practice, not just description
- Use a qualified medical interpreter correctly during a clinical encounter
- Apply specific communication adaptations for patients who vary by language, culture, age, ability, and health literacy
- Use the acknowledge-explain-next-step pattern with upset or grieving patients

## Part 1: From Knowing to Doing

A student in a clinical skills lab can name every component of effective communication. She can list open questions, empathy statements, and check-back without hesitation. Then she walks into a role-play with a peer playing a Limited English Proficient patient, a student playing the interpreter, and a timed scenario, and she addresses the interpreter instead of the patient, uses three compound sentences in a row, and forgets to perform a check-back. Knowing the components and performing them under pressure are two different skills. This chapter is about performance.

### Why demonstration matters

The difference between this chapter and the previous one is captured in a single word: demonstrate. Identifying effective communication is a knowledge task. Demonstrating it in a live encounter is a performance task, and performance only develops through practice, observation, and corrective feedback.

The skill of responding to patient needs begins before any technique is applied. It begins with the opener. The most important question in any clinical encounter is not a diagnostic question. It is an open one: "What brings you in today?" followed by silence. The clinician's job in the first 60 to 90 seconds is to listen while the patient sets the agenda, not to redirect, not to jump to the chief complaint field on the intake form. The Calgary-Cambridge guide names this the "initiating the session" phase, and research consistently shows that patients who are allowed to complete their opening statement in the first visit return with fewer unresolved concerns.

After the opening, the clinician has a fuller picture of what the patient actually needs. That picture changes what happens next, including which adaptations are necessary.

### Communicating with patients who speak a different language

When a patient has limited English proficiency, using a **qualified medical interpreter** is a legal requirement under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 for any healthcare organization that receives federal funding. A qualified interpreter is a trained, vetted professional, in person, by phone, or by video. A family member is not a substitute, and a minor child is absolutely not a substitute. Studies show that family interpretation leads to omissions, errors, and breaches of patient privacy even when the family member is trying to help.

When working through an interpreter, the rules are specific. Speak directly to the patient, not to the interpreter. Say "How long have you had the pain?" not "Ask her how long she's had the pain." Use short, complete sentences with one idea each. Pause after each sentence to allow interpretation. Avoid compound questions ("Are you taking your medication, and has anyone helped you with that?") because the interpreter cannot render two questions simultaneously without something being lost.

The temptation to use whoever is available in the room is strong, especially when time is short. Resist it. The consequences of a family-member interpretation error can include a patient receiving the wrong dose, misunderstanding their diagnosis, or not understanding the urgency of a follow-up appointment.

### Communicating across cultural difference

Culture shapes nearly every aspect of a clinical encounter: eye contact norms, silence norms, family decision-making structures, gender preferences, dietary restrictions, and what kinds of questions feel invasive. The **National CLAS Standards** from the HHS Office of Minority Health provide a federal framework for culturally responsive care, and the Americans with Disabilities Act covers communication access requirements for patients with disabilities.

No clinician can memorize every cultural norm, and attempting to apply a rule for every cultural group leads to stereotyping. The practical approach is to ask. "Is there anything about how I communicate or what I do that would help you feel more comfortable today?" That one open question surfaces more useful information than any cultural checklist.

When a patient requests a **gender-concordant clinician**, meaning a same-gender provider, that request is a communication and modesty adaptation that should be honored whenever staffing permits. Refusing or dismissing the request damages trust and may cause the patient to withhold important clinical information.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A nursing student is conducting a pain assessment with an older Somali patient through a phone interpreter. The patient has been giving brief, one-word answers and making almost no eye contact. The student initially reads this as the patient being unhelpful. Her supervisor reminds her that in many East African cultures, prolonged eye contact with a younger person in an authority role can feel disrespectful, and brief answers to strangers are normal in a first encounter. The student adjusts her approach, slows down, allows longer pauses, and uses more open questions about the patient's experience rather than yes-or-no pain scales. By the end of the encounter the patient is speaking in full sentences.

## Part 2: Adapting for Age, Ability, and Emotional State

### Communicating with pediatric patients

Communicating with children requires adapting to developmental stage, not just age. A five-year-old and a twelve-year-old require completely different vocabulary, completely different levels of explanation, and completely different amounts of caregiver involvement.

For young children, use their words for body parts, be concrete, and be honest about what will hurt. "This will feel like a pinch" is more useful and more ethical than "this won't hurt a bit." Involve the caregiver, but also address the child directly so they are a participant in their own care. When a minor is old enough to understand and participate, obtaining **assent** from the child alongside parental consent is both a communication practice and an ethical one.

For adolescents, Texas law grants specific confidentiality rights for certain categories of care, including reproductive health and substance use. A clinician who immediately involves a parent without asking the adolescent may prevent honest disclosure on topics that are clinically critical. Open the encounter with confidentiality framing: explain what you keep private and what you are required to report.

### Communicating with older adults

Older adults deserve the same respect for autonomy as any other patient, and communication that treats them as less capable is both inaccurate and harmful. **Elderspeak** is the infantilizing tone often used with older patients, including shortened words ("good job"), collective "we" ("how are we feeling today?"), and sing-song pacing. Research connects elderspeak to worse patient outcomes and lower patient satisfaction.

Practical adaptations for older adults include allowing adequate time, checking whether the patient has their hearing aids in and glasses on before beginning, including a **care-partner** with the patient's explicit consent, and confirming understanding through teach-back rather than "does that make sense?"

For patients with mild cognitive impairment, use short sentences, one instruction at a time, repeat key points without frustration, and confirm with the care-partner that the information was accurately received.

### Communicating with patients with disabilities

A Deaf patient has the right to a sign-language interpreter, not to a clinician who writes notes back and forth. Face the patient directly, do not cover your mouth, and do not shout. An interpreter makes communication possible; the clinician's job is still to address the patient, not the interpreter.

For a blind patient, describe the environment and everything you are about to do before you do it. "I'm going to put a blood pressure cuff on your left arm now" orients the patient and reduces anxiety. Read any materials aloud.

For a patient with significant cognitive impairment, the **acknowledge-explain-next-step** pattern is useful: acknowledge the patient's immediate experience first, then offer a brief clear explanation, then name one concrete next step. Avoid presenting multiple options at once.

### Communicating with upset, scared, or grieving patients

Information does not land well when emotion is high. A patient who just received a frightening diagnosis is not in a state to process a detailed treatment plan in the same conversation. The acknowledge-explain-next-step pattern applies here as well: name the emotion first without rushing past it, then offer whatever explanation is appropriate, then offer one concrete next step the patient can focus on.

**Health literacy universal precautions** means assuming limited health literacy by default and using plain language with every patient, not just those who appear to have lower education levels. The AHRQ toolkit establishes that roughly half of US adults read at or below an 8th-grade level, and health stress temporarily lowers everyone's ability to process new information. Teach-back, asking the patient to explain instructions back in their own words, is the gold standard for confirming that information actually landed.

### Key terms

**Qualified medical interpreter:** a trained, vetted interpreter (in person, phone, or video) used with Limited English Proficient patients; family members are not substitutes.

**Teach-back:** the AHRQ technique in which the patient explains instructions back in their own words to confirm understanding.

**Health literacy universal precautions:** the AHRQ framework that assumes limited health literacy by default and uses plain language with every patient.

**Gender-concordant clinician:** a same-gender provider offered when a patient requests one; a cultural and modesty adaptation.

**Elderspeak:** an infantilizing tone used with older adults, including shortened words and sing-song pacing; specifically harmful to patient outcomes and satisfaction.

**Acknowledge-explain-next-step:** the communication pattern for upset, scared, or grieving patients: name the feeling first, then explain, then offer one concrete next step.

**Assent (pediatric):** an age-appropriate agreement obtained from a minor alongside a parent's or guardian's formal consent.

**Care-partner:** a family member or designated support person included in the encounter with the patient's explicit consent, typically for older or cognitively impaired patients.

**National CLAS Standards:** the HHS Office of Minority Health framework for language access, interpreter use, and culturally responsive care in healthcare settings.

### Chapter summary

Demonstrating effective communication means performing it under real conditions, not describing it on a worksheet. The performance starts with the open opener and patient-directed silence, then adapts continuously to the specific patient in front of you. Using a qualified medical interpreter correctly, applying culturally responsive practices without stereotyping, adapting to developmental stage, disability, and cognitive status, and recognizing when emotion must be acknowledged before information can land are all performance skills. Health literacy universal precautions and teach-back apply to every patient by default, not just those who appear to need them. The legal and ethical frameworks, Title VI, the ADA, and the National CLAS Standards, are not background knowledge. They are the floor beneath the clinical practice.

### Review questions

1. A patient's teenage daughter offers to interpret for her mother, who speaks only Spanish. You are running behind schedule. Describe what you do and why, citing the specific rule that applies.
2. A 14-year-old comes in alone for a routine visit and, before you can call the parent in, quietly asks about contraception. What communication approach do you take, and what confidentiality framing do you use?
3. You have just told an older patient that their biopsy came back positive for cancer. Before explaining next steps, what do you say or do first, and why?
