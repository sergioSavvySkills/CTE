# Laws Governing the Health Science Industry

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the major federal and Texas state laws that govern daily clinical practice
- Locate primary legal sources using ecfr.gov and statutes.capitol.texas.gov
- Name the enforcing agency for each major law
- Explain why the same healthcare workflow is often governed by both federal and state law

A new employee at a Texas clinic is asked to witness a patient signing forms during the check-in process. The forms include a Notice of Privacy Practices, a consent to treatment, and an authorization to bill insurance. Each of those three documents exists because of a different law. The Notice of Privacy Practices exists because of HIPAA. The consent to treatment reflects state consent law. The billing authorization connects to Medicare and Medicaid conditions of participation. Knowing which law governs which form is not optional for a healthcare worker. It is foundational.

### Why laws matter in clinical practice

Laws in healthcare do not just regulate what organizations can and cannot do at the institutional level. They reach all the way to individual clinical workflows. The sharps container on the wall, the two-identifier process before a blood draw, the sign on the door about patient rights, the privacy screen on the EHR workstation: each of these exists because a specific law or regulation requires it.

The vocabulary of legal research matters here. A **statute** is a law passed by a legislature: Congress at the federal level, the Texas Legislature at the state level. A **regulation** is an agency-issued rule that implements a statute. Congress passes a law requiring workplace safety; OSHA issues the regulation that specifies exactly what the requirement looks like in practice. Both are binding. The regulation typically has more operational detail than the statute, because it is written by subject-matter experts within the agency.

Primary legal sources, meaning the actual statutes and regulations, rank above secondary sources like textbooks, news articles, and health-law summaries. Secondary sources can be useful for context, but they can also be outdated or incomplete. When you need to know what the law actually says, you go to the primary source.

### How to find a law

For federal laws and regulations, the authoritative sources are available free online. The **eCFR** (electronic Code of Federal Regulations at ecfr.gov) is where federal regulations live. The United States Code (at uscode.house.gov) is where federal statutes live. Agency websites, including HHS, CMS, OSHA, and the FDA, publish their own guidance and plain-language summaries that link back to the primary documents.

For Texas laws, the Texas Statutes are at statutes.capitol.texas.gov. This includes the Texas Occupations Code, the Texas Health and Safety Code, and other relevant codes. The **Texas Administrative Code**, which is where Texas agency regulations live, is at texreg.sos.state.tx.us. The Texas Board of Nursing, the Texas Medical Board, and the Texas Department of State Health Services all publish their own position statements and guidance on their websites.

Credible secondary sources include Cornell Legal Information Institute (law.cornell.edu), KFF (formerly Kaiser Family Foundation), and NCSL (National Conference of State Legislatures). These sources summarize laws accurately and include citations to primary sources, but they are not substitutes for the primary sources themselves when you need to cite or quote the actual law.

### Major federal laws

The table of major federal laws governing US healthcare is long. The survey-level knowledge every student needs covers the following.

**HIPAA** (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, 1996) covers patient privacy, security of health data, and breach notification. Its regulations live at 45 CFR Parts 160 and 164. The enforcing agency is the HHS Office for Civil Rights.

**EMTALA** (Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act, 1986) requires that any hospital with an emergency department must screen every patient who arrives for emergency care regardless of ability to pay, and must stabilize patients with emergency medical conditions before transfer or discharge. Enforcement is handled by CMS and HHS OIG. The statute is at 42 U.S.C. §1395dd.

The **Anti-Kickback Statute** (42 U.S.C. §1320a-7b) and the **Stark Law** (42 U.S.C. §1395nn) prohibit certain financial relationships between healthcare providers and the entities that refer patients to them or receive referrals from them. The concern is that financial incentives should not distort medical decisions. Both are enforced by HHS OIG and the Department of Justice.

The **Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act** gives the FDA authority over drugs, medical devices, and biologics. This is the legal basis for FDA approval of medications and devices before they reach patients.

**CLIA** (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments, 1988) sets quality standards for all clinical laboratories in the US, regardless of size or location. It is enforced by CMS.

The **OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard** (29 CFR 1910.1030) requires healthcare employers to protect workers from exposure to blood and other potentially infectious materials. It mandates exposure control plans, engineering controls (like safety needles), personal protective equipment, training, and post-exposure follow-up. This is the law behind every sharps disposal container and the training that new healthcare workers receive about needlestick prevention.

The **Americans with Disabilities Act** (1990) requires healthcare facilities to provide accessible care, including effective communication with patients who are deaf or have other disabilities.

The **Affordable Care Act** (2010) expanded health insurance coverage, established the marketplace, and introduced value-based payment models in Medicare. It also made certain preventive services available without cost-sharing.

The **21st Century Cures Act** (2016) includes an information-blocking rule that requires healthcare entities to share patient health information with patients and their apps without obstruction.

**GINA** (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act, 2008) protects genetic information from being used in employment decisions or health insurance underwriting.

### Major Texas state laws

Texas adds its own layer of legal requirements, and in some areas goes beyond what federal law requires.

The **Texas Nurse Practice Act** (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 301) defines the scope of practice for RNs, LVNs, and APRNs in Texas, sets education and licensure requirements, and grants the Texas Board of Nursing authority to discipline nurses who violate the act.

The **Texas Medical Practice Act** (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 151) does the same for physicians, with the Texas Medical Board as the enforcing agency.

The **Texas Medical Records Privacy Act** (Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 181) is Texas's state-level health privacy statute. In some respects it is stricter than HIPAA, particularly around the marketing use of patient information and the right of patients to access their records.

The **Texas Medical Liability Act** (Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 74), enacted in 2003, is the state's tort-reform statute. It caps non-economic damages for physician defendants at $250,000 and requires an expert report within 120 days of a malpractice claim filing.

**Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 166** is the Texas Advance Directives Act. It governs directives to physicians (living wills), medical power of attorney, and out-of-hospital DNR orders. Students on clinical rotations will see these documents in use in hospitals and long-term care settings.

The **Texas Administrative Code Title 25**, issued by the Texas Department of State Health Services, is the main body of Texas health regulations covering everything from facility licensing standards to communicable disease reporting.

### When federal and state law both apply

In healthcare, the same workflow is often governed by both a federal law and a Texas state law. This is not a mistake or a redundancy. It reflects the structure of US law, in which states may add requirements on top of federal ones when the state rule is stricter.

The governing principle is called **preemption**. Federal law generally preempts (overrides) state law when the two conflict and Congress has indicated that federal law is exclusive in that area. But when a state law provides greater protection to patients or workers than the federal law does, the state law generally controls in that state.

An example: HIPAA is the federal floor for patient privacy. The Texas Medical Records Privacy Act adds requirements that go beyond HIPAA. A Texas healthcare provider must comply with both. The more protective rule controls, which means a Texas provider faces stricter obligations than a provider in a state without a comparable state privacy statute.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A medical assistant in a Texas clinic receives a call from a marketing company that wants to use the clinic's patient list to advertise a new health product. The caller says the patients already signed HIPAA consent forms. The MA is not sure whether this is allowed. A quick check of the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act, Chapter 181 of the Texas Health and Safety Code, would reveal that Texas law restricts this kind of marketing use of patient information even where a general HIPAA authorization might otherwise permit it. The stricter Texas rule controls. The MA should decline and route the request to compliance.

### Key terms

**Statute:** a law passed by a legislature; the primary legal source at the federal (US Code) or state (Texas Statutes) level.

**Regulation:** an agency-issued rule implementing a statute; published in the Code of Federal Regulations or Texas Administrative Code.

**eCFR:** the electronic Code of Federal Regulations (ecfr.gov); the authoritative source for federal regulations.

**EMTALA:** the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (1986); requires medical screening and stabilization in any hospital ED regardless of ability to pay.

**Anti-Kickback Statute / Stark Law:** federal laws prohibiting certain financial relationships that could distort medical decisions, enforced by HHS OIG and DOJ.

**CLIA:** Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (1988); federal quality standards for all US clinical laboratories.

**OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard:** 29 CFR 1910.1030; workplace-safety rules for blood and other potentially infectious materials exposure.

**Texas Medical Records Privacy Act:** Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 181; state privacy statute, stricter than HIPAA in some respects.

**Texas Administrative Code Title 25:** the main body of Texas health regulations issued by the Department of State Health Services.

**Preemption:** the doctrine by which a federal rule overrides a conflicting state rule (or vice versa when the state rule is stricter and Congress has not preempted that area).

### Chapter summary

Healthcare is one of the most heavily regulated industries in the United States, governed by overlapping layers of federal statutes, federal regulations, state statutes, and state agency rules. Each layer has a specific enforcing agency and a specific set of daily-practice implications. Locating primary sources, the actual statutes and regulations, is a skill that every healthcare worker needs. The eCFR and Texas statutes online are the starting points. When federal and state law both govern the same activity, the stricter rule controls in that state. Understanding which law governs which workflow is not academic preparation for healthcare. It is a daily operational requirement.

### Review questions

1. A hospital employee receives a call from a police officer asking for information about a patient who was in a car accident. The officer says the patient gave verbal permission. Which federal law governs this situation, which agency enforces it, and what steps must the employee take before disclosing any information?
2. A clinical laboratory technician is told that a required proficiency testing document has not been filed this year. Which federal law governs this requirement, which agency enforces it, and what is the consequence of continued noncompliance?
3. A Texas clinic wants to send text-message health promotions to its patient list without individual authorization, arguing that HIPAA permits this for "healthcare operations." Using the preemption framework, explain why this reasoning may be legally incorrect in Texas even if it would be acceptable in another state.
