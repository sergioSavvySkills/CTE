# Cultural and Diverse Influences on Healthcare Delivery

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify specific cultural, linguistic, religious, and socioeconomic influences that shape how patients seek and experience care
- Explain how implicit bias affects clinical decisions and patient outcomes
- Name the federal frameworks that require culturally responsive care
- Connect a contemporary healthcare practice to the cultural or structural influence that created it

A Latinx patient arrives at a Texas clinic with a 10-year-old child acting as interpreter. The physician has a detailed conversation with the child about the patient's symptoms, writes a prescription, and marks the visit complete. What went wrong? Three things: the patient's privacy was compromised in front of a family member, the child may not have accurately translated medical terms, and the physician relied on a minor to carry clinical information that the patient had a legal right to receive in her own language. That scenario happens every day, and it happens because providers have not identified the cultural and linguistic influences shaping the encounter. This chapter names those influences so students can recognize and respond to them.

### Why Culture Is a Clinical Variable

Culture is not background information about a patient. It is a variable that directly affects whether a patient seeks care, what they tell the provider, how they understand the diagnosis, whether they follow through with treatment, and how they make decisions about end-of-life care. A provider who treats culture as irrelevant to clinical decisions is missing information that changes outcomes.

**Social determinants of health**, the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age, include income, housing, food access, education, and neighborhood environment. These socioeconomic influences shape health outcomes more powerfully than most clinical interventions. A patient with type 2 diabetes who cannot afford insulin, lives in a food desert, works two jobs without sick leave, and commutes 45 minutes to the clinic has a different treatment reality than the demographic profile on the chart suggests. Providers who do not identify these influences cannot design treatments that work in a patient's actual life.

**Health disparities**, the documented outcome gaps between population groups, are not random. Black maternal mortality rates in the US are approximately 2.5 times those of white women. Indigenous Americans experience type 2 diabetes at rates far higher than the national average. LGBTQ+ individuals report higher rates of avoiding necessary care due to fear of discrimination. These patterns are rooted in specific cultural, structural, and historical influences that providers can learn to identify and respond to.

### The Influences Worth Knowing

**Race and ethnicity** affect care through two channels: direct clinical bias and historical trust gaps. Research by AHRQ documents that Black and Hispanic patients receive lower-quality pain management, less complete cardiac workups, and different medication choices compared to white patients with identical presentations. These patterns persist after controlling for insurance status and income. Separately, documented historical abuses, the Tuskegee syphilis study, forced sterilizations of Indigenous and Latinx women, the use of Henrietta Lacks's cancer cells without consent, created trust gaps that remain active today. A Black patient who hesitates before agreeing to a blood draw is not being irrational.

**Language** is both a civil-rights requirement and a patient-safety issue. Patients who are **Limited English Proficient (LEP)** have the right to a qualified interpreter in federally funded healthcare settings under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. "Qualified" means trained, vetted, and able to accurately translate medical terminology. Family members and children, including bilingual 10-year-olds, are not qualified interpreters for substantive clinical conversations. Research consistently shows that patients with LEP who receive care through unqualified interpreters have more adverse events, more medication errors, and lower satisfaction than those served by qualified interpreters.

**Religion and spirituality** shape dietary requirements, openness to certain treatments, end-of-life decisions, and comfort during illness. A Jehovah's Witness patient may refuse blood products on religious grounds, requiring alternative surgical planning. A Muslim patient observing Ramadan may need medication timing adjusted. An Orthodox Jewish patient may require kosher meal options during a hospital stay. A patient from many Indigenous traditions may want a traditional healer or spiritual ceremony integrated into their care plan. These are not exceptions to standard care, they are the standard, because patients are always whole people whose beliefs and practices do not stop at the clinic door.

**Immigration status** creates access barriers and safety concerns that directly affect health-seeking behavior. Patients who fear that seeking care will expose them to immigration enforcement may delay care until a condition becomes severe. Emergency-only Medicaid eligibility for undocumented immigrants means that preventive care, prenatal visits, and chronic-disease management may be inaccessible. Sanctuary-policy clinics address this by establishing internal policies limiting cooperation with immigration enforcement, creating conditions under which patients can seek care without fear.

**Gender identity and sexual orientation** shape care in ways that many providers are unprepared for. LGBTQ+ patients face documented disparities including higher rates of depression, suicide risk, and avoidance of preventive care. Incorrect pronoun use, assumptions about anatomy, and experiences of judgment or outright refusal of care by providers create the conditions for those disparities. Affirming care for transgender and gender-diverse patients includes using correct names and pronouns, taking a clinical history based on anatomy present rather than assumed anatomy, and creating a clinical environment where patients do not have to fear discrimination.

**Disability**, including physical, sensory, cognitive, and psychiatric disability, requires both legal accommodations and clinical communication adaptations. The **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** requires healthcare settings to provide accessible facilities, effective communication (sign-language interpretation in the ED, for example), and equal access to services. Beyond legal compliance, clinical encounters with patients who have disabilities require adjustment in language, pacing, and decision-making approach. A patient with an intellectual disability may need information presented more slowly, in plain language, and with a trusted support person present, without the provider talking over them to the support person as though they are not there.

### Implicit Bias and Patient Safety

**Implicit bias** refers to unconscious attitudes and stereotypes that affect clinical decisions without the provider being aware. It is not about bad intentions. Research shows that providers across racial backgrounds hold measurable implicit biases that affect the speed and quality of clinical decisions, particularly for pain management, cardiac workup, and maternal care. The Joint Commission and the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality classify implicit bias as a patient-safety issue, not a political or social issue. Providers who are unaware of their own biases cannot correct for them.

### Federal Frameworks That Require a Response

The **CLAS Standards**, the National Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services Standards published by the HHS Office of Minority Health, provide the federal framework for culturally responsive care. They establish expectations for providing language services, engaging communities in culturally appropriate health promotion, and recruiting and supporting a diverse healthcare workforce. CLAS compliance is built into the accreditation standards of the Joint Commission, meaning that providing qualified interpreters, translated materials, and culturally responsive staff training are not optional practices in accredited facilities.

**Title VI** of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 is the legal foundation for language-access obligations in federally funded healthcare programs. The ADA covers communication and access accommodations for patients with disabilities. **NIMHD**, the NIH National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities, funds the research that documents the disparities this chapter names, and that research drives the policy and clinical changes that follow.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> An emergency department nurse is triaging two patients with identical vital signs and self-reported pain scores of 8 out of 10 for back pain. One is a 45-year-old white male; the other is a 45-year-old Black female with the same presentation. Studies show that in this situation, the Black female patient statistically receives lower doses of analgesic medication and longer waits to initial dosing. The nurse, aware of this research, deliberately slows down before writing the order and asks herself: "Am I recommending this dose because of a clinical reason, or because of an assumption?" She writes the same order for both patients and documents her clinical reasoning. That deliberate pause is a skill, and it has to be practiced to become automatic.

### Key terms

**CLAS Standards:** the HHS Office of Minority Health's National Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services Standards; the federal framework requiring interpreter services, translated materials, and culturally responsive care in healthcare settings.

**Title VI (Civil Rights Act of 1964):** the legal foundation for language-access obligations in healthcare programs that receive federal funding.

**Limited English Proficient (LEP):** the designation for patients whose primary language is not English; entitled to qualified interpretation under Title VI in federally funded healthcare settings.

**Implicit bias:** unconscious attitudes that affect clinical decisions, including pain treatment, cardiac workup, and maternal care; classified as a patient-safety issue by the Joint Commission and AHRQ.

**Health disparity:** a documented outcome gap between population groups, such as Black maternal mortality rates or Indigenous diabetes outcomes, rooted in cultural, structural, and historical influences.

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA):** the federal law requiring communication adaptations and physical access accommodations for patients with disabilities in healthcare settings.

**Social determinants of health:** income, housing, food access, education, and neighborhood environment; the socioeconomic conditions that shape health outcomes more powerfully than most clinical interventions.

**Immigration status:** a demographic factor that shapes healthcare access through eligibility limits, documentation fear, sanctuary policies, and emergency-only Medicaid restrictions.

**NIMHD:** the NIH National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities; the federal research source that documents the outcome gaps this chapter names.

### Chapter summary

Culture is a clinical variable, not background noise. The influences shaping how patients seek care, communicate with providers, and make health decisions include race and ethnicity, language, religion, immigration status, gender identity, disability, and socioeconomic status. Each of these influences has created specific contemporary healthcare practices: qualified interpreter services, chaplaincy departments, BIPOC doula programs, sanctuary clinics, affirming-care programs, ADA accommodations, and sliding-scale clinics. Implicit bias operates below conscious awareness and measurably affects clinical decisions, making it a patient-safety issue that requires active countermeasures. Federal frameworks including CLAS Standards, Title VI, and the ADA provide the legal and operational foundation for culturally responsive care in accredited healthcare settings.

2. A clinic sees many patients who are undocumented immigrants. Several staff members suggest posting information about the clinic's sanctuary policy at the front desk. Using what you know about immigration status as a healthcare influence, explain why this might affect patient outcomes, and cite the relevant federal framework that supports providing care to this population.

A classmate says this is because Black patients communicate pain differently. Using what you know about implicit bias and health disparities, evaluate that explanation. What does the evidence actually show?
