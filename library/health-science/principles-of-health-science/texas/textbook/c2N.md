# Healthcare and the Economy, Scale, Spending, and What Drives Cost

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Describe the scale of the US health services sector as a share of the national economy and workforce
- Identify where healthcare money comes from and where it goes
- Explain the major cost drivers that push US health spending higher than peer nations
- Describe the local economic effects when a hospital expands or closes

Marisol is a junior in high school considering a career in healthcare. Her counselor tells her that healthcare is one of the few sectors in the US economy that is almost always hiring. What the counselor does not explain is why: healthcare represents roughly 17 to 18 percent of the entire US economy, employs more than 20 million workers, and faces demographic forces that guarantee growth for decades. Understanding that economic scale is not just background knowledge for Marisol, it directly shapes which careers will expand, which roles will change, and what pressures every healthcare worker will face on the job.

### The Scale of the Sector

The United States spends more on healthcare, both in total and per person, than any other nation on earth. According to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services **National Health Expenditure Accounts (NHEA)**, the US devotes approximately 17 to 18 percent of its gross domestic product to health spending. **GDP share** of that size means healthcare is larger than defense, larger than education, and larger than any individual manufacturing industry. If US healthcare were a country, its economy would rank among the largest in the world.

Employment reflects the same scale. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that more than 20 million Americans work in healthcare and social assistance, making it the single largest employment sector in the US economy. In many rural counties and smaller cities, the local hospital is the largest private employer, the largest generator of local tax revenue, and the anchor of the surrounding business district. This is not an accident. Every hospital employs physicians, nurses, technicians, food-service workers, security staff, housekeeping, IT professionals, supply-chain managers, and billing specialists. Each of those jobs supports local spending in restaurants, housing, and retail.

### Where the Money Goes, and Where It Comes From

The NHEA breaks health spending into categories. Hospital care accounts for roughly 31 percent of all health spending, the largest single category. Physician and clinical services follow at approximately 20 percent. Prescription drugs account for about 9 percent, a number that surprises many students who expect it to be higher given drug-price news coverage. Long-term care, home health, dental services, public health programs, and administrative costs divide the rest. Administration, meaning the overhead of billing, insurance processing, prior authorization, and compliance documentation, accounts for a meaningfully large share, one that is frequently underestimated.

Payment comes from multiple sources. Private insurance pays for roughly 32 percent of US health spending, the largest single payer. **Medicare**, the federal program covering adults 65 and older and certain disabled populations, contributes about 21 percent. **Medicaid**, the joint federal-state program covering low-income populations, adds approximately 18 percent. **Out-of-pocket spending**, what patients pay directly through copays, deductibles, and uncovered services, accounts for about 10 percent. Employer self-insurance, the Veterans Administration, TRICARE for military families, and other sources cover the remainder.

Together, Medicare and Medicaid pay for roughly 40 percent of all US health spending. That concentration of purchasing power means the rules those programs set, how much they pay for a service, which services they cover, and what quality standards they require, shape the behavior of almost every provider in the country, whether or not those providers primarily serve government-insured patients.

### What Drives Cost Up

If the US spends 17 to 18 percent of GDP on healthcare, why does that not translate into better outcomes than peer nations? Most comparable wealthy countries spend 10 to 12 percent of GDP and achieve similar or better life-expectancy and infant-mortality outcomes. Understanding this gap requires identifying the **cost drivers**.

The aging population is real but does not fully explain the gap. The US is not dramatically older than Germany or Japan, which spend far less per capita. Chronic disease burden matters: the US has high rates of obesity, diabetes, hypertension, and other conditions that generate sustained healthcare use. But the single strongest explanation, supported by OECD and Peterson-KFF data, is prices rather than utilization. The US pays more for the same procedures, the same drugs, and the same devices than comparable nations do, not because Americans use dramatically more healthcare services, but because each unit of service costs more. An MRI, a hip replacement, a brand-name drug, and a hospital day all cost more in the United States than in Canada, Germany, France, or the United Kingdom.

Administrative overhead is a secondary driver. The complexity of billing across hundreds of insurance plans, with different rules for coverage, prior authorization, and payment rates, creates administrative costs that consume a larger share of total spending in the US than in single-payer systems. Those costs do not buy better care, they buy more paperwork.

**Value-based care** represents one of the primary responses to this cost problem. Rather than paying providers for each service delivered (fee-for-service), value-based contracts link payment to patient outcomes, prevention, and efficiency. **Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs)** are a Medicare mechanism that groups providers into networks accountable for both the cost and the quality of care for a defined patient population. When an ACO keeps its patients healthier and out of the hospital, it shares in the savings.

### Local Impact: What Happens When a Hospital Closes

Because hospitals are local economic anchors, their financial stability or failure ripples far beyond healthcare. A **rural hospital closure** removes the largest employer from its community, eliminates local emergency access (patients may face 60 or more miles to the nearest ER), and triggers downstream job losses at local businesses that depended on hospital employees as customers. Real estate values fall. The tax base shrinks. Young families with children may relocate to communities with closer hospital access.

This is not a hypothetical. More than 140 rural hospitals have closed in the US since 2010. Communities that lose their hospital show measurable increases in cardiovascular-disease mortality, delayed cancer diagnosis, and maternal outcomes, outcomes that worsen when travel distance to care increases significantly.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A county economic-development office learns that its only hospital, a 75-bed critical-access facility serving 18,000 rural residents, is projecting negative operating margins for the next three years. Local officials meet with the hospital board and identify the drivers: low Medicare reimbursement rates, a high share of uninsured patients generating uncompensated care, and an inability to recruit specialist physicians. Three possible responses come up: applying for Medicaid expansion funding, joining a regional accountable care organization to share administrative costs, and converting some inpatient beds to a skilled-nursing model that generates better Medicare rates. The economic-development officer notes that without at least one of those levers, the county will lose its largest employer and its only emergency department within five years.

### Key terms

**GDP share:** healthcare's portion of gross domestic product; roughly 17 to 18 percent in the US, the largest single sector of the economy.

**National Health Expenditure Accounts (NHEA):** the CMS federal dataset tracking US health spending by category, payer, and year; the authoritative source for health-spending data.

**Cost driver:** a factor that pushes health spending upward; major US drivers include prices rather than utilization, chronic disease burden, administrative overhead, and aging demographics.

**Medicare:** the federal health insurance program primarily for adults 65 and older; accounts for approximately 21 percent of national health expenditures.

**Medicaid:** joint federal-state health coverage for low-income populations; accounts for approximately 18 percent of national health expenditures.

**Out-of-pocket spending:** the portion of healthcare costs paid directly by patients through copays, deductibles, and uncovered services; approximately 10 percent of total US spending.

**Value-based care:** a payment model linking reimbursement to patient outcomes and efficiency rather than to the volume of services delivered.

**Accountable Care Organization (ACO):** a group of providers accountable for the cost and quality of care for a defined patient population; a key Medicare cost-containment mechanism.

**Rural hospital closure:** the closure of a critical-access hospital that removes the area's largest employer, eliminates local emergency access, and produces measurable downstream health and economic effects.

### Chapter summary

US healthcare is the largest sector of the national economy by GDP share and workforce size, touching the economic health of almost every community in the country. Hospital care is the biggest spending category; private insurance and Medicare are the largest payers. The cost gap between the US and peer nations is explained primarily by prices, not by utilization, with administrative overhead as a secondary driver. Value-based care and ACOs are the primary policy responses to that cost pressure. When a hospital closes in a rural community, the consequences go far beyond healthcare access, affecting employment, tax revenue, real estate, and long-term community viability. Understanding this economic landscape explains why healthcare jobs grow, why cost pressures reshape every role in the field, and why the economics of the sector are inseparable from the quality of care.

### Review questions

1. A classmate says that prescription drugs must be the biggest cost category in US healthcare because drug prices are always in the news. Using NHEA data, correct that misconception. What is the largest spending category, and approximately what share of total spending does it represent?
2. The US spends nearly twice as much per capita on healthcare as comparable wealthy nations. Using what you know about cost drivers, identify two specific factors that best explain that gap, and explain why total utilization is not the primary explanation.
3. A rural county is considering whether to support its struggling 60-bed hospital with a local tax subsidy. A critic argues the money would be better spent elsewhere. Using what you know about the local economic impact of hospitals, explain the economic case for keeping the hospital open.
