# Negligence, Malpractice, and Liability in Healthcare

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Distinguish negligence, malpractice, and liability from each other
- Apply the four required elements of a malpractice claim to a clinical scenario
- Explain how documentation protects both patients and practitioners
- Describe the system-level practices that reduce harm and reduce liability

A nurse administered the wrong medication to a patient. She pulled the correct medication from the drawer, but it was a look-alike vial sitting next to the intended drug. She did not double-check the label at the point of administration. The patient required a 48-hour ICU stay. Did the nurse commit negligence? Malpractice? Is the hospital liable? Is she? Working through those questions requires understanding three related but distinct legal concepts.

### Negligence, malpractice, and liability: what each means

These three words are related, but they are not interchangeable.

**Negligence** is failure to exercise the care that a reasonable person would use under the circumstances. Negligence is not limited to healthcare. Anyone can be negligent: a restaurant worker who leaves a wet floor unmarked, a driver who runs a stop sign, or a maintenance worker who leaves equipment blocking a walkway. The standard is reasonableness, not perfection.

**Malpractice** is professional negligence. It applies specifically to licensed practitioners whose conduct falls below the accepted standard of care within their profession. The critical distinction is the duty: a licensed clinician has a professional duty to patients that the general public does not. When a physician misses a diagnosis that a reasonably competent physician would have caught, that is malpractice, not merely negligence. Malpractice claims require proof that a professional duty existed, which is why a clinician giving casual advice at a dinner party is generally not liable for malpractice if the advice turns out to be wrong.

**Liability** is legal responsibility for the resulting harm, including who is financially on the hook. Liability in healthcare is more complex than in most settings because it can attach to multiple parties at once.

Personal liability attaches to the individual practitioner. **Vicarious liability** attaches to the employer under a legal principle called *respondeat superior*, which holds that an employer is liable for the acts of employees performed within the scope of their employment. Institutional liability attaches to the facility itself for systemic failures, such as unsafe staffing levels, failed credentialing, defective equipment, or inadequate policies. Corporate liability, at the board level, attaches when an organization fails to create and maintain safe systems of care.

### The four elements of a malpractice claim

To succeed in a malpractice lawsuit in any US state, the patient (or their representative) must prove all four of the following elements. If any one element is missing, the claim fails.

The first element is **duty of care**: a clinician-patient relationship must have existed. Formal care creates duty. Hallway advice does not. A physician who sees a patient in her clinic has a duty. A physician who gives an opinion to a neighbor over the back fence typically does not.

The second element is **breach**: the practitioner's conduct fell below the standard of care. Standard of care is not what the best clinician in the country would do. It is what a reasonably competent practitioner in the same role would do under similar circumstances. Expert witnesses establish the standard in court by citing published clinical guidelines, professional standards, and peer-reviewed evidence.

The third element is **causation**: the breach directly caused the harm. Causation is where most malpractice cases actually turn. A physician may have clearly breached the standard of care, but if the patient's harm would have occurred anyway regardless of that breach, causation fails and the claim fails with it. Proving causation requires showing both factual cause ("but for this breach, the harm would not have occurred") and proximate cause (the breach was a sufficiently direct cause, not a remote or unforeseeable one).

The fourth element is **damages**: the patient must have suffered measurable harm, whether physical, financial, or emotional. A clear breach of the standard of care with no resulting harm does not support a malpractice claim, though it may support a licensing-board complaint.

### Common malpractice scenarios

US claims data points to several categories that generate the most malpractice litigation. Diagnostic errors, including missed diagnoses, delayed diagnoses, and misdiagnoses, are the leading category by volume. Medication errors (wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong route, wrong patient) are a close second. Surgical and procedural errors, including wrong-site surgery and retained instruments, generate some of the highest individual settlements. Obstetric injuries, failure to monitor, failure to rescue, and informed-consent failures round out the most common categories.

### Why documentation is often decisive

In most malpractice cases, the medical record is the primary evidence. When a case goes to court, the jury hears what the record shows, not what the clinician remembers. The practical rule is: if it was not documented, it did not happen.

Good documentation protects the patient because it creates an accurate, contemporaneous account of what occurred. It protects the practitioner because it demonstrates that the standard of care was met. A thorough note with specific timestamps, objective findings, clinical reasoning, and a follow-up plan is a better defense than memory alone.

The reverse is also true. A minimal note ("patient fell, assessed, no injury") provides almost nothing to a defense team. And altering records after the fact, called **spoliation of evidence**, is treated as a separate and severe offense. Courts may instruct juries to assume the worst about an altered record. A practitioner who alters a record after a bad outcome often makes their legal situation far worse than the underlying event would have.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A patient recovering from hip surgery reports increasing leg pain and swelling to the night-shift nurse at 2:00 a.m. The nurse documents "patient reports discomfort, repositioned, will continue to monitor." No specific measurements, no clinical reasoning, no escalation. The patient develops a deep vein thrombosis by morning. In the malpractice case that follows, the defense must argue that a competent nurse assessed and responded appropriately. The record does not support that argument. Even if the nurse did perform an adequate assessment, the documentation does not reflect it. A better note would have named the specific findings (degree of swelling, comparison to the other leg, skin temperature), documented the clinical reasoning for or against escalation, and recorded any actions taken.

### System-level protections

Malpractice claims do not only reflect individual practitioner errors. They often reflect system failures that made individual errors more likely. Recognizing this, healthcare systems have invested in several evidence-based practices that reduce both harm and litigation.

Standardized protocols, such as the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist, reduce preventable surgical harm by ensuring consistent performance of critical steps regardless of who is in the room. Root-cause analysis after adverse events is a structured process for identifying the systemic conditions that contributed to a bad outcome, rather than simply blaming the person nearest the patient.

The **Just Culture** framework, developed by David Marx, distinguishes three categories of behavior: human error (an honest mistake that any reasonable person might make), at-risk behavior (a shortcut or workaround that increases risk), and reckless behavior (knowingly ignoring a significant risk). Each category requires a different response: consoling for human error, coaching for at-risk behavior, and disciplinary action for reckless behavior. The framework explicitly warns against treating all adverse events as individual failures, which research shows makes systems less safe, not more, because it discourages honest error reporting.

The AHRQ CANDOR (Communication and Optimal Resolution) toolkit promotes early, transparent communication with patients and families after a harm event, combined with a fair and thorough investigation. Programs using this approach have reduced both patient harm and litigation costs.

In Texas, the state passed significant **tort reform** through the Texas Medical Liability Act (Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 74) in 2003. The reform caps non-economic damages (pain and suffering) for physician defendants at $250,000. This changed the litigation environment in Texas significantly and remains a topic of debate among patients, clinicians, and policymakers.

### Key terms

**Negligence:** failure to exercise the care a reasonable person would use under the circumstances; any person, not only healthcare professionals, can be negligent.

**Malpractice:** professional negligence; a licensed practitioner's conduct fell below the accepted standard of care within their profession.

**Liability:** legal responsibility for resulting harm, including who is financially responsible.

**Duty of care:** the first element of a malpractice claim; a clinician-patient relationship must have existed, not just a casual interaction.

**Breach:** the second element; the conduct fell below the standard of care, established by expert testimony or published guidelines.

**Causation:** the third element; the breach directly caused the harm (both factual and proximate cause); most cases turn on this element.

**Damages:** the fourth element; measurable harm, physical, financial, or emotional; absent damages, no claim stands even when breach is clear.

**Vicarious liability:** employer liability for an employee's acts within the scope of employment, under the legal principle of *respondeat superior*.

**Just Culture (Marx):** accountability framework distinguishing human error, at-risk behavior, and reckless behavior; replaces reflexive individual blame after adverse events.

**Tort reform (Texas):** the 2003 Texas Medical Liability Act (CPRC Chapter 74) capping non-economic damages for physician defendants at $250,000.

**Spoliation of evidence:** altering records after the fact; a separate and severe offense that may make a malpractice case significantly worse.

### Chapter summary

Negligence, malpractice, and liability are related but distinct concepts. Negligence is any failure of reasonable care. Malpractice is professional negligence, requiring a duty of care between a licensed practitioner and a patient. Liability, personal, vicarious, or institutional, determines who is legally and financially responsible for harm. A malpractice claim requires all four elements: duty, breach, causation, and damages. Documentation is often the decisive factor in a malpractice case, because the record is the primary evidence of what occurred and whether the standard of care was met. System-level approaches including protocol standardization, root-cause analysis, Just Culture frameworks, and disclosure-and-apology programs reduce both harm and litigation by addressing the systemic conditions behind individual errors.

1. A patient presents to an emergency department with chest pain. The physician performs a brief evaluation, discharges the patient with a diagnosis of muscle strain, and does not order an EKG. The patient has a heart attack 90 minutes later. Walk through all four elements of a potential malpractice claim in this scenario: identify where each element is strong, where it is contested, and which element is most likely to determine the outcome.
2. A hospital learns that a nurse deliberately altered a patient's intake record after a medication error to make the timing of events less clear. How does this affect the underlying malpractice case, and what is the specific legal term for this action?
3. Using the Just Culture framework, identify the correct response to each of the following: (a) a nurse who drew a look-alike vial from an unlabeled drawer shared with another medication; (b) a physician who knowingly documented a physical exam she did not perform; (c) a tech who skipped a two-patient-identifier check because she "knew" the patient.
