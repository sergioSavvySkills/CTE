**Definitions:**

- **A.** The FDA Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience database; a publicly searchable record of reported device events
- **B.** An event that could have caused harm but did not; still required in most internal reporting systems and a leading indicator of future harm
- **C.** A TJC-defined serious patient-harm event; voluntary but strongly encouraged reporting with device-related criteria
- **D.** A clinical-engineering request to investigate or repair a device, filed alongside the incident report
- **E.** The facility's internal report, often filed in RL6, Midas, or Datix, documenting an adverse event or near-miss; must be factual and non-blaming
- **F.** Keeping the device, packaging, tubing, and associated disposables intact for biomed investigation; discarding evidence is a reporting failure with legal consequences
- **G.** The FDA regulation defining mandatory device-event reporting by user facilities and manufacturers, including the 10-working-day deadline
- **H.** Marx's framework separating blame-free reporting for human error from accountability for reckless behavior; the premise underlying non-punitive reporting systems
- **I.** Mandatory FDA report under 21 CFR Part 803 for device events contributing to death or serious injury; due to the FDA and manufacturer within 10 working days
- **J.** The FDA's voluntary reporting portal (Form 3500) for clinicians, covering adverse events that do not rise to the mandatory MDR threshold

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The CNA failed to: (1) notify the clinical supervisor or charge nurse immediately and directly, not in a hallway comment; (2) take the device out of service and label it "Do Not Use"; (3) document factually in the patient record what she observed; (4) file an internal incident report; (5) submit a work order to biomed with the device information. Any five of these six steps were missed.

2. The five things reporting does at once: triggers a replacement device for the immediate patient, prompts inspection of every identical device on the floor, surfaces preventive-maintenance gaps for the institution, feeds the FDA's national device-safety database so recalls can happen, and protects the individual worker. The one that most directly benefits a patient on a different floor who has not yet been harmed is prompting inspection of every identical device on the floor. If the same pump model is faulty, patients using other pumps of the same model are at risk, and inspecting those devices breaks the failure cycle before another patient is harmed.

3. A device failure with no harm may require an internal incident report depending on facility policy and requires a biomed work order. A near-miss requires an internal incident report and usually risk-management escalation. A device failure contributing to serious patient injury may add a mandatory FDA MDR under 21 CFR Part 803 depending on the seriousness of the injury. A device failure contributing to patient death requires the full set: internal incident report, risk-management escalation, a mandatory FDA MDR within 10 working days, and potentially Texas DSHS notification and a Joint Commission Sentinel Event report.

4. The six steps in order: (1) notify the clinical supervisor or charge nurse immediately and directly; (2) take the device out of service and label it "Do Not Use" with date, time, initials, and description; (3) document factually in the patient record; (4) file the internal incident report; (5) submit a work order to biomed with full device identifiers and a description of the failure; (6) preserve the device and all associated items. Step six cannot be skipped after the incident report is filed because the incident report documents what happened, but the physical investigation by biomed requires the actual device, tubing, strips, or other disposables to determine the root cause. A report without the physical evidence tells biomed that something happened but gives them nothing to examine.

5. MedWatch is the FDA's voluntary reporting portal (Form 3500) for clinicians; any clinician can submit a report for events that do not rise to the mandatory MDR threshold. The mandatory MDR under 21 CFR Part 803 is a required report that user facilities must file with the FDA and the device manufacturer for events contributing to death or serious injury, within 10 working days. The MDR is triggered by the severity of the event (death or serious injury); MedWatch is available for anything below that threshold.

6. The MAUDE database (Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience) is a publicly searchable record of all reported device events and adverse events submitted to the FDA. A student could search a specific device's make and model to see whether other facilities have reported similar failures, what kind of malfunctions have been documented, and whether recalls have been issued. This makes MAUDE a research tool for evaluating a device's safety history before using it or when investigating a current malfunction.

7. Blaming language from the chapter: "The pharmacist was dismissive and almost caused a serious error." Factual rewrite: "At 14:30, I raised a concern about the medication order. The pharmacist reviewed and corrected the order at 14:35." The factual version records who, what, when, and what action was taken, without characterizing the other person's attitude or motivation. Blaming language creates legal exposure and is harder to act on because it focuses on the person rather than the event.

8. Just Culture distinguishes between human error, which is a mistake any worker could make in the same conditions, and reckless behavior, which involves deliberately bypassing safety processes or ignoring known risks. A worker who makes a human error and reports it is not punished; a worker who deliberately bypasses a safety alert is still held accountable. This distinction matters for the reporting system because workers will only report if they believe reporting is safe. If every error leads to punishment, workers stop reporting, the database loses the events it needs to detect patterns, and the same device harms patients across many facilities without triggering a recall.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Step 1: The nurse calls the charge nurse immediately (verbal, direct). Step 2: She labels the pump "Do Not Use" (takes the device out of service). Step 3: Factual documentation is filed in Datix within the hour (patient record documentation is part of this, and incident report documentation is the formal system record). Step 4: She files the internal incident report in Datix with all required identifiers and information. Step 5: She submits a biomed work order. Step 6: She saves the IV tubing and settings printout (device preservation). Risk management notification is handled by the charge nurse. The MDR is initiated two days later by the appropriate clinical or risk management personnel.

2. Saving the IV tubing and the settings printout is device preservation: keeping all associated items intact for biomed investigation. It was clinically important because the tubing and the settings record are the physical evidence that biomed needs to determine whether the failure was a software defect, a programming error, or a hardware malfunction. If the nurse had discarded the tubing after stopping the infusion, biomed would have lost the ability to determine whether the flow-rate error was a one-time programming mistake or a systemic device defect affecting the entire pump model.

3. The documentation in Datix includes: serial number (satisfies device identifier), programmed versus actual rate (verbatim description of the failure), time frame, patient status, and clinicians notified. The chapter's required documentation list also includes: device make, model, and lot number from the device label; software version if applicable; user credentials and training status; alarms and error codes observed; and the outcome including device-preservation status. Accept responses that identify any of the potentially missing items from this list. Full credit for identifying at least one missing element with an explanation.

4. The severity that triggers a mandatory MDR is a device event contributing to patient death or serious injury. The charge nurse and risk management handle the MDR filing, not the student. The chapter states explicitly that students are not expected to file an FDA MDR: their role is to see the event, keep the patient safe, escalate, document factually, and preserve the device. External regulatory reports are routed by the supervising clinician or risk manager.

5. The inspection of three more pumps of the same model illustrates that reporting matters beyond the individual patient. The specific function this represents from the chapter's five-things list is: prompting inspection of every identical device in use, and feeding the national database so that the software defect can be identified and a recall or safety notice can be issued across all facilities using the same model. Without the nurse's report, the defect would have remained undetected and other patients on other units or in other facilities could have received incorrect doses.
