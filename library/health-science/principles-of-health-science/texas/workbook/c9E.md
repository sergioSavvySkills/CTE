### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. A statute is a law passed by a legislature: Congress at the federal level, the Texas Legislature at the state level. A regulation is an agency-issued rule that implements a statute and typically contains more operational detail. Example: Congress passed HIPAA (the statute); HHS issued the Privacy Rule, Security Rule, and Breach Notification Rule (the regulations) that specify exactly what covered entities must do. The two are related: the statute authorizes the agency to write the regulation, and both are legally binding.

2. Primary sources are the actual statutes and regulations, meaning what the law actually says. Secondary sources, such as textbooks, news articles, and health-law summaries, interpret and summarize the law but may be outdated, incomplete, or simplified. A healthcare worker should use primary sources when they need to know precisely what the law requires in a specific situation: for compliance decisions, for routing a question to legal counsel, or for citing the rule. Secondary sources are appropriate for building foundational understanding but should not substitute for the primary source when the exact legal language matters.

3. EMTALA requires that any hospital with an emergency department must provide a medical screening examination to every patient who presents for care, regardless of their ability to pay, and must stabilize emergency medical conditions before discharge or transfer. The textbook's point that knowing which law governs each form is "not optional" reflects the fact that EMTALA generates specific operational requirements: the patient cannot be turned away, denied a screening, or transferred before stabilization purely for financial reasons. Understanding which law requires which step is how a healthcare worker knows why the step is mandatory, not just that it is.

4. The OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) requires healthcare employers to protect workers from exposure to blood and other potentially infectious materials. Three specific requirements it generates: (1) employers must maintain an exposure control plan describing how risks are managed; (2) engineering controls such as safety needles and sharps containers must be provided; (3) all workers must receive training on bloodborne pathogen risks and post-exposure protocols. The standard also requires employers to offer hepatitis B vaccination to at-risk employees.

5. Both laws exist because financial incentives should not distort medical decisions. The Anti-Kickback Statute prohibits giving or receiving anything of value to induce referrals of federal healthcare program patients. The Stark Law prohibits physician self-referrals to entities with which the physician has a financial relationship. Both are enforced by HHS OIG and the Department of Justice. The concern is that a physician who profits from referring a patient to a facility they have a financial stake in has a conflict of interest that could result in unnecessary or inappropriate care.

6. HIPAA is the federal floor for patient privacy. The Texas Medical Records Privacy Act adds requirements that exceed HIPAA, particularly around marketing use of patient information and patient access rights. Under the preemption doctrine, federal law generally overrides conflicting state law, but when a state law provides greater protection to patients and Congress has not preempted that area, the state law controls. In Texas, a healthcare provider must comply with both HIPAA and the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act; where the two differ, the stricter Texas rule controls. A Texas provider faces stricter obligations than a provider in a state with no comparable state privacy statute.

7. The MA should decline the marketing request and route it to compliance because the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act restricts this use of patient information even if a general HIPAA authorization might appear to permit it. Applying the preemption principle: both HIPAA and the Texas law govern patient privacy, but the Texas law is stricter on this specific point, so the Texas rule controls. A healthcare worker in a state without a comparable statute would need to evaluate whether the specific HIPAA authorization actually covers this use, but a Texas worker has a clear answer in state law.

8. The 21st Century Cures Act includes an information-blocking rule that requires healthcare entities to share patient health information with patients and their apps without obstruction. It changes the patient's relationship to their own health information by establishing a legal right to electronic access to their clinical records through apps of their choosing. Healthcare entities that obstruct this access can face significant penalties. This shifts the power dynamic around health data: patients are not required to go through provider portals or paper-record requests; they can direct access to their data through third-party apps.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The caller's claim that patients signed HIPAA consent forms does not resolve the question because HIPAA and the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act are two separate laws. A HIPAA authorization may permit certain marketing uses, but the Texas law restricts marketing use of patient information in ways that go beyond HIPAA's baseline. Signing a HIPAA authorization does not constitute authorization under the stricter Texas standard. In Texas, the more protective rule controls, so the HIPAA authorization is not sufficient.

2. The two laws are HIPAA (the federal law), enforced by the HHS Office for Civil Rights, and the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act (Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 181), enforced in Texas through state law. HIPAA permits certain marketing uses with patient authorization. The Texas Medical Records Privacy Act restricts the marketing use of patient information more narrowly, limiting uses that HIPAA might otherwise allow. Where they differ, the stricter Texas law controls for Texas healthcare providers.

3. Under the preemption doctrine, the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act controls in this scenario because it provides greater protection to patients than HIPAA does on this specific point, and Congress has not preempted Texas privacy law in this area. A MA in a state without a comparable state privacy statute would need to evaluate only whether the specific HIPAA authorization the patients signed actually covers this marketing use, and whether the marketing qualifies under HIPAA's permitted uses or requires separate authorization.

4. Routing to compliance means the MA should not attempt to make the final legal determination independently. The MA's job is to recognize that a legal question exists, decline to act on the caller's assumption, and refer the question to the facility's compliance officer or legal department, who are trained and authorized to make that determination. This is appropriate because the legal analysis requires knowledge of both HIPAA and Texas law, the specific content of the authorizations patients signed, and the facility's compliance policies. Acting unilaterally in either direction, agreeing to provide the list or definitively refusing without legal review, would bypass the appropriate authority.

5. The MA or compliance officer should consult the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act, which is Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 181. The primary source is located at statutes.capitol.texas.gov under the Health and Safety Code. The most relevant section is Chapter 181, specifically the provisions addressing the use and disclosure of health information for marketing purposes. The HHS guidance on HIPAA marketing rules at hhs.gov is the relevant federal primary source to compare.
