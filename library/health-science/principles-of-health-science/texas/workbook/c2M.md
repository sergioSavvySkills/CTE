### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Humoral medicine was a pre-scientific framework attributed to Galen describing health as a balance of four bodily fluids: blood, phlegm, and two types of bile. It produced treatments like bloodletting and purging, which followed logically from the model but were often harmful. The textbook uses this history to remind modern clinicians that confident systems built on incomplete evidence can cause the very harm they intend to prevent, suggesting that humility about current treatments is warranted.

2. Germ theory is the discovery that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases. Louis Pasteur demonstrated microbial contamination in fermentation; Robert Koch established rigorous criteria for linking specific microbes to specific diseases. One practice that followed directly from germ theory is antisepsis: Lister applied germ theory to surgical wounds using carbolic acid, dramatically reducing post-operative infection and death.

3. Nightingale improved sanitation and nursing practice during and after the Crimean War. What was distinctive about her approach was her use of data: she collected information and displayed it in ways that influenced hospital design and nursing education for generations, establishing nursing as a data-driven profession rather than simply a caretaking role.

4. The Flexner Report led medical schools to adopt standardized quality requirements, which raised the overall quality of US medical education. However, it also led to the closure of many schools that trained Black physicians, reducing diversity in medicine in ways that still affect the composition of the healthcare workforce and trust in the medical profession within Black communities.

5. Three public-health advances: clean water systems, tobacco control campaigns, and childhood vaccination programs. A clinician who focuses only on surgical and pharmacological advances would attribute the life-expectancy gain to antibiotics and surgery, but the textbook argues that population-level changes in environment, behavior, and sanitation account for the majority of the improvement.

6. The pharmaceutical marketing timeline was promotional because it listed the company's own products under major medical breakthroughs, reflecting a financial interest in the historical narrative rather than objective documentation. The student's reasoning is that a source produced by an entity with a commercial interest in the story it is telling cannot be treated as neutral evidence.

7. The MMWR (Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report) is the CDC's weekly report on disease surveillance and public-health events. It is a primary-source archive because it is an original government record produced at the time of the events it documents. The textbook recommends it because it provides firsthand accounts of public-health history from the federal agency responsible for tracking disease.

8. The textbook means that Black patients may bring distrust or hesitation into clinical encounters based on their community's awareness of documented abuses. A practical example: a Black patient who is asked to participate in a research study may hesitate or decline, not because of irrational fear, but because of specific historical knowledge about what research participation has meant in their community. A healthcare worker who does not understand that history cannot address it effectively.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. A primary source is an original document or firsthand account, such as an original government publication or archival record. A secondary source is a summary or analysis of primary materials, such as a history book. Both are useful: primary sources provide direct evidence, while secondary sources provide analysis and context. The NLM excerpt is more authoritative for historical facts because it is the original record, not a historian's interpretation of it.

2. The specific feature that revealed bias was that the pharmaceutical company listed its own products under major medical breakthroughs. This is a conflict of interest: the company benefits commercially from presenting its own products as historically significant. Marketing timelines are easy to find online because companies invest in search-engine visibility, but they are written to serve commercial purposes, not historical accuracy.

3. Wikipedia: information can be edited by anyone, citations may be inconsistent, and the article may reflect the most recent popular edits rather than authoritative scholarship. It is a useful starting point for exploring a topic but not a citable source for a research product. Student health blog: written by a non-expert without peer review or editorial standards; no accountability for accuracy or citation of sources.

4. The CDC MMWR issue would be placed in the credible primary-source category, likely alongside the NLM excerpt or above it, since the CDC is the federal public-health authority and the MMWR is a direct government record. It is a primary source because it was produced at the time of the events it documents by the responsible agency.

5. Healthcare professionals evaluate evidence throughout their careers: when choosing treatments, when reading research studies, when advising patients about online health information. The same skills used to evaluate a source for a class project, checking who produced it, what their interest in the narrative is, and whether it documents evidence or promotes a position, are the same skills used to evaluate a clinical study or a news report about a new treatment.
