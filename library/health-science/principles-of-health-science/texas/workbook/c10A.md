**Definitions:**

- **A.** Legal recognition that some minors have sufficient maturity to consent to specific care categories without parental involvement

- **C.** Health and Safety Code §313; the statutory surrogate hierarchy for adults without capacity and without a designated POA

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. As an ethical principle, autonomy establishes that the patient has an inherent right to make decisions about their own body and care, even decisions the clinical team disagrees with. This framing tells us why the obligation exists. As a legal right, autonomy is operationalized in CMS Conditions of Participation, Joint Commission standards, and professional codes, which means it is enforceable: facilities can lose accreditation and practitioners can face disciplinary action for violating it. Having both framings means a healthcare worker who understands the ethics knows why it matters, and the one who knows the law knows what the consequences are.

2. The five elements of informed consent are: (1) Capacity, the patient must have decision-making ability at the time. (2) Disclosure, the patient received information about the diagnosis, proposed treatment, risks, benefits, and alternatives including no treatment. (3) Understanding, the patient demonstrated comprehension. (4) Voluntariness, the decision was made freely without coercion. (5) Documentation, the process and outcome are recorded in the chart. If any single element is missing, the consent is legally insufficient even if a form has been signed.

3. Informed refusal may surprise people because healthcare culture often treats patient compliance as the goal, and a refusal can feel like a failure. The statement corrects that misperception: a patient who refuses a recommended treatment after understanding the risks has exercised exactly the same right as one who agrees. The healthcare worker's job with a refusal is to ensure the refusal is informed, meaning the patient understands the consequences of declining, and to document the refusal with the same completeness required for consent. The team does not attempt to change the patient's mind once those steps are complete.

4. Capacity is a clinical judgment made by a clinician, usually the treating physician, about whether this specific patient has the ability to understand and make an informed choice for this specific decision at this specific moment. Competence is a legal status determined by a court through formal judicial proceedings. In practice, when a clinician questions a patient's capacity, the correct response is to conduct a formal capacity assessment, bring in a specialist if needed, and if capacity is truly absent, identify a surrogate. The clinician does not simply substitute their own judgment for the patient's.

5. The textbook explains that capacity is decision-specific and can fluctuate. A patient with early dementia may retain sufficient understanding of a straightforward low-risk procedure (blood draw) to consent independently, while lacking the cognitive processing needed to weigh the complex risks, benefits, and alternatives of an elective surgical procedure. This illustrates that capacity is not an all-or-nothing determination: clinicians must assess it for each decision, at each point in time, rather than applying a blanket designation based on diagnosis.

6. Substituted judgment is used when the patient's prior wishes are known: the surrogate decides as the patient would have decided based on known values, prior statements, and documented preferences. It gives priority to what the patient actually wanted. The best-interest standard is used when the patient's prior wishes are unknown: the surrogate and clinical team decide what would serve the patient's interests given the available information. The practical difference is that substituted judgment is patient-directed (based on their stated or inferred values) while best-interest is an external assessment of what is beneficial when those values are not known.

7. Autonomy is being honored when: consent or refusal is documented with all five elements; an interpreter is present for patients with limited English proficiency; the patient answers open-ended questions and demonstrates understanding in their own words; and the decision-maker is the patient, not a family member acting for a patient who still has capacity. Autonomy is at risk when: a consent form is signed under sedation; consent is obtained without an interpreter despite a language barrier; family members answer for a patient who appears capable; there is subtle coercion; or a refusal is not documented. If a healthcare worker observes an at-risk signal, the appropriate action is to pause the process, identify the problem, and escalate to a charge nurse or supervisor before the procedure continues.

8. The Texas Consent to Medical Treatment Act (HSC §313) applies when a patient lacks decision-making capacity and has not designated a surrogate through a Medical Power of Attorney. It provides a priority hierarchy for who may make healthcare decisions. The first level is a court-appointed guardian of the person, if one has been appointed. The second level is the patient's spouse. The third level is the patient's adult children, with consensus among those available. The hierarchy continues through the patient's parents, nearest living relative, and finally attending physician with ethics-committee review if no other surrogate is available.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The specific documentation failure is that no capacity assessment was completed before the consent form was signed by the daughter rather than the patient. The element of informed consent most directly at risk is capacity: a valid consent requires that the patient, not a surrogate, have decision-making ability at the time of consent. Because no assessment was documented, there is no evidence that the clinical team ever determined whether the patient had capacity, which means the entire consent may be legally insufficient.

2. The chart's description of the patient as "alert and cooperative" is legally significant because it creates a contradiction: a patient described as alert and cooperative in clinical notes is not automatically presumed to lack capacity, yet the consent form was signed by the daughter rather than the patient. This discrepancy suggests that the patient may have had capacity to consent independently and that the surrogate signature was obtained without any determination that capacity was absent. In a malpractice or patient-rights review, this inconsistency in the documentation would raise serious questions about whether the patient's autonomy was honored.

3. Before the consent form was signed, the surgeon should have: (1) addressed the patient directly to assess understanding and engagement, (2) conducted and documented a capacity assessment for this specific decision (elective knee replacement), using a structured assessment if warranted, (3) if capacity was confirmed, directed all disclosures and questions to the patient, (4) used teach-back or other verification methods to confirm understanding, and (5) obtained the patient's voluntary signature with documentation that all five elements were present. The daughter could be present for support, but the decision-maker should have been identified based on a documented clinical determination, not assumed from the diagnosis.

4. The textbook defines capacity as a judgment about this specific patient, for this specific decision, at this specific moment. A diagnosis of mild cognitive impairment does not eliminate decision-making capacity; it requires assessment. A patient with mild impairment may retain full capacity for a specific, well-explained decision. The capacity assessment was required precisely because the team should not substitute a clinical assumption for an actual evaluation. An assumption that a patient "probably cannot decide" is not the same as a documented finding that capacity is absent. Without assessment, any surrogate consent is legally uncertain.

5. As a nursing student, the appropriate action is to recognize the at-risk signal: family members are answering for a patient who is sitting in the room and appears capable (alert and cooperative). The appropriate response is not to intervene in the surgeon's consent process directly, but to bring the concern to a charge nurse or supervisor immediately and before the procedure begins. The signals from the textbook that apply here are: the decision-maker appears to be a family member rather than the patient who still has capacity, and no capacity assessment is visible in the documentation. A student is not in a position to halt a surgery, but escalating the observation to someone with the authority to pause and review is both ethically appropriate and consistent with the chapter's framework.
