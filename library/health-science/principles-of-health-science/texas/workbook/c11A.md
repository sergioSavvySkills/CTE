### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Knowing the source converts a rule from an unexplained mandate into a traceable decision made by an identifiable body for a specific reason. This lets a worker trust the rule because they understand the reasoning behind it, question it intelligently when something seems wrong, and apply it confidently without needing a supervisor to remind them.

2. The WHO publishes the Five Moments for Hand Hygiene as an international recommendation. The CDC adapts that guidance for the US and incorporates it into its Guideline for Isolation Precautions. The Joint Commission then incorporates CDC guidance into its accreditation standards, and CMS treats TJC accreditation as proof of meeting its Conditions of Participation. Because Texas hospitals depend on Medicare and Medicaid payments, the WHO's recommendation becomes a practical requirement through this chain.

3. CMS deeming authority is the mechanism by which CMS accepts TJC accreditation as proof that a facility meets its Conditions of Participation for Medicare and Medicaid. Almost every US hospital depends on those payments to operate, so if a hospital loses TJC accreditation it can no longer be reimbursed by Medicare and Medicaid, which makes it unable to remain financially viable. A private non-profit's accreditation decision therefore carries quasi-regulatory force.

4. OSHA's jurisdiction is the workplace and the workers in it. The FDA's jurisdiction is products: drugs, devices, and biologics. OSHA example: the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard requiring sharps containers to protect nurses and students from needlestick injury. FDA example: the premarket approval process that a new medication or implant must pass before it can be prescribed or used.

5. NIH funds the research that produces the evidence base underlying every CDC guideline, every FDA drug approval, and every professional-organization standard. When CDC recommends a hand-hygiene technique or FDA approves a drug, the evidence behind that decision traces largely to NIH-funded studies. NIH is foundational in the sense that without the evidence it generates, the agencies that write rules would have no scientific basis on which to write them.

6. WHO: publishes the Five Moments for Hand Hygiene as the international operational framework. CDC: incorporates the framework into the Guideline for Isolation Precautions as the US clinical standard. The Joint Commission: incorporates CDC guidance into accreditation standards and NPSGs. CMS: ties Medicare and Medicaid payments to meeting those accreditation standards, giving the practice its payment-based enforcement.

7. MedWatch is the FDA's voluntary reporting portal for clinicians to submit adverse drug and device events that do not rise to the level requiring a mandatory Medical Device Report. The MDR is mandatory only for events involving death or serious injury; MedWatch captures a broader range of safety signals at lower severity levels. Together the two systems provide a fuller picture of post-market safety than mandatory reporting alone could.

8. The FDA approves a product, not how an individual clinician uses it. A physician may legally prescribe an approved drug for an unapproved indication (off-label prescribing), and the FDA's authority over the product does not extend to that individual clinical decision. The individual clinical decision is governed by state licensure laws and the professional standard of care, both of which sit outside FDA jurisdiction.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The specific regulation is OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030, the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard. More broadly it governs the full set of employer obligations to protect workers from exposure to blood and other potentially infectious materials, including PPE requirements, engineering controls such as sharps containers, training, and a written exposure control plan.

2. The OSHA 300 Log is the facility's required log of work-related injuries and illnesses. Recording the needlestick is required because OSHA mandates that employers document occupational injuries as part of the enforcement mechanism ensuring that exposure events are tracked and can be reviewed during inspections, not merely remembered or described informally.

3. Not recapping needles (using safety-engineered sharps devices and disposing immediately in a sharps container) protects the worker from bloodborne-pathogen exposure. The same practice protects patients because it prevents needles from lying on counters or being reused, which could transmit infection between patients. Worker safety and patient safety overlap in this case because the same unsafe act, recapping and mishandling a used needle, creates risk for both populations.

4. The FDA would have originally approved the medications used in PEP through its premarket-approval or new-drug-application process. The CDC publishes the guidelines clinicians follow when deciding whether and how to administer PEP, drawing on the NIH-funded research that evaluated the effectiveness of post-exposure regimens.

5. The poster reflects OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030). Its enforcement force comes from OSHA's authority under the Department of Labor to inspect workplaces, issue citations, and impose fines for violations, as well as from the facility's own liability exposure if it fails to comply with the standard and a worker is harmed.
