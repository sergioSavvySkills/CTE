### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Shared vocabulary means every person in a clinical conversation is using the same words to point to the same concepts. When a nurse says "this is an autonomy issue," a physician trained in the same framework immediately knows what is at stake: the patient's right to decide for themselves. Without shared vocabulary, ethical discussions can become personal disagreements rather than principled analyses. The four-principles framework gives every profession a common starting point regardless of their specific code.

2. The same example raises non-maleficence. Extending life through a treatment that causes significant suffering may itself constitute iatrogenic harm, the harm caused by the intervention. The non-maleficence principle asks practitioners to weigh risks of action against risks of inaction, and in this case the action (continued aggressive treatment) may impose greater harm than the disease itself would cause.

3. "Correct" implies there is a single right answer that can be proven. "Defensible and documented" means the decision can be explained using the principles framework, was made with the best available information, and was recorded so others can evaluate the reasoning. The textbook favors this framing because most ethical tensions in healthcare involve genuine conflicts between legitimate principles, and the goal is accountability and transparency, not certainty.

4. Two examples: (1) A student who texts a classmate about a patient without using the patient's name may still breach HIPAA if the combination of details (unit, day, diagnosis, age) is specific enough to re-identify the patient. (2) A clinician who accesses the chart of a neighbor out of concern, without a treatment relationship, commits a breach even if they never share the information, because the access exceeds the minimum necessary.

5. The minimum necessary rule requires covered entities to share only the information needed for the specific task. Example: a nurse sending a referral to a cardiologist should send the patient's cardiac history and current medication list, not every historical note in the chart. Accessing a celebrity's chart out of curiosity is a violation because the access itself is not needed for any treatment, payment, or operations purpose. The rule governs what you look at, not just what you share.

6. HIPAA covers all identifiable health information created or held by a covered entity; it permits sharing for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations without patient authorization. 42 CFR Part 2 covers substance-use-disorder treatment records specifically and generally requires explicit patient authorization even for treatment sharing. The law treats these records differently because the stigma attached to substance-use disorders creates special risks: disclosure could result in discrimination in employment, housing, or family relationships in ways that general health information may not.

7. Accept any two categories with one specific harm each. Patient examples: loss of privacy, potential reputational damage, social stigma, identity theft, or physical safety risk (e.g., a domestic violence survivor whose location is disclosed). Breacher examples: termination of employment, license suspension or revocation, personal civil liability, federal criminal charges. Institution examples: HHS OCR investigation, corrective action plan, civil monetary penalties, reputational damage with patients and the community. Care-team examples: workload absorbed during investigation, loss of trust in colleagues, chilling effect on appropriate information sharing.

8. Institutional ethics committees help clinical teams work through the hardest ethical cases, particularly when there is genuine disagreement about which principles should prevail. Teams should involve the ethics committee especially when time allows for consultation, when families and clinical teams disagree, when a patient's capacity or preferences are unclear, and when multiple competing principles make a straightforward resolution impossible.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The conflict is between autonomy and beneficence. Autonomy requires that the patient, who is alert and oriented (has capacity), receive truthful information about her own health because she has explicitly asked for it. Beneficence, as understood by the family, requires withholding the information to protect her from psychological distress. The two principles require opposite actions.

2. Beneficence requires balancing benefit against burden and taking the patient's own perspective on benefit seriously, not just the clinician's or family's. The family's version of beneficence substitutes their judgment about what the patient can handle for the patient's own stated preference. The patient herself has told the nurse she wants the truth, which means the family's framing of protection as a benefit is not the patient's framing. True beneficence in this case aligns with what the patient says she needs.

3. The direction is that the patient who has capacity has the right to her own information, and the family cannot override that right. It matters that two codes reach the same conclusion because it demonstrates that this is not a profession-specific rule but a foundational ethical commitment shared across healthcare. When multiple codes converge, the practitioner can be more confident that following that direction is professionally defensible regardless of which specific code governs their role.

4. If the nurse withholds the diagnosis, she violates the patient's autonomy, which is a right embedded in professional codes (ANA Provision 1) and operationalized through HIPAA's patient-access provisions and CMS Conditions of Participation. Withholding a diagnosis from a patient who asked and has capacity could constitute a violation of the patient's right to access their own health information. Professionally, it could expose the nurse to a licensing-board complaint. If the concealment leads to harm (e.g., the patient cannot make informed decisions about her remaining time), civil liability could follow.

5. Sample response: "I hear how much you love your mother and how much you want to protect her. I have to be honest with you: she asked me directly what her diagnosis is. When a patient who is alert and able to make her own decisions asks for information about her own health, we have a professional and legal obligation to be truthful with her. What I can do is help you and your family be part of that conversation in a way that offers her support. Would it help to ask her care team to be present when she gets that information, so none of you has to face it alone?"
