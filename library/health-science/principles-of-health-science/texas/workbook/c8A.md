### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. A stable team has shared history, established norms, and accumulated trust built over months or years. Teaming is the active, on-the-fly coordination work required when the team is constantly changing and most members have limited or no shared history. The difference matters for training because you cannot wait to build trust slowly: a teaming environment requires explicit, learnable behaviors that create coordination in minutes, not months.

2. In a teaming environment, trust cannot accumulate over time because the people involved may never work together again after this shift. This requires team members to use explicit behaviors, structured introductions, named roles, closed-loop confirmations, rather than relying on rapport they do not have. On a stable team, you can rely on knowing how a colleague thinks or acts; in a teaming environment you must communicate as if every assumption could be wrong.

3. The IOM/NAM quality aims (safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable) give every healthcare team a common framework that all members already hold, regardless of whether they know each other. A code-blue team that assembles in 90 seconds does not need to discuss values or priorities because the shared goal ("keep this patient alive") is already aligned for all of them. This pre-existing alignment substitutes for the shared history that a stable team would use to coordinate.

4. Licensure and scope-of-practice boundaries clarify, in advance and without negotiation, what each role can and cannot do. For example, when a complex task arises during a fast-moving transfer, the team does not need to discuss whether the CNA or the RN should perform the assessment: scope of practice already defines that. This removes a potential source of delay or conflict precisely when time is most limited.

5. Psychological safety is the condition that makes the other three conditions functional. A team can have a shared goal, clear roles, and reliable communication protocols, but if members are afraid to speak up, none of that structure will catch a safety error at the margins. The textbook gives a direct example: a float nurse who notices a labeling error but says nothing "because it's not my unit" operates in a low-psychological-safety environment, and the cost of that silence is borne by the patient.

6. Closing the loop means the receiving person restates what they heard and confirms what they will do: "I heard you say the patient's potassium is 3.1 and you want a recheck in two hours. I'll draw it at 4:30 and call you." The behavior prevents miscommunication from reaching the patient by forcing the sending person to hear whether the message arrived correctly, catching any misheard detail before it becomes a clinical error.

7. The textbook cites Joint Commission Sentinel Event Alerts, analyses of cases where patients were seriously harmed, as evidence that communication failures and coordination breakdowns (teaming failures) are the primary contributing cause. It also connects teaming directly to every IOM/NAM quality aim: fewer sentinel events, faster decisions, better patient experience, and lower burnout. This makes the argument that teaming is not a soft skill but a structural quality requirement.

8. The IPEC Core Competencies (Interprofessional Education Collaborative) is a consensus framework for the interdisciplinary teaming behaviors expected across all US health professions. Its expectation is that every practitioner arrives trained in the behaviors that make teaming work, not that teams will somehow cohere naturally, because the skills (structured communication, psychological safety, loop-closing) are teachable and must be taught deliberately.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Newcomer orientation failed from the moment the student entered the room: the charge nurse did not know her name, no one told her who was leading, what the goal was, or who to go to with questions. Psychological safety failed when the student noticed the IV error but felt uncertain whether speaking up was her role and said nothing; the environment had not signaled that her voice was expected and welcome regardless of her position.

2. Newcomer orientation is the most direct preventive behavior. If the charge nurse had spent 60 seconds at the start of the transfer telling the student who was leading, what the current goal was, and who to approach with concerns, the student would have had a named authority to go to and, more importantly, a clear signal that her observations were wanted. Naming a lead would also have addressed the separate gap (no named coordinator) that compounded the confusion.

3. "Your job is to speak up, always" directly repairs the psychological safety failure by retroactively establishing a norm: observation-sharing is expected of every member regardless of role. "I should have told you that when you walked in" directly repairs the newcomer orientation failure by acknowledging that the charge nurse's responsibility was to give the student that framework at the start, not to wait for the student to figure it out. One sentence does both because psychological safety and newcomer orientation are causally linked in this scenario.

4. The teaming behavior is naming who is leading this moment. Example: "I'm leading this transfer. If anyone has a concern, bring it to me directly." That statement takes under five seconds and removes the ambiguity that contributed to the student's hesitation.

5. The IV pump error was caught 30 minutes late by a different nurse, not the team member who noticed it first. If no one had caught it at all, an incorrect rate would have delivered the wrong dose to a pediatric patient. The textbook's argument is that teaming failures (specifically: absent newcomer orientation and absent psychological safety) are the structural cause, not a personality problem of a hesitant student. The Joint Commission's Sentinel Event analyses show that this pattern, a junior team member with safety information who does not speak, is a primary contributing factor in serious patient harm events. That makes teaming a quality requirement, not an interpersonal nicety.
