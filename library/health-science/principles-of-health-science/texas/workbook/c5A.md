### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers, accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Locating means finding information anywhere it exists. Evaluating means systematically assessing whether that source is trustworthy for the specific purpose. The textbook says skipping evaluation causes confusion because students end up planning around data that reflects a different geography, uses a different methodology, or serves a promotional rather than informational purpose.

2. The OOH is produced by a federal agency with a public mandate to report labor market data objectively, is updated annually, and uses a consistent methodology across all occupations. Salary aggregators compile self-selected user reports that may not represent the full workforce and can be skewed by respondents in high-wage markets or high-wage specialties.

3. Three CRAAP questions: (a) Currency: When was this figure published, and does it reflect current wages or data from several years ago? (b) Authority: Who produced this website, and do they have a public mandate to report accurate data or a financial interest in how the number looks? (c) Purpose: Is this site trying to inform me, recruit me into a program, or sell me something?

4. The median is the midpoint of all wages in that occupation, including workers with many years of experience, multiple certifications, or positions in high-wage cities. An entry-level worker in a new market will almost certainly start below that number. To make the figure useful, you need to know the geographic scope (national vs. Texas vs. city), whether the figure reflects all experience levels or only entry-level, and what the benefit package looks like.

5. Geographic variation means the same occupation pays significantly different wages in different locations, a rural West Texas RN earns substantially less than an RN in the Houston Medical Center. For Texas career planning, using a national median without checking the Texas-specific figure can produce a planning number that is thousands of dollars higher than what is realistic in the student's intended market.

6. "Much faster than average" is a standardized phrase the BLS assigns when a growth rate exceeds a specific numeric threshold, not a judgment about how easy the job is to enter. A small occupation growing 25% may produce fewer absolute new positions than a large occupation growing 6%. Students need to check the raw number of projected new jobs alongside the growth-rate phrase.

7. Most useful: Texas Workforce Commission LMI, because it is a primary source with Texas-specific data and no promotional motive. Second: Glassdoor, because it is current and may reflect recent market conditions, but it is self-selected and not authoritative for planning. Least useful: the community college website, because it is a promotional source with a direct financial interest in making the outcome look appealing and covers only graduates of that program, not the full market.

8. Professional organization pages provide role-specific detail that federal sources sometimes lack, including specialty certifications, typical career ladders, and descriptions of what the work actually involves day to day. Although their tone is promotional, their factual content on education requirements and credential pathways is generally accurate and current because the organizations have reputational incentive to be correct about their own field.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. All three numbers measure something real: the OOH figure is the national median for all RTs; the TWC figure is the Texas-specific median; the Glassdoor figure is an average of self-selected Texas respondents who tend to skew toward more experienced workers. Each measures a different thing. Evaluation, the second step, requires knowing what each source measures before trusting it for planning.

2. Jaylen chooses the TWC figure because it is a primary source (Authority) specific to Texas (Relevance), which makes it the most accurate figure for his actual situation. The OOH is authoritative but national, so it overstates Texas wages by $8,700. The Glassdoor figure is current but not authoritative, drawn from self-selected respondents, and may reflect more experienced workers.

3. Self-selected sampling means only people who chose to report their salary are included, and those people tend to be either highly satisfied or highly dissatisfied, and may skew toward more experienced or higher-earning workers. Jaylen accounts for this by treating the Glassdoor figure as a trend signal (experienced RTs can earn above the median) rather than a planning baseline. He does not discard it but he does not use it as his primary number.

4. Knowing the driver tells Jaylen whether the growth is structural (built into long-term demographic and disease trends) or situational (tied to a temporary policy or technology change). An aging population with increasing COPD and asthma prevalence is a structural driver, meaning the demand is likely to persist and grow. If the driver were situational, such as a one-time funding expansion, the projection might not hold over the full 10-year window.

5. Accept well-reasoned questions. Examples include: What is the entry-level wage for a new RT graduate in Texas (not just the median for all experience levels)? What are the primary employers in my target region of Texas and are they hiring? What does the total compensation package (benefits, shift differentials) look like at typical Texas employers? How long does it take to move from entry-level to median wages in this profession?
