**Definitions:**

- **A.** The quantitative tool that accounts for load weight, horizontal distance, vertical position, asymmetry, frequency, and grip quality to evaluate whether a manual lift is safe
- **B.** The ability of tissues to stretch and return to their original shape; injury occurs when loading exceeds this limit
- **C.** Equipment-assisted programs that mandate ceiling lifts, sit-to-stand devices, slide sheets, and gait belts above defined weight thresholds
- **D.** The area on the floor between the feet; a shoulder-width stance gives a large, stable foundation
- **E.** Rotational force around a joint; multiplies proportionally to the distance the load is held from the body
- **F.** The standard technique to move a weight-bearing patient between surfaces using leg lift and foot pivot rather than spinal rotation

- **H.** Any push or pull, including gravity, applied force, and normal force
- **I.** The point where body weight is balanced; must stay over the base of support during lifting and transferring
- **J.** Pulling force in muscles, tendons, and ligaments; the mechanism by which cumulative patient-handling damage accumulates over time

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers -- accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Torque = 30 lb x 20 in = 600 inch-pounds. To reduce torque without changing the weight, she should bring the box as close to her body as possible. At 4 inches from the spine, torque would drop to 120 inch-pounds -- a five-fold reduction from the same weight. The principle is that torque depends on both force and distance; reducing the distance is the most accessible variable a worker can control.

2. Each repositioning task, even with poor technique, stays below the threshold at which the worker notices acute pain. However, muscles, tendons, and discs are elastic tissues: they absorb tension and are designed to recover between loading events. When loading is frequent enough, tissues remain near their elastic limit between tasks and do not fully recover. Over hundreds of lifts, the accumulated micro-damage degrades the tissue until one ordinary task crosses the elastic limit and produces an injury that appears sudden but is actually the result of months of cumulative tension.

3. Base of support is the floor area between the feet; a wide stance (shoulder-width) provides a large, stable foundation. Center of gravity is the point where body weight is balanced, roughly at navel height in a standing adult. During a lift, if the worker leans forward to reach a patient, her center of gravity moves toward or beyond the edge of her base of support. When the center of gravity moves outside the base of support, the spine must generate significant counterbalancing force to maintain balance, dramatically increasing lumbar loading.

4. Rotating the spine while holding a load creates shear force on lumbar discs: the vertebrae twist in opposite directions while compressed, which is the most damaging combination of forces the disc faces. Pivoting with the feet achieves the same change in direction by rotating the whole body as a unit, eliminating the rotational shear at the disc level. The disc absorbs compression and some lateral force well; it is poorly designed to handle combined compression and rotation.

5. Any three of the following (accept other well-supported explanations): (a) Patient weight: exceeding the manual-lift threshold means forces will exceed the NIOSH Recommended Weight Limit, requiring equipment or additional staff. (b) Cognition/cooperation: a confused patient who moves unexpectedly shifts the load suddenly, increasing torque and fall risk simultaneously. (c) Weight-bearing status: a fully dependent patient cannot share any of the lifting effort, placing the full load on the worker. (d) Lines, tubes, drains: unsecured IV lines or catheters can dislodge during transfer, requiring the worker to stop mid-lift in a compromised position. (e) Number of staff: misjudging how many people are needed means the total force is distributed over fewer workers, increasing each person's share of the torque. (f) Available equipment: if a ceiling lift is present and the lift exceeds manual-handling thresholds, using the equipment is not optional under safe patient handling policy.

6. A worker who is strained, fatigued, or in pain has degraded grip strength, slower reaction time, and less ability to respond to unexpected patient movement. If the patient begins to fall mid-transfer and the worker's response is compromised, the worker cannot provide the corrective force needed to prevent the fall. The shared root cause is mechanical load that exceeds safe limits: loads that injure the worker are the same loads that, when the worker's capacity is reduced, prevent the worker from protecting the patient.

7. Accept any well-described version of the eight steps with correct physics reasoning. Examples: Step 1 (position close to patient) reduces the torque arm before lifting begins. Step 3 (feet shoulder-width apart, one foot forward) creates a wide, stable base of support in the direction of movement. Step 5 (bend at hips and knees) recruits large leg muscles and keeps the back straight, distributing force away from vulnerable lumbar structures. Step 7 (pivot with feet) eliminates rotational shear on lumbar discs while still achieving directional change.

8. A ceiling lift becomes non-optional when the patient's weight and condition exceed the manual-lift threshold defined by the unit's safe patient handling policy. The NIOSH Lifting Equation provides the quantitative standard: lifts that exceed the Recommended Weight Limit as calculated by the equation require equipment or team assistance. The safe patient handling framework makes the use of available equipment mandatory, not discretionary, above those thresholds.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Torque = 40 lb x 24 in = 960 inch-pounds per repositioning. This is a high torque value relative to the OSHA action limit of 3,400 newtons (approximately 760 lb-force) at L5-S1. Performed 30 times per shift, total torque exposure per shift is 28,800 inch-pounds. Over 90 days, this cumulative load degrades the annulus fibrosus of the L4-L5 disc, progressing from micro-tears to the full herniation seen on MRI. Accept reasonable approximations and any explanation that correctly links torque magnitude to cumulative disc risk.

2. Each individual task produces tension in the lumbar tissues that falls within the tissue's elastic range -- enough to stress it but not enough to cause acute pain. However, elasticity requires adequate recovery time between loading events. Thirty repositioning tasks per shift, repeated across 90 days, keeps the tissue near its elastic limit chronically. The accumulation of micro-damage from insufficient recovery is invisible until the final insult crosses the elastic limit of the annulus fibrosus. That final task feels no different from the first 2,699 that preceded it.

3. Knees straight: violates the bend-at-hips-and-knees principle. Keeping knees straight forces the lower back to do the work of the lift rather than the large leg muscles, dramatically increasing lumbar torque. Corrected: bend at the hips and knees to lower the center of gravity and recruit leg muscles. Load 24 inches from spine: violates the keep-the-load-close principle. Holding the draw sheet across the bed at arm's length maximizes the torque arm, tripling or quadrupling the torque compared to holding the load close. Corrected: position as close to the patient as possible before gripping the draw sheet, minimizing the horizontal distance.

4. Two examples: (a) Use a slide sheet or friction-reducing device. This reduces the force needed to reposition the patient, which reduces both the force and therefore the torque in the equation. Less force on the same lever arm produces less torque per task. (b) Position herself at the side of the bed and pull the patient toward her rather than reaching across. This reduces the horizontal distance from her spine to the load, shrinking the torque arm even if the force is similar. Any response that correctly identifies a technique change reducing either force or torque-arm distance should receive credit.

5. Each repositioning task places a compressive and shear load on the L4-L5 disc. The annulus fibrosus (the disc's outer ring) is an elastic tissue that can withstand this loading and recover -- up to its elastic limit. Repeated loading without sufficient recovery creates progressive micro-tears in the annulus fibrosus, gradually degrading its structural integrity. Eventually, enough of the outer ring is compromised that the nucleus pulposus (the inner gel) is able to bulge or extrude outward through the weakened area. The herniated material presses against the L4 or L5 nerve root, which runs adjacent to the disc, producing the characteristic radiating pain down the leg (sciatica). The injury is structural, progressive, and directly caused by repeated loading past the elastic recovery threshold.
