### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers; accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The textbook cites a 2010 meta-analysis of 148 studies showing that strong social relationships increase survival odds by 50%, and that social isolation carries a mortality risk comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. Additionally, the 2023 Surgeon General's advisory declared loneliness a national public-health crisis. These data make relationship quality a clinical concern because the effect sizes are comparable to major documented risk factors like smoking and obesity, meaning screening for and addressing relationship problems is as evidence-based as any other preventive intervention.

2. Active listening is a set of deliberate behaviors signaling full attention and comprehension: paraphrasing, summarizing, asking clarifying questions, and not interrupting. It differs from passive hearing (being present while mentally elsewhere) because it focuses on the speaker's meaning, not just their words. Behaviors that signal active listening include making eye contact and paraphrasing back what was said. Behaviors that signal its absence include looking at a phone during the conversation and offering solutions before the speaker finishes describing the problem.

3. Sample I-statement: "I feel overwhelmed when I see all the household tasks piling up and I'm the only one doing them, because I can't keep up with everything alone and I need us to share the load."

4. The three components are: (1) acknowledging the impact (recognizing the behavior caused harm, regardless of intent), (2) apologizing specifically (naming the exact behavior, not offering a vague "sorry"), and (3) naming the behavior change (describing what will be different going forward). All three are necessary because an apology without acknowledging impact feels hollow; acknowledging impact without a specific apology is vague; and both without a named behavior change leaves the other person with no reason to believe anything will improve.

5. The Four Horsemen are: (1) Criticism, attacking a person's character rather than addressing a specific behavior. (2) Contempt, communicating superiority, disgust, or disrespect. (3) Defensiveness, responding to concern by counter-attacking or denying responsibility. (4) Stonewalling, withdrawing from the interaction entirely. Gottman identifies contempt as the single strongest predictor of relationship failure because it communicates fundamental disrespect toward the other person, which over time undermines their sense of identity and destroys the foundation of trust.

6. A boundary is a statement about what a person will do in response to a specific behavior; it describes one's own action, not a requirement placed on the other person. A demand requires the other person to change. Example of the same situation: Demand: "You have to stop texting during dinner." Boundary: "If texting continues during dinner, I'm going to finish eating and leave the table."

7. Cultural context shapes what communication behaviors mean. Example 1: Direct eye contact signals confidence and attentiveness in many Western contexts, but may be read as disrespect or aggression in some other cultural settings. Example 2: Silence in conversation is comfortable and even respectful in some cultures, while in others it signals discomfort, avoidance, or disinterest. A healthcare worker who assumes their own norms are universal will misread patients who operate by different norms.

8. Psychological barriers to seeking help include shame, fear of being judged, worry about burdening others, and the normalization of harmful patterns over time. Social isolation is especially relevant in the context of intimate-partner violence because abusive partners often deliberately isolate their partners from peer and community networks. This removes the people who would recognize the problem, provide support, and offer a way out, making it both harder to see the situation clearly and harder to access resources.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The I-statement is: "I feel frustrated [I feel] when I see my work attributed to someone else [when], because it affects my grade and my learning [because]." This structure is more effective than a you-statement because it describes the speaker's experience and the concrete impact, rather than attacking the first student's character. It invites the first student to understand the problem rather than defend herself against a character accusation, which makes the conversation possible.

2. Deflecting every attempt to discuss the problem most closely resembles stonewalling, withdrawing from or refusing to engage with the interaction. The textbook says stonewalling often develops as a response to overwhelm, but its effect on the other person is abandonment, making the second student feel that the conflict will never be addressed and that the relationship is not safe.

3. Defensiveness, responding to a concern by counter-attacking or denying responsibility, signals that the listener's priority is self-protection, not understanding the other person's experience. Its antidote, according to the textbook, is taking responsibility rather than deflecting, which is what the first student eventually does when she agrees to the boundary and offers a specific apology.

4. The scenario explicitly mentions a specific apology (one component of repair). The boundary agreement implies some form of named behavior change (she will sign only her own work). What is less clearly established is acknowledgment of impact, that the first student recognized how her behavior specifically affected the second student's grade and learning. A complete repair would require all three components to be explicit. Accept answers that identify any component as potentially incomplete with reasonable justification.

5. Without the I-statement, the second student's frustration would continue to build without a path to resolution. The first student's stonewalling would likely escalate, and the pattern might develop into contempt on the second student's side as resentment grew. Without repair after conflict, the working relationship would probably deteriorate until one or both students requested a different assignment or the quality of their clinical work suffered. The absence of boundary-setting means the documentation problem would continue to affect the second student's learning and grade.
