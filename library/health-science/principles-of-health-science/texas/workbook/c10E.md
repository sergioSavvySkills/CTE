## Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

Read the scenario from the textbook, then answer the questions below.

> A student health worker is opening an encounter with a Hmong family whose grandfather has a serious diagnosis. The family has asked the medical team not to tell the grandfather directly. The student has been taught to prioritize individual patient autonomy, the biomedical default. But she also knows that in many cultures, including this family's, withholding a serious diagnosis from the patient and managing it within the family is not a violation of dignity, it is an expression of love and protection. Rather than making a unilateral decision, she uses the Kleinman explanatory model to open: "Can you help me understand how your family would like us to share information about your grandfather's health? What would feel right to him, and to you?" That question opens a conversation rather than closing one. The ethics consultation the team eventually arranges will navigate the legal and ethical complexity of disclosure and surrogate decision-making. Her job is not to resolve that tension alone, it is to ask the right question and bring in the right support.

1. The student identifies a tension between the biomedical default value of individual patient autonomy and the family's cultural approach to serious diagnosis. How does she navigate that tension without simply choosing one side?

2. The student uses the Kleinman explanatory model. Identify the exact question she asks, and explain why it is a better opening than stating the clinical team's policy on disclosure.

3. The chapter says her job "is not to resolve that tension alone." What resource does the team eventually involve, and what kinds of questions is that resource equipped to handle that the student is not?

4. Apply the concept of "behaviorally specific adaptations" to this scenario. The student's adaptation was a specific question rather than a policy statement. Write two additional behaviorally specific adaptations the student or care team could use in subsequent interactions with this family.

5. The chapter says the student's approach "opens a conversation rather than closing one." Describe a version of this scenario where the conversation is closed rather than opened. What would the student say or do, and what would the likely effect be on the family's trust and engagement with the medical team?

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers, accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Knowing about a cultural influence means recognizing, for instance, that LEP patients are entitled to qualified interpretation. Demonstrating understanding means being able to say specifically: "Before I take a history from this patient, I will request a qualified video interpreter, not a family member, because family interpreters create privacy problems and because medical terminology requires professional training to translate accurately." The second formulation names the specific behavior and the reasoning behind it, not just the fact.

2. Cultural competence frames cultural knowledge as a finite body of information to learn and then apply. The limitation is that cultures are too internally varied and too dynamic to be fully mapped. The chapter's example is a provider who memorized "Jehovah's Witnesses refuse blood products" but does not think to ask the specific patient about their personal beliefs and how far they extend them. Cultural humility requires ongoing curiosity about each patient's specific context, not a pre-loaded set of cultural facts.

3. Accept any two of the following: (1) Chaplaincy referral when a patient's religious beliefs are affecting treatment decisions, because chaplains are trained for these conversations and students are not. (2) Social-work referral when a patient's circumstances include housing instability, food insecurity, or a possible coercive situation. (3) Ethics consultation when a family's approach to disclosure or surrogate decision-making creates a legal or ethical conflict the clinical team cannot resolve alone.

4. Accept well-reasoned responses. The chapter suggests "examine yourself" is often overlooked because self-examination is less visible and less taught than the other three components. Students entering clinical settings typically receive training on specific adaptations (call an interpreter, use ASL) but less structured guidance on identifying their own defaults and the behaviors they will use to check them.

5. Family members may edit information they find alarming or embarrassing, may lack the medical vocabulary to translate accurately, and their presence may prevent the patient from disclosing sensitive concerns. The textbook also notes that even bilingual family members are not qualified interpreters for substantive clinical conversations because medical interpretation requires professional training.

6. Self-examination means identifying your own cultural defaults: ways of communicating, assumptions about decision-making, and beliefs about what "good care" looks like. These defaults feel neutral because they are the defaults of the surrounding system. The US biomedical system values individual autonomy, directness, efficiency, and technology. A provider who assumes every patient shares these values will consistently misread patients who come from cultures with different values around family decision-making, directness, or the role of technology in healing.

7. Her choice reflects the Title VI requirement for qualified medical interpretation and the CLAS Standards framework. The previous shift's use of the patient's teenage daughter had resulted in nodded-along non-understanding of discharge instructions. The student's three-minute pause prevented a medication error and a preventable readmission that would have resulted from the patient not understanding her discharge instructions.

8. Accept answers that draw on the five elements of informed consent from c10A: a patient answering closed questions without showing understanding in their own words, or a family member answering for a patient who appears capable. From c10E: rushing through an encounter because the interpreter service takes time, or assuming cognitive impairment in a patient because they cannot hear.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The student does not choose between the biomedical default and the family's approach. She recognizes she is not equipped to resolve the legal and ethical tension alone and does not attempt to. Instead, she asks a question that surfaces the family's preferences without imposing the biomedical norm. She then routes the complexity to an ethics consultation, which is the appropriate resource. Her job is to open the conversation and bring in the right support, not to adjudicate it.

2. The exact question is: "Can you help me understand how your family would like us to share information about your grandfather's health? What would feel right to him, and to you?" This is better than stating the clinical team's disclosure policy because it treats the family as partners in navigating the situation rather than informing them of a rule they may experience as a cultural violation. It gathers information before making any decision.

3. The team involves an ethics consultation. Ethics consultants are equipped to navigate the legal and ethical complexity of disclosure and surrogate decision-making, including when patient autonomy rights and cultural family-decision frameworks appear to conflict. The student is not trained in that area of clinical ethics and could cause harm by making an uninformed unilateral decision.

4. Accept any two behaviorally specific adaptations. Examples: "I will schedule longer appointment blocks for this family so they have time to confer before we ask for decisions." "When sharing information about the grandfather's condition, I will direct it to the family group and ask who among them would like to be the primary contact for clinical updates." "I will confirm at each visit who should be in the room before discussing clinical information."

5. A conversation-closing version: the student enters and states "Our clinical policy requires that we inform Mr. [name] directly of his diagnosis. We can have a family member present when we do that." The family is likely to feel dismissed, may disengage from the medical team, and may try to manage the situation independently in ways that complicate care. The student would have imposed the biomedical default without gathering information, losing the opportunity to understand the family's specific values and to navigate toward a workable solution.


### Part E: Reflection

No single correct answer. Award full credit for responses that: (1) use at least three chapter vocabulary terms accurately, (2) name specific, concrete behaviors rather than general intentions, and (3) demonstrate understanding that cultural humility is an ongoing practice, not a body of knowledge to memorize.
