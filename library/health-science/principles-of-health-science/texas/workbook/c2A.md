**Definitions:**

- **A.** A fraction equal to 1 that relates two units (for example, 2.54 cm / 1 in), used to change units without changing the underlying value
- **B.** The rule that whole-number doses must never be written with a decimal point and trailing zero (write 5 mg, not 5.0 mg) to prevent tenfold misreads
- **C.** Two units that differ by a factor of 1,000; confusing them has caused fatal medication errors and is explicitly addressed by ISMP
- **D.** The clinical standard system of measurement (g, kg, mL, L, cm, m, degrees C) used in US healthcare documentation
- **E.** Calculating a dose from the patient's body weight (mg/kg); the pediatric dosing standard and the highest-stakes use of unit conversion
- **F.** The rule that any dose less than one must be written with a zero before the decimal point (0.5 mg, not .5 mg) to prevent tenfold overdose misreads
- **G.** The unit-canceling method; write the given value, multiply by conversion factors as fractions, and cancel units until only the target unit remains
- **H.** Pounds, ounces, teaspoons, tablespoons, inches, feet, and degrees Fahrenheit; what US patients often report in and what clinicians must convert from

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The chapter opens with a pediatric nurse receiving an order for 20 mg/kg who must convert a parent-reported weight of 44 pounds to kilograms before drawing up the dose. If she skips the conversion and uses 44 as kilograms instead of 20, she gives more than twice the intended dose. The conversion is not optional because the patient's safety depends on it directly.

2. Dimensional analysis works by writing the starting value and then multiplying by conversion factors written as fractions so that unwanted units appear in both a numerator and a denominator and cancel. The key feature is that every unit cancellation is visible and checkable; there is no guessing about whether to multiply or divide, and any error in the setup is visible before the calculation reaches a patient.

3. This is a unit-assumption mistake, the most common error pattern in weight-based dosing. The clinician saw the number 77 and assumed it was already in kilograms. Writing "77 lb" and "35 kg" (the correct converted value) on separate lines before computing the dose, and never working with a bare number, would have prevented the error.

4. ISMP calls out mcg vs. mg because the two units differ by a factor of 1,000, meaning a tenfold error is not the risk here but a thousandfold error. Handwriting makes the distinction worse: a lowercase "c" can disappear in a rushed scrawl, making "mcg" look like "mg." A dose of 0.05 mg and 50 mcg are identical, but they live in different places on a drug label and in different columns of a drug handbook.

5. The leading zero rule states that any value less than 1 must have a zero before the decimal point (0.5 mg, not .5 mg). It exists because a reader who misses the decimal point reads ".5" as "5," a tenfold overdose. The chapter notes this error has killed patients, most often children receiving sedatives or anticoagulants at ten times their intended dose.

6. The trailing zero rule states that whole-number doses must never be written with a decimal and a trailing zero (write 5 mg, not 5.0 mg). A "5.0" can become "50" if the decimal point smears on a faxed or photocopied order, producing a tenfold overdose. The error is invisible once the decimal disappears.

7. 37 x 1.8 = 66.6; 66.6 + 32 = 98.6 degrees Fahrenheit. Accept any correct step-by-step arithmetic showing the formula applied.

8. Metric-to-metric conversions (kg to g, L to mL) also appear in clinical work because orders may be written in one sub-unit and measured in another. Their advantage is that they are all powers of ten, making them easier to move through mentally than US customary conversions, which use arbitrary ratios such as 2.2 and 2.54.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Units used: lb, kg, mg, kg (in the denominator of the dose rate), mg (the answer). Writing units at every step ensures that each cancellation is visible and verifiable; a unit that fails to cancel is an immediate signal that a conversion step is missing or wrong, before the number reaches the patient.

2. The habit of writing calculations on a scratch sheet before entering anything into the system addresses the "skipping dimensional analysis and estimating" error pattern. It also addresses "decimal misplacement," because writing the full calculation makes the position of every decimal point visible and reviewable.

3. Applying 40 mg/kg directly to 33 lb: 33 lb x (40 mg / 1 lb) = 1,320 mg/day. Divided by two doses = 660 mg per dose. The correct dose is 300 mg per dose. The erroneous dose is 2.2 times higher (the same ratio as lb to kg). In a pediatric patient, receiving 660 mg of amoxicillin when the intended dose is 300 mg is a significant overdose with potential for gastrointestinal harm and, at higher levels, seizure risk.

4. Documenting only 600 mg/day without the per-dose amount leaves the incoming clinician to perform the division themselves, which introduces a calculation step and a potential error. If that clinician misreads the order as 600 mg per dose rather than per day, the patient receives twice the intended dose. Audience-first documentation includes the per-dose figure so the reader has the number they will actually act on.

5. The assistant should confirm the current weight at the start of the visit before calculating the dose. Relying on a weight from a previous visit without re-checking addresses the "accepting a patient-reported weight without confirming the unit" error pattern; it also addresses general unit-assumption risk because the child's weight will have changed since the last visit and a pediatric dose calculated on an outdated weight may be incorrect.
