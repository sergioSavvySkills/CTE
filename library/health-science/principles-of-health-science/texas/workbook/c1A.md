**Definitions:**

- **A.** Asking a patient to explain instructions back in their own words to confirm understanding
- **B.** Sentence structure in which the subject performs the action; names the actor and is shorter
- **C.** The ISMP/Joint Commission list of abbreviations banned from clinical documentation because they cause medication errors
- **D.** Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation; the standard structured handoff format
- **E.** The receiver repeats a spoken message back to the sender to confirm accurate receipt
- **F.** Adjusting word choice, detail level, and tone for the specific reader or listener
- **G.** Using everyday words over jargon so the message is understandable on the first read
- **H.** Using specific, measurable terms over vague ones; essential for clinical documentation

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers, accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. A soft skill implies a personality trait you either have or don't have (being friendly, articulate). A clinical skill is learnable and observable. The textbook's evidence: The Joint Commission identifies communication failure as a leading root cause of serious patient-harm events, injuries that are preventable when the right techniques are used.

2. The sentence uses clinical jargon, "administer," "acetylsalicylic acid," "analgesic and antipyretic", that most patients won't understand. A plain-language rewrite: "We're going to give you an aspirin tablet (325 mg) by mouth. It will help with the pain and bring down your fever."

3. S (Situation): What is happening right now, in one sentence. B (Background): Relevant history, current medications, what has already been done, 2-3 sentences. A (Assessment): The sender's clinical judgment about what is going on, not just a list of facts. R (Recommendation): The specific action being requested of the receiver.

4. Plain means simple; precise means exact. They work together when the simple word is also the exact word. Example: "laceration", one word, specific to a cut that penetrates the skin (precise), and well understood by any clinician (plain in that context). For a patient, "cut" can be both plain and precise when combined with a measurement.

5. "The nurse inserted the catheter at 14:00."

6. The Assessment step communicates clinical judgment, the sender's interpretation of what the data means. Simply re-listing vital signs leaves the receiver to draw their own conclusion, which takes more time and may produce the wrong conclusion. Saying "I'm concerned this could be cardiac" tells the physician exactly why the call is urgent and what decision they need to make.

7. Read-back is for spoken communications between healthcare workers (verbal orders, critical lab values), the receiver repeats back what they heard so the sender can confirm accuracy. Teach-back is for patient education, the patient restates the information in their own words to confirm they understood. Use read-back when a misheard word between providers could cause harm; use teach-back when a patient's compliance or self-care depends on their understanding.

8. The "Do Not Use" list exists because certain abbreviations are routinely misread in ways that cause patient harm. Example: "U" for "units" looks like the number 0, and has caused insulin overdoses ten times the prescribed dose. The rule is to write out the full word "units" every time.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Two examples of precise language: (a) "approximately 103°F", gives a specific number rather than "high fever" or "really hot"; (b) "Child age 4", gives an exact age rather than "young child," which matters because fever thresholds and treatment decisions differ by age. Accept other valid examples from the note.

2. Read-back occurs when Maria reads her documented note back to the physician over the intercom. Read-back was appropriate here because the handoff was spoken (intercom, not a shared EHR view), the information included a specific number (103°F), and a misheard detail could affect the physician's decision. Teach-back would not be appropriate here because the physician is a clinician, not a patient, teach-back is for patient education to confirm their comprehension for self-care.

3. Rectal temperatures are typically 0.5–1°F higher than oral readings. A rectal 103°F means a true temperature around 103°F; an oral reading of "about 103" could mean the child's core temperature is actually 103.5°F or higher. Documenting "measurement method unclear" applies the principle of precise language, if you don't know, document what you know (that the method is unknown) rather than guessing. Documenting false precision is worse than documenting acknowledged uncertainty.

4. Sample SBAR:
   - **S:** "I just took a call from a mother about her 4-year-old with a fever she reports at approximately 103°F. She's unsure if it was measured rectally or orally."
   - **B:** "The child has no other reported symptoms. Mother called in; no chart in the system for this patient yet. I haven't spoken with the child directly."
   - **A:** "I'm not sure if this needs same-day care or if we can give home guidance, the measurement method being unclear makes the temperature's significance uncertain."
   - **R:** "Can you advise? Should I call the mother back for more information or direct her to an urgent care?"

5. Sample break in the loop: The physician receives Maria's read-back but doesn't verbally confirm, he nods or waves her off while looking at another chart. Maria interprets the non-response as confirmation and ends the interaction. The physician then sees the note 20 minutes later and realizes the fever reading was flagged but no follow-up was documented. The loop was never confirmed closed.
