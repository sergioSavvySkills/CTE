**Definitions:**

- **A.** Texas statutory form designating a surrogate decision-maker under HSC Chapter 166; requires two eligible witnesses or notarization
- **B.** The requirement that consent was given freely, without sedation, coercion, or duress; one of the five elements verified on document review
- **C.** The HIPAA-required document (45 CFR 164.520) given at first encounter; the patient acknowledges receipt, which is not the same as consenting to treatment
- **D.** A witness disqualified under Texas advance-directive statutes, including the attending physician, any heir or beneficiary, and anyone with a financial interest in the healthcare facility
- **E.** The facility-specific statement of patient rights per Joint Commission and CMS Conditions of Participation; provided to patients, not signed by them
- **F.** Documentation of informed refusal of continued care; requires evidence of the risks explained and the patient's voluntary decision, not just a signature
- **G.** Texas statutory "living will" form (HSC Chapter 166); takes effect if the patient has a terminal or irreversible condition and cannot communicate
- **H.** The procedure-specific document capturing patient identifiers, procedure description, risks, benefits, and alternatives, with witnessed signatures and accurate timing
- **I.** Texas-specific form signed by the patient (or surrogate) and physician directing EMS and non-hospital settings not to resuscitate

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers, accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Active document review requires knowing: (1) what each document should contain, (2) what a complete form looks like, (3) what signatures and witnesses are required by law, and (4) what happens if something is missing. A passive reviewer may note that a form is signed; an active reviewer checks whether the right person signed, at the right time, with the required witnesses, and with all required fields accurate.

2. The NPP acknowledgment means the patient received the document; it does not mean the patient agreed to treatment, consented to a procedure, or waived any rights. Treating them as equivalent is a legal error because the informed consent form, not the NPP, is the document that satisfies the legal requirement for patient agreement before a procedure. It is an ethical error because a patient who only signed an NPP has not been given the opportunity to exercise the consent right that protects their autonomy.

3. Two specific problems: the attending physician is an ineligible witness under Texas HSC Chapter 166 by name, and an adult son who stands to inherit may be financially interested in the patient's estate, making him an ineligible witness as well. The corrective action depends on whether the patient is still able to communicate: if so, the patient should execute a new directive with two eligible, unrelated witnesses or a notary. If the patient can no longer communicate, the document is legally insufficient and surrogate decision-making under the Texas Consent to Medical Treatment Act would apply.

4. The most consequential failure is consent signed after sedation was given. It is especially serious because it eliminates the voluntariness element entirely: a patient who has received premedication cannot meaningfully make a free, uncoerced decision. The correct remedy when discovered before the procedure proceeds is to pause the procedure, wait until the patient is alert and has capacity, and conduct a new consent conversation with a new form signed with the correct time. Proceeding on the insufficient consent exposes the facility and provider to legal and ethical liability.

5. The AMA discharge form documents that a patient has left against medical advice. This means the form must contain evidence that the risks of leaving were explained, the alternatives were offered, and the patient's voluntary decision to leave was recorded despite that information. Treating it as a punishment label means using it punitively or withholding care from patients who previously left AMA, which the chapter says is incorrect. A patient who leaves AMA retains the right to return to the same facility for care without any legal consequence.

6. A complete release of records authorization must include: the name of the person or entity to whom disclosure is made, a description of the information to be disclosed, the purpose of the disclosure, and an expiration date or event. Each element matters because the authorization must be specific enough for the patient to know exactly what is being shared, with whom, and for how long. An authorization without an expiration allows indefinite use of the patient's health information, which is both a privacy risk and inconsistent with HIPAA requirements.

7. The student is demonstrating the records-review skill this chapter is built around: knowing what a complete document should look like, identifying a specific gap, and routing the concern to the person with authority to act on it. Routing is important because the student's role is not to halt a procedure or to make the legal determination alone; it is to bring the discrepancy to someone with the authority and training to evaluate it and take appropriate action. Ignoring the discrepancy would leave the documentation failure unaddressed, and acting alone would exceed the student's scope.

8. Three documents requiring witnesses or notarization: (1) Texas Directive to Physicians: two eligible witnesses or notarization; witnesses may not be the attending physician, any heir or beneficiary, or anyone with a financial interest in the facility. (2) Medical Power of Attorney: two eligible witnesses or notarization, with the same witness restrictions as the Directive. (3) Out-of-Hospital DNR: signed by the patient or surrogate and a physician; the physician signature serves a different function than witnessing, but the form requires both the patient/surrogate signature and the physician's attestation.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The specific failure is that the consent form was signed at 7:45 a.m., fifteen minutes after the patient received IV midazolam at 7:30 a.m. The element that is missing is voluntariness: consent must be given freely, without sedation, coercion, or duress. A patient who has received IV midazolam is sedated and cannot make a truly voluntary decision. The signature on the form does not satisfy the voluntariness element because the capacity to make a free choice was compromised at the time of signing.

2. The completed form was still insufficient because legal sufficiency requires not only that all fields be filled in and a signature be present, but that the signature was obtained under conditions that satisfy all five elements, including voluntariness. A form that looks complete on its face can fail on timing: the sequence of events documented in the anesthesia record contradicts the validity of the consent. This illustrates that document review is not a checkbox exercise; it requires cross-referencing the form against other records to verify that the process behind the signature was sound.

3. The student's role as a reviewer is to identify gaps and route them to the appropriate person, not to make clinical decisions or halt procedures independently. Halting the procedure alone would exceed the student's scope and authority. Ignoring the discrepancy would leave a documented voluntariness failure unaddressed and allow the procedure to proceed on legally insufficient consent. Routing to the charge nurse was correct because the charge nurse has the authority to review the concern, contact the surgeon, and pause the procedure through the appropriate chain.

4. Re-obtaining consent was necessary because voluntariness cannot be retroactively established. Noting the time discrepancy in the chart would document the problem but would not correct it: the procedure would still proceed on a consent that did not satisfy the voluntariness element. The legal and ethical remedy is to obtain new consent from the patient when she has capacity, conducts the consent conversation without sedation, and signs a new form with an accurate time. This creates a valid consent record and gives the patient the opportunity to make a genuine, uncoerced decision.

5. A parallel discrepancy on a Texas Directive to Physicians might look like this: the document is signed and dated by the patient, the signature lines are filled in, and two witnesses are named. On closer review, one witness is listed with the same surname as the patient (suggesting a family relationship that may indicate financial interest) and the other is listed with the title "RN" at the facility (suggesting an employee of the healthcare organization, which would disqualify them under Texas law). The document looks complete but has two ineligible witnesses, making it legally insufficient. The correct routing action is to flag the witness disqualifications to the charge nurse or patient-rights advocate and, if the patient still has capacity, arrange for the patient to execute a new directive with eligible witnesses or a notary.
