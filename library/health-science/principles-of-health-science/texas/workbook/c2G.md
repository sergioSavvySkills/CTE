### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers -- accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The three components are the sensor (receptor), which monitors a variable such as body temperature; the control center, which compares the reading to a setpoint and decides whether corrective action is needed; and the effector, which performs the corrective action. Example: thermoreceptors in the skin (sensor) report rising temperature to the hypothalamus (control center), which activates sweat glands (effector).

2. Dynamic equilibrium means the body's internal values are not perfectly still but are constantly fluctuating within a narrow range around a setpoint and being continuously corrected. The distinction matters because it means the system must be actively maintained at all times, not just restored occasionally. Even small perturbations trigger ongoing adjustments by sensors, control centers, and effectors.

3. Negative feedback loops push variables back toward a stable setpoint, making them ideal for maintaining the constant internal conditions cells need to survive. Positive feedback loops amplify changes and could be lethal if unchecked, so the body only uses them when a process needs to escalate quickly to a clear endpoint. The endpoint event itself breaks the loop: delivery ends the oxytocin-contraction cycle; a sealed vessel ends the platelet-aggregation cycle.

4. Exercise produces carbon dioxide as a byproduct of cellular respiration. CO2 combines with water in the blood to form carbonic acid, which releases hydrogen ions and lowers blood pH. The respiratory system compensates by increasing breathing rate to blow off CO2, which shifts the equation back toward neutral and raises pH. The specific system is the bicarbonate buffer system working alongside the respiratory system.

5. Sweating is the primary effector for cooling the body when temperature rises above the setpoint. If sweating has stopped in a patient whose temperature is still rising, the evaporative cooling mechanism has failed and the feedback loop can no longer remove excess heat. Without this effector, the control center has no effective way to bring temperature back toward the setpoint, allowing body temperature to keep climbing to dangerous levels that can damage the brain and other organs.

6. Type 1 diabetes destroys the insulin-producing cells of the pancreas, breaking the blood glucose negative feedback loop. Without insulin, cells cannot absorb glucose from the blood, so glucose accumulates (hyperglycemia). The body, starved of cellular fuel, breaks down fat and produces ketoacids, lowering blood pH (metabolic acidosis). The kidneys try to flush excess glucose by pulling water into the urine (polyuria), causing dehydration and intense thirst (polydipsia). The endocrine, metabolic, renal, and acid-base systems are all disrupted by one failing loop.

7. Insulin and glucagon are chemical effectors, hormones released into the bloodstream that signal target cells to take action (absorb or release glucose). Shivering is a mechanical effector, the rapid contraction of skeletal muscles that generates heat as a byproduct. Both are activated by a control center (the pancreas for glucose, the hypothalamus for temperature) in response to a sensor reading that has drifted from the setpoint, and both produce corrective action designed to return the variable to the setpoint.

8. The bicarbonate buffer system absorbs or releases hydrogen ions to keep blood pH between 7.35 and 7.45. During exercise, CO2 produced by muscles enters the blood and forms carbonic acid, lowering pH. The lungs respond by increasing breathing rate to exhale more CO2, which reduces carbonic acid formation and raises pH back toward normal. Breathing fast is the respiratory contribution to acid-base homeostasis, not simply a response to oxygen demand.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Blood pH of 7.28: this is below the 7.35 lower limit, representing a failure of the bicarbonate buffer system to neutralize ketoacids produced by fat breakdown. Bicarbonate of 14: bicarbonate has been consumed buffering the acid load; this is a marker of system failure, not compensation. Respiratory rate of 28: this is a compensatory response -- the respiratory system is acting as an effector, increasing CO2 elimination to partially raise blood pH.

2. "Treating the numbers" means administering bicarbonate to raise pH into the normal range, which would relieve the immediate crisis but would not stop ketoacid production. "Restoring the homeostatic system" means addressing the root cause -- the lack of insulin -- so the glucose feedback loop can function again, cells can use glucose for fuel, fat breakdown and ketoacid production stop, and the bicarbonate system is no longer overwhelmed. Without insulin, acidosis would recur.

3. The respiratory system is acting as an effector in this scenario. The signal it is responding to is the drop in blood pH (low pH means excess hydrogen ions), which is detected by chemoreceptors that serve as sensors. The chemoreceptors signal the brainstem control center, which increases respiratory rate to blow off CO2 and reduce the acid load. This is a compensatory negative feedback response to the metabolic acidosis.

4. IV bicarbonate addresses the immediate acid-base failure: it raises blood pH by adding bicarbonate to buffer the ketoacids already present. Insulin addresses the root cause: it restores the glucose feedback loop, allowing cells to absorb glucose and stopping the fat breakdown that is producing ketoacids. Bicarbonate alone would not be enough because without insulin, ketoacid production would continue and pH would fall again. Both treatments are needed to interrupt the failing loop at different points.

5. Excessive urination (polyuria): when blood glucose is very high, the kidneys cannot fully reabsorb glucose from the filtrate, so glucose spills into the urine. Glucose in the urine draws water along with it through osmosis, producing large urine volumes. This represents a failure of the normal renal reabsorption process, driven by the glucose homeostatic loop failure. Intense thirst (polydipsia): the loss of water through polyuria depletes blood volume and raises blood osmolarity. Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect the increased concentration and signal thirst, triggering drinking behavior to restore fluid balance. This is actually the ADH/water-balance feedback system responding correctly to the dehydration created by the glucose loop failure.
