> A pregnant patient in her third trimester tells her nurse that she plans to do zuo yuezi, a postpartum confinement period of 30 days, with her mother and mother-in-law providing care and cooking traditional foods. Rather than dismissing it or ignoring it, she uses the Kleinman explanatory-model framing: "Can you tell me what the confinement period will look like for you? What foods are you planning? 3. The scenario says this "prevented a conflict that would have damaged the therapeutic relationship." Explain what that conflict might have looked like. How does a damaged therapeutic relationship affect patient outcomes?

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Research requires locating sources from two different literatures: the biomedical literature (PubMed, Cochrane Reviews, NCCIH databases) that evaluates practices using clinical trial evidence, and the ethnographic literature (Social Science and Medicine, Culture, Medicine, and Psychiatry) that documents what a practice means, who performs it, and in what context. Compare requires placing practices side by side using a consistent six-dimension framework so the comparison is fair and does not apply different standards to Western versus non-Western practices.

2. The six dimensions and their questions: (1) Problem: What health challenge is the practice addressing? (2) Practice: What exactly does the practitioner do, and what does the patient experience? (3) Mechanism: What explanation does the cultural framework offer for why the practice works? (4) Evidence: What does the scientific literature say about effectiveness? (5) Accessibility: Who can access this practice, and at what cost? (6) Integration with biomedicine: Is this practice used alongside, in place of, or in parallel with biomedical care?

3. The pitfall is Orientalism. An example from the chapter: describing acupuncture as "sticking needles in meridian points based on ancient superstition" while describing Western pain management as "evidence-based pharmacology" applies an unfair standard. The non-Western practice is described dismissively and without evidence engagement, while the Western practice is described in technical terms that assume its evidence base without scrutiny.

4. False equivalence means treating all practices as equally valid regardless of evidence, simply because both originate in cultural tradition. Orientalism means treating non-Western practices as inherently less valid than Western ones without engaging evidence. Orientalism applies an unfairly low standard to traditional practices; false equivalence removes the evidence standard entirely for all practices. Avoiding both requires applying the same rigorous evidence evaluation to every practice regardless of its cultural origin.

5. The chapter cites the 2017 Journal of Pain meta-analysis, which concluded that acupuncture shows statistically significant effects for chronic low-back, neck, shoulder, and osteoarthritis pain compared with sham controls. The NCCIH rates acupuncture as having strong evidence for several chronic pain conditions. By contrast, the chapter rates long-term opioid therapy for chronic non-cancer pain as having mixed evidence, with limited sustained efficacy and significant evidence of harms including dependence and overdose.

6. The qualifier "for low-risk deliveries" is critical because the evidence for midwifery care applies specifically to patients without complications. High-risk pregnancies, those with gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, fetal malpresentation, or other complications, require physician-level monitoring and intervention capacity. The evidence tells you what is true for a specific patient population in specific conditions, not what is universally superior. Removing the qualifier would be a misrepresentation of what the evidence shows.

7. The doula is a trained non-clinical support person present during labor and delivery. It expanded in BIPOC communities in the US because those communities have measurably higher maternal mortality rates, and doula support has been shown to reduce cesarean rates, preterm birth, and low birth weight in multiple studies. The evidence base is strong: the doula role exists at the intersection of cultural knowledge, social support, and the biomedical system.

8. The initial biomedical response to Ebola, which focused on isolation, barrier nursing, and contact tracing, collided with community burial practices that were central to the cultural understanding of respectful death. What was missing was cultural knowledge: understanding of why the burial practices existed and what they meant to the community. When public-health teams worked with community and religious leaders to adapt burial practices rather than eliminate them, the response became more effective.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The Kleinman explanatory model starts with the patient's own understanding of what is happening and why. The nurse opens with a question rather than corrections because she does not yet know which elements of zuo yuezi the patient plans to follow or whether any of them create clinical concerns. Leading with corrections would impose the biomedical framework before understanding the patient's specific plan, potentially shutting down the patient's disclosure and creating the exact conflict the nurse is trying to avoid.

2. The nurse applies different evidence evaluations to each element. Salt restriction aligns with standard postpartum guidance (biomedical evidence supports it). Cold-food restriction has no known clinical harm and carries significant cultural meaning, so affirming it respects the patient's cultural context without creating a clinical risk. No showering for 30 days raises a specific clinical concern, hygiene and wound care after delivery, which the nurse addresses directly because this element has a potential evidence-based safety implication.

3. The conflict might have looked like the nurse dismissing the entire zuo yuezi plan, the patient feeling judged or disrespected, and the patient becoming less forthcoming about her plans for the postpartum period. A damaged therapeutic relationship reduces a patient's likelihood of sharing information honestly, following through on agreed clinical plans, and returning for follow-up care, all of which directly affect health outcomes.

4. The nurse should raise the concern directly but without framing the practice as primitive or wrong. For example: "I want to make sure we talk about the no-showering practice because after delivery, especially if you have stitches, keeping the area clean reduces the risk of infection. Can we talk about what that might look like for you?" This acknowledges the clinical concern, invites the patient into a problem-solving conversation, and does not dismiss the tradition. The nurse should avoid saying anything that characterizes the practice as dangerous, superstitious, or culturally inferior.

5. The textbook mentions the four-minute duration to anticipate the objection that cultural humility and patient-centered conversation are too time-consuming for a clinical workflow. By specifying that the entire culturally informed conversation took four minutes and prevented a conflict that would have required far more time and potentially a readmission to resolve, the chapter makes the case that these adaptations are efficient, not burdensome.
