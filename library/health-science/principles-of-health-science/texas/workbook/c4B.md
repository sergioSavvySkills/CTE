**Definitions:**

- **A.** Situation, Behavior, Impact; a structured format for giving feedback about behavior, not character
- **B.** Owning outcomes and following up on commitments; flows both ways in a delegation relationship
- **C.** A TeamSTEPPS safety-escalation script used to speak up under an authority gradient: "I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue"
- **D.** A TeamSTEPPS post-event review identifying what went well, what did not, and what to change; the most powerful and most often skipped of the three tools
- **E.** A TeamSTEPPS start-of-shift tool to establish a shared mental model of goals and roles; typically three to five minutes
- **F.** Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound; the framework used for personal and team goal-setting
- **G.** The NCSBN framework for safe delegation: right task, right person, right circumstance, right direction and communication, right supervision and evaluation
- **H.** The TeamSTEPPS norm obligating a team member to raise a safety concern twice; if dismissed twice, escalate to a third party
- **I.** A TeamSTEPPS mid-shift regroup when something changes; not a status update but a re-alignment of plan and roles

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. A vague goal cannot be measured, cannot be evaluated, and cannot be improved. "I'll communicate better this semester" fails at least three SMART criteria immediately: it is not Specific (it does not name a skill or define who is involved), not Measurable (there is no evidence standard), and not Time-bound (the semester is a duration, not a deadline tied to a defined milestone). Without those elements, there is no agreed-upon way to know if the goal was met.

2. A shared mental model is everyone's understanding of who is doing what, what the goal is, and what to do when something changes. The brief is designed to create this at the start of a shift. It covers three things: the goal for this period, the roles of each person, and any known risks. The brief's job is to make sure no one starts the work with a different picture of what is happening.

3. Debriefs are most often skipped because after a demanding shift or difficult event, the natural impulse is to leave. It takes extra intention to pause and review. The textbook says a team that only executes without reviewing tends to repeat the same errors: it converts no experience into improvement because it never does the review work that converts raw experience into a lesson the team can act on next time.

4. Delegation is a structured decision about who is best positioned to do a specific task right now; off-loading is simply moving a task off your list without that structure. A valid delegation requires all five rights to be met: the task must be appropriate to delegate, the person must have verified scope and competency, the timing and workload must be right, the instructions must be clear, and a follow-up plan must exist. If any one of the five is not met, the delegation carries risk even if the other four are fine.

5. The person delegating remains responsible for the outcome; they do not hand off the task and walk away. The textbook is explicit: accountability flows both ways. The delegating clinician must follow up to check that the task was completed correctly. If something changes mid-task, the line of communication goes immediately back to the delegator, and the delegator is still on the hook for the result.

6. Statements like "you're irresponsible" or "you always do this" are character judgments. They put the receiver on the defensive, which makes behavior change less likely because the receiver's energy goes into protecting themselves rather than hearing and acting on the information. SBI delivers the same information by naming the specific situation, describing only what was observed, and explaining the actual effect on the team or patient, none of which is a character attack.

7. The correct receiving behaviors are: listen to completion without interrupting, ask one clarifying question if needed, thank the giver for the information, and then change the behavior. The last step, changing the behavior, is the one that matters most: the textbook frames the entire goal of receiving feedback as improvement, not winning the conversation or defending oneself.

8. The CUS script escalates: "I'm Concerned" is the first signal, marking that something is off. "I'm Uncomfortable" is the second level, signaling greater urgency. "This is a Safety issue" is the final escalation, naming the concern as a patient-safety matter. The two-challenge rule is triggered when the first CUS statement (and then the second) is dismissed: at that point, the team member is obligated to escalate to a third party rather than dropping the concern.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Opening with what went well gives the team psychological safety to then discuss what did not, because it signals that the debrief is an evaluation of performance, not a blame session. If the charge nurse had opened with "what went wrong," team members would likely have become defensive, minimized their contributions, or gone silent to avoid being named as the cause of the error. The positive opening earns trust that makes the critical part of the debrief possible.

2. The outcome demonstrates that the debrief, when run well, converts experience into learning that the team owns. The protocol was not imposed: it came from the team's own analysis of what failed. Because the team generated it themselves, they did not need to be told to follow it. This illustrates the textbook's claim that debrief converts experience into improvement, and it also shows what accountability looks like when it operates at the team level rather than being assigned from above.

3. Sample SBI statement: "At last night's code on Bed 6 (Situation), the crash cart was not in its designated position at the start of the shift and took 90 seconds to locate during the emergency (Behavior). That delay meant compressions were interrupted and the team had to work without key equipment during the most critical window (Impact)." Accept any specific, neutral, and behavior-focused statement that follows the three-element structure.

4. Sample SMART goal: "By the end of next week's two shifts, I will complete and document a crash-cart check at the start of each of my shifts, verified by the charge nurse's initials on the daily checklist, so that equipment is confirmed ready before any emergency occurs." Evaluate student goals against all five criteria: Specific (names the task and who), Measurable (charge nurse initials, each shift), Achievable (within the student's scope), Relevant (directly addresses the cart-location failure), Time-bound (end of next week's two shifts).

5. The team members who ran the check without being told demonstrated accountability (Chapter 4A): they owned the outcome of the protocol they agreed to in the debrief and followed through without external prompting. Accept also integrity (doing what you said you would do) or willingness to develop others if the student argues that running the check modeled the protocol for teammates new to the protocol. Award credit for any well-supported connection to a specific Chapter 4A trait.
