### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Negligence is failure to exercise reasonable care and can apply to anyone. Malpractice is professional negligence and applies only to licensed practitioners whose conduct falls below the accepted standard of care within their profession. A non-healthcare worker cannot commit malpractice because malpractice requires a professional duty of care that arises from a licensed clinical relationship. A restaurant worker who causes a customer to fall could be negligent; they cannot commit malpractice.

2. Example: a nurse administers the wrong medication from an unlabeled drawer. The nurse bears personal liability for the administration error. The hospital bears vicarious liability under respondeat superior because the nurse was acting within the scope of employment. The hospital also faces potential institutional liability if the systemic failure, an unlabeled shared drawer, reflects unsafe policies or inadequate procedures that the administration was responsible for maintaining.

3. (1) Duty of care: a clinician-patient relationship must exist; a court would look for evidence of a formal care encounter, such as a chart, an admission record, or a documented visit. (2) Breach: the conduct must fall below the standard of care; expert witnesses would testify about what a reasonably competent practitioner in the same role would have done. (3) Causation: the breach must have directly caused the harm; the court examines whether the patient's harm would have occurred regardless of the breach or whether the breach was the actual and proximate cause. (4) Damages: the patient must have suffered measurable harm; records of injury, treatment costs, lost wages, and pain are evaluated.

4. Breach is often relatively straightforward to establish with expert testimony comparing the practitioner's conduct to published guidelines. Causation is harder because even when a breach occurred, the defense may argue that the patient's harm would have resulted anyway from the underlying condition, a pre-existing comorbidity, or another cause unrelated to the breach. Proving "but for this breach, the harm would not have occurred" requires showing that the breach was both the factual and proximate cause of a specific, measurable injury, which often requires complex medical and statistical evidence.

5. In a legal context, it means the medical record is the jury's window into what happened. A clinician's memory of doing a thorough assessment is not evidence; the record is. A protective clinical note includes specific timestamps, objective findings stated in measurable terms, comparison to baseline, the clinician's documented reasoning for or against escalation, and actions taken. These details allow a defense team to reconstruct what the clinician did and why it was consistent with the standard of care.

6. Spoliation of evidence is altering a medical record after a bad outcome in order to change the apparent sequence of events or obscure what occurred. Courts may instruct juries to assume the worst about an altered record, essentially treating the alteration itself as evidence that the original record contained unfavorable information. A practitioner who alters a record compounds the original error with a second, potentially criminal act that signals consciousness of wrongdoing, which makes the legal situation far worse.

7. Human error is an honest mistake that any reasonable person might make; the appropriate response is consoling. At-risk behavior is a shortcut or workaround that increases risk; the appropriate response is coaching to help the practitioner recognize and correct the behavior. Reckless behavior is knowingly ignoring a significant risk; the appropriate response is disciplinary action. The framework warns against treating all adverse events as individual failures because research shows that blame-focused cultures discourage honest error reporting, which prevents systems from identifying the actual conditions that make errors more likely.

8. The AHRQ CANDOR toolkit promotes early, transparent communication with patients and families after a harm event, combined with a fair and thorough investigation. It is designed to solve the problem of defensive silence after adverse events, which historically led to increased patient anger, damaged relationships, and costly litigation. In terms of the four elements, CANDOR does not change whether the elements are present, but it addresses the patient's and family's experience of the aftermath, often reducing the likelihood that a legitimate grievance escalates to a lawsuit.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Duty: present. The nurse had an established care relationship with the patient recovering from surgery. Breach: contested. The documentation suggests the nurse did not perform or document specific measurements, clinical reasoning, or escalation, which may fall below what a reasonably competent nurse would do for a post-surgical patient reporting increasing pain and swelling. If the nurse did perform an adequate assessment but simply failed to document it, breach is still contested. Causation: contested. The central question is whether appropriate escalation at 2:00 a.m. would have prevented the DVT by morning. This depends on how rapidly the thrombosis developed and whether intervention at that point would have changed the outcome. Damages: present. The patient developed a DVT, a measurable physical harm.

2. Clinical performance and legal protection are not the same thing. A clinician who does excellent work but documents it poorly is legally vulnerable because the record, not the memory, is the evidence. Every student should understand that documentation is not administrative busywork. It is the legal record of clinical reasoning and action. A practitioner who cannot prove they assessed a finding, reasoned about it, and acted appropriately has no legal defense even if they did exactly that.

3. Sample better note: "02:00. Patient reports increasing right leg pain rated 6/10 and visible swelling noted proximal to the right calf, circumference approximately 2 cm greater than the left at mid-calf level. Skin warm to touch bilaterally. No erythema or skin breakdown observed. Patient is one day post right hip arthroplasty. Clinical concern for possible DVT given post-surgical status and asymmetric swelling. Physician on call notified at 02:05. Orders received for lower-extremity Doppler ultrasound in the morning and continued elevation of the extremity. Patient instructed to notify staff if pain increases. Will reassess at 04:00." Accept any note that includes specific objective measurements, bilateral comparison, documented clinical reasoning, escalation action with time, and follow-up plan.

4. The nurse's behavior is most consistent with at-risk behavior in the Just Culture framework. At-risk behavior is a shortcut or workaround that increases risk. Writing a vague, non-specific note rather than a thorough assessment is a common clinical shortcut, not a deliberate decision to harm the patient and not fully reckless. It suggests the nurse may not have recognized the risk inherent in inadequate documentation. The appropriate Just Culture response is coaching to help the nurse understand why specificity in post-surgical documentation matters and what a complete assessment note should include.

5. If the nurse had escalated appropriately at 2:00 a.m. and the DVT still developed, causation would be the element most affected. If the defense could show that a physician was notified, that appropriate orders were placed or considered, and that the DVT developed despite reasonable intervention, the plaintiff would need to prove that something else the nurse should have done would have prevented the harm. The claim might still proceed if the plaintiff could show a specific missed intervention that would have changed the outcome, but the causation argument becomes much harder to sustain when the record shows appropriate assessment and escalation.
