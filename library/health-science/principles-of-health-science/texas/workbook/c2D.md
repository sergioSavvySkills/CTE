**Definitions:**

- **A.** The second step in the writing sequence; gathering facts, data, and source citations; requires citing original sources, never fabricating numbers, and flagging weak evidence
- **B.** The first step in the writing sequence; establishing the audience, purpose, and key points before any material is gathered or prose is drafted
- **C.** A factual, non-blaming organizational record of an adverse event or near-miss; written in objective language only, not editorializing
- **D.** The healthcare report format that leads with the main point or headline, then background, then findings, then recommendations
- **E.** The pre-submission review checking every document for clarity, precision, and completeness before it is submitted
- **F.** A specific reference to the original data source or guideline; required in healthcare writing, with quotation used sparingly
- **G.** The default style reference for US medical writing, covering citation, abbreviation, and numerical conventions
- **H.** A patient-facing document written to plain-language health-literacy standards, targeting approximately a 6th-grade reading level

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Documents produced with the full sequence require fewer revisions and contain fewer factual errors because the structure and the evidence are already in place before the first sentence is drafted. Skipping organize and going straight to writing produces documents organized around what the writer found interesting rather than what the reader needs, which usually requires a full rewrite.

2. A fact is an observable event: "the patient asked what year it was." An interpretation is a conclusion drawn from observations: "the patient was confused." In an incident report, "the patient asked what year it was" belongs; "the patient was confused" does not. The report must record what was observed, not what the observer concluded, because conclusions can be disputed and may create legal liability.

3. Over-reporting is penalized because a handoff card that lists all 14 routine vital sign entries is harder to use than one that lists the three things the incoming team needs to watch. The five-bullet standard means covering everything that matters and nothing that does not. The principle is audience-first: the reader's ability to act quickly on the right information is what the document exists to enable, and noise makes that harder.

4. "The nurse was inattentive" is an interpretation, not a fact, and "caused a delay" is an editorial conclusion that assigns blame. Rewrite: "At 09:40, the medication was scheduled for administration. Administration was completed at 10:15." This version records what happened and when without interpreting the cause or assigning responsibility.

5. The target reading level for most health materials is 6th grade, which means short sentences, common words, active verbs, and no jargon without immediate explanation. The implication is that technical terms must be defined or replaced, sentence structure must be simple, and the layout must support a reader who may have low health literacy. A handout that uses clinical jargon is not usable regardless of how accurate the information is.

6. In healthcare, the stakes of acting on false or unsupported information are clinical, not academic. Citing original data tells the reader exactly where to verify the claim. Paraphrasing a second-hand summary may introduce errors, drop restrictions, or overstate confidence in a finding. A report that presents a preliminary finding as established fact misleads whoever reads it, which in a clinical context can mean a patient receives care based on incorrect information.

7. The editing pass checks: (1) clarity, asking whether every sentence adds something (example: delete a sentence that only restates what the previous sentence said); (2) precision, asking whether all values are measurable (example: replace "the patient improved" with "the patient's O2 saturation rose from 88% to 96%"); and (3) completeness, asking whether the reader's most likely next question is already answered (example: if the report mentions a medication dose but not the route, the incoming clinician will immediately need to ask).

8. The four questions are: Who will read this? What do they need to know? What question are they trying to answer? What action do you want them to take after reading? Answering them in writing rather than thinking through them locks in the structure before drafting begins. A written outline cannot shift mid-draft the way unrecorded mental notes can, and it keeps the document organized around the reader's needs rather than the writer's sequence of memory.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. "Inattentive" is an interpretation of the pharmacist's mental state, not an observable fact, and "nearly caused a serious error" editorializes about what would have happened rather than describing what did happen. Incident reports must contain only facts, not interpretations, because editorializing creates legal liability and is harder to use for quality-improvement analysis. The supervisor's correction reflects the chapter's rule that only observable events belong in this type of document.

2. The time stamp gives reviewers an accurate timeline to reconstruct what happened and when, which is essential for root-cause analysis. The broader principle is precision: all values, including time, should be measurable and specific. "At 14:15" is precise. "In the afternoon" is not. Precise time stamps also protect clinicians by creating a clear record that is difficult to dispute.

3. Citing ISMP by name grounds the report in an external standard rather than personal judgment. A reader reviewing the report for quality improvement can verify the standard independently, which makes the report more authoritative and defensible. The chapter's guidance on citation states that citing original sources tells the reader where to verify a claim, and naming ISMP does exactly that.

4. Incident reports feed the quality-improvement process, and near-misses are as important to that process as actual harm events, because a near-miss reveals a system vulnerability before a patient is harmed. Documenting "no patient harm occurred" is not a reassurance. It is the final factual entry in the event record, completing the timeline and allowing quality reviewers to understand the full sequence: the error, the catch, and the outcome.

5. The audience of an incident report is the quality-improvement team, risk management, and potentially accreditation reviewers, not the patient and not the public. The decision it supports is: what system change is needed to prevent this class of error from recurring? Audience-first structure for this audience means leading with what happened (the triggering event and the type of error), then the timeline, then the outcome, so reviewers can immediately begin analyzing the system factors without sifting through editorial commentary.
