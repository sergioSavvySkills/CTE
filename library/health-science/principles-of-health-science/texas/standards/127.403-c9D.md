## Learning objective

Students will be able to examine the concepts of negligence, malpractice, and liability in US healthcare — distinguish them, apply the four elements required for a malpractice claim to short cases, and identify the documentation and system practices that protect both patients and practitioners.

## Content stub

- **Three related but distinct concepts:**
  - *Negligence* — failure to exercise the care a reasonable person would use under the circumstances; any person can be negligent (spilling water in a hallway, leaving equipment in a walkway)
  - *Malpractice* — *professional* negligence; a licensed practitioner's conduct falls below the accepted standard of care within their profession; requires a professional duty to the patient
  - *Liability* — legal responsibility for the resulting harm, including who is on the hook financially (personal liability of the practitioner; vicarious liability of the employer; institutional liability of the facility)
- **The four required elements of a malpractice claim (in every US jurisdiction):**
  - *Duty of care* — a clinician-patient relationship existed (formal care, not hallway advice)
  - *Breach of that duty* — the conduct fell below the standard of care; established by expert testimony or published guidelines
  - *Causation* — the breach directly caused the harm (both factual cause and legal/proximate cause); most cases turn on causation
  - *Damages* — the patient suffered measurable harm (physical, financial, emotional); missing damages means no claim even when breach is clear
- **Liability types in healthcare:**
  - *Personal liability* — the practitioner is named individually
  - *Vicarious liability* — the employer is liable for the employee's acts within the scope of employment (*respondeat superior*)
  - *Institutional liability* — the facility itself is liable for systemic failures (staffing, credentialing, equipment, policies)
  - *Corporate liability* — board-level responsibility for failing to ensure safe systems
- **Malpractice insurance:**
  - Physicians, NPs, PAs typically carry individual coverage
  - Hospitals typically carry facility-wide coverage that extends vicariously to employees
  - Nurses, CNAs, and other employees may or may not be named individually depending on the case
- **Common malpractice scenario categories (drawn from US claims data):**
  - Diagnostic errors (misdiagnosis, missed diagnosis, delayed diagnosis) — leading category by volume
  - Medication errors (wrong drug, dose, route, patient)
  - Surgical and procedural errors (wrong site, retained instruments, unintended injury)
  - Obstetric injuries
  - Failure to monitor / failure to rescue
  - Informed-consent failures
- **Why documentation is often decisive:**
  - In most cases the record is the primary evidence; "if it wasn't documented, it didn't happen" is the operational rule
  - Timely, objective, specific documentation protects both patients (because it captures what actually happened) and practitioners (because it demonstrates standard of care was met)
  - Altering records after the fact is a separate and severe offense (spoliation of evidence)
- **System-level protections that reduce harm and claims:**
  - Standardized protocols (e.g., surgical safety checklists — WHO Surgical Safety Checklist is evidence-based)
  - Root-cause analysis after adverse events rather than individual blame
  - "Just culture" frameworks (Marx) that distinguish human error from at-risk and reckless behavior
  - Disclosure and apology programs (AHRQ CANDOR) that reduce both harm and litigation
- **Tort reform and Texas context:** Texas passed significant medical-malpractice reform in 2003 (including a $250,000 cap on non-economic damages for physician defendants); reform significantly changed the state's litigation environment — worth knowing for Texas-specific examples

## Vocabulary

- **Negligence** — failure to exercise the care a reasonable person would use under the circumstances; any person can be negligent.
- **Malpractice** — professional negligence: a licensed practitioner's conduct fell below the accepted standard of care within their profession.
- **Liability** — legal responsibility for resulting harm, including who is financially on the hook.
- **Duty of care** — the first element of a malpractice claim: a clinician-patient relationship existed (not hallway advice).
- **Breach** — the second element: the conduct fell below the standard of care, established by expert testimony or published guidelines.
- **Causation** — the third element: the breach directly caused the harm (both factual and legal cause); most cases turn here.
- **Damages** — the fourth element: measurable harm (physical, financial, emotional); absent damages, no claim stands.
- **Vicarious liability** — employer liability for an employee's acts within scope of employment (*respondeat superior*).
- **Just Culture (Marx)** — accountability framework distinguishing human error from at-risk and reckless behavior; replaces blame-the-individual after adverse events. *(also in c11C, c12C)*
- **Tort reform (Texas)** — the 2003 Texas Medical Liability Act (CPRC Chapter 74) capping non-economic damages for physician defendants at $250,000.
- **Spoliation of evidence** — altering records after the fact; a separate and severe offense beyond the underlying malpractice claim.

## Assessment stub

- Classification drill: given 10 workplace incidents (a wet floor fall caused by unmarked spill; a nurse administering the wrong medication due to look-alike label; a physician's failure to follow up on an abnormal lab; a medical assistant exceeding scope of practice; a hospital that failed to renew a specialist's privileges), label each as negligence, malpractice, both, neither, or insufficient information; defend each label
- Four-elements analysis: for 5 clinical scenarios, systematically walk through duty, breach, causation, and damages; conclude whether a malpractice claim would stand and explain which element is weakest; reference a published standard of care (e.g., AHA guideline, AAP guideline, USPSTF recommendation) where relevant
- Documentation audit: given two sample chart notes for the same incident (one thorough, one minimal), identify how each would play in a malpractice defense and rewrite the weak one to meet professional documentation standards
- Short response: in 200–300 words, explain how a Texas cap on non-economic damages affects patient recovery for the same injury compared to a state without a cap; identify one argument in favor of tort reform and one argument against, from the perspective of different stakeholders (patient, clinician, institution)

## Activities

- **Four-Elements Gate** — 5 clinical scenarios (delayed diagnosis with patient death, wrong-site surgery with no harm, a missed follow-up lab, hallway advice that the visitor acted on and was harmed by, a pediatric med error with 48-hour ICU stay) flow through four sequential gates: Duty / Breach / Causation / Damages. Student must make a Yes/No call at each gate with a one-sentence rationale. If any gate fails, the claim dies and the sim shows the student which element was weakest. Gate 3 — causation — is where most cases actually turn, and the sim deliberately stresses that point.
  → *analysis, decision under constraint*

- **Documentation-That-Defends Drill** — two sample chart notes for the same adverse event appear side-by-side: one minimal ("patient fell, assessed, no injury"), one robust (timestamps, specific vitals, explicit assessments, witnesses named, follow-up plan). Student must rewrite the minimal note to meet professional documentation standards under a 6-minute clock. Auto-check validates presence of time, objective observations, specific measurements, no blame language, no retroactive alterations. A rewrite that slips into subjective framing ("appeared fine") bounces back.
  → *procedural fluency, metacognition*

- **Just-Culture Accountability Sort** — 10 adverse-event cards (a nurse pulled a wrong-look-alike vial in a high-stress code; a physician knowingly documented false vitals to cover a missed reassessment; a CNA skipped a two-identifier check because "she knew the patient"; a resident bypassed a time-out because the surgeon was "impatient") slide across the screen. Student sorts each into Marx's three categories — Human Error / At-Risk Behavior / Reckless — and picks the corresponding response (consoling / coaching / disciplinary). The sim penalizes "blame-first" impulses on human-error cases.
  → *ethical reasoning, analysis*

## References

- **Restatement (Second) of Torts §282 and §299A** — foundational common-law sources for the four-elements framework used in US malpractice law.
- **Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code, Chapter 74 (Medical Liability)** — the Texas statute embodying the 2003 tort-reform framework, including damage caps and expert-report requirements.
- **Texas Board of Nursing and Texas Medical Board disciplinary action reports** — publicly available case summaries that illustrate real negligence and malpractice outcomes in Texas.
- **CRICO Strategies (Harvard) Malpractice Data** — national benchmark data on malpractice-claim categories and root causes.
- **AHRQ CANDOR Toolkit (Communication and Optimal Resolution)** — evidence-based post-harm disclosure-and-apology program.
- **WHO Surgical Safety Checklist** — evidence-based protocol demonstrating how system design reduces preventable harm.
- **David Marx, *Whack-a-Mole: The Price We Pay for Expecting Perfection* (Just Culture framework)** — framework distinguishing human error, at-risk behavior, and reckless behavior for accountability decisions.
- **Joint Commission Sentinel Event Policy** — the accreditation-level framework for identifying, investigating, and responding to serious adverse events.
