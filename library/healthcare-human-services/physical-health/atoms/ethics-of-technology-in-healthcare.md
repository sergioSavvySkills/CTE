## Learning objective

Students will be able to discuss ethical issues raised by emerging healthcare technology — AI, genomics, telehealth, wearables, and end-of-life technology — and apply ethical principles to evaluate them.

## Content stub

- **AI and machine learning:** diagnostic algorithms may encode bias from training data; autonomy and consent issues when AI drives decisions; accountability when AI is wrong
- **Genomics and testing:** who owns your genetic data, who sees it, what it reveals about relatives who never consented, genetic discrimination (GINA protects some but not all contexts)
- **Telehealth and remote monitoring:** access expansion vs. digital divide; quality of exam over video; licensure across state lines; continuous data streams and privacy
- **Wearables and consumer health tech:** data accuracy, false reassurance or false alarms, employer/insurer access to data, consent for research use
- **End-of-life technology:** ventilators, ECMO, artificial nutrition — when technology keeps a body going past what the person would have wanted; advance directives
- **Applying the four principles:** every new tech raises tradeoffs among autonomy (consent), beneficence (benefit), non-maleficence (harm), and justice (access)

## Assessment stub

- Case analysis: pick three of the six categories; for each, write a short case and identify the ethical tension using the four principles
- Policy critique: read a news article on an AI diagnostic tool; identify two ethical risks and a mitigation for each
- Reflection: describe one piece of healthcare technology used in your family; identify a privacy or consent concern you had not previously considered
