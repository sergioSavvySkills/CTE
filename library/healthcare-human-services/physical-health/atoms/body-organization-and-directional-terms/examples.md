# Worked examples — body-organization-and-directional-terms

Each example asks: *which anatomical term names this location or view?*
The goal is translating everyday language into the shared anatomical
map, not mastering the organ physiology that happens inside it.

## Example 1 — Locating RLQ pain on a patient

**Scenario:** A 19-year-old arrives at urgent care pointing to the
lower right part of their abdomen, below the navel and toward the hip,
saying the pain has been sharp for six hours. Your supervisor asks you
to chart the location precisely.

**What to work out:** Which abdominopelvic quadrant is it, which
organs commonly live there, and how do you describe the exact spot
using directional pairs?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Put the patient in anatomical position in your head. Draw one
   midsagittal line down the midline and one transverse line through
   the navel. The lower right segment is the RLQ (right lower
   quadrant) — from the patient's perspective, not yours.
2. Name the RLQ organs most likely responsible for pain: appendix,
   cecum (start of the large intestine), parts of small intestine,
   right ovary and right Fallopian tube in female anatomy, right
   ureter [Mayo-Abdominal].
3. Describe the pain's exact spot using a directional pair:
   approximately 5 cm superior and medial to the right anterior
   superior iliac spine.
4. Chart: "RLQ pain, sharp, 6 hours duration, located approximately
   5 cm superior and medial to the right ASIS."

**Answer:** The pain is in the right lower quadrant; two common
organs there are the appendix and the cecum; its exact location is
superior and medial to a fixed surface landmark (the right ASIS).

**Why it matters:** Any physician reading that note, including one
who has never met the patient, now has the same mental picture of
where the pain is. That is the whole job of anatomical language —
turning a hand gesture into text a stranger can act on.

## Example 2 — Naming the plane of a CT image

**Scenario:** You are shown a CT image of the abdomen. The slice shows
a ring of body wall with organs visible from above — you can see the
liver on one side, the stomach on the other, and a slice through both
kidneys. No head, no feet are in the image; just a cross-section.

**What to work out:** Which plane is this, and which directional
terms describe the cut?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Ask what the cut separates. This image separates the body into an
   upper (superior) part above the cut and a lower (inferior) part
   below it.
2. Superior-vs-inferior is the split produced by a transverse plane
   [OpenStax §1.7]. The frontal plane would separate anterior from
   posterior; sagittal planes would separate left from right.
3. Confirm: the image shows a "slice" as if the body were cut like a
   loaf of bread — that is the signature look of transverse-plane
   imaging.
4. Describe the image: "transverse CT of the abdomen at the level of
   the kidneys, viewed from below (standard convention)."

**Answer:** The image is a transverse (horizontal) plane cut; it
separates superior from inferior parts of the body.

**Why it matters:** Every CT and MRI report names the plane of the
image in the first line. If you can read that name, you can picture
the cut before you look at the picture — a skill healthcare workers
lean on at every chart review [OpenStax §1.7].

## Example 3 — Describing an IV placement with directional pairs

**Scenario:** A nurse tells you, "I placed an 18-gauge IV in the
left forearm, superficial, about 3 cm distal to the antecubital
fossa, on the lateral aspect." You need to picture exactly where the
catheter is.

**What to work out:** Turn each directional term into a concrete
position on the arm.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Left forearm: the patient's left arm, between elbow and wrist.
2. Superficial: near the surface of the skin, not deep in the
   tissue — so the catheter sits just under the skin.
3. Distal to the antecubital fossa: farther from the trunk than
   the elbow crease, so the site is below the elbow (toward the
   wrist, on a standing patient).
4. Lateral aspect: away from the midline of the body — on a forearm
   held in anatomical position, that is the thumb side, where the
   cephalic vein runs.
5. Put it together: the IV is in the forearm skin, thumb side, about
   3 cm below the elbow crease.

**Answer:** The IV sits just under the skin on the thumb side of the
left forearm, about 3 cm below the elbow crease.

**Why it matters:** The three directional pairs — superficial/deep,
proximal/distal, medial/lateral — locate almost every superficial
procedure (blood draws, IV starts, injections) in four words or
fewer. Students who internalize them read the procedure note and see
the spot; students who do not, reach for a diagram every time.

## Example 4 — Tracing one path through the levels of organization

**Scenario:** An instructor asks: "Start with an oxygen atom and trace
one path all the way up to the organism, naming the level at each
step."

**What to work out:** Apply the hierarchy chemical → cellular →
tissue → organ → organ system → organism to one specific path, and
name the level you are at in each step.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Oxygen atom — chemical level [Tortora §1.4]. It is an atom, so it
   is as small as the hierarchy names.
2. Oxygen is carried by a hemoglobin molecule inside a red blood
   cell — still chemical until you get to the cell.
3. The red blood cell — cellular level. One cell.
4. Many red blood cells plus plasma make up blood — a tissue (a
   connective tissue, unusually, but still a tissue).
5. Blood fills the heart and vessels — the heart is an organ,
   organ level.
6. The heart plus blood plus vessels form the cardiovascular system
   — organ-system level.
7. The cardiovascular system plus ten others form you — organism
   level [OpenStax §1.6].

**Answer:** Oxygen atom (chemical) → red blood cell (cellular) →
blood (tissue) → heart (organ) → cardiovascular system (organ
system) → person (organism).

**Why it matters:** Any anatomy question begins with "what level of
organization is this word naming?" Getting that right prevents the
most common category errors — saying "the kidney is a tissue" or
"hemoglobin is an organ" — that cost trainees points on every
early-course exam.
