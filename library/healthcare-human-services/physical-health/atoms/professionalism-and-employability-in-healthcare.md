## Learning objective

Students will be able to describe the professional standards and employability expectations required in the health science industry and demonstrate behaviors that meet them.

## Content stub

- **Why professionalism matters in healthcare:** patients trust you with their bodies, stories, and secrets; co-workers depend on you during stressful, time-critical work; employers are held to compliance and accreditation standards that flow through every employee
- **Communicating ideas clearly:** concise spoken and written communication — one main point per sentence, avoid jargon without explanation, confirm understanding (teach-back, read-back)
- **Cooperating and collaborating as a team member:** reliability, stepping up when workload shifts, asking before acting outside your role, sharing information up and down the team, credit for shared work
- **Employer expectations:** punctuality (arriving ready, not just present), attendance (call-out protocols, no-shows harm patients), time management (prioritize by acuity, finish tasks, document in real time), organizational skills, productive work habits (focus, minimal phone use, care with supplies)
- **Dress, grooming, and appearance:** uniform standards, name badges visible, nails short, hair secured, minimal jewelry — these are infection-control and identification requirements, not vanity rules
- **Receiving feedback and continuous improvement:** assume feedback is for patient benefit, ask clarifying questions, change behavior; document your growth in a portfolio

## Assessment stub

- Self-assessment: rate yourself on 10 employer expectations (punctuality, attendance, teamwork, communication, etc.); identify three to improve and describe concrete actions for each
- Scenario: analyze 5 workplace scenarios (late arrival, phone use at work, sharing patient gossip, refusing to cover for a teammate, dress-code violation) and describe the professional response and its rationale
- Portfolio artifact: draft a one-page "professional standards commitment" describing how you'll show up on clinical rotations; include specific observable behaviors
