## Misconception 1 — GINA fully protects people from genetic discrimination

**What students often think:**
"There is a law — GINA — that makes it illegal to use genetic
information against someone. So once you know that, genetic testing is
safe."

**What's actually true:**
GINA blocks genetic discrimination in U.S. health insurance and
employment, but it does not cover life insurance, disability insurance,
or long-term-care insurance, and it does not apply to the U.S. military
[GINA-Law]. A patient who tests positive for a cancer-risk variant may
still be denied affordable life insurance decades later. And GINA is a
U.S. law — it does not follow data that crosses borders to a research
consortium or a foreign service provider. See book.md *Genomics and
genetic testing*.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Schools teach GINA in one sentence, and students are used to "there's a
law against it" meaning "you're covered." The limits of the statute are
rarely in that one sentence.

**How to check:**
Ask: *"You test positive for a high-risk variant at age 20. What kinds
of insurance can still use that result against you?"* A student with
the correct model names life, disability, and long-term-care insurance
and explains that GINA covers only health insurance and employment.

## Misconception 2 — Telehealth always improves healthcare access

**What students often think:**
"Telehealth means more people can see a doctor. It's a justice win, full
stop."

**What's actually true:**
Telehealth expands access for patients who have broadband, a device, a
private room, and the digital skills to use all three. For patients
without those — often the same rural, low-income, or elderly patients
the program meant to reach — the **digital divide** means the new
option is not an option at all, and may crowd out the in-person visits
that used to exist [ATA-Telehealth]. Access improves on average and
contracts for some. See book.md *Telehealth and remote monitoring*.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
"Access" is usually reported as a total or an average, and the averages
rose. The within-population inequity is a second, subtler finding
that takes practice to see.

**How to check:**
Ask: *"A rural clinic replaces half its in-person visits with video
visits. Name one group of patients for whom access improves and one
group for whom it gets worse, and say why."* A correct answer names
patients with reliable broadband as the winners and patients without it
(or without privacy at home) as the losers.

## Misconception 3 — A wearable reading is a medical-grade measurement

**What students often think:**
"If my watch says my heart rate is 180, my heart rate is 180. If it says
I have atrial fibrillation, I have atrial fibrillation."

**What's actually true:**
Most consumer wearables are not regulated as medical devices and have
meaningful false-positive and false-negative rates for any single
reading [FDA-AIML]. A single watch rhythm alert is a reason to get
evaluated, not a diagnosis. Treating a consumer-device reading as
diagnostic can violate non-maleficence in two directions: false alarms
cause harm through anxiety and unnecessary testing, and false
reassurance causes harm when a real problem is dismissed. See book.md
*Wearables and consumer health tech*.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Watches look like clinical equipment and report numbers to one decimal
place. The framing makes the data feel more certain than it is, and
marketing reinforces the impression.

**How to check:**
Ask: *"Your smartwatch flags an irregular heartbeat during a workout.
What are two reasons the alert might be wrong, and what is the correct
next step?"* A correct answer names false positives from motion or
sensor error, names that the device is not a diagnostic tool, and
proposes following up with a clinician rather than either panicking or
ignoring.

## Misconception 4 — End-of-life decisions are obvious once the family is asked

**What students often think:**
"When a patient can't speak for themselves, you just ask the family what
to do. The family knows what the patient would have wanted."

**What's actually true:**
Families often disagree among themselves, guess wrong about the
patient's wishes, or make decisions shaped by guilt or hope rather than
by the patient's values. That is precisely why an **advance directive**
written earlier — when the patient could speak for themselves — carries
ethical weight that family preference alone does not [Hastings-EOL].
Without a directive, clinicians and families are reconstructing the
patient's autonomy from evidence, and reasonable people reach different
conclusions. See book.md *End-of-life technology*.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Movies and TV resolve end-of-life scenes in one conversation because
drama needs resolution. Real families take days, split over the
decision, and sometimes never agree.

**How to check:**
Ask: *"A 78-year-old patient on a ventilator has no advance directive.
Three adult children disagree about whether to continue life support.
Whose view carries weight, and what is the role of the **autonomy**
principle when the patient can't speak?"* A correct answer recognizes
that autonomy still centers on the patient's prior wishes (reconstructed
from evidence), names the advance directive as the missing anchor, and
treats the family disagreement as a reason to bring in an ethics
consult rather than a vote to be tallied.
