# Worked examples — cultural-competence-in-healthcare

## Example 1 — Intake with a qualified medical interpreter

**Scenario:** Ms. Ahmed is a 34-year-old arriving in triage with
early labor symptoms. Her preferred language is Somali. Her husband
is with her and offers to interpret so "everything goes faster."
Neither speaks English fluently.

**What to work out:** How do you run the intake so the patient gets
accurate information, confidentiality is preserved, and the
communication habits stay correct throughout?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Offer a **qualified medical interpreter** by video through the
   facility service, in Somali, before starting the clinical
   intake [AHRQ-Interpreter]. Explain why in one sentence: the
   interpreter is trained in medical vocabulary and bound by
   confidentiality.
2. Confirm the offer directly with Ms. Ahmed. If she prefers the
   interpreter, her husband becomes a support person, not a
   translator. If she declines and prefers her husband, document it
   and still have the interpreter available for anything clinical.
3. Start the conversation with the interpreter on video. Face
   Ms. Ahmed. Say "How are you feeling right now?" — not "Ask her
   how she's feeling." Speak in short sentences, one idea at a time.
4. Pause after each sentence so the interpreter can render it
   cleanly. Avoid idioms ("out of the woods," "on the same page").
5. At the end of the assessment, use a **teach-back**: ask
   Ms. Ahmed to say back, in her own words through the interpreter,
   what will happen next — what the plan is, what to watch for,
   when to call for help.
6. Document the interpreter's ID number, language, and modality
   (video) in the note, per **CLAS standards** expectations
   [CLAS-Standards].

**Answer:** Qualified medical interpreter via video, spouse as
support person, direct address to patient, short sentences, teach-
back at the end.

**Why it matters:** Using the spouse would have been faster by a few
minutes and would have handed the conversation off to someone with
no clinical training and a relationship to the content. The habits
above are what keep a patient with **limited English proficiency**
from getting worse information than the patient in the next bed.

## Example 2 — Pain assessment and implicit bias

**Scenario:** Mr. Jackson, a 28-year-old Black man, presents to the
emergency department with severe abdominal pain. He is diaphoretic,
guarding his abdomen, and rates his pain 9/10. A clinician mentally
notes that he "seems dramatic" and orders a lower-strength analgesic
than the standard pathway would suggest.

**What to work out:** What is the risk pattern here, and what habit
would counteract it?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Name the pattern. Studies have documented that Black patients
   receive less adequate pain treatment than white patients
   presenting with the same diagnosis [IOM-Unequal]. The word
   "dramatic" in a chart note is a marker to pause on.
2. Consider the source. The clinician is not declaring prejudice;
   they are acting on an automatic association. That is
   **implicit bias**, and it does not disappear by being denied.
3. Apply a habit that bypasses the bias. Use the facility's
   standard pain-assessment tool and the standard pathway for the
   presentation. A standard tool, used the same way every time,
   reduces the variance that bias produces.
4. Re-check the plan against the presentation, not against the
   patient's demeanor. Guarding, diaphoresis, and a 9/10 report are
   signs, not drama.
5. Document objectively. "Patient reports 9/10 pain, guarding,
   diaphoretic" is a clinical sentence; "patient seems dramatic" is
   not.

**Answer:** Use the standard pain-assessment tool and standard
pathway; treat the presentation, not the impression.

**Why it matters:** No single clinician can solve a national pattern.
But a clinician who uses a standard tool in a standard way in each
encounter contributes to the aggregate fix. That is what translating
**cultural humility** into practice looks like — a habit that runs
whether or not your gut agrees with it in the moment.

## Example 3 — Consent conversation with family decision-making

**Scenario:** Mrs. Nguyen is 78 and needs a decision about whether
to start dialysis. Her adult daughter tells the care team, "In our
family, the family decides together; please talk to all of us, not
just my mother." The clinician is used to addressing the patient as
the sole decision-maker.

**What to work out:** How do you honor the family's practice while
keeping the patient's autonomy intact?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Ask Mrs. Nguyen directly (with an interpreter if needed) how she
   wants the conversation to happen: alone with the clinician, with
   specific family members, or with the full family. Do not assume
   the daughter's statement represents her mother's wish.
2. If Mrs. Nguyen wants family involved, convene the conversation
   she described. Explain the medical facts to the group in the same
   sentences you would use one-on-one.
3. Keep consent with the patient. In U.S. practice, the decision is
   legally and ethically hers unless she has formally delegated it.
   Family consensus informs, it does not replace.
4. Check understanding with **teach-back** from the patient herself,
   not from the family: "Mrs. Nguyen, in your own words, what does
   starting dialysis mean for your daily life?"
5. Document the communication pattern the patient requested, the
   family members who participated, and the patient's decision.

**Answer:** Invite the family with the patient's permission, explain
to the group, confirm consent with the patient.

**Why it matters:** Neither assumption — "she must decide alone" or
"the family decides" — would fit Mrs. Nguyen. Both would miss the
actual preference by treating a cultural pattern as a rule for the
individual. This is the central move the atom trains: ask, then
adapt to the answer, not to the **stereotype**.

## Example 4 — Discharge planning through a social-determinants lens

**Scenario:** Mr. Walker is being discharged with a new diagnosis of
heart failure, a daily weight log, two new medications, and follow-up
in one week. He lives alone in a rural county, works hourly shifts at
a warehouse, and drives 45 minutes to the nearest pharmacy.

**What to work out:** How do you build a discharge plan that is
actually likely to work?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Assess the **social determinants of health** that will determine
   whether the plan survives outside the hospital [NAM-SDOH]. Ask
   about transportation to the pharmacy and to the follow-up visit;
   insurance and medication cost; working hours versus appointment
   hours; food access (heart failure diet requires fresh foods);
   who at home will notice if he gets worse.
2. Identify barriers from the answers. A 45-minute drive to the
   pharmacy plus an hourly job with no paid sick time is a real
   adherence barrier, not a character flaw. A rural address far from
   the follow-up clinic is a geographic **health disparity** built
   into the plan.
3. Adapt the plan. Use a pharmacy that mails prescriptions, or the
   long-acting formulation that needs fewer refills. Schedule
   follow-up as a telehealth visit if the facility supports it, at a
   time outside his shift. Provide written instructions at an
   appropriate reading level and confirm with **teach-back**.
4. Involve a social worker or care manager early for
   transportation, medication assistance programs, and low-cost food
   resources. These are not nice-to-haves; they are the mechanism
   the plan runs on.
5. Document the barriers identified and the adaptations made. The
   next clinician needs to see what was adjusted and why.

**Answer:** The "default" plan would have failed. The adapted plan
uses mail-order pharmacy, telehealth follow-up, social-work linkage,
and teach-back confirmation.

**Why it matters:** A clinician who treats every patient identically
would have handed Mr. Walker the same discharge packet as a patient
living next door to the hospital and called it fair. **Health
equity** is the habit of asking what this patient actually needs to
succeed, and adjusting until the plan fits.
