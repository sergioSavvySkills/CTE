# Worked examples — health-economics-and-government-impact

## Example 1 — Level-match: which layer of government does each job?

**Scenario:** You are reviewing a short list of healthcare-related
actions and need to label each as primarily federal, primarily
state, or primarily local.

**What to work out:** For each action below, identify the primary
layer of government responsible and name the main agency or role.

**Step-by-step:**

1. "Approves a new cancer drug before it can be sold in pharmacies."
   This is federal — the **FDA** decides whether a drug may be
   marketed in the United States.
2. "Issues a license to a new registered nurse." This is state —
   **licensure** of individual clinicians is a state function, even
   though nurses practice under federal facility rules.
3. "Inspects a neighborhood restaurant after a foodborne-illness
   complaint." This is local — county or city health departments
   run food-safety inspection [NACCHO-LHD].
4. "Pays for hospital stays for a patient age 72." This is federal —
   **Medicare** coverage is a CMS program.
5. "Pays for a pregnant adult's prenatal care under a state low-
   income program." This is shared federal-state — **Medicaid** is
   co-administered by CMS and the state's Medicaid agency
   [KFF-Medicaid].
6. "Runs the ambulance that responds to a 911 call." This is local
   — EMS is usually organized at the county, city, or district
   level.
7. "Awards a multi-year grant to a university lab studying
   Alzheimer's." This is federal — the **NIH** is the largest
   biomedical research funder.
8. "Operates a county hospital that serves uninsured patients."
   This is local — some counties and cities operate public
   safety-net hospitals directly.
9. "Writes workplace safety rules for bloodborne-pathogen exposure
   in clinics." This is federal — OSHA sets those rules.
10. "Requires schoolchildren to be immunized before enrollment."
    This is state — state law sets school immunization
    requirements, usually enforced through local schools.

**Answer:** Federal: 1, 4, 7, 9. State: 2, 10. Shared federal-state:
5. Local: 3, 6, 8.

**Why it matters:** Sorting by layer is the fastest way to predict
which agency can change a rule, where to direct a complaint, and how
a news story about "government healthcare policy" will actually land
on patients.

## Example 2 — Reading the scale claim

**Scenario:** A news article reports that "the U.S. spends about
$4.8 trillion on healthcare, or close to 17-18% of GDP, and the
sector employs over 20 million workers." You are asked in class to
explain what this sentence means and why it matters.

**What to work out:** Translate the claim into plain language and
say what it implies about the economy and about policy.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify the two anchor numbers. "17-18% of GDP" is the
   **national health expenditure** share of **gross domestic
   product** — the total spent on healthcare divided by the total
   value of U.S. output [CMS-NHE]. "20 million workers" is the
   employment figure from the Bureau of Labor Statistics'
   Occupational Outlook Handbook [BLS-OOH].
2. Put the GDP share in perspective. About one in every six dollars
   the country produces goes to healthcare. That is a larger share
   than most peer countries and has grown for decades.
3. Put the employment figure in perspective. Healthcare is one of
   the largest employment categories in the country — nurses,
   physicians, medical assistants, home-health aides, technologists,
   therapists, administrative staff, and support roles all count.
4. Notice the precision caveats. Both numbers change every year. The
   sentence in the article is order-of-magnitude — it is correct to
   say "approximately 17-18% of GDP" and "20+ million workers," but
   a specific dollar total needs a year and a source to be
   meaningful.
5. State the policy implication. A sector this large means small
   percentage changes translate into large dollar and workforce
   shifts. That is why federal, state, and local governments pay
   close attention to the **cost drivers** — technology, drug
   prices, administrative complexity, chronic disease, and aging —
   that push the share upward over time [CBO-HealthSpending].

**Answer:** The sentence says U.S. healthcare is about one-sixth of
the national economy and a top-tier employer, and that policy
attention to the sector is proportional to its size.

**Why it matters:** Scale claims are the most-quoted and least-
examined parts of healthcare news. Being able to restate them with
the right caveats is part of being a healthcare professional who
thinks clearly about the system they work inside.

## Example 3 — A new community health center opens

**Scenario:** A mid-sized city announces a new community health
center in a low-income neighborhood. The announcement says the
center will serve uninsured and Medicaid patients, will be staffed
by nurse practitioners and a small team of physicians, and is
funded in part by a federal grant.

**What to work out:** Which layers of government are involved, and
through which levers?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Federal role. **HRSA** provides the grant funding that supports
   startup costs and ongoing operations for the center as a
   **safety-net provider** [HRSA-Overview]. **CMS** rules shape how
   the center bills **Medicare** for older patients and how
   **Medicaid** reimbursement flows through the state agency.
2. State role. The state **licensure** board licenses the
   clinicians who work at the center, and the state health
   department licenses the facility. The state Medicaid agency
   signs the provider agreements that let the center bill
   Medicaid.
3. Local role. The county health department coordinates with the
   center on **public health** functions — immunizations,
   communicable-disease reporting, school health referrals — and
   local zoning and building inspection cleared the space before it
   opened.
4. Identify the levers in play. Federal **funding** pays for
   startup. Federal and state **coverage** rules determine which
   services are paid for. State licensure determines who can deliver
   care. Local regulation covers the building and public-health
   coordination.
5. Predict the effects. Patients gain access to primary care closer
   to home. Providers gain a new employer in a shortage area. The
   local workforce grows by 20-30 jobs. The local hospital may see
   fewer non-emergency ED visits as routine care moves to the new
   center.

**Answer:** All three layers — federal funding plus coverage rules,
state licensure plus Medicaid administration, local public-health
coordination and inspection — combine to stand up a single
community clinic.

**Why it matters:** One visible change in a neighborhood is usually
a stack of government actions that had to line up. Seeing the stack
is how you move from "healthcare is complicated" to "I can predict
which agency does what."

## Example 4 — Analyzing a public-policy change

**Scenario:** A state legislature passes a law allowing pharmacists
to administer additional vaccines without a separate physician
order. You are asked to analyze likely effects on patients,
providers, and the workforce.

**What to work out:** Walk through which government lever changed,
who is affected, and in what direction.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify the lever. The change is in scope of practice within
   **licensure**, which is a state-level function. No federal rule
   changed; no local rule changed. The state has adjusted what one
   licensed profession is allowed to do.
2. Patient effects. Access to vaccination rises because pharmacies
   are more numerous and have longer hours than primary care
   offices in most communities. Same-day availability improves,
   especially in rural counties where the nearest clinic is far
   away. Copayment and out-of-pocket costs depend on coverage rules
   that the law itself did not change.
3. Provider effects. Primary care offices may see a small drop in
   vaccine-only visits; they may welcome that, because vaccine-only
   visits are often break-even at best under current coverage.
   Pharmacies see increased foot traffic and new workflow demands
   — documentation, counseling, adverse-event handling, cold-chain
   storage.
4. Workforce effects. Pharmacy technicians and pharmacists take on
   more work per hour. Nursing staff in clinics may shift time
   toward other tasks. Over several years, pharmacy schools may
   emphasize immunization training more heavily in curricula.
5. Interaction with other layers. Even though the law is state, its
   effects show up in federal data — Medicare pays for many
   pharmacist-administered vaccines, and CDC immunization
   surveillance will see the shift. The **CDC** and state health
   departments track coverage rates and can respond to any new gaps
   that appear.

**Answer:** A licensure-level change at the state produces patient-
access gains, mixed provider effects, and a workforce shift toward
pharmacy, with federal coverage and surveillance systems feeling
the downstream effects.

**Why it matters:** Real policy change almost always involves one
lever at one layer and a ripple across the others. Being able to
trace the ripple is the analysis move the atom's learning
objective asks for.
