# Common misconceptions — health-economics-and-government-impact

## Misconception 1 — The federal government runs U.S. healthcare

**What students often think:**
Healthcare in the United States is run by the federal government,
so almost every important rule comes from Washington.

**What's actually true:**
The federal government is one of three layers, and it does specific
jobs — it runs **Medicare**, co-administers **Medicaid** with the
states, regulates drugs and devices through the **FDA**, leads
national public health through the CDC, funds research through the
NIH, and supports safety-net care through **HRSA**. But states
license clinicians and facilities, set public-health law, and shape
their own Medicaid programs, and local governments operate county
health departments, EMS, public hospitals, and food-safety
inspection [CMS-NHE, NACCHO-LHD, KFF-Medicaid]. A single patient
visit may touch all three layers without any of them being "in
charge" of the visit.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Federal headlines are louder. Medicare and FDA decisions make
national news; a county restaurant inspection does not. Students
hear the federal stories and generalize, missing the quieter
state and local work that actually touches most everyday care.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"Name one healthcare function that is primarily federal, one
that is primarily state, and one that is primarily local."* A
student with the correct model might say "Medicare coverage" for
federal, "nurse licensure" for state, and "restaurant inspection" or
"EMS dispatch" for local — and can explain that the layers work
together rather than one running the others.

## Misconception 2 — "The government pays for healthcare" is a yes-or-no question

**What students often think:**
Either the government pays for U.S. healthcare or it does not, and
the answer is mostly "no" because most people have private
insurance.

**What's actually true:**
Government is a dominant purchaser in the U.S. system even though
most working-age adults get coverage through employers. **Medicare**
and **Medicaid** together cover a large share of the population, and
government spending accounts for a substantial share of total
**national health expenditure** each year [CMS-NHE]. Because
Medicare is the single largest purchaser of hospital care, its
payment rules set de facto standards for private payers too. The
right mental model is a payment mix — public programs, private
insurance, employer-sponsored coverage, and out-of-pocket — with
government shaping the whole mix through rules even where it is not
the direct payer.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Debates about healthcare often pose a binary ("government vs.
private") that hides the real mix. Students who pay a monthly
premium through their parent's employer plan do not see the federal
or state money that flows through the same system.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A patient has employer-based private insurance for a hospital
stay. Does government funding affect their care?"* A student with
the correct model says yes — because Medicare payment rules anchor
what hospitals charge private payers, because the facility is
licensed by the state, and because patient-safety rules the hospital
follows come partly from federal regulation.

## Misconception 3 — Rising healthcare costs are mostly about people making bad choices

**What students often think:**
Healthcare costs keep going up because patients eat poorly, smoke,
skip exercise, or overuse the emergency department. Fix personal
behavior, fix the cost problem.

**What's actually true:**
Individual behavior matters, but the main **cost drivers**
identified in federal analyses are structural: new **technology**,
rising **drug prices**, administrative complexity, a growing burden
of **chronic disease** across the population, and an **aging**
population [CBO-HealthSpending]. Even a population with excellent
personal health habits would see costs rise because people live
longer, newer therapies cost more, and administering a complex
payment system is expensive. That is why cost-containment strategies
target system design — for example, **value-based care** and
**bundled payment** models — rather than only patient behavior
[CMS-ValueBased].

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Personal responsibility is a morally clean explanation and makes
individuals feel in control. Structural explanations feel abstract
and leave students with less to do today. The mismatch is what makes
the wrong answer stick.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"If every adult in the country started exercising daily and
eating a healthy diet tomorrow, what would happen to total
healthcare spending over 20 years?"* A student with the correct
model says some costs would fall (preventable chronic disease care),
but total spending would still rise because the aging population,
new technology, drug prices, and administrative complexity are the
larger drivers.

## Misconception 4 — Value-based care is a specific Medicare program

**What students often think:**
"Value-based care" is the name of a specific Medicare program with
particular rules, and if I memorize those rules I will understand
value-based care.

**What's actually true:**
**Value-based care** is a framework idea: pay for outcomes and
efficiency, not volume of services [CMS-ValueBased]. Many specific
programs implement that idea in different ways — **bundled payment**
for episodes, quality-based bonuses for primary care, shared-savings
arrangements for groups of providers — and the detailed mechanics
belong to policy atoms that come after this one. At this level, what
you need is the lever and its purpose: payers tie money to outcomes
so that providers have a financial reason to prevent problems rather
than only treat them.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
The term sounds like a product name. Students looking for something
concrete to hold onto assume it must be a single program rather than
a design philosophy.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"In one sentence, what is value-based care trying to change
about how providers are paid, and why?"* A student with the correct
model says it ties payment to outcomes or total cost of an episode
instead of the number of services delivered, because paying per
service rewards volume and paying for outcomes rewards coordination
and prevention.
