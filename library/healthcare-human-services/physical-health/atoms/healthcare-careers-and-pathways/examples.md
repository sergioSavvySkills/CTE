# Worked examples — healthcare-careers-and-pathways

## Example 1 — Leo, hands-on with tight money

**Scenario:** Leo is a high school junior. He is steady with
his hands, likes small machines, does well in biology, and is
not excited by writing essays. He has savings for about one
year of community college and needs to earn a paycheck by the
end of year two. He is squeamish about blood draws but fine
around imaging equipment.

**What to work out:** Which **career family** fits his
interests and which starting credential fits his constraints?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify interests. Hands-on, machine-oriented, biology-
   comfortable, low tolerance for blood draws. This maps onto
   **diagnostic services** — imaging and sonography — more
   than onto **therapeutic services** (nursing, therapy) or
   **support services** [OnetOnline].
2. Identify constraints. One year of paid tuition, needs
   wages by year two. Rules out a four-year **bachelor's
   degree** start and certainly a **graduate degree** plan.
3. Match constraints to credential level. A one-semester
   **certificate** (e.g., sterile processing) or a two-year
   **associate degree** in radiologic technology both fit.
   An associate in radiologic technology licenses him as an
   RT and pays well above certificate level [BLS-OOH].
4. Pick the starting rung. The associate-level radiologic
   technology program fits his skills and his constraint —
   two years, paid employment by year two.
5. Plan the next rung. Once working as an RT, short add-on
   credentials in CT or MRI stack on the base license and
   raise pay without another full degree — a **stackable
   credential** pattern [BLS-OOH].

**Answer:** Start with an associate degree in radiologic
technology; stack a CT or MRI credential after a year or two
of work.

**Why it matters:** A student who applied "more school is
always better" would aim at a four-year radiology-adjacent
bachelor's he cannot afford, stall out, and lose two years.
**Self-alignment** turns a generic interest into a plan he
can actually finish.

## Example 2 — Priya, aiming high with time and support

**Scenario:** Priya is a strong student, interested in patient
care and long-term relationships with patients. Her family
supports a longer training timeline. She has shadowed a primary-
care clinic and liked it. She is weighing nursing, physician
assistant, and medicine.

**What to work out:** Which pathway fits her interests and
which credential level fits her constraints? How do the three
candidate pathways differ?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify the family. Direct patient care with long-term
   relationships puts her in **therapeutic services**, the
   family shared by nursing, PA, and physician routes
   [BLS-OOH, AAMC-Careers].
2. Map the education ladders. Nurse practitioner sits at
   **graduate degree** level after a BSN (MSN or DNP) with
   clinical hours [NCSBN-Pathways]. Physician assistant is a
   master's-level graduate program after a bachelor's with
   clinical prerequisites. Physician (MD or DO) is an even
   longer graduate-plus-residency route [AAMC-Careers].
3. Weigh time and money. Nursing is the most flexible:
   Priya can earn a BSN in four years, work as an RN,
   earn income, and ladder into NP later as a **stackable
   credential** step [NCSBN-Pathways]. PA is four years
   plus two or three more, with most of that as a student.
   MD is four plus four plus residency.
4. Check **self-alignment** against interest specifics.
   If she loves the patient-education and ongoing-care side
   of primary care, NP and PA both deliver it; the MD route
   adds more years and a wider clinical role.
5. Pick a pathway that keeps options open. Starting with a
   BSN lets Priya work as an RN, confirm the fit, and then
   choose NP later without having locked in a decade of
   school up front.

**Answer:** Start with a BSN. Plan an NP program as a
next-rung option; keep PA or MD open by taking required
science prerequisites during the bachelor's.

**Why it matters:** The "pick one final job" trap (see
Misconception 4) would have her decide between NP, PA, and MD
today. The stackable path lets her decide from inside the
field, after seeing the work, which is a better decision than
from outside it.

## Example 3 — Marcus, already working and raising a family

**Scenario:** Marcus is 29. He works full-time as an
environmental services worker at a hospital and has two young
kids. He likes the hospital, gets along with the nurses on his
unit, and is curious about moving into a clinical role. He
cannot leave work to be a full-time student.

**What to work out:** Which **stackable credential** path
starts from where he already is, and what is the realistic
first next rung?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Identify his family starting point. He is in **support
   services** today. That is a valid entry-level rung, not a
   dead end — support roles are a common launch pad into
   clinical roles.
2. Match his constraints. He needs income continuity and
   schedule flexibility. A full-time residential program is
   not available to him. Evening, weekend, or online programs
   are required.
3. Scan first-rung options. A **certificate** program in
   patient care technician or medical assistant can be
   completed in months on an evening schedule and opens a
   clinical-floor role at higher pay than EVS [BLS-OOH].
   A CNA certificate is even shorter and is the classic first
   rung in the nursing ladder [NCSBN-Pathways].
4. Project a multi-year ladder. CNA this year → LVN night
   program (about 12–18 months) → bridge to **associate
   degree** nursing (RN) over two more years → optional
   RN-to-BSN online bridge while working [NCSBN-Pathways].
   Each rung raises pay and keeps him earning.
5. Name a realistic stopping point. Marcus does not have to
   commit to the whole ladder. Finishing at LVN or at ADN-RN
   is a complete career. The plan only commits to the next
   rung.

**Answer:** Start with a CNA certificate; plan LVN as the next
rung; keep RN open as a later step.

**Why it matters:** Without the stackable-credential framing,
Marcus might conclude that a clinical career requires quitting
work for two or four years — which he cannot do — and stay
stuck. With the framing, he has a realistic three-to-five-year
plan that adds a rung at a time.

## Example 4 — Aisha, interested in data, not bedside

**Scenario:** Aisha is a high school senior who likes
computer science, is comfortable with spreadsheets and
databases, and is squeamish about clinical settings. Her
family has told her that "healthcare is the growing field,"
but she cannot picture herself in scrubs. She assumes this
rules healthcare out.

**What to work out:** Is there a realistic healthcare pathway
that matches her interest in data rather than patients?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Name the family. **Health informatics** is the career
   family that manages, analyzes, and protects health data
   [BLS-OOH]. It has grown fast since electronic health
   records became the norm.
2. Check interest fit. Medical coding, health information
   management, and informatics analyst roles all center on
   data work — structured record systems, billing codes,
   analytics dashboards, data governance. Clinical patient
   contact is minimal.
3. Map the education ladder. Entry-level medical coding
   starts at a **certificate** level; health information
   technician is an **associate degree** role; health
   information management and many informatics analyst
   positions expect a **bachelor's degree**; clinical
   informatics leadership often expects a **graduate
   degree** [BLS-OOH].
4. Weigh **job outlook**. Informatics roles are among the
   faster-growing inside healthcare because the aging
   population and technology-adoption drivers both increase
   the volume and complexity of records [BLS-OOH,
   HRSA-Workforce].
5. Pick a pathway. A bachelor's in health information
   management starts her in a bachelor's-level informatics
   role on graduation. If she wants to start earning sooner,
   a two-year health information technician associate degree
   puts her in the field in two years, with a bridge to the
   bachelor's later as a **stackable credential** move.

**Answer:** Start with a bachelor's in health information
management, or an associate degree in health information
technology with a plan to bridge to the bachelor's later.

**Why it matters:** "I cannot do healthcare because I do not
want to be near patients" is one of the most common reasons
students rule themselves out of the field. Naming the
non-bedside families — informatics, biotechnology, support,
much of diagnostic services — puts them back in the map.
