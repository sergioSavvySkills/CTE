## Learning objective

Students will be able to map how care settings, payers, and the continuum of care fit together, and identify common access barriers that shape patient outcomes.

> **Scope note:** this is a survey/integrator atom that sits upstream of
> the deeper-dive atoms `healthcare-careers-and-pathways` (provider types),
> `healthcare-regulatory-agencies` (regulators), and
> `health-economics-and-government-impact` (payers and macro policy). It
> does not primary-own a §127.403 standard; it exists to give students the
> "how does all this connect" picture before the deep dives.

## Content stub

- **Care settings:** hospitals (acute, critical access, children's, specialty), outpatient clinics, ambulatory surgery, long-term care, home health, hospice, urgent care, telehealth — and which acuity each fits
- **Payer basics:** private insurance, employer-sponsored plans, Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, CHIP, self-pay; the payer ≠ provider distinction and why it matters for billing, referrals, and access
- **Continuum of care:** preventive → primary → acute → specialty → post-acute → end-of-life; care-transitions are where errors and readmissions happen (handoffs, med reconciliation, follow-up)
- **Access barriers:** cost, geography (rural and provider shortage areas), language, insurance status, transportation, digital access — and how each drives outcomes and disparities
- **How the pieces connect:** a single patient episode typically crosses multiple settings, providers, and payers — health-system design is mostly about reducing friction at those seams
- **For deeper dives** on provider families see `healthcare-careers-and-pathways`; on the major regulators (WHO/CDC/OSHA/FDA/Joint Commission/NIH) see `healthcare-regulatory-agencies`; on payer economics and government role see `health-economics-and-government-impact`.

## Assessment stub

- Map it: given a patient scenario (e.g., a 68-year-old with a new MI), diagram every setting they pass through across one full episode — hospital ED → ICU → floor → cardiac rehab → primary care — and note who pays at each step
- Short answer: distinguish payer, provider, and regulator, and give one example of each
- Transition-of-care analysis: pick one high-risk handoff (hospital → SNF, ED → home) and identify two common failure modes plus one specific mitigation
