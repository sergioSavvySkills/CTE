## Learning objective

Students will be able to read and use common medical abbreviations correctly and avoid the "do-not-use" list that contributes to medication errors.

## Content stub

- **Frequency and timing:** q, qd, bid, tid, qid, qh, prn, ac, pc, hs, stat — and how they show up on orders
- **Routes and sites:** PO, IV, IM, SC, SL, PR, OD/OS/OU, AD/AS/AU — why route abbreviations matter for safety
- **Body and status:** BP, HR, RR, T, Sats, WNL, NAD, NKDA, DNR, NPO — quick documentation shorthand
- **Orders and procedures:** ABG, CBC, BMP, CXR, EKG/ECG, MRI, CT, H&P, D/C, ADM — high-frequency acronyms in charts
- **Joint Commission "do not use" list:** U/u, IU, QD/QOD, trailing zero (1.0), lack of leading zero (.5), MS, MSO4, MgSO4 — errors each has caused
- **Institution-specific variation:** some abbreviations are safe at one hospital and banned at another; always check the facility's approved list

## Assessment stub

- Translation: decode 20 shorthand orders into plain English, including dose/route/frequency
- Error hunt: given a sample order sheet with 8 deliberate do-not-use violations, identify and rewrite each
- Reflection: pick one do-not-use abbreviation, research a real-world error it caused, and explain the harm
