# The Body's Organ Systems at a Glance

## Why this matters

Walk into any clinic, hospital, or long-term care facility and you'll hear
the same conversations repeated in different rooms: a patient's kidneys
aren't filtering well, their heart is working harder to push blood, their
blood pressure is climbing, and the endocrine doctor is asking about their
diabetes. To a beginner, that sounds like four unrelated problems. To a
healthcare worker, it's one story about four organ systems that talk to each
other constantly, and when one falters, the others feel it within hours
[NIH-NHLBI-HBP].

Every career in health science — nursing, medical assisting, EMT, pharmacy
tech, lab tech, health information — starts from the same map: the human body
is organized into eleven **organ systems**, each with a primary job, each
made of specific organs, and each linked to the others through shared
plumbing, shared signals, and shared regulation [Tortora §1.3]. You can't
read a medical chart, interpret vital signs, or explain a patient's
medication list without this map in your head. This chapter is that map.

## What you should already know

Two ideas from prior work make this chapter easier. First, the body is built
in levels: cells form tissues, tissues form organs, organs form systems,
and systems form the organism as a whole [OpenStax §1.2]. Second, many of
the terms you'll meet — *anterior*, *posterior*, *cranial*, *caudal* — are
directional words that let two clinicians describe the same pain in the same
way. If either of those feels shaky, glance back at the atoms
*Body organization and directional terms* and *Homeostasis and biological
processes* before diving in.

## Core concept

### From cells to a body

Before we name the systems, pin down the hierarchy. An **organ** is a
structure made of two or more **tissues** that cooperate on a job — the
heart, for example, is built from muscle tissue, connective tissue, and
nervous tissue all working together to pump blood [OpenStax §1.2]. Stack
organs with shared purposes and you get an **organ system**. The stomach,
small intestine, liver, and pancreas collaborate so closely on digestion
that we package them as one system rather than four. Stack all eleven
systems and you get an organism: you [Tortora §1.3].

This levels-of-organization idea is load-bearing. When a textbook says "the
kidney," it means an organ; when it says "the urinary system," it means
that kidney plus the ureters, bladder, and urethra working as a unit; when
it says "homeostasis," it usually means several systems cooperating at the
organism level [OpenStax §1.2]. Keep the word you're reading attached to
the level it names.

### A tour of the eleven systems

Here is the standard eleven, with the single primary function you should
keep in your head for each. Details and disorders fill in later atoms; this
is the survey view [Tortora §1.3, Marieb Ch. 1].

- **Integumentary system** — skin, hair, and nails. Provides the body's
  barrier, its main tool for temperature regulation, and the sense of touch
  [OpenStax §1.2].
- **Skeletal system** — bones, joints, and cartilage. Provides support and
  protection, stores calcium, and houses the marrow that makes blood cells
  [NIA-Aging-Bone].
- **Muscular system** — skeletal, smooth, and cardiac muscle. Generates
  movement, maintains posture, and produces body heat [Marieb Ch. 1].
- **Cardiovascular system** — heart, blood, and vessels. Transports oxygen,
  nutrients, hormones, and waste throughout the body [Tortora §1.3].
- **Lymphatic system** — lymph vessels, lymph nodes, spleen, thymus.
  Returns leaked fluid to the bloodstream and runs the immune defense
  against pathogens [CDC-Immune-Basics].
- **Respiratory system** — airways and lungs. Moves oxygen into the blood
  and carbon dioxide out of it [OpenStax §1.2].
- **Digestive system** — GI tract plus liver, pancreas, and gallbladder.
  Breaks food into molecules and absorbs them into the blood [Marieb Ch. 1].
- **Urinary system** — kidneys, ureters, bladder, urethra. Filters blood,
  regulates water and salt balance, and produces urine [NIDDK-CKD].
- **Endocrine system** — hormone-producing glands. Provides slower-acting
  chemical regulation that reaches every tissue [OpenStax §17.1].
- **Nervous system** — brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves.
  Coordinates control and communication across every other system
  [Marieb Ch. 1].
- **Reproductive system** — ovaries or testes and accessory structures.
  Produces offspring and releases the sex hormones that drive puberty. The
  gonads are endocrine glands as well as reproductive organs — a first
  clue that system boundaries are not as crisp as the list suggests
  [OpenStax §17.1].

Textbooks sometimes count differently (splitting off immune or sensory as
separate systems). Eleven is the standard, and it's the count used
throughout this curriculum [Tortora §1.3, Marieb Ch. 1].

### A map of common disorders

Each of the eleven systems has a short list of disorders that healthcare
workers see so often the names become shorthand. You don't need to master
any of these yet — you only need a pointer in your head for where each
belongs, so you can route the idea to the right system when it shows up
in a later course or a real patient chart [Mayo-Organ-Systems].

- **Integumentary:** burns, melanoma, eczema, pressure injuries
- **Skeletal:** fractures, **osteoporosis**, arthritis, scoliosis
- **Muscular:** muscular dystrophy, strains and sprains, myasthenia gravis
- **Cardiovascular:** **hypertension**, coronary artery disease, heart
  failure, **stroke** (which straddles cardiovascular and nervous)
- **Lymphatic / immune:** lymphoma, **autoimmune disease**, HIV
- **Respiratory:** asthma, COPD, pneumonia
- **Digestive:** IBD, GERD, cirrhosis, diverticulitis
- **Urinary:** UTI, **chronic kidney disease**, kidney stones
- **Endocrine:** **diabetes mellitus**, hypothyroidism, Cushing's syndrome
- **Nervous:** **stroke**, epilepsy, neuropathy, traumatic brain injury
- **Reproductive:** infertility, reproductive cancers, sexually transmitted
  infections

Notice that some entries appear on two lists — stroke is a problem of
cardiovascular supply and a problem of nervous-system function. That
overlap is not a bug; it is the whole point of thinking in systems rather
than in isolated organs. A **disorder** of one system is, almost always,
a trigger for changes in others [Mayo-Organ-Systems].

### Homeostasis — what every system is really doing

Most of what the eleven systems do, day in and day out, is keep a small
number of internal values within a safe range: core temperature, blood
pressure, blood glucose, fluid and electrolyte balance, and so on. That
stability is **homeostasis**. When a value drifts out of range, one or
more systems respond to bring it back; when a value stays out of range for
too long, a disease begins. Deeper mechanics of feedback loops live in
the *Homeostasis and biological processes* atom [OpenStax §1.5].

### System interactions — why clinicians don't think one system at a time

The table of eleven systems is a study tool, not a picture of how the body
actually works. In practice, every important regulatory job is shared
across three, four, or five systems at once. Blood pressure, for example,
is kept in range by the cardiovascular, nervous, endocrine, and urinary
systems working together — four systems on one job [NIH-NHLBI-HBP]. When
you see a patient whose blood pressure is off, the real question is
*which of those four systems isn't pulling its weight?* Call any process
like this a **system interaction**. The name is short; the idea is the
whole point of this chapter. Four worked cases (standing up, sprinting,
a diabetic patient, and an infected cut) live in `examples.md`.

## Worked through an example

One compact case: a patient with long-standing **diabetes mellitus** whose
kidney filtration is declining and whose blood pressure is rising. They do
not have three separate diseases. They have one upstream endocrine failure
that has damaged the urinary and cardiovascular systems as a consequence —
and nervous-system complications follow if the sequence continues. See
`examples.md` for this case walked step by step, plus three others
[NIH-NHLBI-HBP, NIDDK-CKD].

## Common confusions

A few ideas feel intuitive but turn out to be wrong or incomplete. Bones
are not dead, the heart rate alone doesn't tell you about the respiratory
system, and system boundaries are more blurred than a textbook table makes
them look. Full corrections and diagnostic probes live in `misconceptions.md`.

## Key terms to remember

Three anchor terms hold the whole atom together: **organ system**,
**homeostasis**, and **system interaction**. Add the names of the eleven
systems themselves and the three cross-system disorders that appear on
multiple lists (**hypertension**, **diabetes mellitus**, **stroke**) and
you have the seventeen terms that carry the learning objective. They live
next to this file in `vocabulary.csv` with definitions, example sentences,
and flashcard metadata. System-internal vocabulary — nephron, alveolus,
cardiac output, insulin, and the rest of the words you saw bolded in the
tour — belongs to the deep-dive atom for each system, which is where you
will master it.
