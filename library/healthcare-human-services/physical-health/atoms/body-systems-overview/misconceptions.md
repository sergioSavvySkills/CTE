## Misconception 1 — Bones are inert scaffolding

**What students often think:**
Bones hold the body up but don't really *do* anything once growth is done.

**What's actually true:**
Bone is living tissue with its own blood supply, nerves, and metabolism.
The **skeletal system** also stores calcium the rest of the body draws on
and houses the marrow that makes blood cells [NIA-Aging-Bone].

**How to check:**
Ask: *"Why does spending weeks in a cast weaken a leg bone, and why do
astronauts lose bone mass in microgravity?"* A student with the correct
model will reach for "bone rebuilds in response to load."

## Misconception 2 — Heart rate tells you about the cardiovascular system only

**What students often think:**
If you want to know about the heart, check the pulse. Breathing is a
separate issue handled by the respiratory system.

**What's actually true:**
Heart rate and breathing rate rise together when the body needs more
oxygen or is under stress. Reading one without the other misses half the
picture. Vital signs are read as a pattern across systems, not one number
at a time [OpenStax §19.4].

**How to check:**
Ask: *"A patient has pulse 130 and breathing rate 30. Which systems are
responding, and why?"* A student with the correct model names
**cardiovascular**, **respiratory**, and **nervous** — all three — and
treats the coordination as the point.

## Misconception 3 — Each organ belongs to exactly one system

**What students often think:**
Organs fit into one and only one organ system, the way a file fits into
one folder. Memorize the chart and you're done.

**What's actually true:**
Several organs do double duty. The **pancreas** is a digestive organ
(releasing enzymes into the small intestine) *and* an endocrine organ
(releasing hormones into the blood). The gonads are reproductive organs
and endocrine glands. The skin contributes to integumentary defense,
temperature regulation, and immune function [OpenStax §17.1, OpenStax §1.2].

**How to check:**
Ask: *"Which system does the pancreas belong to?"* A student with the
one-folder model answers "digestive" or "endocrine." A student with the
correct model answers "both" and names one function in each role.

## Misconception 4 — Serious diseases hit one system at a time

**What students often think:**
A heart attack is a cardiovascular event. Diabetes is an endocrine
disease. Kidney failure is a urinary disease. Each one files under one
system.

**What's actually true:**
Almost every chronic illness is a **system interaction** problem. Diabetes
damages the kidneys, the nerves, the retina, and the cardiovascular
system. Chronic kidney disease drives hypertension. A stroke is both a
cardiovascular event (clot or bleed) and a nervous-system event (damaged
brain tissue). Healthcare teams include several specialists for one
patient because the disease doesn't respect textbook chapter boundaries
[NIH-NHLBI-HBP, NIDDK-CKD].

**How to check:**
Ask: *"Name three systems besides the endocrine system that a long-
standing diabetes patient is likely to have problems in."* A student with
the correct model names some combination of **urinary**, **nervous**,
**cardiovascular**, and **integumentary** (slow wound healing).
