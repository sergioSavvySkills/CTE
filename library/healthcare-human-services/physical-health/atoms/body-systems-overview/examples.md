# Worked examples — body-systems-overview

Each example asks: *which organ systems are involved here, and how are they
working together?* The goal is pattern recognition at the system level,
not the physiology of any single system.

## Example 1 — Standing up from a chair

**Scenario:** You sit at your desk for an hour, then stand quickly to grab
a file. For a moment the room feels like it tilts; within two seconds,
you feel normal.

**Which systems are working together, and what is each one doing?**

1. Gravity pulled blood toward your legs when you stood, briefly dropping
   the pressure at your head.
2. The **nervous system** detected the drop and signaled a correction —
   speeding up the heart, constricting vessels.
3. The **cardiovascular system** responded: higher heart rate, higher
   vascular tone.
4. The **endocrine system** released hormones that told the kidneys to
   hold onto more water and salt.
5. The **urinary system** responded by producing less urine.

**Four systems** collaborated to reverse a brief pressure drop. Patients
who cannot complete that loop quickly — older adults, patients on blood-
pressure drugs, dehydrated patients — are at risk of falls from
orthostatic hypotension [NIH-NHLBI-HBP].

## Example 2 — Sprinting for the bus

**Scenario:** You see the bus and sprint for it with a backpack on. Your
heart is pounding, you're breathing hard, your face feels hot.

**Which systems are working together, and what is each one doing?**

1. The **nervous system** initiated the response before you were
   consciously aware.
2. The **muscular system** went from idle to heavy work, demanding oxygen
   and producing heat.
3. The **cardiovascular system** raised heart rate to move more blood.
4. The **respiratory system** raised breathing rate to move more oxygen
   in and more carbon dioxide out.
5. The **integumentary system** opened skin vessels and started sweating
   to release heat.
6. The **endocrine system** released stress hormones to sustain the
   effort.

**Six systems** responded together. No one of them could have handled the
sprint alone. The same coordination pattern shows up under stress, fear,
and infection [OpenStax §19.4].

## Example 3 — A diabetic patient with declining kidney function

**Scenario:** A 62-year-old patient arrives at clinic with blood pressure
158/98, a long-term glucose marker (HbA1c) of 9.2%, and declining kidney
filtration. Their feet have been tingling for months.

**Which systems are involved, and in what order did the dysfunction arise?**

1. **Endocrine system** first: glucose regulation has been failing for
   months. Diagnosis: **diabetes mellitus**, poorly controlled.
2. **Urinary system** next: high glucose damages small vessels in the
   kidneys, which lowers filtration. Diagnosis: **chronic kidney disease**
   [NIDDK-CKD].
3. **Cardiovascular system** next: damaged kidneys can't regulate blood
   pressure as well, so pressure climbs. Diagnosis: **hypertension**
   [NIH-NHLBI-HBP].
4. **Nervous system** next: high glucose damages peripheral nerves.
   Symptom: foot tingling (neuropathy).

One upstream endocrine failure has cascaded into four systems. This is
the shape of almost every chronic-disease patient a healthcare worker
sees.

## Example 4 — An infected cut

**Scenario:** A construction worker nicked their forearm on rusty rebar
three days ago. The cut is now red, swollen, warm, with yellow discharge.
Vital signs: temperature 38.3 °C, heart rate 100, respiratory rate 22.

**Which systems are working together, and what is each one doing?**

1. **Integumentary system**: barrier was breached, letting bacteria in.
2. **Lymphatic / immune system**: responding locally — redness, swelling,
   warmth are the response, not the infection [CDC-Immune-Basics].
3. **Nervous system**: raised the body's temperature set point, producing
   fever.
4. **Cardiovascular system**: raised heart rate to support higher
   metabolic demand.
5. **Respiratory system**: raised breathing rate in step with heart rate.

**Five systems** are actively responding. The vital-sign changes are
features of a working immune response, not bugs. Healthcare workers read
vital signs as a picture of system coordination, not as isolated numbers
[AHA-Vital-Signs].
