# Worked examples — body-systems-overview

Each example walks through one clinical or everyday scenario and asks:
*which organ systems are involved, and how are they working together?*
These are integrative cases rather than linear problems because the atom's
whole point is that systems interact.

## Example 1 — Standing up from a chair

**Scenario:** You sit at your desk for an hour, then stand up quickly to
grab a file. For a moment the room feels like it tilts. Within two seconds,
you feel normal again.

**What to work out:** Which organ systems just collaborated, and what were
they each doing during those two seconds?

**Step-by-step:**

1. When you stood, gravity pulled blood toward your legs, dropping the
   blood pressure at your head for an instant.
2. **Baroreceptors** in the walls of your carotid arteries sensed that
   pressure drop and sent a signal up the nerves to the brainstem. That's
   the sensor in a feedback loop.
3. The nervous system responded by speeding up your heart rate and
   constricting blood vessels in your legs and gut — the first wave of
   correction, within a second.
4. The endocrine system released hormones (including a small burst of
   aldosterone and ADH) that tell the kidneys to retain water and salt,
   slower-acting reinforcement that keeps the pressure up.
5. The urinary system responded to those hormones by producing less urine
   while your pressure stabilized.

**Answer:** Four systems cooperated — cardiovascular, nervous, endocrine,
and urinary — to reverse a brief drop in blood pressure. The "room tilting"
feeling was the gap between the drop and the correction.

**Why it matters:** Patients with autonomic problems, dehydration, or blood-
pressure medications can't complete that loop fast enough. That's why
falls from rising too quickly ("orthostatic hypotension") are a major safety
concern in older adults and in hospitalized patients [NIH-NHLBI-HBP].

## Example 2 — Sprinting for the bus

**Scenario:** You see the bus pulling up at the corner and sprint for it
with a backpack on your shoulder. By the time you make it, your heart is
pounding, you're breathing hard, and your face feels hot.

**What to work out:** Which systems are responding, what's each one
changing, and why?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Your nervous system started the response before you were consciously
   aware of it — you saw the bus, sympathetic signals flooded out, and
   heart rate began rising within a second.
2. The muscular system went from near-idle to heavy work. Skeletal muscle
   demanded much more oxygen and produced much more carbon dioxide and
   heat.
3. The cardiovascular system raised **cardiac output** (more beats per
   minute and more blood moved per beat) to meet that demand.
4. The respiratory system sped up breathing to move more oxygen into the
   blood and more carbon dioxide out through gas exchange at the alveoli.
   That's **cardiorespiratory coupling** in action.
5. The integumentary system opened skin blood vessels and started sweating
   to dump the extra heat. Your face felt hot because the cooling was
   being routed to the surface.
6. The endocrine system released cortisol and epinephrine, which kept
   glucose flowing to working muscle and kept the nervous-system signal
   amplified.

**Answer:** Six systems responded together — nervous, muscular,
cardiovascular, respiratory, integumentary, and endocrine. No one system
could have handled a 30-second sprint alone.

**Why it matters:** The same coordination pattern shows up under stress,
during infection, and during fear. Understanding the normal version is how
clinicians recognize the pathological version — a patient whose heart rate
and breathing won't come back down after exertion is telling you something
about recovery, not just fitness [OpenStax §19.4].

## Example 3 — The stressed kidney patient

**Scenario:** A 62-year-old patient arrives at clinic. Their chart shows a
blood pressure of 158/98, a glomerular filtration rate of 40 mL/min, and a
HbA1c (long-term glucose marker) of 9.2%. They mention their feet have
been feeling tingly and numb for months.

**What to work out:** Which organ systems are involved, in what order did
the dysfunctions likely arise, and why is each new finding not surprising?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Start with the endocrine finding. An HbA1c of 9.2% means blood glucose
   has been too high for months — the loop involving **insulin** and the
   pancreas isn't keeping up. Diagnosis: **diabetes mellitus**, poorly
   controlled.
2. High glucose damages small blood vessels, especially in the kidney's
   **nephrons**. That damage shows up as a falling glomerular filtration
   rate — here, 40 mL/min, which is consistent with stage-3 **chronic
   kidney disease** [NIDDK-CKD].
3. Damaged kidneys can't regulate blood pressure as effectively: they
   retain too much fluid and produce signals that raise vascular tone.
   Result: **hypertension** at 158/98 [Guyton-Renal-BP, NIH-NHLBI-HBP].
4. High glucose also damages small nerves, particularly in the legs and
   feet where the peripheral nervous system's neurons are longest. Result:
   the tingling and numbness (diabetic neuropathy).
5. Four systems are now involved — endocrine (the origin), urinary,
   cardiovascular, and nervous. Each problem is amplifying the others.

**Answer:** One upstream failure (insulin regulation) has cascaded into
four systems. The clinical priority isn't to treat four diseases but to
get glucose under control, protect the kidneys from further damage, and
manage the blood pressure the kidneys can't manage on their own.

**Why it matters:** Nearly every chronic-disease patient you will meet in
a healthcare setting has this shape: one upstream system failure with
downstream effects in three or four others. Recognizing the pattern is
the first step to contributing to their care [NIDDK-CKD, Mayo-Organ-Systems].

## Example 4 — The infected cut

**Scenario:** A construction worker nicks their forearm on a rusty piece
of rebar three days ago. Today the cut is red, swollen, warm to the touch,
and has some yellow discharge. Their temperature is 38.3 °C, heart rate
100, respiratory rate 22.

**What to work out:** Which organ systems are doing what, and what do the
vital-sign changes mean?

**Step-by-step:**

1. The integumentary system's barrier was breached three days ago. That
   let bacteria into the dermis and underlying tissue.
2. The lymphatic / immune system responded locally. Immune cells moved
   into the area, which is why the wound is red, swollen, and warm —
   those are signs of the local **immune response**, not signs the body
   is failing [CDC-Immune-Basics].
3. The nervous system detected the rising pathogen load (via chemical
   signals from the immune cells) and raised the body's thermostat set
   point, producing fever. 38.3 °C is about 1.3 degrees above normal.
4. The cardiovascular system raised heart rate to 100 to support the
   increased metabolic demand of the immune response and to match the
   nervous system's sympathetic drive.
5. The respiratory system raised breathing rate to 22, both because
   metabolic rate is up (cardiorespiratory coupling again) and because
   fever itself accelerates ventilation.

**Answer:** Five systems — integumentary, lymphatic/immune, nervous,
cardiovascular, and respiratory — are actively responding. The vital-sign
changes are features of the response, not bugs. If the response is working,
the infection is contained and the patient recovers with minor treatment.
If the response is outpaced, the same cascade can progress toward sepsis
and multi-organ failure.

**Why it matters:** Every healthcare worker learns to read vital signs not
as isolated numbers but as a picture of what the systems are doing together.
This example is the kind of reasoning behind every nurse's check-in with a
postoperative patient or every EMT's field assessment [AHA-Vital-Signs,
Mayo-Organ-Systems].
