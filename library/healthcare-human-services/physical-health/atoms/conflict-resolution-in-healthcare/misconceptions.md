# Common misconceptions — conflict-resolution-in-healthcare

## Misconception 1 — Conflict is bad and avoiding it is professional

**What students often think:**
A good healthcare worker keeps their head down, goes along to get
along, and avoids conflict whenever possible. Speaking up makes
trouble.

**What's actually true:**
Avoidance is one of the five **conflict styles**, not the
professional default [Thomas-Kilmann]. On a clinical team,
unresolved small conflicts grow into missed information, skipped
double-checks, and workaround culture — all of which are tied to
preventable patient harm [TJC-Workplace]. The professional move
is usually **collaborate**, not avoid; avoiding is right only
when the issue is trivial or emotions need time to cool. The
TeamSTEPPS tools exist specifically to make speaking up the safe
default [TeamSTEPPS].

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Many students have been rewarded in school and in family settings
for being "agreeable." The move from agreeable to clear takes
practice, and the first time feels rude.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A coworker is about to give a dose that looks wrong to
you. She is senior to you and busy. What do you do?"* A student
with the correct model names **CUS** or the **two-challenge
rule** and speaks up; a student without it says "I would feel
uncomfortable bringing it up."

## Misconception 2 — Collaborating means splitting the difference

**What students often think:**
If two people disagree, collaborating means they each give up
half of what they wanted and meet in the middle. Collaborate is a
nicer word for compromise.

**What's actually true:**
**Compromise** is middle-meeting — each side gives up something
to reach "good enough" [Thomas-Kilmann]. **Collaborate** is
different: each side surfaces the underlying interests, and the
group builds a solution that meets both sets of interests, not a
50/50 split. Collaboration takes longer and is the right choice
when stakes are high enough to justify the time. On a clinical
team, the collaborate style is the default because a patient's
plan rarely benefits from two clinicians each conceding half of
what they know.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
In ordinary conversation "collaborate" and "compromise" are used
interchangeably. The Thomas-Kilmann model gives them sharply
different definitions, and students hear the words as synonyms.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A nurse and a respiratory therapist disagree about
whether to wean a patient off oxygen today. What does
collaborating on this decision actually look like, minute by
minute?"* A student with the correct model describes surfacing
each clinician's underlying concern (safety margin, mobilization
goal) and building a plan that addresses both — not "they each
give a little."

## Misconception 3 — Apologizing to a patient or family admits legal fault

**What students often think:**
Saying "I'm sorry" to an upset patient or family admits that
staff did something wrong, so you should never apologize.

**What's actually true:**
An expression of empathy — "I am sorry this has been so hard
today" — is not the same as a disclosure of fault, and it is a
core move in **de-escalation** [AHRQ-CANDOR]. The AHRQ CANDOR
model is built around the finding that honest empathy, delivered
early, reduces complaints and litigation rather than increasing
them. There is a separate, formal disclosure process for actual
clinical errors, which staff do not attempt alone. Refusing all
apology language leaves families stranded in their feeling and
makes the conflict worse.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Popular culture — and some older risk-management advice — teaches
that saying "sorry" is admitting guilt. The CANDOR evidence
pushed the field the other way, but that update has not reached
every student or every workplace culture.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A family member is yelling about a three-hour wait. Name
the first sentence you say."* A student with the correct model
leads with an empathy sentence ("I can see this wait has been
awful") and a concrete next step. A student without it jumps to
defending the schedule or refusing to say "sorry."

## Misconception 4 — A bad day from a coworker is the same as disruptive behavior

**What students often think:**
Any coworker who snaps, raises a voice, or is short with me is
engaging in disruptive behavior and should be reported.

**What's actually true:**
**Disruptive behavior** is a pattern — repeated public put-downs,
intimidation, refusal to engage, sabotage — that the profession
explicitly names as requiring escalation rather than tolerance
[ANA-Bullying, TJC-Workplace]. A single stressed moment, a short
reply during a code, or a one-time snap is not the same thing
and is usually handled with a direct, private conversation using
an **I statement**. Applying the disruptive-behavior label to
every rough interaction both burns out reporting systems and
skips the ordinary conversation that resolves most peer conflict.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
"Disruptive behavior" has become a loosely used term in training
videos, where a single shouting scene is labeled disruptive for
the camera. The policy definition requires a pattern, which is
harder to dramatize.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"Your coworker snapped at you once this week during a
busy shift. Is that disruptive behavior?"* A student with the
correct model says no, describes the private I-statement
conversation they would have, and notes the line ("if it
becomes a pattern or I see it with patients"). A student
without it jumps straight to reporting.
