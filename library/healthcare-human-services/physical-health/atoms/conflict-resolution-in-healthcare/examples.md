# Worked examples — conflict-resolution-in-healthcare

## Example 1 — De-escalating an upset parent in the hallway

**Scenario:** You are a medical assistant on a pediatric unit.
Ms. Rivera, the mother of a 7-year-old patient, is pacing in the
hallway in tears. Her son's MRI was scheduled for 11 a.m. It is
now 3 p.m. The scan has been rescheduled twice and no one has
come to explain why. Her son has not eaten since the night
before. Her voice is loud. Several visitors are watching.

**What to work out:** What is the right first sentence, and how
do you move from that sentence to a workable next step?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Stop and turn your body toward Ms. Rivera. Do not walk past
   her. Lower your volume slightly so she has to quiet down to
   hear you. The tone shift is part of **de-escalation**
   [AHRQ-CANDOR].
2. Acknowledge first. "Ms. Rivera, I can see how hard this wait
   has been — four hours with a hungry child, and no updates.
   That's not okay." This is not agreeing that staff did
   anything wrong; it is naming the feeling and the facts.
3. Offer a brief, honest apology for the experience. "I am so
   sorry today has been this hard." Empathy is not fault
   [AHRQ-CANDOR].
4. Explain briefly what you actually know. "The scanner has
   been backed up since an emergency case this morning. I do
   not have the new time yet."
5. Offer a concrete next step. "I am going to call radiology
   right now and come back to you in the next ten minutes with
   a real number. If I cannot get one, I will bring the charge
   nurse with me." Not "we will keep you posted."
6. Do the step. Come back in the promised window, even if the
   answer is still "not yet."

**Answer:** Acknowledge, apologize for the experience, brief
explain, concrete next step — the CANDOR four-move pattern.

**Why it matters:** Families rarely need an answer immediately.
They need to be heard and to know someone owns the next move.
Skipping acknowledgement and jumping to a defense of the
schedule is how a hallway complaint becomes a written one.

## Example 2 — Using CUS to stop a wrong-looking dose

**Scenario:** You are a new nurse on a med-surg unit. A senior
nurse, Janelle, is drawing up what she says is a scheduled
dose of furosemide for the patient in bed 4. Looking at the
MAR yourself, you see the order was discontinued yesterday.
Janelle is in a hurry and has already turned toward the
patient's room.

**What to work out:** How do you stop the dose without
triggering a fight?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Say her name first so she knows the message is for her.
   "Janelle — one second." Stay calm, short, direct.
2. Raise the first level of **CUS**. "I am concerned. I just
   saw in the MAR that this furosemide order was discontinued
   yesterday" [TeamSTEPPS].
3. If she pauses and checks, the conflict is already over —
   **collaborate** on confirming the order, move on.
4. If she brushes past you — "I saw the order this morning,
   it's fine" — use the next level. "I am uncomfortable. Can
   we stop and check the MAR together before you give it?"
   This is the first half of the **two-challenge rule**
   [TeamSTEPPS].
5. If she still dismisses, use the third level, out loud,
   with your body between her and the patient if needed.
   "This is a safety issue. I need us to pause." Then
   escalate to the charge nurse up the **chain of command**
   [TJC-Workplace].
6. After the event, document in neutral language what was
   observed, what was said, and what was done. No break-room
   debrief with uninvolved coworkers.

**Answer:** CUS escalating in three steps, with the
two-challenge rule applied when the first concern is
dismissed, and escalation up the chain of command if needed.

**Why it matters:** "It felt awkward to bring it up" is the
sentence behind a large share of preventable medication
errors. CUS gives a junior staff member a fixed script and a
senior staff member a fixed cue that pausing is the expected
response [TeamSTEPPS].

## Example 3 — Choosing the right conflict style for a scheduling dispute

**Scenario:** You and a coworker, Dev, both want the 12–1
lunch slot tomorrow. Dev wants it for a phone call with his
child's school; you want it because you are meeting a friend.
Your charge nurse has asked the two of you to sort it out
before the shift starts.

**What to work out:** Which **conflict style** fits, and why
is it not "collaborate"?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Assess stakes. This is a scheduling preference, not a
   safety issue. Neither person is wronged by a middle
   solution [Thomas-Kilmann].
2. Assess time. You have about ten minutes before shift
   starts. A long needs-surfacing conversation is out of
   scope.
3. Compare styles against the situation. **Collaborate**
   fits high-stakes, time-available problems and is
   overkill here. **Compete** would damage a working
   relationship over lunch. **Accommodate** would mean you
   silently give in, which leaves resentment.
   **Compromise** — each gives up something for a middle —
   fits stakes and time.
4. Reframe the problem. "You need to take a call at 12:15.
   I want to see my friend. Can you take 12:15–1:00 for the
   call and I take 12:00–12:45, and we overlap on the floor
   for fifteen minutes?" That is **reframing** a preference
   clash into a scheduling puzzle.
5. Confirm the plan out loud. Notify the charge nurse.
   Done.

**Answer:** Compromise, using reframing to turn two
incompatible wants into a workable schedule.

**Why it matters:** Style is situational. A student who
defaults to collaborate on every disagreement burns time
that better belongs to clinical work. A student who
defaults to accommodate on every disagreement quietly builds
a pattern of being overruled that is hard to reverse later.

## Example 4 — When the answer is to escalate, not to resolve

**Scenario:** For three weeks, a technician on your unit has
been publicly mocking a newer aide — imitating her accent in
front of patients, interrupting her in huddles, and twice
refusing to help her with a transfer that required two
staff. The aide has asked you what to do. She is considering
quitting.

**What to work out:** Is this a peer conversation, or does
it belong outside the pair? If outside, to whom?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Apply the "pattern" test. One bad day is not the same as
   a pattern. Three weeks of repeated public put-downs with
   at least one patient-safety implication (refusing the
   two-person transfer) is a pattern — this is
   **disruptive behavior** as the profession names it
   [ANA-Bullying, TJC-Workplace].
2. Coach the aide on the I-statement conversation if she
   wants to try it — "I feel undermined when the
   imitations happen in front of patients." Offer to be
   present. Many patterns do stop once a clear private
   statement is made.
3. Regardless of whether the I-statement conversation
   happens, the case already meets the threshold for
   escalation beyond peers. The aide should not have to
   carry this alone, and patient-safety implications
   require reporting.
4. Route through the **chain of command**. Direct
   supervisor first; the supervisor owns the next steps
   (formal conversation with the technician, HR referral,
   patterns documentation). The ethics committee or an
   employee assistance program may also be relevant
   [TJC-Workplace].
5. Document — objectively, not angrily — the incidents the
   aide has named. "On date X, in front of patient Y,
   technician Z did A." Facts, not labels.
6. Do not text or message about the situation, do not vent
   in break rooms with uninvolved coworkers, and do not
   screenshot anything without guidance.

**Answer:** Escalate up the chain of command. This is
beyond a peer conflict because of both the pattern and the
patient-safety implication.

**Why it matters:** Healthcare workers lose colleagues — and
patients lose staff — when disruptive behavior is treated as
something the target has to personally absorb. The
profession explicitly requires escalation so that the cost
is not pushed onto the person least able to carry it
[ANA-Bullying].
