# Conflict Resolution in Health Care

## Why this matters

A daughter is pacing the hallway outside her mother's room because
the discharge has been delayed three times. A respiratory therapist
and a nurse disagree about whether a patient is ready to come off
oxygen. A new medical assistant is being talked over in every
huddle by a coworker who has been there for fifteen years. None of
these situations is a medical emergency. All of them are conflict,
and all of them can harm a patient if they are handled badly.
Conflict that festers on a healthcare team shows up later as
missed information, skipped double-checks, and silent workarounds
[TJC-Workplace]. Conflict with a patient or family that is met
with defensiveness turns into complaints, into lost trust, and
sometimes into legal action [AHRQ-CANDOR].

The skill this atom teaches is not conflict avoidance. Avoiding
conflict on a unit is how small problems become safety events.
The skill is to choose the right approach for the moment, keep
the tone calm, and move a tense interaction toward a workable
next step — with patients, with families, and with coworkers.

## What you should already know

This atom builds on basic **therapeutic communication**: how to
greet a patient, introduce yourself, use a calm tone, and listen
without interrupting. You should also know the general shape of a
clinical team — nurses, aides, techs, charge nurses, unit
managers, physicians — so that language like "escalate" and
"supervisor" maps to real roles. You do not need any psychology
background; one model of conflict styles is introduced by name
and used, and that is the only theory you are asked to learn.

## Core concept

### The five conflict styles and when each one fits

The most widely used model names five recurring ways people
respond when their interests clash [Thomas-Kilmann]. Think of each
as one **conflict style** a person picks up or puts down by
choice. The five are:

- **Avoid** — step away from the issue, at least for now. Right
  when emotions are too hot, when the issue is trivial, or when
  you genuinely have no standing to decide.
- **Accommodate** — give in on your preference to preserve the
  relationship or yield on something small. Right when the issue
  matters more to the other person than to you, or when you were
  wrong.
- **Compete** — push hard for your position. Right when a safety
  line has been crossed and there is no time to negotiate, or
  when an unpopular rule has to be enforced.
- **Compromise** — each side gives up something to reach a
  middle. Right when stakes are moderate, time is short, and a
  "good enough" answer beats a perfect one.
- **Collaborate** — dig into what each side actually needs and
  build a solution that meets both sets of interests.
  Time-expensive, but the default on a clinical team because
  patient safety depends on everyone's input being heard.

None of the five is "good" or "bad." They are tools. The skill is
reading the situation — how urgent, how high-stakes, how
relational — and picking the fit. A nurse who always accommodates
becomes silent when a dose looks wrong. A nurse who always
competes runs out of colleagues to call for help. A healthy
clinician uses all five and uses **collaborate** as the steady
state with teammates.

### Core communication skills that make any style work

Style is the choice; skill is the execution. The same handful of
habits do most of the work regardless of which style fits.

**Tone before content.** The first three seconds of your voice
set the tenor of the whole interaction. A calm, slightly slower
pace, a moderate volume, and an open posture signal "I am safe
to talk to." Crossed arms, a fast clipped voice, or standing
between a family member and the door signal the opposite before
a word of content lands.

**Active listening.** This is not silent nodding. **Active
listening** means attending with your whole face, letting the
speaker finish, and reflecting back the gist in your own words
before you respond. "So you are worried that the medication was
given late, and nobody told you — did I get that right?" The
speaker who hears an accurate reflection feels heard; the
speaker who hears a rushed rebuttal does not. Reflecting feels
slow, and it is, and it is how tense people calm down
[AHRQ-CANDOR].

**Separate the person from the problem.** An upset family
member is not the same thing as the problem of a delayed
discharge. A coworker who raises her voice is not the same thing
as the issue she is raising. Naming the problem — the delay,
the order, the handoff — and setting the person's character to
one side keeps the conversation workable.

**Validate feelings before problem-solving.** A person in the
middle of a strong feeling cannot hear a plan. A sentence like
"It makes sense you are frustrated — three hours is a long time
to wait for news" is not agreement that staff did something
wrong; it is acknowledgement that the feeling is real. Only
after the feeling is named can the next step be offered.

**Use I statements.** An **I statement** describes what you feel
or observe without diagnosing the other person's character. "I
feel talked over when I try to give report and the TV is on"
lands. "You are rude and you never listen" does not [ANA-Bullying].
The I statement keeps ownership of the feeling with the speaker,
which is both more accurate and less likely to trigger defense.

**Reframe the complaint.** **Reframing** is the move from "you
never listen to me" to "we need a clearer handoff process." Same
underlying problem, different surface. The reframe takes a
statement the team cannot act on and turns it into one they can.

### De-escalating an upset patient or family

Upset patients and families usually are not angry at you. They
are angry at a system that delayed a test, withheld information,
or left them sitting in a waiting room while a relative was in
pain. The work is not to defend yourself or the system. The work
is to lower the heat enough that a plan can be offered.

A standard pattern from AHRQ's CANDOR toolkit gives four moves
in order [AHRQ-CANDOR]:

1. **Acknowledge.** Name what has happened and what the person
   seems to be feeling. "I can see you have been waiting a long
   time and nobody has come to update you."
2. **Apologize where appropriate.** An expression of empathy —
   "I am sorry this has been so hard today" — is not the same as
   admitting fault for a clinical error, and it is almost always
   safe and useful. For true errors, facility policy and risk
   management guide the more formal disclosure.
3. **Explain.** Briefly, honestly, at a layperson's level. What
   has happened, what is happening now, what is still unknown.
4. **Offer the next step.** Something concrete: "I will go check
   on the lab result and come back in ten minutes" is worth more
   than an open-ended "we will keep you posted." **De-escalation**
   is built out of these four moves used in order.

Two habits undo the whole sequence. The first is defensiveness —
turning the conversation into a defense of your team, your shift,
or your facility. The second is offering more information than
the person can absorb when they are upset. Acknowledgement and a
concrete next step are almost always the right opening, no
matter how tempting a full explanation feels.

### Team conflict — speaking up and being heard

Team conflict looks different from patient conflict because the
power gradient is different. A junior staff member often has
information that a more senior teammate needs but does not want
to hear. The TeamSTEPPS program, developed by AHRQ, gives two
structured tools for exactly that moment [TeamSTEPPS].

**CUS** is a three-step script, one level stronger than the last:

- "I am **C**oncerned."
- "I am **U**ncomfortable."
- "This is a **S**afety issue."

Saying "CUS" out loud on a well-trained team is a known signal
that the speaker wants the action paused. It gives a nervous
speaker a fixed script to follow and a listener a fixed cue to
respect.

The **two-challenge rule** handles the case where the first
concern is brushed aside. The speaker raises the concern once,
clearly. If it is ignored or dismissed, the speaker repeats it
once more, explicitly. If it is still not addressed, the speaker
escalates — not as an insult to the teammate, but as the rule
the team agreed to in advance. The rule exists because a single
polite mention is too easy to miss, and three tries is too many
when seconds matter.

When a direct conversation does not resolve the issue, staff
escalate through the **chain of command** — the ordered list of
people above the point of conflict [TJC-Workplace]. For a floor
nurse, that is typically the charge nurse, then the unit manager,
then the house supervisor, then risk management. The chain exists
so that a concern has a path even when peers disagree. Using it
is not "telling on" a coworker; it is following the structure
the facility has built for contested decisions.

Good team documentation is the quiet half of team conflict.
Chart what was observed, what was said, what was done, in
neutral language and on the record — not in a break-room
conversation. Objective documentation protects everyone; venting
in the hallway protects no one.

### When to get help beyond the team

Some situations are past the point where two teammates can
resolve them, and recognizing that line matters as much as any
communication skill. Four categories clearly cross it:

1. **Threats of violence** — verbal or physical — toward staff,
   patients, or visitors. Security and the supervisor are
   involved immediately [TJC-Workplace].
2. **Suspected impairment of a coworker.** You do not
   investigate. You report to a supervisor and let policy take
   over.
3. **Scope-of-practice violations.** A coworker asking you to
   perform a task outside your role, or performing one outside
   theirs, is not a conversation for peers alone.
4. **A pattern of disrespectful behavior** — not one bad
   day, but a pattern. Repeated public put-downs, intimidation,
   sabotage — this is **disruptive behavior**, and the
   profession explicitly calls for it to be reported rather
   than absorbed [ANA-Bullying, TJC-Workplace].

The resources beyond a peer conversation include the supervisor
first, then risk management, the ethics committee, employee
assistance programs, and security for safety threats. "Going
around" a coworker feels uncomfortable; letting one of these
situations continue is worse.

### Pitfalls — the habits that turn a solvable conflict into a career issue

Several moves feel natural and make the situation worse.

- **Winning at all costs.** Some conflicts end with everyone
  bruised and a "winner" whose patient care suffers from a now-
  toxic team. Winning a shift argument is often losing the unit.
- **Gossiping with uninvolved coworkers.** Venting to the one
  person who was not there is not "processing"; it is damaging
  the absent colleague's standing without giving them a chance
  to respond. Take the concern to the person involved or to a
  supervisor.
- **Texting or messaging about the conflict.** Screenshots
  travel. A frustrated text becomes evidence in an HR file or a
  legal matter later. Keep conflict discussions verbal and in
  appropriate settings.
- **Apologizing for things you shouldn't admit legally.** A
  sincere "I am sorry this has been a hard morning" is almost
  always safe. "I am sorry we gave the wrong medication" is a
  clinical-error disclosure that should be made with facility
  policy in the room [AHRQ-CANDOR]. The CANDOR model exists in
  part to protect well-intentioned staff from confusing the two.

## Worked through an example

Picture a mother at 3 p.m. in the hallway of a pediatric unit.
Her son's MRI was supposed to be at 11 a.m.; he has not eaten
since 10 p.m. the night before; no one has told her why the scan
keeps getting pushed. She is in tears and she is loud. The right
opening is not "ma'am, please lower your voice." The right
opening is acknowledgement and a concrete next step. See
`examples.md` for this full scenario, plus three others: a peer
conflict using CUS, a team conflict that goes to the two-challenge
rule, and a scenario where the right answer is to escalate rather
than to resolve.

## Common confusions

Students often confuse being "nice" with resolving conflict,
confuse collaboration with compromise, and confuse a single bad
interaction with a pattern of disruptive behavior. Each of those
confusions changes what a student would actually do in the
hallway. Full corrections, with diagnostic probes, live in
`misconceptions.md`.
