# Healthcare Team Roles and Scopes

## Why this matters

Walk onto any hospital unit on a weekday morning and count the
badges. You will see a dozen different roles within the first
ten minutes, and a single patient in a single bed is touched by
most of them before lunch. A physician writes the plan, a nurse
carries it out, a pharmacist checks the drug list, a respiratory
therapist manages the ventilator, a medical assistant sets up
for the next procedure, a social worker arranges the discharge,
and a transporter wheels the patient to imaging. No one person
holds the whole picture. A student who cannot tell these roles
apart — or who thinks "everyone in scrubs is basically a nurse"
— will misread who can do what, ask the wrong person for help,
and miss the cross-checks that keep patients safe
[IOM-QualityChasm]. The point of this atom is to give you the
map of the team: who does what, what each role can and cannot
do, and why the team together is safer than any of them alone.

## What you should already know

You should have a rough sense of the healthcare career families
from the prerequisite atom on careers and pathways — therapeutic,
diagnostic, support, informatics, nursing, and biotech. You do
not need to know licensure law, specific state rules, or the
details of any single role's education route. Those live in
other atoms. Here we care about role identity on a working team.

## Core concept

A healthcare team is a group of workers with different licenses,
skills, and job scopes who share responsibility for the same
patients. The modern term for this kind of team is an
**interprofessional team** [IPEC-Core]. Five role categories
cover almost everyone you will meet on one, and understanding
the category makes it easy to predict what each person can and
cannot do.

### Physicians and advanced practice providers

The **MD** (doctor of medicine) or DO (doctor of osteopathic
medicine) is the physician. Physicians diagnose disease, write
the overall care plan, prescribe medications, and perform
procedures within their specialty. They carry the broadest
scope on the team and the final accountability for the plan
[BLS-OOH].

Alongside physicians work two kinds of **advanced practice
provider**: the **PA** (physician assistant) and the NP (nurse
practitioner). Both can diagnose, prescribe, and manage care
for most patients an average clinic sees. The difference is
historical and regulatory: PAs trained on the medical model and
practice under physician **supervision**; NPs trained through
the nursing model and in many states practice with more
independence. In day-to-day clinic work, you often cannot tell
which one you are seeing, and that is by design. What neither
an MD, a PA, nor an NP can do alone is deliver the whole plan —
they write it, but someone else carries most of it out.

### The nursing ladder

Nursing is organized as a ladder of increasing scope, and
understanding it is the single fastest way to predict who on a
floor can do what.

- **Certified nursing assistant (CNA).** Takes vital signs,
  helps with bathing and feeding, assists with mobility, records
  intake and output. A CNA is **certified**, not licensed, and
  cannot administer medications or perform clinical assessments
  [BLS-OOH].
- **Licensed practical or vocational nurse (LPN/LVN).** Holds a
  license, administers many oral and injectable medications,
  reinforces teaching, and performs basic assessments — but
  typically does not start IVs with certain medications, push IV
  drugs, or do the initial nursing assessment on a new admission.
  What an LPN can do varies by state.
- **Registered nurse (RN).** The workhorse of the hospital floor.
  An **RN** performs the full nursing assessment, administers
  all routes of medication, develops the nursing care plan,
  coordinates with the rest of the team, and **delegates**
  appropriate tasks to the CNA and LPN. Prepared through an
  ADN or BSN program and **licensed** by the state after passing
  the NCLEX-RN [NCSBN-Delegation].
- **BSN, MSN, and the NP.** A BSN is a bachelor-level RN; an MSN
  is master's preparation that leads into advanced roles,
  including the nurse practitioner.

The ladder matters for scope and for **delegation**. An RN who
asks a CNA to push IV morphine has asked for something outside
the CNA's scope — a safety event, not a favor. The RN keeps
responsibility for any task they delegate, so they must match
the task to the right person's scope [NCSBN-Delegation].

### Allied health

**Allied health** is the large, varied category of clinical
workers who are not physicians, nurses, or dentists [BLS-OOH].
They specialize deeply in one slice of care and their scope
follows that specialty.

- **Respiratory therapists (RTs)** manage ventilators and
  breathing treatments, do pulmonary-function tests, and run
  rapid-response airway calls. They do not diagnose lung
  disease, but they assess respiratory status closely.
- **Radiology technologists** and sonographers run imaging —
  X-ray, CT, MRI, ultrasound — positioning the patient and
  operating the equipment. The radiologist physician interprets
  the image; the tech captures it.
- **Medical laboratory scientists** and technicians run blood,
  urine, and tissue tests. They can flag abnormal results but
  do not treat the patient directly.
- **Pharmacists** verify every medication order, check for
  interactions and dosing, counsel patients, and in many systems
  now round with the team and adjust drug regimens under a
  collaborative agreement.
- **Physical therapists (PT), occupational therapists (OT),
  and speech-language pathologists (SLP)** rehabilitate
  movement, activities of daily living, and speech or
  swallowing. Each is licensed and has a defined scope;
  PT assistants and OT assistants work under their supervision.
- **Medical assistants (MAs)** work in clinics rooming
  patients, taking vitals, giving certain injections, and
  documenting. MAs are generally **certified** rather than
  licensed, and their scope is narrower than any nurse's.

### Support and administrative staff

A hospital stops running within hours if the support teams stop.
These roles are often invisible in training videos and essential
in practice.

- **Unit clerks** and ward secretaries manage the unit's paper
  and phone traffic, track admissions and discharges, and keep
  the chart workflow moving.
- **Patient access staff** register patients, verify insurance,
  and gather consents.
- **Medical coders and billers** translate the chart into the
  coded record that reimburses the visit. Informatics analysts
  maintain the electronic health record.
- **Environmental services, food service, and patient transport**
  keep rooms clean, deliver meals that match dietary orders,
  and move patients between departments.

None of these roles make clinical decisions, but each affects
patient outcomes. A missed bed-turn delay cascades into an
emergency-department hold; a coded allergy entered wrong cascades
into a drug event.

### Behavioral and social support

The last category is the one medical students most often
underestimate. Behavioral and social support roles address the
parts of a patient's life that a prescription cannot reach.

- **Social workers** assess a patient's home situation, connect
  them with housing, food, and transportation resources, and
  coordinate discharge planning for complex cases.
- **Case managers** — often nurses or social workers by
  background — coordinate long-running care across settings,
  manage insurance approvals, and keep the plan on track.
- **Chaplains** provide spiritual care for patients and
  families, across faith traditions and for those without one.
- **Counselors and behavioral-health clinicians** treat mental
  health conditions, coordinate with psychiatrists, and run
  group and individual therapy.

A discharge that ignores the social worker is a discharge that
boomerangs — the patient returns to the same unsafe housing and
the same missed medications, and ends up readmitted within the
week.

### Why teams beat individuals

Even a brilliant physician, working alone, is less safe than an
average team. The reason is not talent; it is structure
[IOM-QualityChasm, AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS].

- **Complementary expertise.** No one person can know everything
  a complex patient needs. A pharmacist catches drug interactions
  a physician missed; a physical therapist notices a new weakness
  a nurse could not have flagged. The team covers gaps no
  individual can close.
- **Cross-checks.** Medication administration runs through the
  ordering physician, the verifying pharmacist, and the
  administering nurse. Each is a check on the others. The same
  error has to pass three gates to reach the patient.
- **Handoffs.** Patients stay; clinicians rotate. Every shift
  change is a **handoff**, and every handoff is a risk. Teams
  that use a structured format — most commonly **SBAR**
  (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) — lose
  fewer critical details than teams that freelance the transfer
  [AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS].
- **Reduced errors.** Studies of team-training interventions
  show measurable drops in adverse events and missed diagnoses
  when teams adopt standardized communication and defined role
  handoffs [AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS, IOM-QualityChasm]. That is the
  evidence base for why team-based care is the current
  standard, not a style preference.

Scope discipline is what makes the cross-checks work. If the
CNA believes they can administer medications, the medication
check collapses to one person. If the pharmacist believes their
job is only to fill orders, the interaction check disappears.
Each role's narrower scope is what lets the team's combined
scope cover the patient safely.

## Worked through an example

Picture Mrs. Chen, a 74-year-old admitted through the emergency
department for pneumonia and a new atrial fibrillation. Between
arrival and discharge five days later, she is cared for by the
ED physician, an ED nurse, a radiology tech, a lab scientist,
the hospitalist MD, an RN and CNA on the medical floor, a
pharmacist, a respiratory therapist, a physical therapist, a
care manager, and a social worker. Each does a slice; none of
them could do the whole thing alone. The full walkthrough of who
touches the chart when — and what each can and cannot do — lives
in `examples.md`.

## Common confusions

Students often assume that "the doctor is in charge of everything,"
that "nurses all do the same thing," and that the support staff
are optional. Each of those beliefs changes how a student would
behave on the team. Full corrections, with diagnostic probes,
live in `misconceptions.md`.

## Key terms to remember

The anchor concepts are **scope of practice**, **delegation**,
**handoff**, **SBAR**, and **interprofessional team**. The
category labels — **allied health** and **advanced practice
provider** — tell you where a role sits on the team. The
distinction between **licensed** and **certified** tells you
what kind of authority a role carries, and **supervision**
names the working relationship between roles of different
scope. A few high-weight role abbreviations — **RN**, **MD**,
and **PA** — anchor the rest. All entries live in
`vocabulary.csv`.
