# Worked examples — healthcare-team-roles

## Example 1 — Mrs. Chen: pneumonia and new atrial fibrillation

**Scenario:** Mrs. Chen is 74. She arrives at the emergency
department short of breath with a fast, irregular pulse. Over
five days she is diagnosed with pneumonia and new atrial
fibrillation, treated, and discharged home. Follow who touches
her chart, and for each person, name what they can and cannot
do.

**What to work out:** Which roles contribute to her care, and
what does each one's **scope of practice** allow them to do
(and not do) within this admission?

**Step-by-step:**

1. **ED physician (MD).** Diagnoses pneumonia and new atrial
   fibrillation, orders antibiotics, a rate-control drug, and
   admission. Cannot, alone, draw the blood, run the imaging,
   or deliver the medications — those belong to other scopes
   [BLS-OOH].
2. **ED nurse (RN).** Performs the initial nursing assessment,
   places an IV, administers the ordered medications, monitors
   vital signs. Cannot diagnose a new condition or prescribe a
   new drug. Can call the physician if Mrs. Chen deteriorates
   and escalate — that is inside the RN scope
   [NCSBN-Delegation].
3. **Radiology tech and medical laboratory scientist.** The
   tech positions Mrs. Chen and captures the chest X-ray; a
   radiologist physician (not present bedside) reads it. The
   lab scientist runs her blood cultures and electrolytes and
   flags abnormal values. Neither treats her [BLS-OOH].
4. **Hospitalist MD and floor RN.** Once admitted, the
   hospitalist manages the medical plan day to day. The floor
   RN coordinates care, administers all medications, and
   **delegates** vital signs and intake/output to the CNA
   [NCSBN-Delegation].
5. **Pharmacist.** Verifies every medication order, checks the
   new rate-control drug for interactions with her home warfarin,
   and recommends a dose adjustment the physician accepts. The
   pharmacist's independent judgment is a cross-check, not a
   formality [AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS].
6. **Respiratory therapist.** Administers scheduled nebulizer
   treatments and monitors her oxygen needs. Does not decide
   which antibiotic to use — that is not inside RT scope.
7. **Physical therapist and social worker.** PT evaluates her
   mobility before discharge; social worker assesses whether she
   can manage medications at home alone and arranges a home
   health referral. The MD could not safely discharge her
   without both assessments [IPEC-Core].
8. **Handoffs.** At every shift change, the outgoing RN gives
   an **SBAR handoff** to the incoming RN. The physician team
   signs out between day and night hospitalists using a similar
   structured format [AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS].

**Answer:** Mrs. Chen's admission is the work of at least ten
distinct roles across the five categories in this atom. Each
does what their scope allows; no single role could do the
whole thing.

**Why it matters:** A student who thinks "the doctor takes care
of the patient" underestimates how much of the plan is delivered
by others. Counting the roles on one real admission makes the
**interprofessional team** model concrete.

## Example 2 — The delegation decision at 2 a.m.

**Scenario:** It is 2 a.m. on a busy medical floor. The RN has
four patients. A new admission just came up from the ED, a
second patient needs a scheduled IV antibiotic, a third needs
to be turned and have their dressing checked, and a fourth
needs vital signs. The CNA is on shift with her. How should
the RN distribute the work?

**What to work out:** Which tasks can the RN **delegate** to
the CNA, and which must she do herself?

**Step-by-step:**

1. **New admission.** The initial nursing assessment is part of
   the RN's scope and cannot be delegated to a **certified**
   (not licensed) CNA. The RN must do this herself
   [NCSBN-Delegation].
2. **IV antibiotic administration.** Administering IV
   medications is outside the CNA's scope. The RN must push
   or hang the antibiotic.
3. **Turning and dressing check.** Turning the patient is well
   inside a CNA's scope. A simple dressing observation — "does
   it look clean, dry, and intact" — can be delegated; a new
   assessment of the wound (staging, signs of infection) is a
   nursing judgment and stays with the RN.
4. **Vital signs on the stable fourth patient.** Vital signs are
   the classic delegable task for a CNA. The RN reviews the
   numbers when the CNA reports them and acts on anything
   abnormal [NCSBN-Delegation].
5. **Who keeps accountability.** Even the tasks the RN delegates
   are still her responsibility under the "five rights of
   delegation." If the CNA misses a fever, the RN is answerable
   for not having checked the reported number
   [NCSBN-Delegation].

**Answer:** The RN does the admission assessment, the IV
antibiotic, and any clinical judgment about the wound herself.
She delegates the turn, the dressing observation, and the stable
patient's vital signs to the CNA, and she checks all reported
numbers.

**Why it matters:** A student who does not understand the
CNA–RN scope difference might assume either that "the CNA can
do anything the RN asks" (unsafe) or that "the RN has to do
everything herself" (impossible on a four-patient load).
Correct **delegation** is what makes the team's throughput work
without breaking scope.

## Example 3 — SBAR handoff in the ICU

**Scenario:** You are the day-shift RN in a surgical ICU. Your
patient had abdominal surgery two days ago. Today the drain
output dropped sharply and his blood pressure trended down over
the last four hours. He is stable now after a fluid bolus, but
you are worried. Your shift ends in ten minutes and the night
RN is walking over for report.

**What to work out:** How should you structure the **handoff**
so that no critical information is lost between shifts?

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Situation.** Start with the patient's identity and current
   status in one or two sentences. "Mr. Park, bed 12,
   post-op day 2 from an open abdominal surgery. Stable now
   but concerning trend over the last four hours."
   [AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS]
2. **Background.** Give the admission reason, relevant history,
   and what has happened so far. "Admitted for bowel
   obstruction, surgery on Monday; JP drain in place;
   previously stable vitals until 10 a.m."
3. **Assessment.** State what you think is going on — this is
   the step freelance handoffs most often skip. "Drain output
   dropped from 200 to 20 mL in four hours; BP from 125/80 to
   100/60 before a 500 mL bolus. I am worried about either a
   clogged drain or developing intra-abdominal bleeding."
4. **Recommendation.** State what the incoming clinician should
   watch and do. "Please recheck vitals every 30 minutes
   overnight, monitor drain output, and call the surgeon if BP
   drops below 100 systolic or drain output is bloody."
5. **Close the loop.** Ask the incoming RN to repeat back what
   they will watch. A read-back confirms the recommendation
   landed as intended [AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS].

**Answer:** A four-segment **SBAR** handoff plus a read-back
produces a complete transfer of responsibility in under two
minutes.

**Why it matters:** If you had freelanced the handoff and
narrated events in the order they happened, you would likely
have omitted the assessment and recommendation pieces. Those are
the pieces the incoming RN needs most when the patient changes
overnight.

## Example 4 — Who sees the patient first in a primary-care clinic

**Scenario:** A patient calls her primary-care clinic on Monday
morning for a routine medication refill and a question about a
new rash. The clinic staffs a physician, a nurse practitioner,
a medical assistant, and a front-desk patient-access
representative. Trace which team member handles each piece.

**What to work out:** Who does what, and why is this split the
way it is?

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Patient access representative.** Answers the phone, schedules
   the visit, verifies insurance, and routes the refill request
   to the clinical team. Does not make any clinical decision;
   does control whether the visit happens at all [BLS-OOH].
2. **Medical assistant.** Rooms the patient, takes vital signs,
   updates the medication list in the chart, and asks a
   structured set of intake questions. The MA is **certified**
   rather than **licensed** and does not diagnose or prescribe —
   but the MA's intake is what the clinician walks in with
   [BLS-OOH].
3. **Nurse practitioner (advanced practice provider).** Examines
   the rash, diagnoses a mild contact dermatitis, prescribes a
   topical steroid, and renews the chronic medication. The NP
   works at the top of the nursing ladder with prescribing
   authority; in this clinic, routine visits like this are
   handled by the NP rather than the physician [BLS-OOH].
4. **Physician.** Does not see this patient today but is
   available if the NP needs a consult. If the rash had looked
   atypical or the diagnosis was uncertain, the NP would have
   pulled the physician in.
5. **Handoff back to the front desk.** The NP documents the
   visit; the MA checks the patient out and schedules follow-up;
   patient access handles any billing questions. The loop closes
   with the non-clinical roles again [IPEC-Core].

**Answer:** Four roles, four different scopes, one routine visit
— and the physician never needed to touch it.

**Why it matters:** A student who assumes the physician sees
every patient misreads how modern primary care runs. The
**advanced practice provider** role plus a well-trained MA team
is what allows the clinic to see the volume of patients it does,
with the physician reserved for complex cases.
