# Common misconceptions — healthcare-team-roles

## Misconception 1 — The physician is in charge of everything on the team

**What students often think:**
The doctor runs the team, gives orders, and everyone else just
executes what the doctor decides. If the doctor is the smartest
person in the room, more of the decisions should sit with them.

**What's actually true:**
The physician writes the medical plan and holds broad scope, but
the team does not work as a chain of command with the physician
on top [IPEC-Core]. Each licensed clinician has independent
accountability inside their own **scope of practice**. The
pharmacist is the decision-maker on drug-interaction safety;
the RN is the decision-maker on minute-to-minute patient
response and whether the plan is still appropriate; the
respiratory therapist is the decision-maker on ventilator
settings within the ordered parameters; the social worker owns
the discharge safety plan. A physician who overrides those
clinicians outside their own expertise undermines the cross-checks
that keep the patient safe [IOM-QualityChasm, AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS].
Team-based care works because responsibility is distributed.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Medical TV shows feature one heroic doctor making every call,
and the legal term "physician order" sounds like a military
command. Students map that onto a command hierarchy even though
the working team is structured more as a set of overlapping
accountabilities.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"The physician writes an order. Name two team members
whose independent clinical judgment could stop that order from
reaching the patient, and why."* A student with the correct
model names the pharmacist (interaction or dosing check) and
the RN (clinical status check on administration), and explains
that each is doing the job their license requires, not
second-guessing. A student without it says "no one — the doctor
ordered it."

## Misconception 2 — Everyone in scrubs is basically a nurse

**What students often think:**
The hospital is staffed by "nurses" plus a few doctors. CNAs,
medical assistants, techs, and RNs all do roughly the same
job — nursing — at different levels.

**What's actually true:**
The nursing ladder (**CNA** → LPN → **RN** → BSN → MSN → NP) is
one of several role families on the team, and the rungs inside
it have sharply different scopes [NCSBN-Delegation, BLS-OOH].
A CNA is **certified**, not **licensed**, and cannot administer
medications or do a nursing assessment. An LPN is licensed but
has narrower scope than an RN. An RN administers medications,
assesses patients, and **delegates** tasks downward. Separately,
a medical assistant is neither a nurse nor on the nursing
ladder at all — they work in clinics under physician
supervision, with a scope defined by that clinic and state law.
Respiratory therapists, radiology techs, lab scientists, and
pharmacists are all in scrubs too, and none of them are nurses.
Calling everyone "nurse" erases the scope differences that
determine what each person can safely do.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Hospitals dress many roles in similar scrubs, and the word
"nurse" is used loosely in everyday speech ("ask the nurse for
a blanket") to mean almost anyone at the bedside. The
distinction only becomes visible when a task hits the edge of
someone's scope.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A patient needs IV morphine pushed right now. Which team
member can push the drug, and why not the others on the unit?"*
A student with the correct model identifies the RN and explains
that a CNA has a narrower scope (no medications), an MA is not
on the nursing ladder, and an LPN often cannot push certain IV
drugs depending on state rules. A student without it treats any
of the above as interchangeable.

## Misconception 3 — Support staff don't really affect patient outcomes

**What students often think:**
Environmental services, food service, transport, unit clerks,
and coders are "not clinical" and therefore not part of patient
care. They are overhead, not team members.

**What's actually true:**
Support and administrative roles shape outcomes as directly as
clinical ones, even though they do not touch the medical
decision [IOM-QualityChasm]. A delayed bed-turn extends an
emergency-department hold and worsens outcomes for everyone
waiting. A patient who gets the wrong tray for their diabetic
or cardiac diet receives a real clinical harm. A coder who
enters an allergy wrong can cascade into a drug event. A
transporter who brings the wrong patient to imaging creates a
near-miss that the radiology tech has to catch. The modern
quality-and-safety frame treats the whole team — clinical and
non-clinical — as producing the outcome together
[AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS]. Excluding support roles from the "team"
picture makes the safety model fail at exactly the points where
most errors actually happen.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Training content focuses on clinical decisions because those are
the glamor of medicine. Support roles are invisible in the
curriculum and in media, so students infer they are peripheral
to outcomes.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"Name a non-clinical team role and describe one way a
mistake in that role could cause patient harm."* A student with
the correct model names transport, dietary, EVS, coding, or unit
clerking and traces a specific cascade to a clinical outcome. A
student without it defaults to "support doesn't really affect
care."

## Misconception 4 — A handoff is just telling the next person what happened

**What students often think:**
When a shift changes, the outgoing clinician tells the incoming
one what happened with the patient. How it is said does not
matter as long as the facts are conveyed.

**What's actually true:**
Unstructured handoffs are one of the largest sources of
preventable error in healthcare, because without a structure
each handoff drops something different and important
[AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS]. A **handoff** is a formal transfer of
responsibility, and using a standardized format — most commonly
**SBAR** (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) —
measurably reduces missed information and patient-safety events
[AHRQ-TeamSTEPPS]. The format forces the outgoing clinician to
state not just what happened but what they think is going on
(assessment) and what they recommend the incoming clinician do
next (recommendation). Teams that freelance the handoff lose
those last two pieces most often, and those are the pieces the
incoming clinician needs most.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
In casual life, "catching someone up" is a loose conversation
and it works fine. Students import that intuition and don't see
why handoffs would need a template. The risk only surfaces when
the template is missing and something slips.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"Your shift ends in ten minutes. Give a handoff for a
patient admitted last night with chest pain, now stable after
nitroglycerin, awaiting a stress test in the morning. Use
SBAR."* A student with the correct model produces four
recognizable segments — situation (patient and current status),
background (admission reason and recent course), assessment
(what they think is going on), recommendation (what to watch
and what to do). A student without it narrates events in the
order they happened without the assessment and recommendation
pieces.
