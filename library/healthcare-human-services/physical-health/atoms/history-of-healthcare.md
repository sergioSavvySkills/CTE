## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify major eras and turning points in healthcare history and explain how each shaped current practice.

## Content stub

- **Ancient and pre-modern:** Hippocratic tradition (~400 BCE), Galen's humors, Islamic Golden Age hospitals, European guild medicine, traditional healing systems across cultures
- **Scientific revolution (1500s-1800s):** anatomy (Vesalius), circulation (Harvey), germ theory (Pasteur, Koch), antisepsis (Semmelweis, Lister), nursing reform (Nightingale)
- **Modern medicine (1900s):** antibiotics (penicillin 1928), vaccines (polio 1955, smallpox eradication 1980), imaging (X-ray 1895, MRI 1970s), organ transplant, ICUs
- **Public health wins:** clean water and sanitation, vaccination programs, tobacco control, seatbelt laws, HIV treatment — often more lives saved than any single clinical advance
- **Policy and access:** Medicare/Medicaid (1965), EMTALA (1986), HIPAA (1996), ACA (2010) — access and coverage shape who benefits from advances
- **Recent turning points:** human genome (2003), mRNA vaccines (COVID-19), AI/ML in diagnosis, telehealth expansion, ongoing health-equity reckoning

## Assessment stub

- Timeline: build a timeline of 12 healthcare milestones with date, person/event, and impact in one sentence each
- Compare: pick two eras and compare what "good care" meant in each, including who had access and who did not
- Short essay: choose one advance (e.g., germ theory, mRNA vaccines) and explain how practice today would differ without it
