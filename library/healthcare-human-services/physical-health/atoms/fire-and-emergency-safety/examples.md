# Worked examples — fire-and-emergency-safety

## Example 1 — Small trash-can fire in an oxygen room

**Scenario:** Mrs. Ramirez is on two liters of nasal-cannula oxygen
in a private room. A discarded electric heating pad has ignited a
small, contained fire in the bedside trash can. Her nurse, Jordan, is
in the room. A PASS-ready ABC extinguisher is on the wall outside
the door.

**What to work out:** What order do the RACE steps happen in, what
specifically is done about the oxygen, and is this an Extinguish or
an Evacuate situation?

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Rescue first.** Jordan unclips the oxygen tubing from the wall,
   rolls Mrs. Ramirez in her bed out of the room, and leaves her in
   the hallway with a colleague [TJC-EM].
2. **Alarm second.** Jordan pulls the **pull station** at the end of
   the hall and dials the operator to announce a **Code Red** with
   the room number.
3. **Contain third.** Jordan closes the door to the room, cutting
   off the flow of air to the fire and holding smoke back. A closed
   rated door buys twenty minutes or more [NFPA-101]. In the same
   motion, Jordan shuts the zone **oxygen shutoff valve** for that
   wing so any remaining tubing does not feed the fire
   [NFPA-HealthcareFire].
4. **Extinguish or Evacuate — decide.** The fire is still contained
   to the trash can and Jordan has a clear exit. Jordan grabs the
   extinguisher and runs PASS: **P**ull the pin, **A**im at the base
   of the fire (not the smoke above it), **S**queeze the handle,
   **S**weep side-to-side [OSHA-EAP].
5. After the flames are out, Jordan watches for re-ignition and
   waits for the responding fire crew to verify the room.

**Answer:** RACE in order — Rescue, Alarm, Contain (including shutting
the oxygen), Extinguish with PASS because the fire is small and the
path is clear.

**Why it matters:** Every step above is an act that prevents the next
escalation. Rescue protects the patient from smoke. Alarm gets help
moving. Contain and oxygen-off starve the fire. PASS is the last
step, not the first, because a worker who aims a small extinguisher
at a growing fire without the first three steps ends up in a smoke-
filled room with a 15-second tool.

## Example 2 — Large hallway fire, wrong side of the door

**Scenario:** A laundry cart in the hallway has caught fire outside
Mr. Okafor's room. The flames are waist-high, the smoke is thickening,
and the fire is between Mr. Okafor's door and the nearest stairwell.
The opposite direction leads to a **smoke compartment** door thirty
feet away.

**What to work out:** Is this an Extinguish or an Evacuate situation,
and if evacuate, in what direction?

**Step-by-step:**

1. This is not an Extinguish situation. The fire is larger than a
   trash can, it is between you and one escape path, and the smoke is
   growing. A portable extinguisher discharges in ten to twenty
   seconds, which is not enough for a waist-high hallway fire
   [OSHA-EAP].
2. Run RACE, but expect the E to be Evacuate.
3. **Rescue.** Move Mr. Okafor and any other ambulatory patients
   nearby *away* from the fire, toward the smoke-compartment door in
   the opposite direction. Ambulatory patients go first; they can
   walk and they free up staff [NFPA-101].
4. **Alarm.** Pull the nearest **pull station** on the way and have
   a colleague call **Code Red** with the location.
5. **Contain.** Close every door between the fire and the retreating
   group, including the smoke-compartment door once everyone is
   through. That closed barrier is rated to hold the smoke and heat
   for the minutes the suppression system and fire crew need
   [NFPA-101].
6. The strategy is **horizontal evacuation** — sideways to the next
   compartment, same floor — not down the stairs. **Vertical
   evacuation** is only considered if the new compartment also
   becomes threatened and a direct download path is safe.

**Answer:** Evacuate horizontally to the next smoke compartment, in
the direction away from the fire; do not attempt to extinguish and do
not use the stairwell unless forced to.

**Why it matters:** The biggest error in a hallway fire is a worker
pointing a small extinguisher at a fire they cannot realistically put
out, while patients wait behind doors that nobody has closed.
Horizontal evacuation to the next compartment is the engineered
safe move and the one **defend-in-place** strategy is built for.

## Example 3 — Overhead Code Blue on the unit

**Scenario:** You are charting at the nursing station when the
overhead speaker announces, "Code Blue, Room 412." You are a new aide
on Unit 4. Room 412 is across the hall.

**What to work out:** What does this **emergency code** mean, and
what is your initial response as an aide?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Recognize the code. **Code Blue** in most U.S. hospitals is a
   cardiac or respiratory arrest — someone has stopped breathing or
   their heart has stopped, and the resuscitation team is converging
   on the room [TJC-EM].
2. This is not a RACE situation. RACE is for a fire (Code Red).
   Different code, different first response.
3. Your job as a unit aide is usually to clear the path. Move
   visitors and extra equipment out of the hallway. Make sure the
   crash cart and the code team have an unobstructed route to Room
   412.
4. Do not self-assign to the bedside unless the team needs a specific
   set of hands and your facility's policy allows it. Your patients
   on the unit still need monitoring; someone has to be watching them.
5. Be ready to help with documentation or runner tasks if asked.

**Answer:** Clear the hallway, keep the team's path open, and continue
watching the rest of the unit. Do not go into the code room unless
specifically directed.

**Why it matters:** Knowing the code by sound and knowing your
specific role as a non-responder is a piece of the atom's second
learning objective — identifying common emergency codes. A student
who thinks every overhead code means "run toward it" creates chaos at
the scene and abandons the rest of the unit.

## Example 4 — Filing an incident report after a near-miss

**Scenario:** During a routine walk-through, you notice a fire
extinguisher mounted in the main hallway with its inspection tag
missing and its pin slightly bent. You wiggle the pin and it does
not come out smoothly. There has been no fire.

**What to work out:** Does this need an **incident report**, and what
should the report include?

**Step-by-step:**

1. A missing inspection tag and a stuck pin together mean the
   extinguisher may not function in a real fire. This is a near-miss:
   the event that would have caused harm did not happen, but the
   conditions that would have caused it are present [CMS-EmergencyPrep].
2. Take it out of service. Report it immediately to your charge
   nurse, to maintenance, or through whichever channel your facility
   uses for equipment concerns. Replace or flag the unit so a
   colleague does not grab it during a real event.
3. File the incident report. Document **what** (missing tag, bent
   pin, pin stuck), **when** (date and time discovered), **where**
   (the specific hallway or room), **who** (you, and whoever you
   notified), and **what was done** (took out of service, notified
   maintenance).
4. Do not worry that the report will punish the last person who
   inspected the extinguisher — that is not its purpose. Reports are
   how the facility finds the *pattern* — an inspection schedule
   missed, a vendor not performing, a training gap
   [CMS-EmergencyPrep, TJC-EM].

**Answer:** Yes, file a near-miss incident report, and take the
extinguisher out of service immediately while maintenance responds.

**Why it matters:** The extinguisher the team reaches for during
Example 1 is only trustworthy because someone, at some point, filed
reports like this. A reporting culture that treats near-misses as
valuable data is what prevents the actual emergency from turning into
the one nobody was prepared for.
