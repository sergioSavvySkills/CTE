# Common misconceptions — fire-and-emergency-safety

## Misconception 1 — RACE is a menu, not an order

**What students often think:**
RACE lists the things you might do in a fire, and a smart worker picks
whichever one fits the situation — maybe extinguish first, maybe pull
the alarm, depending.

**What's actually true:**
RACE is a fixed sequence: **R**escue, **A**larm, **C**ontain,
**E**xtinguish or Evacuate, executed in that order [TJC-EM]. Each
step protects the next. Rescue happens before the alarm so the person
in immediate danger is not left for thirty seconds while you run to a
**pull station**. Alarm happens before containment so help is moving
toward the fire before the door closes. Containment happens before
extinguishment so the fire cannot grow behind you while you work
[OSHA-EAP]. Skipping steps or re-ordering them is how a small fire
becomes a big one.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Each letter of RACE is an action that sounds independently reasonable.
Students who memorize the letters without the "in order" instruction
treat them as a checklist of options instead of a protocol.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A small wastebasket fire starts in a patient room. You have an
extinguisher in your hand. What do you do first?"* A student with the
correct model moves the patient out of the room first, then pulls the
alarm, then closes the door, and only then considers the extinguisher.
A student without it skips to Extinguish because the tool is already
in hand.

## Misconception 2 — "Fire in a hospital" means evacuate the building

**What students often think:**
If there is a fire anywhere in the hospital, everyone needs to get
out as fast as possible, the way you would evacuate a school or an
office.

**What's actually true:**
Healthcare buildings are designed around **defend-in-place**. Patients
are moved only as far as the next safe **smoke compartment** on the
same floor, and most are not moved at all [NFPA-101]. Full-building
evacuations are rare because most patients cannot walk out and
because the building's fire-rated walls, doors, and sprinkler system
are built to contain the fire long enough for suppression crews to
respond [NFPA-HealthcareFire]. **Horizontal evacuation** is always
tried before **vertical evacuation**, and elevators are not used at
all during a fire.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Every fire drill a student has done since kindergarten was a get-out-
of-the-building drill. The healthcare strategy is counter-intuitive
until you picture a unit full of bedbound patients on IV lines.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A Code Red is called for a fire in the kitchen on the first
floor. You are on the fourth floor with twelve bedbound patients. Do
you start evacuating the unit to the stairwell?"* A student with the
correct model says no — they close doors, verify their unit is
contained, and wait for instructions, because defend-in-place and
horizontal evacuation come first.

## Misconception 3 — The five codes I memorized are the same everywhere

**What students often think:**
Code Red is fire, Code Blue is cardiac arrest, Code Silver is
active shooter — those are the codes, full stop.

**What's actually true:**
The five commonly taught **emergency code** phrases are *common*, not
*universal*. Different facilities use different colors, some use
plain-language announcements, and the same color can mean different
things in different systems [TJC-EM]. National efforts have pushed
toward standardization, but enough variation remains that assuming is
dangerous. The practical rule is to learn your facility's code list
on your first day and confirm the meanings — the posted card near the
nursing station is authoritative, not the memory from your CTE class.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Teachers drill the five-code list because students need *some* set to
think with, and the list becomes the mental model. The "varies by
facility" footnote is the first thing forgotten.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"You start a new job in a hospital across town. You hear a
'Code Orange' announced — a color you have never learned. What do you
do?"* A student with the correct model looks at the posted code
reference, asks a colleague, or calls the operator. A student without
it guesses based on color logic.

## Misconception 4 — Small incidents and near-misses don't need a report

**What students often think:**
If a small fire was put out or nobody got hurt, there's no point
filing paperwork about it — incident reports are for real emergencies.

**What's actually true:**
Near-misses and small events are exactly the data safety programs
need [CMS-EmergencyPrep]. A missing extinguisher pin, an alarm that
did not sound, a door that was propped open, a trash-can fire that
was stamped out — each one is a pattern the facility can fix before
the pattern produces a larger event. The **incident report** is a
system-improvement tool, not a blame form, and accreditation bodies
require that facilities collect and act on it [TJC-EM]. Under-
reporting is one of the most damaging habits in healthcare safety
because it hides the small signals that would predict the next big
one.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Staff often associate "reports" with getting written up. In cultures
where reporting has historically been used for discipline, workers
learn to minimize small events. Teaching reporting as a safety-
improvement tool — not a punishment channel — is a deliberate culture
change.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"You notice a fire extinguisher in the hallway with the
inspection tag missing, but there has not been a fire. Do you file
anything?"* A student with the correct model files a near-miss or
maintenance incident report and explains that the goal is to fix the
pattern, not catch someone out.
