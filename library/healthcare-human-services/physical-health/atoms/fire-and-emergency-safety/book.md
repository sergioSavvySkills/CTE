# Fire and Emergency Safety in Healthcare Settings

## Why this matters

A hospital room is the worst place in America to run from a fire.
Half the people in it are connected to something — oxygen, a drip,
a monitor, a catheter — and the other half cannot walk. Most will
never make it out of the building under their own power, and they
will not have to, because healthcare buildings are engineered to let
staff move patients sideways to safety instead of down and out
[NFPA-101]. That engineering only works if the people on the floor
know what to do in the first ninety seconds. This atom teaches the
two responses a healthcare worker is expected to execute on reflex:
the fire protocol known as **RACE**, and the portable-extinguisher
procedure known as **PASS**. It also teaches a small handful of
**emergency code** phrases that will come over the overhead speaker
before you can see the emergency yourself [TJC-EM].

Most healthcare fires never become the disaster a layperson pictures.
Cooking fires in kitchens, electrical fires in equipment rooms, and
smoldering trash fires in patient areas are the common ones, and
almost all are contained early by a staff member who did the first
few steps right [NFPA-HealthcareFire]. The skill is not heroism. It
is a sequence.

## What you should already know

You should know that healthcare workplaces run on routines that get
drilled, not improvised. You should know what a wall-mounted fire
alarm **pull station** looks like and that there is usually one near
every stairwell. You should have a rough sense that patients vary in
how much they can move — some walk, some roll in a wheelchair, some
cannot leave the bed. You do not need any chemistry or fire-science
background; the atom stays at the worker-response level.

## Core concept

### RACE — the four-step fire response

The letters stand for **R**escue, **A**larm, **C**ontain,
**E**xtinguish or Evacuate, and they happen in that order
[TJC-EM, OSHA-EAP].

- **Rescue.** Get anyone in immediate danger out of the fire room.
  If a patient is in the bed where the flames are, that patient
  comes first. "Immediate danger" is narrow — the person inside the
  burning room, not everyone on the unit.
- **Alarm.** Activate the **pull station** on the wall, then call
  the facility's fire code (in most U.S. hospitals, that is an
  overhead **Code Red** page plus a phone call to the operator or
  switchboard). The pull station tells the building; the code tells
  the rest of the staff [NFPA-101].
- **Contain.** Close the door of the fire room. Close any doors
  between the fire and the rest of the unit. Doors in healthcare
  buildings are rated to hold back smoke and heat for twenty minutes
  or more [NFPA-101]. A closed door is the most powerful fire tool
  a worker has and the one most often forgotten.
- **Extinguish or Evacuate.** If the fire is small — roughly the
  size of a trash can — and you have a clear escape path, you may
  use a portable extinguisher. If it is larger, or if it is between
  you and the door, or if the room is filling with smoke, you
  evacuate and leave suppression to the fire department
  [OSHA-EAP].

The order matters. Rescue-before-alarm protects the person in
immediate danger; alarm-before-contain gets help moving; contain-
before-extinguish makes sure the fire cannot grow behind you while
you work.

### PASS — using a portable extinguisher

A worker who has decided to fight a small fire runs **PASS**
[OSHA-EAP]:

- **Pull** the pin at the top of the extinguisher handle.
- **Aim** the nozzle at the *base* of the fire, not at the flames
  themselves. Flames are the visible burn; the fuel is what you
  cool and smother.
- **Squeeze** the handle to discharge the agent.
- **Sweep** side-to-side across the base of the fire until it is
  out, then watch for re-ignition.

Most modern extinguishers in healthcare settings are labeled "ABC,"
which means they work on ordinary combustibles, flammable liquids,
and energized electrical equipment. You do not need to know the
chemistry beyond that label. You do need to know that a typical
unit discharges for only ten to twenty seconds, which is why a
worker who decides to fight a fire commits quickly and gives up
quickly — if the flames are not visibly shrinking within the first
few seconds of a good aim, the right move is to back out, close the
door, and leave the fire to the suppression system and the
responding crew [OSHA-EAP].

### Oxygen and fire — a healthcare-specific hazard

Medical oxygen does not burn, but it makes everything around it burn
faster and hotter [NFPA-HealthcareFire]. A material that would
smolder in room air can flash in an oxygen-enriched atmosphere.
Three habits follow from this.

- **Shut the source.** In any fire near a patient on oxygen, a
  staff member shuts the flow at the wall or finds the zone
  **oxygen shutoff valve** for the area. Facilities post the
  shutoff valves at staff-visible locations, and it is a pre-fire
  job — not a during-fire job — to know where they are.
- **Keep heat and spark away.** Nothing with a flame or a high-
  temperature element belongs near oxygen tubing. That includes
  candles, electric warming devices, and electrostatic-discharge
  sources like untreated synthetic blankets in extreme cases.
- **Route tubing away from traffic.** Long oxygen tubing across a
  floor is both a trip hazard and a fire accelerant if it
  contacts a heat source.

The oxygen step is often what makes a healthcare fire different from
a house fire in the student's mental model. Shutting the gas
supply is part of RACE's Contain step even though the letter C
does not spell it out.

### Emergency codes — the overhead language

Most U.S. hospitals use short color-word phrases so that staff can
be alerted over the intercom without panicking patients and
visitors. A typical set looks like this:

- **Code Red** — active fire or reported smoke
- **Code Blue** — cardiac or respiratory arrest, resuscitation
  team to the location
- **Code Gray** — combative or threatening person, not armed
- **Code Pink** — missing or abducted infant or child
- **Code Silver** — active shooter or weapon threat

These five are the most commonly announced ones, and a healthcare
student is expected to recognize them on hearing [TJC-EM]. The
critical caveat is that **code names are not universal**. A
hospital on the other side of your city may use different colors
or use plain language ("Attention: fire alarm, fourth floor east").
National efforts have pushed toward standardization, but variation
remains real. The rule is: when you start at a new facility, learn
*that* facility's codes on day one. Do not assume the colors you
memorized in school match the board in the break room.

Initial response to each code is also short and standard. On a
**Code Red**, run RACE. On a Code Blue, clear the hallway and let
the response team through. On a Code Gray, move away and secure
patients per your facility's workplace-violence plan. On a Code
Pink, watch exits and report anyone leaving with an infant who
does not belong. On a Code Silver, use the federal **Run, Hide,
Fight** framing — get away if you can, hide behind a lockable barrier
if you cannot, fight only if you have no other option
[Ready-RunHideFight]. Deeper tactical training belongs in a
security-specific course.

### Evacuation — horizontal before vertical

Healthcare buildings are designed around **smoke compartments** —
sections separated by rated doors and barriers that hold smoke back
for a defined time [NFPA-101]. Because of that engineering, the
default healthcare strategy is **defend-in-place**: move patients
only as far as the next safe smoke compartment and wait for the
fire department. Full-building evacuations are rare and carry real
risks for fragile patients.

When movement is needed, staff use two principles in sequence. The
first is the priority order: ambulatory patients first (they move
themselves and free up staff), then wheelchair patients, then
bedbound patients, who require the most effort and equipment. The
second is the direction: **horizontal evacuation** first — through
the nearest smoke-compartment door — and only **vertical
evacuation** down stairs if horizontal is no longer safe. Stairwells
are slow, crowded, and difficult for non-ambulatory patients.
Elevators are not used during a fire because elevator shafts act
like chimneys and elevator recall systems route cars to the ground
floor automatically [NFPA-101].

### Incident reporting — why near-misses count too

After any fire, near-fire, or emergency-code activation, staff
complete an **incident report** describing what happened, when, who
was there, and what was done [CMS-EmergencyPrep]. Near-misses — the
extinguisher that was missing its pin, the alarm that did not sound,
the door that was propped open — are reported on the same form.

The purpose of the report is *system improvement*, not discipline.
Reports that would punish an individual worker discourage reporting,
which in turn hides the patterns that would prevent the next event
[CMS-EmergencyPrep]. Joint Commission accreditation and CMS
participation both require that facilities collect and learn from
these reports, and strong facilities treat a well-written near-miss
report as evidence of safety culture, not evidence of failure.

## Worked through an example

A patient on two liters of nasal-cannula oxygen notices her bedside
trash can smoking from a carelessly discarded electric heating pad.
Her nurse is in the room. The question is not "what should the
nurse do?" in general — it is which steps of RACE happen first,
what gets done about the oxygen, and whether this is an extinguish
or an evacuate situation. `examples.md` walks this case through
step by step, plus three more (a large hallway fire, an overhead
Code Blue on a unit, and an incident report after a near-miss).

## Common confusions

Students often treat RACE's letters as options rather than an order;
think that "the building is on fire" always means "get everyone
outside"; and assume the five common emergency codes are the same
everywhere. Full corrections, with diagnostic probes, live in
`misconceptions.md`.
