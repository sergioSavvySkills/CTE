# Common Medical Abbreviations and Safe Documentation

## Why this matters

A nurse reads an order at 3 a.m. It says *give 4U insulin stat*. The nurse
reads the *U* as a zero and gives 40 units. Forty units is roughly ten
times the intended dose of a rapid-acting insulin; the patient's blood
glucose crashes, the rapid-response team is called, and a preventable
event becomes a line in an incident report. That exact case — the letter
U misread as a zero — is the reason *U* is the very first entry on The
Joint Commission's banned **do-not-use list** [TJC-DoNotUse]. Every
abbreviation you will meet in this atom sits somewhere on the same
spectrum: helpful shorthand on one end, a named cause of a **medication
error** on the other [AHRQ-Errors].

Healthcare workers write fast because the workload demands it. Shorthand
saves seconds on every order, every chart note, and every handoff. But
speed without discipline is the most common upstream cause of dose and
route errors in U.S. hospitals [ISMP-ErrorProne]. This atom teaches two
habits: recognize the shorthand you will see every day, and *avoid* the
short list of forms that keep showing up in patient-harm cases.

## What you should already know

You should have worked through *Medical terminology word parts* before
this atom. That prior work teaches the root-prefix-suffix system behind
words like *cardiomyopathy*. This atom is different: the focus is on
**abbreviations** — compact symbols whose meaning depends on the
institution's approved abbreviation list, not on word-part rules
[TJC-NPSG]. If you can already decode a long medical term, you are ready
to decode the short symbols that show up on top of it.

## Core concept

### Shorthand categories you will see every shift

Medical charts cluster their abbreviations into a handful of functional
groups. You do not need to memorize every abbreviation ever used; you
need to recognize which *kind* of information each slot on an order is
carrying and where to look up the ones you are unsure about
[ISMP-ErrorProne].

#### Frequency and timing

When is the dose given? Orders use a small set of Latin-derived
frequency codes. *Bid* (twice a day), *tid* (three times a day), and
*qid* (four times a day) cover routine dosing. *qh* means every hour.
*ac* is before meals and *pc* is after. *hs* is at bedtime. *prn* means
*pro re nata*, "as needed" — the drug is given only when a documented
criterion is met, such as a pain score above a threshold. **stat**, from
Latin *statim*, means *immediately*; a stat order jumps the queue and
must be given now, not at the next scheduled round. Two common frequency
codes — *q* alone (meaning *every*) and *qd* (once a day) — show up on
older charts and are banned in new orders for safety reasons covered
below [TJC-DoNotUse].

#### Routes and sites

How does the dose enter the body? *PO* (per os) means by mouth; *IV* means
intravenous; *IM* means intramuscular; *SC* (or *subQ*) means subcutaneous;
*SL* means sublingual; *PR* means per rectum. Eye and ear routes use
initials that students sometimes invert: *OD*, *OS*, *OU* name the right
eye, left eye, and both eyes; *AD*, *AS*, *AU* name the right ear, left
ear, and both. Getting a route wrong can turn an injection into an oral
dose and an oral dose into an IV push, with very different absorption and
very different risk [ISMP-ErrorProne]. The eye/ear letter pairs are
error-prone enough that many facilities spell them out in full.

#### Body values and patient status

Some abbreviations record observations rather than orders. *BP*, *HR*,
*RR*, *T*, and *Sats* are the vital signs (blood pressure, heart rate,
respiratory rate, temperature, and oxygen saturation). *WNL* means
within normal limits; *NAD* means no acute distress. **NKDA** documents
that a patient has reported *no known drug allergies*. **NPO** (nil per
os) means the patient takes nothing by mouth — usually before surgery or
certain imaging. **DNR** documents a physician-signed decision not to
attempt CPR if the patient arrests. These four — NKDA, NPO, DNR, and the
vital-sign set — are the shorthand you will see in every chart, every
day [TJC-NPSG].

#### Orders and procedures

Chart acronyms for the most common tests and visits save space. *ABG*
(arterial blood gas), *CBC* (complete blood count), and *BMP* (basic
metabolic panel) are the labs you will see drawn every shift; *CXR*
(chest X-ray), *EKG* or *ECG* (electrocardiogram), *MRI*, and *CT* name
the imaging studies. *H&P* is the history and physical admitted with a
new patient; *D/C* is discharge (or sometimes *discontinue*, context
decides); *ADM* is admission. You are not expected to interpret these
studies yet; you are expected to recognize them when you read an order
and to ask the right question — *which D/C does this chart mean?* —
when context is thin [ISMP-ErrorProne].

### The do-not-use list

The Joint Commission — the body that accredits most U.S. hospitals —
publishes a short, official **do-not-use list** of abbreviations and
dose forms that must not appear in patient records [TJC-DoNotUse]. The
list is minimum, not maximum; many facilities ban more. Each entry is
on the list because a real patient was harmed by that exact form.

- **U or u** for "unit" has been misread as a zero, a 4, or "cc,"
  producing ten-fold insulin overdoses. Write *unit* in full.
- **IU** for "international unit" has been misread as *IV* (intravenous)
  and as the number *10*. Write *international unit* in full.
- **QD** (once daily) and **QOD** (every other day) are easily confused,
  and the period after Q can look like an *I*. Write *daily* and *every
  other day* in full.
- **Trailing zero** (writing *1.0 mg*) — the decimal can be missed and
  the dose read as 10 mg. Write *1 mg*.
- **Missing leading zero** (writing *.5 mg*) — the decimal can be missed
  and the dose read as 5 mg. Write *0.5 mg*. Together with the
  trailing-zero rule this is called **decimal discipline** [FDA-SafeDose].
- **MS**, **MSO4**, and **MgSO4** — *MS* is ambiguous between morphine
  sulfate and magnesium sulfate, and MSO4 / MgSO4 are easy to swap.
  Both are high-alert drugs with very different actions. Write the drug
  name in full [ISMP-HighAlert].

**ISMP** — the Institute for Safe Medication Practices — maintains a
longer error-prone list beyond the Joint Commission minimum. It adds
items like *cc* (misread as *U*), *µg* (microgram, misread as *mg*),
slash marks between drug names, and trailing dosage expressions like
*qhs*. Treat the ISMP list as the standard your facility will probably
adopt, even if only the Joint Commission subset is strictly required
[ISMP-ErrorProne].

### Facility variation — the approved abbreviation list

A shorthand that is safe at one hospital may be banned at another. Every
accredited facility keeps a written **approved abbreviation list** that
names which forms are acceptable in its records, and audits charts
against it [TJC-NPSG]. When you begin a rotation or a new job, one of
your first documentation tasks is to find that list. Do not assume that
abbreviations you learned in class, saw on a different floor, or used at
a former employer are acceptable on the chart in front of you.

### The habit this atom is really teaching

The content above is large, but the habit is small. Three moves:

1. **Recognize.** When you read a chart, name the category — frequency,
   route, status, procedure — of every abbreviation you see. If you
   cannot name the category, you cannot safely execute the order.
2. **Look up.** When you do not recognize an abbreviation, stop and
   check the facility's approved abbreviation list or ask the prescriber.
   Never guess on a dose.
3. **Avoid the banned forms.** Never write a do-not-use entry in a new
   order or chart note — not even informally. Rewrite the moment you
   notice one on an order you are about to carry out.

## Worked through an example

Consider a written order: *Morphine 4U IV q4h prn pain*. Three separate
problems sit inside one line: the *U* after the number should be spelled
as *unit*; the drug name should not be abbreviated as MS or MSO4 (it is
here written in full, which is correct); and the frequency and route
are acceptable, but a careful reader should still confirm the pain
threshold. Four worked examples — translating orders, spotting
violations, rewriting dangerous forms, and checking a facility list —
live in `examples.md`.

## Common confusions

Students often treat abbreviations as a vocabulary list to memorize and
then stop. That move misses the point. The do-not-use list is not a
trivia quiz; it is a pattern of *shapes* (single letters, trailing
zeros, look-alike acronyms) that cause harm. Students also assume that
what was taught in class is what the hospital uses, and that the older
a chart looks, the safer its shorthand. Full corrections live in
`misconceptions.md`.
