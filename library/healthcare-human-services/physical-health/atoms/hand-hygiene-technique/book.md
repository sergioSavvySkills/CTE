# Hand Hygiene Technique and the Five Moments

## Why this matters

A nurse finishes adjusting an IV pump, walks two steps to the next bed,
and lowers the side rail to check on a sleeping patient. It took four
seconds. In those four seconds she carried whatever was on the first
patient's skin, on the pump keypad, and on the rail of the first bed
onto the rail of the second bed and onto her own hands. If she does
not clean her hands before the next task, the second patient is now
exposed to the first patient's flora. Multiply that by every worker,
every room, every shift, and you have the mechanism of a
**healthcare-associated infection** outbreak [WHO-HandHygiene].

Hands transmit most healthcare-associated infections, and **hand
hygiene** is the single most effective measure for preventing them
[WHO-HandHygiene, CDC-HandHygiene]. That is why this skill — not
antibiotics, not isolation gowns, not ultraviolet light — sits at the
top of every accreditation checklist in the country [TJC-NPSG-07].
The whole job is two things: know *when* to clean your hands, and
know *how*.

## What you should already know

You should know the idea of standard precautions and the chain of
infection at the level of the prerequisite atom — that microorganisms
move from a source, through a route of transmission, to a susceptible
host. You do not need microbiology beyond that. You should also know
what a sink, a paper-towel dispenser, and a wall-mounted
alcohol-based-hand-rub dispenser look like in a clinical setting, and
that gloves are not a substitute for cleaning your hands.

## Core concept

### The two techniques

Healthcare workers use two techniques, and knowing which one the
situation requires is half the skill.

The first is the **alcohol-based hand rub**, often abbreviated
**ABHR**. It is the default for most moments of care [CDC-HandHygiene].
You apply enough gel, foam, or liquid to cover every surface of both
hands — palms, backs, between the fingers, thumbs, fingertips, and
the wrists — then rub for about 20 seconds until the product is fully
dry. You do not wipe it off on your scrubs or a paper towel; the
drying *is* the process, because the alcohol kills organisms while the
product is wet. ABHR works because its 60–95% alcohol content
disrupts the membranes and proteins of most bacteria and viruses on
the skin [WHO-HandHygiene]. It is faster than washing, is less drying
when used with built-in emollients, and can be placed at every
bedside, which is why most hospitals now use it as the primary method
between patients [CDC-HandHygiene].

The second is the **soap-and-water handwash**. You wet your hands
under running water, apply soap, and lather while scrubbing every
surface for at least 20 seconds — including under the nails and
around the thumbs, which are the most commonly missed areas. Rinse
under running water with your fingertips pointed down, dry with a
single-use paper towel, and use the same towel to turn off the tap
so you do not re-contaminate your clean hands on the handle
[WHO-HandHygiene]. Twenty seconds is roughly the time it takes to
sing "Happy Birthday" through twice; staff are taught a specific cue
because most people drastically underestimate how long 20 seconds is.

### When each technique is required

ABHR is the right choice for most moments, but there are three
situations when **soap-and-water handwash** is mandatory instead
[CDC-HandHygiene, CDC-CDiff]:

1. Hands are **visibly soiled** — with blood, body fluid, dirt, or
   food. Alcohol does not clean; it only disinfects. Visible soil has
   to be mechanically removed with water and surfactant.
2. After caring for a patient with **Clostridioides difficile**
   (*C. diff*) or with **norovirus**. These organisms form a **spore**
   or a protein-shell structure that alcohol does not kill. Only the
   mechanical friction of a proper handwash physically removes the
   **spore** from the skin [CDC-CDiff].
3. After using the restroom, which the CDC treats as a hand-hygiene
   indication in its own right because of fecal-oral organisms of
   the same resistance class.

A mental rule: if there is any chance the organism is alcohol-resistant
or the hands are soiled, it is a soap-and-water moment. Everything
else is an **ABHR** moment.

### The Five Moments — when to clean your hands

Technique answers *how*. The **Five Moments** answer *when*
[WHO-FiveMoments]. The World Health Organization organizes hand
hygiene around five indications that are defined by what you are
about to do or what you just did, not by where you happen to be in
the room. Learn them as a story of a single patient encounter.

**Moment 1 — Before patient contact.** You clean your hands right
before you touch the patient. The reason is the patient zone: you
are about to introduce organisms from the rest of the environment
onto the patient's skin. Crossing the threshold into the room does
not count; walking up to the bedside and then cleaning your hands
does.

**Moment 2 — Before an aseptic task.** Immediately before any
procedure that enters a normally sterile site — starting an IV,
handling a central-line port, inserting a Foley catheter, doing a
dressing change on an open wound. Even if you cleaned your hands at
Moment 1 and have not left the bedside, you clean again, because
anything you touched in between — your stethoscope, the bedrails, the
patient's gown — has re-contaminated your hands. The **aseptic task**
Moment is the one staff miss most often.

**Moment 3 — After body-fluid exposure risk.** Immediately after any
contact (or potential contact) with blood, mucous membranes, broken
skin, secretions, excretions, or contaminated items — even through
gloves, because gloves leak and gloves tear. This Moment happens
before you leave the bedside, not after. If you just emptied a
urine collection bag, you clean your hands at the bedside before
touching the chart, your pen, or the door handle. A **body-fluid
exposure** is the Moment most likely to become an outbreak if missed.

**Moment 4 — After patient contact.** When you are done with hands-on
care and about to leave the **patient zone**. This is the Moment
staff generally perform reliably because it feels intuitive.

**Moment 5 — After contact with patient surroundings.** After you
have touched the environment around the patient — bedrail, IV pole,
bedside table, call button, chair — even if you never touched the
patient. The surfaces in the **patient zone** carry the patient's
flora. Dropping off a tray and straightening a blanket is a Moment 5
trigger even if the patient was in the bathroom the whole time.

The five are not five separate rules; they are one rule ("clean at
every boundary of the patient zone, and always before an aseptic
task") seen from five angles.

### Practical barriers and how institutions remove them

Hand hygiene fails for predictable reasons. Jewelry and artificial
nails trap organisms and make proper technique impossible, which is
why most U.S. hospital policies prohibit artificial nails in direct
care and limit rings to a plain band [CDC-HandHygiene]. Chapped
skin makes workers avoid alcohol-based hand rub because it stings,
so facilities stock ABHR formulations with built-in emollients and
provide hand lotion. Time pressure — "I have ten rooms left" —
predicts missed moments more than any other factor, which is why
**hand-hygiene compliance** is measured, reported, and built into
staff performance expectations under The Joint Commission's National
Patient Safety Goal 07 [TJC-NPSG-07]. Empty dispensers are a
surprisingly common failure mode; a good facility treats a bare
dispenser the way it treats a missing crash cart — a near-miss that
triggers a maintenance ticket, not a shrug.

The individual skill of hand hygiene lives inside this institutional
frame. You perform the technique; the facility gives you working
dispensers, stocked sinks, a uniform that does not trap flora, and a
culture in which your colleagues expect you to stop and clean your
hands before touching a central line, even if they are in a hurry.

## Worked through an example

Picture a morning round: one nurse, two patients in a shared room.
Patient A needs an IV dressing changed. Patient B needs to be
repositioned. The sequence looks small — a few steps between beds,
a quick reach for supplies — and inside it sit four or five
Five-Moments indications. `examples.md` walks through that sequence
and three more: a before-aseptic-task sequence, a response to a
body-fluid exposure, and the special case of a *C. diff* room.

## Common confusions

The two most common wrong models are "gloves replace hand hygiene"
and "ABHR kills everything." Both feel reasonable; both fail
patients. The misconceptions file also covers the intuition that
hand hygiene is important but optional under time pressure, and the
idea that the Five Moments are about entering and leaving the room
rather than about the patient zone. Diagnostic probes live in
`misconceptions.md`.

## Key terms to remember

The anchor vocabulary is **hand hygiene**, the **Five Moments**,
**alcohol-based hand rub** (**ABHR**), **soap-and-water handwash**,
**aseptic task**, and the **patient zone**. You will also meet
**body-fluid exposure**, **healthcare-associated infection**
(**HAI**), **spore**, and **hand-hygiene compliance** — these name
the reasons the technique matters, the organisms that push you to
soap-and-water, and the way institutions hold staff accountable. The
full list and definitions are in `vocabulary.csv`.
