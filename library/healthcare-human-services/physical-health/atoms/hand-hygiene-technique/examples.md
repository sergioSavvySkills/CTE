# Worked examples — hand-hygiene-technique

## Example 1 — Before an aseptic task (IV dressing change)

**Scenario:** You enter Mr. Nguyen's room to change a peripheral IV
dressing that is loose. His skin is intact, he has no isolation
precautions, and you already rubbed ABHR at the door on the way in.

**What to work out:** How many hand-hygiene moments does this visit
require, and which technique at each moment?

**Step-by-step:**

1. At the bedside, before introducing yourself and touching Mr. Nguyen:
   perform **Moment 1 — Before patient contact**. Use **ABHR**. About
   a palm-full of gel, rub all surfaces for roughly 20 seconds until
   dry [WHO-FiveMoments, CDC-HandHygiene].
2. Gather supplies, take his arm, and inspect the IV site. These are
   patient-contact activities, not an aseptic task yet.
3. Immediately before you open the sterile dressing kit: perform
   **Moment 2 — Before aseptic task**. ABHR again. Yes, again — the
   bedrail, the gown, and your own stethoscope have re-contaminated
   your hands since Moment 1 [WHO-HandHygiene].
4. Don clean gloves, remove the old dressing, clean the site, apply
   the new dressing.
5. Remove gloves. Perform **Moment 3 — After body-fluid exposure
   risk** (the old dressing had dried blood). Use ABHR because
   nothing is visibly soiled on your hands [CDC-HandHygiene].
6. Document at the bedside, straighten the blanket, step away.
   Before leaving the **patient zone**, perform **Moment 4 — After
   patient contact**. ABHR, 20 seconds.

**Answer:** Four hand-hygiene moments in one short visit (1, 2, 3,
4), all performed with ABHR because hands stay clean of visible soil
and Mr. Nguyen is not on spore-forming precautions.

**Why it matters:** The aseptic-task moment is the one workers miss
most often because they just rubbed in at the door and feel "already
clean" [WHO-FiveMoments]. Re-rubbing before the sterile step is how
you keep organisms out of the line.

## Example 2 — Responding to a body-fluid exposure

**Scenario:** You are emptying Ms. Okafor's urinary drainage bag
when the bag slips and urine splashes onto your gloved hand and the
sleeve of your gown.

**What to work out:** What is the correct immediate sequence, and
which technique do you use?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Stop. Do not touch anything clean — not your face, not the chart,
   not the pen, not the door handle. Your gown sleeve is a
   **body-fluid exposure** and anything you touch becomes a transfer
   point.
2. Dispose of the drainage contents per protocol, then remove the
   gown carefully so the contaminated surface folds inward, and
   remove the gloves. Drop both in the biohazard bag.
3. Perform **Moment 3 — After body-fluid exposure risk**
   *immediately* at the bedside. Because the exposure soaked through
   to the skin of your wrist, your hands count as visibly soiled —
   use **soap-and-water handwash**, not ABHR [CDC-HandHygiene].
4. Wet, lather, scrub every surface for at least 20 seconds
   including under the nails and around the thumbs, rinse with
   fingertips down, dry with a paper towel, and turn off the tap with
   the towel [WHO-HandHygiene].
5. Replace the gown, don clean gloves if further care is needed, and
   continue. Moment 4 (after patient contact) still applies when you
   leave the **patient zone**.

**Answer:** Soap-and-water handwash, performed at the bedside,
before any other action — because the exposure was visible and wet.

**Why it matters:** The instinct after a splash is to rush to a door
or a chart and "get out of the way" before cleaning up. That
instinct spreads the exposure through the unit. Moment 3 is a
bedside moment, not a hallway moment [WHO-FiveMoments].

## Example 3 — Caring for a patient with C. difficile

**Scenario:** Mr. Brooks is on contact precautions for *C. difficile*
infection. You go in to assist him to the commode, clean him, and
return him to bed.

**What to work out:** Which technique do you use on exit, and why?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Perform **Moment 1 — Before patient contact** at the bedside
   with ABHR before direct patient contact, per standard workflow.
   Don gown and gloves for contact precautions.
2. Provide the care: help him to the commode, assist with hygiene
   after the bowel movement, return him to bed. This sequence
   involves multiple body-fluid contacts.
3. At the bedside, remove gloves and gown into the bin, working
   contaminated side in. Do not leave the room yet.
4. Perform hand hygiene as a **soap-and-water handwash**, not ABHR.
   *C. difficile* produces **spores** that are not killed by alcohol;
   only the mechanical friction of soap and running water physically
   removes them from the skin [CDC-CDiff, CDC-HandHygiene].
5. Scrub for at least 20 seconds, paying attention to under the
   nails and around the thumbs where spores lodge. Rinse fingertips
   down. Dry with paper towel; use the towel to turn off the tap and
   to open the door on the way out.
6. Once outside, most facilities have you perform another
   soap-and-water handwash, not ABHR, on any subsequent Moment
   affecting this patient's flora. The "hallway rub" habit is the
   single fastest way to walk a *C. diff* spore to the next room
   [CDC-CDiff].

**Answer:** Soap and water on exit, not ABHR, because alcohol does
not kill spores.

**Why it matters:** The same reasoning applies to norovirus and to
any patient whose hands are visibly soiled. The default is ABHR; the
exceptions are specific and memorable [CDC-HandHygiene].

## Example 4 — Two patients in a shared room

**Scenario:** You are doing morning vitals for two patients in a
semi-private room separated by a curtain. Both are on standard
precautions only. Patient A is in bed A near the door; Patient B is
in bed B by the window.

**What to work out:** How many Five-Moments hand-hygiene events
happen across this short encounter, and which technique is each?

**Step-by-step:**

1. Approach Patient A at the bedside. Perform **Moment 1 — Before
   patient contact** with ABHR [WHO-FiveMoments]. Take A's blood
   pressure, pulse, and temperature. Document on the bedside tablet.
2. Patient A is finished. Straighten A's blanket. You are about to
   leave A's **patient zone** to cross to B. Perform **Moment 4 —
   After patient contact** with ABHR at A's bedside. The same rub
   also satisfies Moment 5 (contact with A's surroundings).
3. Step across the curtain to Patient B. Before touching B,
   perform **Moment 1 — Before patient contact** for B, with ABHR.
   Yes, you already rubbed 10 seconds ago at A's bedside — but you
   touched the curtain, and you are entering a new patient zone, so
   the moment applies again [WHO-HandHygiene].
4. Take B's vitals. Document. Straighten B's blanket.
5. Perform **Moment 4 — After patient contact** for B with ABHR
   before stepping away from the bed.
6. Discard the used blood-pressure-cuff cover, exit the room, and
   continue your round.

**Answer:** Four distinct Moments (1 and 4 for A, 1 and 4 for B),
each performed with ABHR. Some facilities also credit a separate
Moment 5 after A if you touched A's overbed table — the rule is
err toward more rubs, not fewer.

**Why it matters:** The shared-room case is where the
door-in-door-out mental model fails. A worker who cleans hands only
on room entry and exit has just moved A's flora onto B's skin,
which is exactly the transmission pathway that produces outbreaks
in shared rooms [WHO-FiveMoments, WHO-HandHygiene]. The patient
zone, not the door, is the unit of hand hygiene.
