# Common misconceptions — hand-hygiene-technique

## Misconception 1 — Gloves replace hand hygiene

**What students often think:**
If I wore gloves for the contact, I don't need to clean my hands
after — the gloves kept my hands clean.

**What's actually true:**
Gloves are an addition to hand hygiene, not a substitute for it.
Gloves leak. Gloves tear. Organisms get on the skin during removal
because the outside of the glove — which is contaminated — brushes
the bare hand as it comes off [WHO-HandHygiene]. Every WHO moment
that applies before gloves go on applies again after they come off,
and in particular Moment 3 (after body-fluid exposure risk) requires
hand hygiene at the bedside even after glove removal [CDC-HandHygiene].
Assuming the glove did the job is how workers become carriers of the
exact organism they gloved up to avoid.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Gloves feel protective. Students think of them as sealing off the
hands, and on healthy intact skin they mostly do. The failure happens
at removal and at the wrist — two places students do not see until
they are shown.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"You wore gloves to empty a Foley bag. You are now walking to
the next patient. Do you clean your hands?"* A student with the
correct model cleans hands immediately after glove removal at the
first patient's bedside, before touching anything, regardless of
whether the gloves stayed intact.

## Misconception 2 — Alcohol-based hand rub kills everything

**What students often think:**
ABHR is hospital-grade disinfectant, so it kills whatever I might
have on my hands. If I used it, I'm good.

**What's actually true:**
Alcohol-based hand rub kills most bacteria and most enveloped
viruses, but it does not kill a **spore**. *Clostridioides difficile*
produces spores, and norovirus has a protein shell that also resists
alcohol [CDC-CDiff]. Both pass right through an ABHR-only regimen.
After caring for a patient with either organism you must wash with
soap and water — the friction of the wash physically removes the
spores from the skin [CDC-HandHygiene]. Alcohol also does nothing for
visible soil; it disinfects but does not clean.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
"Hospital-grade" and "kills 99.9%" are marketing phrases students
carry in from consumer products. Nobody mentions the 0.1%, and the
0.1% contains the organisms that cause outbreaks.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"You just took a stool sample from a patient with C. diff.
Your hands look clean. You used ABHR three times on the way out of
the room. Are your hands safe to touch the next patient?"* A student
with the correct model says no and names the spore-removal reason —
soap and water are required, not another round of ABHR.

## Misconception 3 — The Five Moments are about entering and leaving rooms

**What students often think:**
I clean my hands on the way in and on the way out of the room.
That's the rule.

**What's actually true:**
The Five Moments are defined by the **patient zone** and by specific
tasks, not by doors. Moment 1 is before touching the patient, not
before entering the room. Moment 2 is before any **aseptic task**,
which may happen in the middle of a visit without any door being
involved. Moment 3 is at the bedside immediately after a body-fluid
exposure, before touching anything else. Moment 5 applies even if
you never touched the patient — straightening the bed counts
[WHO-FiveMoments]. The door-based rule misses several moments per
encounter and is how a single worker becomes a vehicle for
cross-contamination between patients in a shared room.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Doors are visible and memorable; the patient zone is an invisible
bubble. New staff latch onto what they can see, and facility signs
that say "Clean your hands" live on doorframes.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Give the scenario of two patients in a shared room with a curtain
between them and ask how many hand-hygiene moments a nurse owes
during a routine check of both. A student with the correct model
identifies at least four (before A, after A / before B, before any
aseptic task on B, after B), not just two (in and out).

## Misconception 4 — Twenty seconds isn't literal

**What students often think:**
Nobody actually times it. A quick rub or a fast wash with soap is
fine as long as you hit all the surfaces.

**What's actually true:**
Twenty seconds is a specific evidence-based minimum for both
techniques. Under 15 seconds, most real hands miss coverage on the
backs, thumbs, and fingertips, and ABHR that has not fully dried has
not finished disinfecting [WHO-HandHygiene, CDC-HandHygiene].
Observation data consistently show that workers think they are doing
20 seconds when they are doing 6–10 — almost everyone
underestimates. The number is why training programs use cues like
"sing Happy Birthday twice" and why compliance observers time the
rub. Short-cutting the timing short-cuts the disinfection.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Twenty seconds feels much longer than it is, especially on a busy
shift. Students watch experienced staff move fast and mistake speed
for correct technique; a lot of experienced staff are also doing it
short.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Have the student demonstrate ABHR or a handwash while silently
timed. A student with the correct model runs at or past 20 seconds
and covers palms, backs, interlaced fingers, thumbs, and
fingertips — not one who finishes the whole procedure in 8 seconds
and stops when the hands feel dry.
