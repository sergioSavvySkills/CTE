# The Healthcare Delivery System

## Why this matters

A single hospital admission for a 74-year-old with pneumonia touches
at least four buildings, three payers, and a half-dozen different
teams before the patient is home again. The ambulance crew, the
emergency department, the inpatient medical floor, the skilled
nursing facility, the home-health nurse, the primary-care clinic
that schedules the follow-up visit, the pharmacy benefit manager,
Medicare, a supplemental plan — every one of them is part of the
healthcare delivery system. None of them is "the system" on its own.

This atom is about how those pieces fit together. You will not leave
knowing how to price a drug, staff a nursing unit, or appeal a claim.
You will leave able to answer a more useful question: when a patient
has a problem, which setting is the right one, who is paying, what
comes next along the **continuum of care**, and where are the most
likely places for a hand-off to fail. That map is what separates a
student who has memorized a list of hospitals from one who can think
like a healthcare worker.

## What you should already know

You should know that healthcare in the United States is a mix of
public and private organizations, and that people get coverage from
several sources — an employer plan, a government program, the
individual market, or sometimes nothing at all. You should have
encountered the idea that clinicians work in many different settings,
not just hospitals, and that healthcare costs money that somebody has
to pay. You do not need prior coursework in insurance or policy;
every term this atom uses is defined as it appears.

## Core concept

### Care settings and acuity

A **care setting** is a place where care is delivered — physical or
virtual — and each one is designed for a certain level of patient
**acuity**. Acuity is how sick or unstable a patient is at a given
moment. Matching acuity to setting is the first question in delivery-
system design, because the wrong match wastes resources and can harm
patients [KFF-Overview].

Hospitals deliver the highest-acuity care. Within the hospital
category live general acute-care hospitals, critical-access hospitals
(small rural facilities with a federal designation), children's
hospitals, specialty hospitals (for example, cardiac or orthopedic),
and academic medical centers that combine care with teaching and
research. Inside a hospital, acuity varies from the intensive-care
unit down to an observation bed.

Outside the hospital walls, outpatient clinics handle visits where
the patient goes home the same day. That category includes **primary
care** offices, specialty clinics, community health centers, and
ambulatory surgery centers where procedures that used to require an
overnight stay now take a few hours. Urgent care clinics sit between
the primary-care office and the emergency department: same-day care
for problems that are not life-threatening but cannot wait a week.

**Post-acute care** picks up where the hospital stops. Skilled
nursing facilities provide short-term rehabilitation after a hospital
discharge. Long-term care — nursing homes and assisted living —
supports people who can no longer live independently. Home health
sends nurses and therapists into the home for patients who can
recover there with help. Hospice specializes in comfort and
end-of-life care, usually for people expected to live six months or
less.

**Telehealth** is a setting defined by the technology rather than the
building. A telehealth visit can be a video appointment with a
primary-care clinician, a remote consultation with a specialist, or
monitoring of blood pressure or blood sugar from the patient's own
home. Telehealth is well suited to follow-up, behavioral health,
triage, and rural access — it works less well for hands-on
assessment or procedures.

The acuity match matters because cost, staffing, and outcomes depend
on it. Treating low-acuity problems in the emergency department
crowds out patients who need that setting; treating high-acuity
problems in a primary-care office is unsafe. A well-designed system
tries to route each patient to the lowest-intensity setting that
still meets their needs [NASEM-Access].

### Payers and the payer-provider distinction

A **payer** is the organization that pays the bill for a healthcare
service. A **provider** is the organization or person that delivers
the care. Students routinely merge the two and get confused — the
hospital that treats you is usually not the entity paying the claim.
Keeping the distinction clean is one of the small vocabulary moves
that opens up everything else in the system.

The U.S. payer landscape has two main families [KFF-Overview]. Public
payers are government programs. **Medicare** covers most adults at
age 65 and some people with disabilities [CMS-About]. **Medicaid** is
a joint federal-state program for people with low income and certain
other eligible groups, and it covers a large share of children,
pregnant patients, and long-term nursing-home residents.
**CHIP** — the Children's Health Insurance Program — covers children
in families who earn too much for Medicaid but cannot afford private
coverage. TRICARE is the coverage program for uniformed-service
members, retirees, and their families. Veterans have their own
system through the VA.

Private payers are commercial insurance companies. Most working-age
Americans under 65 get coverage through an employer-sponsored plan
that an insurer administers. A smaller group buys coverage directly
in the individual market, often through a state or federal
marketplace. And some people pay for care out of pocket with no
insurance at all — this is called self-pay, and it is the most
fragile arrangement of all.

One patient can have more than one payer at once. A 68-year-old
retiree may have Medicare as the primary payer and a private
supplemental plan as the secondary. A child may have private
insurance through one parent and Medicaid as a secondary payer for
services the private plan does not cover. Coordination of benefits —
deciding who pays first — is a system-level job that patients rarely
see.

The payer side of the system is where policy fights are loudest, but
for an entry-level map you only need three ideas: payer is not the
same as provider, most people are covered by some combination of
public and private payers, and coverage rules determine what
providers can actually deliver at scale.

### The continuum of care

The **continuum of care** is the full sequence of services a person
may use over a lifetime, arranged from least intensive to most
intensive. A common framing has six phases [NASEM-Access]. Prevention
and wellness keep people healthy before problems start — vaccines,
screenings, health counseling. Primary care is first-contact,
continuing care for common problems and for coordinating with
specialists. Acute care is short-term, intensive treatment — usually
in a hospital — for severe illness or injury. Specialty care is
focused expertise in a specific organ system or disease. Post-acute
care supports recovery after a hospital stay. End-of-life care
(palliative and hospice) supports comfort and decision-making when
cure is no longer the goal.

A real patient does not move through these phases in a clean line. A
person with diabetes may bounce between primary care (medication
management), specialty care (endocrinology), acute care (a
hospitalization for an infected foot ulcer), and post-acute care
(home health wound care) in a single year. The continuum is a map of
the pieces, not a schedule.

Each move between phases is a **care transition**. Transitions are
fragile. The patient's medications change; the electronic record
does not always travel with them; the next clinician may not know
what was tried last week. Federal research shows that readmissions,
medication errors, and missed follow-up are concentrated at
transitions rather than inside any single setting [AHRQ-Transitions].
This is why the line "health-system design is mostly about reducing
friction at the seams" belongs in the opening paragraphs of the
textbook, not in a footnote.

### Access barriers

An **access barrier** is anything that keeps a person from getting
needed care, independent of whether that care exists somewhere in the
system. The barriers cluster into six kinds [RWJF-SDOH].

**Cost** is the most visible barrier: premiums, deductibles,
copayments, prescription prices, and surprise bills. Cost keeps
people from filling prescriptions, skipping follow-up appointments,
and delaying care until problems are emergencies.

**Geography** is uneven. Some counties have too few physicians,
hospitals, or specialty services to meet local demand. HRSA
designates Health Professional Shortage Areas where the
population-to-provider ratio crosses a federal threshold; the
designation triggers federal support but also signals that patients
there must travel farther, wait longer, or both [HRSA-ShortageAreas].
Rural counties are disproportionately affected — distance to the
nearest hospital can be fifty miles or more [CDC-RuralHealth].

**Insurance status** is itself a barrier. Uninsured patients avoid
the system until they cannot, and they are more likely to be
diagnosed late and to accumulate medical debt. The payer-mix data
show uninsurance rates vary by state, age, and income.

**Language** keeps patients from understanding instructions,
describing symptoms precisely, or consenting to care that is
explained only in English. Hospitals that serve multilingual
communities use certified medical interpreters to close this gap;
many smaller settings do not.

**Transportation** is quieter than the others and just as consequential.
A patient without a car, a bus line, or a neighbor who can drive may
miss appointments, skip dialysis, or delay picking up a prescription.
Missed appointments cost the system more than they save.

**Digital access** is newer on the list but now essential. Scheduling,
patient portals, test results, telehealth visits, and prior-
authorization appeals all assume a stable internet connection and a
device. Patients without reliable broadband are on the wrong side of
a quickly hardening divide.

These barriers rarely appear alone. A rural, Spanish-speaking,
uninsured patient carries four barriers at once; each one compounds
the others, and the outcome gap widens accordingly [NASEM-Access].

### How the pieces connect — system seams

The three pieces above — settings, payers, continuum — meet at the
seams. A patient episode almost never stays inside one piece. A
stroke at home becomes an EMS call (local), an emergency-department
visit (hospital acute care), an inpatient admission (acute care),
rehabilitation (post-acute), a home-health period (home setting), and
a series of primary- and specialty-care follow-ups — and it touches
Medicare, a supplemental insurer, the hospital's billing office, and
perhaps a state Medicaid program for services Medicare does not cover.
Each move is a **care transition**, and each transition is a risk
point.

System design, at the level this atom asks you to see, is mostly
about reducing friction at those seams. Electronic records that
travel with the patient, discharge instructions the patient actually
understands, follow-up calls that catch problems before they become
readmissions, transportation support for the appointment a week after
discharge — these are seam-smoothing moves. They look small next to
"new hospital" or "new drug," but the evidence says they move
outcomes more reliably than either [AHRQ-Transitions].

That is the map. Care settings are the where; payers are the how-
it's-paid-for; the continuum of care is the when; access barriers
are who falls out of the map; seams are where everything gets lost
if no one is watching.

## Worked through an example

Consider an older adult who falls at home and breaks a hip. The
path runs through at least five settings and two payers: EMS to
the emergency department, the inpatient surgical unit, a skilled
nursing facility for rehabilitation, home health for continued
therapy, and primary-care follow-up — billed primarily to Medicare
with secondary coverage through a supplemental plan. See
`examples.md` for this case plus three more tracing similarly
complex patient journeys across settings, payers, and the full
continuum.

## Common confusions

Students most often collapse the payer-provider distinction, assume
urgent care and the emergency department are interchangeable, treat
"access" as if it were only about cost, or think telehealth is a
separate system rather than a care setting inside the existing one.
Full corrections, with diagnostic prompts, live in
`misconceptions.md`.

## Key terms to remember

Four anchors carry this atom. **Care setting** is the where;
**payer** is the who-pays; **continuum of care** is the when; **access
barrier** is what keeps patients out of the map. Supporting terms —
**acuity**, **primary care**, **acute care**, **post-acute care**,
**telehealth**, **Medicare**, **Medicaid**, **CHIP**, **care
transition** — each pin down one piece of the scaffold. Every term
appears above and is defined in `vocabulary.csv`.
