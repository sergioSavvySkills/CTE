# Common misconceptions — healthcare-delivery-system-overview

## Misconception 1 — The hospital both treats you and pays for your care

**What students often think:**
The hospital that treats you is the same organization that pays for
your care — if you stayed at "Memorial Hospital," then "Memorial
Hospital" is the one the bill comes from and the one that owes the
nurse her paycheck.

**What's actually true:**
The **payer** and the provider are almost always separate. The
hospital is a provider; it delivers services and then sends a claim
to a **payer** — Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, a private insurer, a
self-insured employer plan, or the patient out-of-pocket. The payer
decides what is covered, at what rate, and after what copay; the
provider decides what clinical care to deliver. A patient can pay
one hospital through several different payers across a single
admission, and a single payer (for example, Medicare) pays thousands
of different hospitals [KFF-Overview, CMS-About]. Keeping the
distinction clean is the vocabulary move that makes the rest of the
payer side of the system legible.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
In most consumer transactions the provider and the payer are the
same — you pay the coffee shop directly. Healthcare's three-party
structure (patient, provider, payer) is unusual, and early exposure
to "hospital bills" tends to fuse the two ideas in memory.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A patient is admitted to a private hospital, treated by a
physician employed by that hospital, and covered by Medicare. Name
the provider(s) and the payer."* A student with the correct model
names the hospital and the physician as providers and Medicare as
the payer, and can explain that the hospital sends a claim to
Medicare rather than charging the patient directly.

## Misconception 2 — Urgent care and the emergency department are interchangeable

**What students often think:**
Urgent care and the emergency department do basically the same thing
— both are walk-in, both are for "sudden problems," so you can go to
either one and it doesn't much matter which.

**What's actually true:**
Urgent care and the emergency department are different **care
settings** designed for different **acuity** levels. Emergency
departments are staffed and equipped for life-threatening conditions
— chest pain suggesting a heart attack, stroke symptoms, severe
trauma, major bleeding, difficulty breathing. Urgent care handles
problems that are too urgent for a next-week primary-care
appointment but not life-threatening — minor fractures, stitches,
most infections, sprains. The settings are not interchangeable
because cost, staffing, and wait times are very different: an ED
visit costs several times more than an urgent-care visit, and an ED
operating near capacity for low-acuity problems has fewer resources
for the high-acuity ones it was designed for [KFF-Overview,
NASEM-Access].

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Both settings say "walk in, no appointment" and both have flashy
signs. A student who has only been to a doctor's office may map
everything above that level to "ED." The differences become obvious
only once you see a bill or wait seven hours with a sprained wrist.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"Give one patient problem that clearly belongs in an ED and
one that clearly belongs in urgent care, and explain the acuity
reasoning."* A student with the correct model picks something like
chest pain or stroke symptoms for the ED and something like a minor
laceration or a probable ear infection for urgent care, and can
name acuity (severity and instability) as the sorting variable.

## Misconception 3 — Access barriers are mostly about insurance

**What students often think:**
"Access" in healthcare is basically about insurance — if you are
insured you have access, and if you are not you don't. Everything
else is details.

**What's actually true:**
Insurance is one of six common **access barriers**, not the whole
category. The others — cost even when insured (deductibles,
copayments, surprise bills), geography (rural and shortage areas),
language, transportation, and digital access — all shape whether a
patient actually gets needed care, independent of whether a card is
in their wallet [RWJF-SDOH, HRSA-ShortageAreas, CDC-RuralHealth]. A
rural patient with insurance may still lose access because the
nearest specialist is fifty miles away and there is no bus line. A
patient fluent in a language other than English may skip follow-up
because the discharge instructions were not translated. A patient
without reliable broadband may be cut out of telehealth, patient
portals, and prior-authorization paperwork. Insurance status matters
— it is often the biggest single barrier — but treating it as the
only barrier hides the others that produce measurable outcome gaps.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Insurance is the barrier that dominates U.S. policy debate, so
students hear about it constantly and assume it captures the whole
problem. The quieter barriers — transportation, language, digital
access — get less airtime and feel like minor logistics instead of
structural access issues.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"Name three non-insurance access barriers and describe how
each one could keep a fully insured patient from getting needed
care."* A student with the correct model offers examples such as
distance from a provider, language mismatch with a clinician, lack
of transportation to appointments, cost-sharing even with coverage,
or no reliable internet for telehealth — and explains that insurance
alone does not close any of these gaps.

## Misconception 4 — Telehealth is a separate healthcare system

**What students often think:**
Telehealth is its own system, parallel to regular healthcare — like
a whole different app or company that replaces in-person care for
people who use it.

**What's actually true:**
**Telehealth** is a **care setting** within the existing delivery
system, not a parallel system. The clinician on the video call is
usually licensed and credentialed the same way as one you would see
in person, often by the same practice or health system; the visit is
typically billed to the same **payer**, enters the same medical
record, and connects to the same referrals and prescriptions
[KFF-Overview]. Telehealth expands access for specific use cases —
follow-up, behavioral health, triage, rural patients — and is less
well suited to hands-on assessment or procedures. Treating it as a
separate system obscures the real design question, which is which
visits belong in which setting along the continuum of care.

**Why the confusion is natural:**
Telehealth products are marketed as standalone apps, and during the
2020 surge many patients encountered telehealth through direct-to-
consumer services disconnected from their regular provider. The
marketing and the experience made telehealth feel like its own
thing.

**How to check whether a student has the correct model:**
Ask: *"A patient sees their regular primary-care clinician by video
instead of in person. Which setting is this, and does the payer and
medical record change?"* A student with the correct model answers
that it is a telehealth visit — a care setting within the same
delivery system — and that the payer, provider, and medical record
are typically the same as for an in-person visit with the same
clinician.
