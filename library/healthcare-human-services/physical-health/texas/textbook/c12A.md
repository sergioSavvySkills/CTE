# Technological Equipment Across the Five Health Science Systems

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify representative technological equipment in each of the five health science systems
- Explain the basic function of each piece of equipment in its clinical or research context
- Use authoritative sources to research a specific device end-to-end
- Recognize how equipment and credentials are linked across the five systems

A radiologic technology student steps into the imaging suite, powers up the CT scanner, and confirms that the contrast injector is primed. Down the hall, a respiratory therapist checks the ventilator settings on a patient who arrived overnight with pneumonia. In the central sterile processing department one floor below, a technician loads a tray into the autoclave. Every one of those workers is defined, at least in part, by the equipment they operate. This chapter maps the technology across all five health science systems you first saw in Chapter 7.

## Part 1: Diagnostic and Therapeutic Equipment

### Equipment fills each system

In Chapter 7 you learned that the five health science systems are Diagnostic Services, Therapeutic Services, Health Informatics, Support Services, and Biotechnology Research and Development. At the time, the focus was on careers. Now the focus is on the equipment those careers depend on. The same five-system framework organizes everything, so the mental model you already have carries directly into this chapter.

A key idea before the survey begins: equipment rarely sits in only one system. A continuous glucose monitor (CGM) generates data that is diagnostic, delivers alerts that are therapeutic in effect, and sends readings into an EHR platform that belongs to Health Informatics. That kind of crossover will appear throughout the chapter, and it is one of the reasons the five systems have to be understood as a network, not as five isolated boxes.

### Diagnostic services: seeing inside the patient

Diagnostic equipment answers the question "what is happening in this patient's body?" The largest category is imaging. **Radiography (X-ray)** uses ionizing radiation to produce images of bone and dense tissue. **Computed tomography (CT)** takes a series of X-ray slices and reconstructs them into a three-dimensional picture. **Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)** uses magnetic fields and radio waves, no ionizing radiation, to image soft tissue with high resolution. **Ultrasound** uses sound waves and is the preferred imaging method for pregnancies and situations where radiation must be avoided. **Positron emission tomography (PET)** scanners detect metabolic activity and are used heavily in oncology. **DEXA scanners** measure bone density and are the standard tool for diagnosing osteoporosis.

Cardiac diagnostic equipment includes the **EKG/ECG machine**, which records the heart's electrical activity through leads placed on the skin, and the **Holter monitor**, which records a continuous EKG over 24 to 48 hours as a patient goes about normal life. Pulmonary diagnostic equipment includes the **spirometer**, which measures how much air a patient can move and how fast, and the **pulse oximeter**, which estimates oxygen saturation through the skin using infrared light.

The clinical laboratory holds its own equipment ecosystem. **Automated chemistry analyzers** run panels such as the comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP) and lipid panel. **Hematology analyzers** produce the complete blood count (CBC). **PCR (polymerase chain reaction) platforms** detect the genetic material of viruses, bacteria, and other pathogens, and are what powered most COVID-19 testing. All lab equipment requires strict quality control (QC) procedures governed by CLIA (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments).

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A 52-year-old patient comes to the emergency department with chest pain. The triage nurse attaches a pulse oximeter to measure oxygen saturation and places EKG leads to get a 12-lead tracing. The emergency physician orders a chest X-ray and a CT angiogram of the chest to rule out pulmonary embolism. The lab runs a troponin and a D-dimer on the automated chemistry analyzer. In under two hours, five separate pieces of diagnostic equipment have produced the data the team needs to make a life-or-death decision. Each device was operated by a credentialed professional: radiologic technologist, ECG technician, lab scientist.

### Therapeutic services: treating with technology

Therapeutic equipment delivers treatment rather than information. **Smart infusion pumps** deliver IV medications at a programmed rate and include a drug library that alerts the nurse if a dose falls outside a safe range. **Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) pumps** let patients self-administer a preset dose of pain medication within locked limits. **Insulin pumps** deliver continuous subcutaneous insulin, most commonly for Type 1 diabetes.

Respiratory therapy equipment includes **mechanical ventilators**, which breathe for patients who cannot breathe adequately on their own, and **CPAP/BiPAP** devices, which deliver pressurized air to keep the airway open during sleep or mild respiratory failure. Cardiac therapy equipment includes **defibrillators**, both the automated external type (AED) found in public spaces and the manual type used by clinical teams, and **implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs)** placed surgically in patients at high risk of sudden cardiac arrest.

Surgical and procedural equipment includes the **electrosurgical unit (ESU)**, which cuts and cauterizes tissue using radiofrequency electrical current, the **anesthesia machine**, and the **da Vinci surgical robot**, which allows a surgeon to perform minimally invasive procedures through small incisions with robotic arms controlled from a console. Dialysis equipment includes **hemodialysis machines** that filter waste products from the blood of patients whose kidneys have failed. In radiation oncology, the **linear accelerator (LINAC)** delivers precisely targeted beams of ionizing radiation to destroy tumor cells while sparing surrounding tissue.

## Part 2: Informatics, Support Services, Biotech R&D, and How to Research a Device

### Health informatics: the data layer

Health Informatics equipment manages the information that flows through every clinical encounter. The most visible device at the bedside is the **workstation on wheels (WOW)**, a mobile computer that gives nurses and physicians access to the EHR without leaving the patient's room. Tablets and handheld **barcode scanners** are used to verify medication administration by scanning the patient's wristband and the medication before it is given.

**Central telemetry monitors** display real-time EKG rhythms from multiple patients at a nurses' station, allowing continuous cardiac monitoring across a unit. **Remote patient monitoring (RPM) devices** let clinicians track vital signs, blood glucose, weight, and other parameters from patients who are at home. The data those devices generate moves through **health information exchange (HIE) networks** built on the **FHIR** standard, the modern API that lets different EHR systems share structured data with each other.

**Clinical decision support (CDS) systems** sit inside the EHR and alert clinicians when a drug order might interact dangerously with another medication, when a patient's labs match a sepsis alert criterion, or when an imaging triage algorithm flags a finding for priority review. These systems are the bridge between raw data and clinical judgment.

### Support services: what makes everything else possible

Support services equipment does not touch the patient directly, but patient safety depends on it completely. The central sterile processing department runs **steam sterilizers (autoclaves)**, which use pressurized steam to kill all microorganisms on surgical instruments, and **low-temperature sterilizers** using ethylene oxide or hydrogen peroxide plasma for heat-sensitive equipment. **Washer-disinfectors** clean instruments mechanically before sterilization. Every sterilization cycle is verified with a **biological indicator**, a test that uses live bacterial spores to confirm that sterilization actually occurred.

Environmental services uses **UV-C room disinfection units**, which emit ultraviolet light to inactivate pathogens on room surfaces after a patient is discharged, particularly important for controlling C. difficile and other drug-resistant organisms. **Airborne Infection Isolation Rooms (AIIRs)** use negative air pressure and HEPA filtration to prevent airborne pathogens from spreading into hallways.

Facility safety systems include **medical gas systems** with zone shutoff valves for oxygen, air, and vacuum, **emergency power generators** that activate within seconds of a utility outage, and **nurse-call and code-blue alert systems** that route emergencies to the right responders instantly.

### Biotechnology R&D: the discovery end of healthcare

Biotechnology research and development equipment sits mostly in research labs and pharmaceutical facilities, but the equipment is increasingly crossing into clinical use. **PCR thermocyclers** cycle a sample through precise temperatures to amplify a specific segment of DNA or RNA, the foundation of molecular diagnostics. **Next-generation sequencing (NGS) platforms** sequence millions of DNA fragments in parallel, enabling genomic medicine, cancer mutation profiling, and infectious-disease surveillance. **Biosafety cabinets (Class I and Class II)** protect both the worker and the sample by filtering air flow in both directions when handling infectious or hazardous biological materials.

**Flow cytometers** analyze individual cells at thousands per second, identifying cell populations by the proteins on their surface, a technique essential in leukemia diagnosis and immunology research. **Confocal microscopes** use laser light to image cells in three dimensions at extremely high resolution. At the pharmaceutical end, **bioreactors** grow living cells at scale to produce biologic drugs such as monoclonal antibodies.

### How to research a device

The content standard requires more than a list. It requires the ability to research a specific device using authoritative sources. The most important source for US medical devices is the **FDA 510(k) Premarket Notification Database**, which contains publicly searchable summaries for every moderate-risk medical device that has been cleared for sale. The summary tells you what the device does, what it is substantially equivalent to, and what the manufacturer claims about performance.

Other authoritative sources include manufacturer documentation and instructions for use (IFU), **ECRI Institute** device-safety reports and the annual Top 10 Health Technology Hazards list, professional-organization references from ASRT (imaging), AARC (respiratory), ASCP (lab), and APTA (physical therapy), and peer-reviewed medical literature available through PubMed. The Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook tells you which credentialed profession operates each device.

A complete device research profile includes: what the device does, its clinical use case, the manufacturer, the approximate cost range, the credentialed role that operates it, and at least three authoritative sources. That profile is also the format of this chapter's assessment.

### Key terms

**FDA 510(k):** the FDA premarket-notification pathway for most moderate-risk medical devices; the publicly searchable database of cleared devices.

**Smart infusion pump:** an IV-therapy pump with a drug library and dose-error reduction alerts; representative therapeutic-services equipment.

**Linear accelerator (LINAC):** the radiation-therapy delivery device used in most external-beam cancer treatment.

**Autoclave:** steam sterilizer used in central sterile processing; a canonical support-services device.

**Biosafety cabinet:** a Class I or Class II enclosure used in cell biology and molecular work to protect the worker and the sample from each other.

**Next-generation sequencing (NGS):** a high-throughput DNA and RNA sequencing platform used in biotech R&D and increasingly in clinical diagnostics.

**FHIR:** Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources; the modern API standard underlying health-information-exchange infrastructure.

**Workstation on wheels (WOW):** a mobile EHR workstation used at the bedside; representative health-informatics equipment.

**AIIR:** Airborne Infection Isolation Room; a negative-pressure room in the support services system used to contain airborne pathogens.

**ECRI Institute:** an independent authoritative source on medical-device safety and the annual Top 10 Health Technology Hazards list.

### Chapter summary

The five health science systems each depend on a distinct ecosystem of technological equipment, and understanding which equipment belongs to which system is the foundation of healthcare technology literacy. Diagnostic equipment produces the data that drives clinical decisions, from imaging to lab analyzers. Therapeutic equipment delivers treatment, from smart infusion pumps to linear accelerators. Health informatics equipment manages the data flow, from bedside WOWs to clinical decision support systems. Support services equipment, including autoclaves and UV-C disinfection units, makes safe care possible behind the scenes. Biotechnology R&D equipment, from PCR thermocyclers to NGS platforms, generates the discoveries that become tomorrow's treatments. Equipment and credentials co-evolve: the best way to understand a device is to research it systematically using the FDA 510(k) database, manufacturer documentation, ECRI Institute reports, and professional-organization references.

### Review questions

2. Using the FDA 510(k) database as a starting point, describe the steps you would take to build a complete research profile for a smart infusion pump. What five elements must the profile include, and which source would best answer each one?
3. A hospital's central sterile processing department reports that a biological indicator came back positive after a sterilization cycle. Identify which system this department belongs to, explain what a positive biological indicator means for patient safety, and describe the immediate action that should follow.
