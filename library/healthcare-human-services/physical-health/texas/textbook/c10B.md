# Wellness Strategies for Disease Prevention

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify specific, named evidence-based strategies at the primary, secondary, and tertiary levels of prevention
- Distinguish between individual-level and population-level prevention strategies
- Use USPSTF evidence grades to evaluate the strength of a prevention recommendation
- Explain why effective disease prevention typically requires strategies at all three levels simultaneously

A 19-year-old college student gets a flu shot, never misses a dental cleaning, and eats well. A 55-year-old skips his recommended colorectal cancer screening for three years running because he has no symptoms and feels fine. Both patients are making prevention decisions, but only one of them understands that prevention is not just about feeling good today. Knowing the specific strategies that work, and when each applies, is the difference between health-seeking behavior and health-avoiding behavior dressed up as optimism.

### From concepts to strategies

In the previous chapter (c2K), you learned the concepts of health and wellness and the three levels of prevention. This chapter goes a step further: instead of naming the levels, you need to identify the specific, named, evidence-based interventions that belong at each level, and understand the evidence behind them.

The framework for evaluating prevention evidence in the United States comes from the **US Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF)**. The USPSTF reviews research on preventive interventions and assigns **USPSTF grades**, A, B, C, D, or I, to its recommendations. Grade A and B recommendations have strong evidence of benefit and are covered without cost-sharing under the Affordable Care Act. Grade D recommendations have evidence of harm or no benefit; Grade I means evidence is insufficient. Understanding grades matters: "everyone should get this" is almost never the right framing, recommendations are population-specific and risk-specific.

### Primary prevention, stopping disease before it starts

**Primary prevention** acts on healthy people who do not yet have the disease being prevented. The goal is to eliminate exposure to risk or build resistance before the biological process of disease begins.

**Immunization** is the most powerful primary-prevention tool in modern medicine. The **ACIP schedule**, maintained by the CDC Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices, specifies which vaccines are recommended, at what ages, and for which populations. Vaccines prevent individual disease and, at sufficient coverage levels, produce **herd immunity**, population-level protection that shields people who cannot be vaccinated (newborns, immunocompromised patients).

Behavioral strategies include tobacco cessation, limiting alcohol, regular physical activity, balanced nutrition, adequate sleep, and stress management. These are not soft suggestions, they have USPSTF recommendations behind them. Tobacco cessation counseling carries a Grade A recommendation for adults who smoke. The evidence that physical inactivity is a direct cause of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and several cancers is not ambiguous.

Environmental and structural strategies include seatbelt laws, helmet use, sunscreen, home fire-alarm maintenance, and poison-control programs. **Hand hygiene** deserves special mention: the WHO identifies it as the single most effective infection-prevention strategy in both healthcare and community settings. Clean water, sanitation, air quality, and vector control are population-level primary-prevention infrastructure.

**PrEP** (pre-exposure prophylaxis) is a pharmacologic primary-prevention strategy, a daily medication that prevents HIV infection in people at high risk. It is the clearest example of how primary prevention can use medication, not just behavior.

### Secondary prevention, catching disease early

**Secondary prevention** acts on people who appear healthy but may have subclinical disease, disease that has started biologically but has not yet caused symptoms the patient would notice. The strategy is screening: testing before symptoms appear, when treatment options are widest and outcomes are best.

Screening is not appropriate for every disease, it is only justified when there is a reliable test, the disease has a meaningful preclinical window, earlier detection improves outcomes, and the benefits of screening outweigh the harms (false positives, anxiety, unnecessary procedures). The USPSTF grades exist precisely to help clinicians and patients navigate these trade-offs.

Major Grade A and B screening recommendations include:

- **Colorectal cancer screening** beginning at age 45 for average-risk adults
- **Cervical cancer screening** (Pap smear and/or HPV testing) per age-specific intervals
- **Mammography** for breast cancer in women aged 40–74, with shared decision-making
- **Blood pressure screening** in adults, hypertension is largely asymptomatic until it causes organ damage
- **HIV and hepatitis C screening** for at-risk populations
- **Lung cancer screening** (annual low-dose CT) for eligible current or former heavy smokers aged 50–80
- **Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) screening**, one-time ultrasound for men aged 65–75 who have ever smoked

The **newborn screening panel** is one of the most important secondary-prevention programs in Texas. The Texas DSHS mandates screening for dozens of conditions, including PKU, congenital hypothyroidism, sickle cell disease, and hearing loss, within days of birth, when intervention can prevent or dramatically reduce disability. Newborns have no symptoms; the screening finds disease before damage occurs.

### Tertiary prevention, limiting damage from established disease

**Tertiary prevention** acts on people who already have a diagnosed condition. The goal is not to undo the disease, it is to prevent complications, slow progression, improve function, and maintain quality of life.

**Cardiac rehabilitation** is a Grade A-supported tertiary-prevention program for patients after a heart attack or cardiac surgery. It combines supervised exercise, education about medication adherence and diet, and psychological support. Patients who complete cardiac rehab have lower mortality rates and fewer rehospitalizations than those who do not.

**Stroke rehabilitation** uses physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy to help patients recover function after a stroke. Every hour of rehabilitation in the early post-stroke period matters, the window for neuroplasticity-driven recovery is real.

**Diabetes self-management education** helps people with type 2 diabetes control blood glucose, recognize complications early, and prevent the downstream damage, neuropathy, nephropathy, retinopathy, limb amputation, that uncontrolled diabetes produces. Tertiary prevention here does not reverse diabetes; it prevents the disease from producing worse disease.

Mental health relapse prevention, medication adherence, therapy, and peer support for people with serious mental illness, is tertiary prevention in a domain that is often underfunded relative to the need.

**Falls prevention programs** for older adults at risk, including balance training (evidence-based Tai Chi programs) and home-safety assessments, reduce injury from falls that cause the most hospitalizations and functional decline in people over 65.

### Individual strategies and population strategies

Some prevention strategies act at the individual level: PrEP adherence, personal tobacco cessation, attending one's own cancer screenings. These depend on the individual's choices, access, and motivation.

Other strategies require population-level infrastructure that no individual can build alone: clean municipal water, seatbelt laws, fluoride in water systems, tobacco taxation, herd immunity from vaccination campaigns. The **Community Guide**, evidence reviews from the Community Preventive Services Task Force, synthesizes the evidence on community-level strategies and informs policy.

Effective disease prevention typically requires both. A tobacco-cessation program that only offers individual counseling, without addressing nicotine-product marketing restrictions, tobacco taxation, or smoke-free air laws, produces smaller effects than one that layers both levels. The strongest public-health programs combine individual-level and population-level strategies at all three prevention levels.

### Cost, access, and justice

Prevention strategies are only effective if they reach the people who need them. Insurance coverage, geographic distance, language access, transportation, and cost all function as barriers that determine who actually benefits from evidence-based prevention. The USPSTF Grade A and B mandate for cost-free coverage under the Affordable Care Act is designed specifically to remove financial barriers, but it only applies to insured patients.

Understanding access barriers is not supplemental material: an intervention that exists but is unreachable is functionally the same as an intervention that doesn't exist for the patient in front of you.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A 47-year-old patient at a community health clinic tells her MA: "My doctor told me I should get a colonoscopy but I haven't gotten around to it." The MA flags this in the chart. The nurse practitioner, reviewing it, notes the patient is three years overdue on colorectal cancer screening (secondary prevention, Grade A recommendation for her age), has a BMI of 34 and a family history of type 2 diabetes (two primary-prevention targets, weight management and diabetes risk reduction, for which she has never had formal counseling), and has poorly controlled hypertension (a tertiary-prevention priority, medication adherence, low-sodium diet education). All three levels of prevention are relevant to this one patient at this one visit. The NP addresses all three in order of urgency, starting with the overdue screening.

### Key terms

**Primary prevention**: interventions that stop disease before it starts (vaccines, seatbelts, hand hygiene, PrEP, smoking cessation).

**Secondary prevention**: interventions that catch disease early through screening (mammography, colonoscopy, BP check, newborn screen).

**Tertiary prevention**: interventions that limit damage after disease is established (cardiac rehab, stroke rehab, diabetes self-management).

**USPSTF grade**: letters A, B, C, D, or I that rate the strength of a prevention recommendation and the evidence behind it; drives insurer coverage.

**ACIP schedule**: the CDC Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices schedule of recommended childhood and adult vaccines.

**Newborn screening panel**: Texas-mandated state newborn screens; a secondary-prevention infrastructure example.

**PrEP**: pre-exposure prophylaxis for HIV; a pharmacologic primary-prevention strategy for people at high risk.

**Cardiac rehabilitation**: a supervised exercise, education, and counseling program after a cardiac event; a Grade A tertiary-prevention program.

**Herd immunity**: population-level primary prevention achieved when enough individuals are immune to interrupt transmission chains.

**The Community Guide**: evidence reviews from the Community Preventive Services Task Force on community-level prevention strategies.

### Chapter summary

Disease prevention requires specific, named, evidence-based strategies, not just awareness that prevention matters. Primary prevention stops disease before it starts through immunization, behavioral change, environmental safety, hand hygiene, and pharmacologic tools like PrEP. Secondary prevention catches disease during the preclinical window using USPSTF-graded screenings and newborn testing programs. Tertiary prevention limits damage and complications once disease is established through rehabilitation, self-management education, and relapse-prevention programs. Effective prevention uses strategies at all three levels, at both individual and population scales, and requires addressing the access barriers that determine who benefits. The USPSTF grading system is the tool for evaluating whether a specific intervention is backed by enough evidence to recommend.

### Review questions

1. A 58-year-old male patient who smoked one pack per day for 30 years stopped smoking 3 years ago. According to USPSTF guidelines, is he eligible for any secondary-prevention screening that specifically relates to his smoking history? Name the screening, the age range it covers, and what it looks for.
2. Explain why "everyone should get this screening" is almost never the right way to describe a secondary-prevention recommendation. Use the concept of USPSTF grading and one specific example of a recommendation that applies to a defined population, not all patients.
3. Pick one disease (type 2 diabetes, colorectal cancer, or cardiovascular disease). Identify one evidence-based strategy at each of the three prevention levels for that disease. Then explain in 2–3 sentences why using only one level, even if done perfectly, would produce worse outcomes than using all three together.
