# Professional Organizations, Credentialing Bodies, and How Credentials Are Made

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Distinguish a professional organization from a credentialing body and identify examples of each
- Explain how professional organizations prepare practitioners and govern credentialing standards
- Describe the role of accreditation in connecting education programs to individual certification eligibility
- Evaluate a credential for legitimacy using publicly available tools

A nursing student receives a mailer advertising a "Healthcare Professional Certification" for $99 and a weekend online course. She is excited until her instructor asks one question: "Is the certifying body accredited by the NCCA?" She looks it up. It is not. The credential would not be recognized by a single employer in Texas, and might signal to hiring managers that she does not know how to screen credentials. Understanding who makes credentials, and how to check whether they are legitimate, is a practical skill with direct consequences for your career.

### Two entities, two different jobs

Every health profession has at least one **professional organization**, a body that advocates for practitioners, publishes standards, develops educational content, and builds the professional community. Examples include the American Nurses Association (ANA), the American Medical Association (AMA), the American Association of Physician Associates (AAPA), the American Physical Therapy Association (APTA), the American Occupational Therapy Association (AOTA), the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA), and the American Association for Respiratory Care (AARC).

Separately, sometimes under the same roof, more often in a distinct organization, each profession also has a **credentialing body**: the entity that develops exams, determines who is eligible to sit for them, issues credentials after passing, and sets the rules for renewal. Examples include the National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN, which administers NCLEX), the American Nurses Credentialing Center (ANCC), the American Registry of Radiologic Technologists (ARRT), the ASCP Board of Certification, and the National Commission on Certification of Physician Assistants (NCCPA).

These two entities are not the same thing, and confusing them leads to real errors. The ANA advocates for nurses in the US Senate. The NCSBN writes the NCLEX and decides who passes it. If you want to influence nursing policy, you join ANA. If you want to sit for the nursing licensure exam, you register through NCSBN. They have different missions, different structures, and different authority over your career.

### What professional organizations actually do

Professional organizations work on multiple levels at once. Understanding their work helps you evaluate which ones are worth joining and what their credentials actually represent.

**Preparing practitioners**, Professional organizations set the standards that accreditation bodies use to evaluate education programs. The standards the APTA publishes influence what a physical therapy program must include to receive CAPTE accreditation. They publish scope-of-practice documents that define what members are authorized to do in clinical practice. They produce continuing-education content, conferences, journals, online modules, that practitioners use to maintain their skills and their credentials.

**Governing credentialing and certification**, Many professional organizations define the blueprint for what a certification exam tests by conducting regular job-task analyses (surveys of what practitioners actually do in practice). They set the ethical standards that certified practitioners must follow. They establish the continuing-education requirements for renewal. In some cases they also investigate and publicly discipline certified holders who violate those standards, which is why a credential from a reputable credentialing body carries actual weight with employers.

**Advocating**, Professional organizations lobby state and federal legislatures on scope-of-practice laws, reimbursement rules, staffing ratios, and workforce policies. The outcome of that advocacy directly affects what you can do legally in a clinical role and how much you are paid for it.

**Publishing**, Most major professional organizations publish peer-reviewed journals and position statements. These are primary sources for clinical practice guidelines and are what researchers and educators cite.

### The special case: when the organization is also the credentialer

For most professions, the professional organization and the credentialing body are separate. The ANA does not administer NCLEX; the NCSBN does. The AMA does not administer USMLE; the NBME and FSMB do in partnership.

But for some professions, the professional organization runs the credentialing operation directly. The American Association of Medical Assistants (AAMA) both advocates for medical assistants and administers the Certified Medical Assistant (CMA) credential. The American Association of Nurse Anesthesiology (AANA) both represents CRNAs and governs their credential through an affiliated board.

The separation that exists in most professions exists for a reason: a credentialing body must make decisions that are psychometrically sound and legally defensible. If the same organization is also advocating for its members' economic interests, there is a risk of conflict. When the two are combined in a single organization, that organization must maintain internal walls between the advocacy function and the credentialing function to preserve the credential's integrity.

### Accreditation of programs vs. certification of individuals

There is a third player in the credentialing ecosystem that students often overlook: the **accreditation** body. Accreditation bodies evaluate and recognize education programs, not individuals, that meet defined quality standards. The Commission on Collegiate Nursing Education (CCNE) and the Accreditation Commission for Education in Nursing (ACEN) accredit nursing programs. The Liaison Committee on Medical Education (LCME) accredits medical schools. The Accreditation Review Commission on Education for the Physician Assistant (ARC-PA) accredits PA programs. The Commission on Accreditation of Allied Health Education Programs (CAAHEP) accredits a wide range of allied health programs.

The reason this matters to you: most credentialing bodies require that you graduate from an accredited program before you are eligible to sit for their exam. The sequence is: accredited program → graduate → eligible for certification exam → credential. If your program is not accredited by a recognized body, you may complete every course and clinical hour and still be ineligible to take the exam your career requires. Accreditation is not a formality, it is the gate.

### How to check whether a credential is legitimate

Not every credential offered in healthcare is worth holding. Some are offered by organizations with no independent oversight, no job-task analysis behind the exam, and no employer recognition. The fastest screen is accreditation.

The **National Commission for Certifying Agencies (NCCA)** and its parent organization, the **Institute for Credentialing Excellence (ICE)**, accredit certification programs that meet rigorous standards for exam development, security, and governance. If a certifying body's credential is NCCA-accredited, that is a signal that the exam is defensible and the credential is recognized. The NCCA database is publicly searchable.

The US Department of Education maintains a database of recognized accreditors for education programs. Cross-checking a program's claimed accreditation against this database takes five minutes and can save years.

A credential from a **diploma mill**, a non-accredited body that issues credentials without meeting standards, is not neutral. It is a liability on a resume: it signals either that the holder did not know how to screen credentials, or that they knew and accepted the shortcut.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> Marcus is researching a career in radiologic technology. He finds two credentials online: the R.T.(R) from ARRT and a "Certified Imaging Specialist" from a company he has never heard of. He runs both through the NCCA database. ARRT appears; the other does not. He checks the US Department of Education accreditor database for the program offering the second credential. It is not listed. He closes the tab and focuses his research on AAS programs accredited through JRCERT and the ARRT pathway. He will spend two years and real money on this credential. He checks before he commits.

### The student on-ramp

You do not have to wait until you hold a credential to engage with the professional organizations that govern your field. **HOSA, Future Health Professionals** is the student organization at the secondary and post-secondary level specifically designed for CTE students in Texas and across the country. HOSA membership provides access to competitive events, leadership development, and professional-organization networks at a student rate. Most national professional organizations also offer discounted student memberships. Engaging now, while you are still in school, is the fastest way to understand what the profession expects, before you are expected to deliver it.

### Key terms

**Professional organization**: an advocacy, education, and standards-setting body for practitioners (ANA, AMA, AAPA, APTA); may or may not also be the credentialing body.

**Credentialing body**: the entity that develops exams, determines eligibility, and issues credentials (NCSBN, ANCC, ARRT, ASCP, NCCPA).

**Accreditation**: the recognition that an education program meets standards (CCNE, ACEN, LCME, ARC-PA, CAAHEP); typically required for graduates to be certification-eligible.

**Certification (individual)**: voluntary recognition that an individual has met a credentialing body's competency standards after completing an accredited education.

**Scope of practice (professional-organization role)**: the document a professional organization publishes defining what members are authorized to do.

**NCSBN**: National Council of State Boards of Nursing; the credentialing body that administers NCLEX, separate from ANA, the professional organization.

**NCCA / ICE**: National Commission for Certifying Agencies and Institute for Credentialing Excellence; accreditors of certification programs themselves.

**HOSA**: Future Health Professionals; the student organization at the secondary and post-secondary level, relevant to Texas CTE.

**Diploma mill**: a non-accredited program issuing credentials without meeting standards; accreditation is the fastest screen against these.

### Chapter summary

Every health profession operates through two related but distinct structures: a professional organization that advocates, educates, and sets standards, and a credentialing body that develops exams, issues credentials, and enforces renewal requirements. In most professions these are separate, the ANA and NCSBN in nursing, for example. In some professions they are combined, which requires careful internal governance to protect the credential's integrity. Accreditation of education programs is the gate between completing a program and being eligible for certification: graduates of non-accredited programs are typically ineligible to sit for credentialing exams. Credentials from non-accredited certifying bodies carry little employer value and can damage a resume. The NCCA database and the US Department of Education accreditor database are free tools for checking credential legitimacy. Students in Texas can begin engaging with professional organizations now through HOSA.
