# Connecting Safety Practices to Their Sources

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Trace daily clinical safety practices back to the specific rule or standard that requires them
- Identify the agency that issued or enforces each rule
- Name the most common barriers that cause good safety practices to fail
- Describe a respectful, specific speak-up when a safety rule is being skipped

A student on rotation watches her preceptor prepare a medication. The preceptor asks the patient's name but does not check the armband. The student knows the patient and knows it is the right person. She also knows there is a rule, a specific numbered rule from a named organization, that requires two identifiers, not one. Knowing the rule, knowing where it came from, and knowing how to say something respectfully is exactly what this chapter is about.

### From names to connections

In this course, you have already named the six regulatory agencies (c11A) and the safety standards those agencies produce (c11B). This chapter takes the final step: connecting every daily clinical practice to the specific rule behind it and understanding why good rules sometimes fail in practice.

The verb in the TEKS standard is "relate." That word carries a specific expectation. You are not just listing agencies. You are tracing the line from a practice you perform, or watch, on a clinical rotation to the rule that requires it and the body that enforces it. When you can do that, you understand why the practice matters in a way that pure memorization cannot give you.

### Daily practices and the rules behind them

The table below maps practices a healthcare student will see or perform to their regulatory sources. This is not an exhaustive list. It is the foundation. Every practice a facility requires traces to a source in this network, even if the facility's policy manual does not always cite it explicitly.

Hand hygiene before and after every patient contact traces to the WHO Five Moments for Hand Hygiene and the CDC Guideline for Isolation Precautions. The Joint Commission and CMS have both adopted this guidance into accreditation and payment requirements, so the same practice is reinforced by four bodies simultaneously.

Wearing gloves and a gown for contact-precaution patients traces to both the CDC isolation guideline and OSHA's **29 CFR 1910.1030**, the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard. The CDC defines when precautions are needed; OSHA makes the PPE requirement enforceable as a worker-safety rule.

The **two-patient-identifier rule** before any procedure or medication is TJC National Patient Safety Goal 01.01.01. Two identifiers means two independent pieces of information, typically name and date of birth, confirmed against the patient's armband, not just from memory or a glance at the room number.

The **Universal Protocol**, specifically the surgical **Time-Out**, is a TJC requirement that stops the operating room team before incision to verbally confirm the patient's identity, the planned procedure, and the surgical site. The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist covers similar ground; TJC requires facilities to have a Universal Protocol.

The requirement to avoid "Do Not Use" abbreviations in orders and clinical documentation traces to TJC's Official "Do Not Use" List, supplemented by the Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) list of error-prone abbreviations. These abbreviations appear on the list because they have caused actual patient harm: the abbreviation "U" for units has been misread as a zero, turning a 4U insulin dose into a 40-unit dose.

Specimen labeling at the bedside, not at the nurses' station, traces to TJC NPSGs and to CLIA standards enforced by CMS. Labeling at the bedside, with the patient present and the label confirmed against two identifiers, prevents wrong-patient laboratory errors.

**Verbal-order read-back** (repeating a verbal or telephone medication order back to the prescriber) is required by TJC NPSG 02.03.01 and operationalized through the TeamSTEPPS check-back technique from AHRQ.

Reporting a broken piece of equipment that may have contributed to patient harm is not optional. The FDA's **Medical Device Report (MDR)** regulation, 21 CFR 803, requires facilities to file a report when a medical device is suspected of contributing to a death or serious injury. Annual staff immunizations, including influenza, are recommended by CDC's ACIP and increasingly required by facility policy and some TJC standards.

When a chemical spills, the first reference is the **Safety Data Sheet (SDS)** for that substance, required to be accessible on every unit under OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard (29 CFR 1910.1200). When a needlestick occurs, the response protocol, including wound care, source-patient testing, post-exposure prophylaxis evaluation, and OSHA 300 log entry, is governed by OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard and CDC PEP guidelines together.

### Why good rules fail: adherence barriers

Understanding the rule is only half the challenge. Understanding why the rule gets skipped is the other half, and it is what allows you to design a workplace where the rule is actually followed.

Time pressure is the single most common reason safety practices get shortcut. Hand hygiene, double-checks, and documentation are the most frequent targets. When a unit is understaffed and a worker is responsible for eight patients, the seconds spent at the hand-hygiene dispenser between each contact feel like a meaningful drain. The response to time pressure is not to remind people to try harder. It is to make the safe behavior faster and more automatic, by placing dispensers at the point of care, by removing steps from the process, by redesigning workflow.

**Alert fatigue** is the desensitization that occurs when EHR warning pop-ups, alarms, and notifications fire so frequently that clinical staff stop reading them. When every medication entry triggers four alert overrides, the clinician learns to click through them without reading, and the one alert that actually matters gets dismissed with the rest. Alert fatigue is a system problem, not a personal-failure problem.

**Normalization of deviance** is the process, described by sociologist Diane Vaughan in her analysis of the Space Shuttle Challenger disaster, by which small departures from procedure that do not immediately cause harm gradually become the new normal. A nurse skips one double-check during a busy shift. Nothing bad happens. Over weeks, the skipped double-check becomes the routine. Every staff member who sees it starts to treat it as standard practice. When the harm finally occurs, it was built by dozens of small normalized shortcuts, not one dramatic failure. The **Swiss cheese model** described by James Reason explains the same phenomenon from a system perspective: no single layer of protection has a hole large enough to cause harm alone, but when the holes in multiple layers align, a pathway opens all the way through.

The **authority gradient** is the hierarchy effect that makes a student or junior staff member hesitant to speak up when a more experienced person skips a safety step. The preceptor who skips the armband check has more status, more experience, and more apparent certainty. Speaking up feels presumptuous. AHRQ's TeamSTEPPS program provides two specific tools for this situation: the CUS technique (Concerned, Uncomfortable, Safety issue, stating each in turn) and the two-challenge rule (if a concern is ignored the first time, it must be raised a second time before the action proceeds).

Inconvenient design is a barrier that gets blamed on individuals but is really a system failure. PPE stored in the wrong location, a sharps container that is full, a soap dispenser without soap, a hand-rub dispenser that is empty: each of these is a moment when a busy worker faces a choice between the safe behavior and getting something done. The safe behavior loses more often than anyone wants to admit. The fix is not a reminder. The fix is restocking, relocating, and redesigning.

### Why students matter in safety culture

Students on clinical rotation have a capability that experienced staff have largely lost: fresh eyes. A practice that has been normalized over years is invisible to the people practicing it. A student who knows the standards sees the gap. That capacity is not a liability or a sign of inexperience. It is a genuine contribution to the safety of patients.

Speaking up on a clinical rotation is not overstepping. It is practicing the behavior that licensed practitioners are expected to perform every shift. The student who respectfully raises a concern about a skipped identifier check is building the professional reflex that will protect patients for a career.

When you do not know the source of a rule, the path to finding it is direct. Ask your preceptor or supervisor what rule a specific practice traces to. Search the facility's policy library, most facilities cite CDC, OSHA, TJC, and CMS in their policy footers. Go to the agency website directly: CDC, OSHA, FDA, TJC, and CMS publish their standards openly and at no cost. When two rules overlap and seem to conflict, the stricter standard controls.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> Over several shifts, a student notices that a preceptor has stopped performing two-identifier checks before administering medications, explaining that it "slows down the med pass." On the fourth shift, two other nurses have adopted the same shortcut. The student recognizes the pattern: a safety practice is being normalized out of use in real time. On the fifth shift, the student uses the CUS framework: "I'm Concerned that we haven't been checking two identifiers before meds. I'm Uncomfortable because I know this is a TJC requirement from NPSG 01.01.01. I think this is a Safety issue we need to address." The preceptor is initially defensive, then thanks the student at the end of the shift. The behavior returns to standard. The **Sentinel Event** that would have resulted from a wrong-patient medication error did not happen.

### Key terms

**Two-patient-identifier rule:** TJC National Patient Safety Goal 01.01.01 requiring two independent patient identifiers confirmed before any procedure or medication administration.

**Universal Protocol:** TJC's pre-procedure protocol, including a site marking and surgical Time-Out, designed to prevent wrong-site, wrong-procedure, and wrong-patient surgery.

**Time-Out:** the required pause before any surgical incision when the team verbally confirms the patient's identity, the planned procedure, and the marked surgical site.

**Verbal-order read-back:** TJC NPSG 02.03.01 requirement that the person receiving a verbal or telephone order repeats it back to the prescriber for confirmation before acting on it.

**Medical Device Report (MDR):** the mandatory FDA report, filed under 21 CFR 803, required when a medical device is suspected of contributing to a patient death or serious injury.

**Sentinel Event:** a Joint Commission-defined serious patient safety event that results in death, permanent harm, or severe temporary harm, triggering a required root-cause analysis.

**Normalization of deviance:** the process by which repeated small departures from safe procedure, without immediate harmful consequence, become accepted as the new normal practice.

**Swiss cheese model:** James Reason's framework explaining how system accidents occur when the holes in multiple independent layers of protection happen to align, creating an unobstructed pathway to harm.

**Alert fatigue:** the desensitization to EHR and monitoring alarms that occurs when warnings fire so frequently that clinical staff stop reading them carefully.

**Authority gradient:** the hierarchical dynamic that makes junior staff hesitant to speak up when a more senior person is skipping a safety step; addressed through tools like CUS and the two-challenge rule.

**Just Culture:** the accountability framework, described by David Marx, that distinguishes human error from at-risk behavior from reckless behavior, allowing organizations to respond proportionally and preserve safe reporting.

### Chapter summary

Safety practices in healthcare are not arbitrary rules. Each one traces to a specific standard, issued by an identifiable agency, for a documented reason. Hand hygiene is WHO and CDC science, enforced through TJC and CMS. Two patient identifiers is TJC NPSG 01.01.01. Sharps handling is OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030. Device-failure reporting is FDA 21 CFR 803. Knowing those sources lets you understand why the practice matters, defend it when it is being skipped, and find the answer when you encounter a practice whose source you do not yet know. Good rules fail because of time pressure, alert fatigue, normalization of deviance, authority gradient, and inconvenient design. The fixes for those barriers are system-level changes, not personal reminders. Students who enter clinical settings knowing these connections arrive ready to protect patients from day one.

### Review questions

1. A coworker uses the abbreviation "QD" in a handwritten medication note. Identify the specific organization whose official list prohibits this abbreviation, explain why the abbreviation is dangerous, and describe in 2 to 3 sentences what a respectful speak-up using the CUS framework would sound like in this situation.
2. A student observes that a sharps container in a patient room is full and overfilled needles are being placed on the counter beside it. Identify the OSHA standard that is being violated, explain what specific harm the violation creates, and propose one system-level fix (not a reminder to staff) that would prevent the condition from recurring.
3. Explain normalization of deviance using a specific healthcare example. Then apply the Swiss cheese model to show how the normalized deviance could eventually combine with other system failures to cause patient harm. What is one point in the sequence where a student's speak-up could interrupt the chain?

### References

- **All references from c11A (WHO, CDC, OSHA, FDA, TJC, NIH)** apply here; c11C is the integrative standard.
- **The Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals (current year):** updated annually; the operational source for many bedside safety practices including two-identifier checks, verbal-order read-back, and specimen labeling.
- **Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) List of Error-Prone Abbreviations, Symbols, and Dose Designations:** the ISMP list that informs the TJC "Do Not Use" list.
- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0:** operational speak-up tools including CUS and the two-challenge rule for addressing authority gradient and adherence barriers.
- **Diane Vaughan, The Challenger Launch Decision:** canonical sociological source for normalization of deviance as a safety-culture failure mode.
- **James Reason, Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents:** the Swiss cheese model of how system defenses fail; foundational for understanding how safety practices relate to system design.
- **AHRQ Patient Safety Network (PSNet):** current case studies, commentaries, and evidence on how safety practices function in real US healthcare settings.
- **Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS):** state-level enforcement and reporting for many of the safety practices covered in this chapter.
