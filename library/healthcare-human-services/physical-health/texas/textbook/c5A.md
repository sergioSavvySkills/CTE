# Researching and Evaluating Healthcare Career Options

---
**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the primary authoritative sources for US healthcare career and employment information
- Evaluate a career-information source for currency, authority, and bias
- Interpret occupational data — duties, education, credentials, wages, and outlook — accurately and without common misreading errors
---

A student researches nursing salaries and finds four different numbers: $58,000 on one site, $75,000 on another, $89,000 on a third, and a recruiter's starting offer of $42,000. None of them are wrong. All of them are useless without knowing what each number actually measures, where it comes from, and what it means for her specific situation in Texas. Finding career information is easy. Evaluating and interpreting it is the skill.

### Locate, evaluate, interpret — a three-step sequence

Career research has three distinct steps that are often collapsed into one, which is where the confusion starts. **Locating** information means finding it. **Evaluating** means assessing whether it is trustworthy for the purpose. **Interpreting** means reading it correctly once you know it's reliable. Skipping evaluation — going straight from finding a number to trusting it — is how students end up planning around salary aggregator data that reflects California wages when they intend to work in Lubbock.

### The primary authoritative sources

Not all career information is equally reliable. For healthcare career research, four primary sources are the starting point before any other.

The **Occupational Outlook Handbook (OOH)**, published by the US Bureau of Labor Statistics (bls.gov/ooh), is the federal government's official occupational reference. It is updated annually and covers every major healthcare occupation with standardized data: a summary of typical duties, the most common entry-level education, any required licenses or certifications, the national median annual wage, and a 10-year employment projection. Because it is federal and methodology-consistent across every occupation, it is the fairest comparison tool available.

**O\*NET OnLine** (onetonline.org) is the US Department of Labor's occupational database and goes deeper than the OOH on skills, knowledge, work activities, and work context. Where the OOH tells you what a physical therapist's median wage is, O*NET tells you which specific skills a physical therapist uses most frequently, what tasks fill a typical day, and what work conditions to expect. The two sources complement each other.

**CareerOneStop** (careeronestop.org), also run by the US Department of Labor, is a career-exploration and job-search portal that links to both OOH and O*NET data and adds local job postings, training-program finders, and state-level wage data.

**Professional organization career pages** — the American Nurses Association for nursing, the American Association of Medical Assistants for medical assisting, the American Society for Clinical Pathology for laboratory science, and so on — provide role-specific depth that federal sources sometimes lack: specialty certifications, typical career ladders, and industry-written descriptions of the work. These sources have a promotional tone (they want people to enter their profession), but their factual content on education and credentials is generally accurate and current.

For Texas-specific data, the **Texas Workforce Commission Labor Market Information** portal (twc.texas.gov/lmi) provides state and regional wage data that often differs substantially from national figures.

### Sources to use with caution

Salary-aggregator sites — Glassdoor, Indeed, Salary.com, and similar platforms — publish wage data that is compiled from user reports, employer postings, and surveys. They are not authoritative, but they are often current and reflect recent market conditions. The gap between BLS median and aggregator data can exceed 20% for the same occupation, partly because BLS lags real-time markets by 1–2 years and partly because aggregators capture self-selected respondents rather than a representative sample. Use them for context and trend signal, not for a planning number.

Single-employer or single-school pages are marketing materials. The wages they post may be accurate for their specific institution, but they tell you nothing about the market overall.

### Evaluating a source: the CRAAP check

The **CRAAP framework** — Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose — gives a systematic way to evaluate any information source [ACRL CRAAP Framework]. For healthcare career research, three of the five matter most:

**Currency** matters because healthcare wages and employment projections change. An OOH entry from three years ago may be significantly off. Always check the date; BLS identifies the publication year on every occupation page.

**Authority** is the question of who produced the information and why. A federal agency with a public mandate to report labor market data objectively is more authoritative than an employer trying to recruit or a school trying to fill enrollment slots.

**Purpose** asks what the source is trying to do. Informing you, recruiting you, and selling you training are three different purposes, and only the first is compatible with unbiased data.

### Interpreting wage data correctly

The most common misreading of career data is treating the median as a guaranteed wage or a starting salary. The **median wage** is the midpoint of the wage distribution — half of all workers in that occupation earn more, and half earn less. It says nothing about entry-level pay or about wages in a specific state or city.

Geographic variation is large in healthcare. A registered nurse in rural West Texas earns substantially less than an RN in the Houston Medical Center, who earns substantially less than an RN in San Francisco. Before using any wage figure for planning, confirm the geographic scope: is this a national median, a Texas median, or a specific city figure?

**Total compensation** is broader than hourly wage. Employer-paid health insurance, retirement matching, shift differentials, and tuition reimbursement are common in healthcare and can add $10,000–$20,000 of annual value above the stated salary. A lower starting wage with strong benefits may be more financially advantageous than a higher wage with no benefits.

### Interpreting employment projections correctly

The BLS phrases its 10-year projections in standardized language: "much faster than average," "faster than average," "as fast as average," "slower than average," and "decline." Each phrase corresponds to a specific numeric growth-rate range. "Much faster than average" does not mean the job is abundant or easy to enter — it means the percentage growth rate is above a specific threshold. A small occupation growing 25% may still produce fewer new jobs than a large occupation growing 6% [BLS OOH Healthcare].

Always pair the growth rate with the reason behind it. Healthcare occupations currently growing fastest are those tied to chronic-disease management, aging-population care, and outpatient care migration — knowing the reason tells you whether the growth is structural (lasting) or situational (temporary).

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> Jaylen is researching a career as a respiratory therapist. He finds the BLS OOH page, which lists the 2023 national median wage as $70,540 and projects 13% growth over 10 years. He then checks the Texas Workforce Commission LMI page, which shows the Texas median at $61,820 — $8,700 lower than the national figure. He checks Glassdoor and sees user-reported data averaging $74,000 for Texas RTs, but notes the sample size is 312 self-selected respondents. He decides to plan around the TWC figure as the most authoritative Texas-specific number, use the Glassdoor data as a signal that experienced RTs may earn above the median, and note the 13% growth rate alongside the reason — aging population with increased prevalence of COPD and asthma — to assess whether that growth is likely to continue.

### Key terms

**Occupational Outlook Handbook (OOH)** — the BLS federal reference (bls.gov/ooh) for occupational duties, education, wages, and 10-year projections, updated annually.

**O\*NET** — the US Department of Labor occupational database (onetonline.org) with detailed skills, knowledge, and work-activity descriptors.

**Median wage** — the midpoint of the wage distribution; half of workers earn more, half earn less. Not a starting salary or a guaranteed number.

**Employment projection** — BLS's 10-year growth estimate for an occupation; expressed in standardized language with specific numeric thresholds.

**CRAAP framework** — Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose; standard criteria for evaluating information-source credibility.

**Primary source** — a federal agency or professional organization publication; more authoritative than salary aggregators or employer marketing.

**Salary aggregator** — sites like Glassdoor or Indeed that compile self-reported wage data; useful for trend context, not authoritative for planning.

**Texas Workforce Commission LMI** — Texas-specific wage, demand, and training-provider data (twc.texas.gov/lmi).

### Chapter summary

Effective career research requires three steps: locating information, evaluating it, and interpreting it correctly. The most authoritative sources for healthcare careers are the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook, O*NET, CareerOneStop, and professional-organization career pages — supplemented by the Texas Workforce Commission for state-specific data. Salary aggregators are current but not authoritative; single-employer and school pages are promotional. When interpreting data, never confuse median wage with entry-level or starting salary, always check the geographic scope, and pair employment growth rates with the underlying driver to assess whether the growth is structural.

### Review questions

1. You find two wage figures for medical assistants in Texas: BLS OOH shows $38,270 (national median) and the Texas Workforce Commission shows $34,150. Which figure is more useful for planning a career in San Antonio, and why?
2. A website states that healthcare "is one of the fastest-growing industries" and lists a 22% job-growth figure. Using the CRAAP framework, what three questions would you ask before trusting that figure?
3. A recruiter at a community college says their radiologic technology graduates "start at $55,000." What additional information do you need to evaluate that claim usefully?
