# Using Precise Language in Clinical Communication

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Replace vague verbs and approximate values with specific, measurable clinical language
- Formulate responses that answer the question asked, not a related question
- Apply precision rules to verbal orders, handoffs, and documentation
- Identify and correct "Do Not Use" abbreviation violations in written orders

A nurse documents: "Patient seemed uncomfortable, some redness was noted near the IV site." A second nurse reading that note an hour later does not know whether "some redness" means a 1 cm blush or a 5 cm area of warmth and induration, and she does not know whether "uncomfortable" means the patient winced when the site was touched or requested pain medication. She treats it conservatively and monitors. Six hours later, a phlebitis that could have been caught earlier is now clinically significant. No one lied. No one meant to cause harm. But imprecise language allowed a gap in care to go unaddressed.

### Precision is a distinct clinical requirement

Clarity and precision are related but different. Clarity means the message is understandable on the first read. Precision means the specific words chosen are accurate and measurable, leaving no room for the reader or listener to substitute their own interpretation. A note can be perfectly clear and still be imprecise: "the patient's blood pressure is high" is clear, but a clinician reading it does not know whether to call the physician.

The standard for clinical communication is both. Clear enough that any reader can follow the message. Precise enough that no reader has to guess at the specifics.

Precision is a patient-safety requirement, not a writing preference. Verbal orders, telephone communication, shift handoffs, and medication orders all depend on unambiguous language. When precision fails in any one of those contexts, the consequences are not a misunderstanding. They are a medication error, a delayed intervention, or a continuity-of-care break between shifts.

### Specific verbs over vague ones

**Specific verbs** name the exact action. **Vague verbs** name a general category of action and leave the reader to infer the specifics. In clinical contexts, the inference is often wrong.

"The nurse gave the medication" and "the nurse administered 5 mg of morphine sulfate IV at 14:15" both describe an event. The first is legally and clinically useless in a conflict or review. The second is a record.

Common vague verbs and their specific replacements:

- "gave" becomes "administered"
- "felt" becomes "palpated"
- "walked" becomes "ambulated 50 feet in the hallway"
- "checked" becomes "auscultated" or "palpated" or "assessed," depending on what was actually done
- "put on" becomes "applied"
- "stopped" becomes "discontinued"
- "changed" becomes "titrated" or "adjusted," depending on the action
- "went up" becomes "escalated"

The pattern is the same in every case: the vague verb names a category, and the specific verb names the actual clinical action. Choosing the specific verb is not about using longer words. It is about naming what actually happened.

### Measurable values over approximations

A **measurable value** is a quantified observation. An approximation is a qualitative judgment. In clinical communication, approximations create documentation gaps that cannot be reliably filled in later.

"Blood pressure is up" versus "BP 162/98." "A small cut" versus "a 2 cm linear laceration on the dorsal forearm, 3 cm proximal to the wrist." "The patient urinated" versus "the patient voided 280 mL clear yellow urine." "The wound looks better" versus "the wound margin is approximated, no erythema, no exudate, decreased from 2.5 cm to 1.8 cm since yesterday."

Each specific version answers questions the approximate version does not. "Blood pressure is up" does not tell the next clinician whether this is a new trend or a one-time reading. "BP 162/98" does. Measurable values also create a record that can be compared over time, which is the basis of clinical monitoring.

### Answering the question asked

**Precise language** requires first hearing the question precisely. A precise answer to a different question is not precision. It is misdirection.

When a physician asks, "What is the patient's current urine output?" the precise answer names a number per unit of time: "280 mL over the last 4 hours." An answer of "the patient voided earlier and the family says she seems okay" does not answer the question asked. It also does not demonstrate clinical precision. It demonstrates avoidance of the question, which in a clinical context raises its own concerns.

Formulating a response means hearing the question, identifying what it is actually asking, and then constructing an answer that addresses that specific question. If the clinician does not have the answer, the precise response is "I don't have that number in front of me, I'll pull the chart and call you back within 5 minutes." That answer is both honest and precise.

### Precision in verbal orders and the "Do Not Use" list

**Verbal orders** carry the highest precision risk in clinical communication. A spoken order that is misheard, misunderstood, or imprecisely delivered can result in the wrong drug, the wrong dose, or the wrong route reaching the patient. The Joint Commission's NPSG 02.03.01 requires read-back for verbal orders precisely because the sender and receiver may both believe communication was successful when it was not.

A **closed-loop confirmation (check-back)** closes the precision loop: the receiver repeats the order exactly as heard, the sender confirms or corrects. "I'm ordering morphine 2 mg IV now." "Read back: morphine 2 milligrams IV now." "That's correct." The loop is closed. Without the read-back, both clinicians might assume the order was received correctly when a digit or unit was wrong.

The **"Do Not Use" list** from the Joint Commission and ISMP represents the abbreviation and notation errors that precision failures in written orders have caused. The list exists because each item on it has been directly linked to a patient-harm event. Writing "U" instead of "units" has caused insulin overdoses. Writing ".5 mg" instead of "0.5 mg" has caused tenfold dosing errors when the decimal point was missed. These are not hypothetical risks. They are documented events with documented victims.

The items healthcare workers must write correctly:

- "U" for units, write "units"
- "IU" for international units, write "international units"
- "QD" for daily, write "daily"
- "QOD" for every other day, write "every other day"
- A trailing zero after a whole number (5.0 mg), write "5 mg"
- A missing leading zero before a decimal (.5 mg), write "0.5 mg"
- "MS," "MSO4," "MgSO4," write "morphine sulfate" or "magnesium sulfate"
- "μg" for micrograms, write "mcg"

**SNOMED CT** and other controlled clinical terminologies used in electronic health records enforce precision by presenting standardized options rather than free-text fields. When a clinician must select a term from a controlled vocabulary, spelling variation and idiosyncratic abbreviations cannot enter the record. Controlled vocabularies are one of the structural solutions to the precision problem that individual care cannot fully solve.

### Precision with patients: pairs, not replacements

Precision is not a license for jargon when speaking with patients. With a peer clinician, "the patient has bilateral lower-extremity pitting edema rated 2+" is precise and appropriate. With the patient themselves, that sentence is not communication. It is exclusion.

**Medical terminology** paired with a plain-language explanation is the standard for patient communication. "You have swelling in both legs, a type of swelling where if you press on the skin it leaves a dent for a few seconds. That can be a sign that the heart is having trouble pumping fluid out." The precise term is said. The meaning is explained. The patient can now engage with their own care.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> During a telephone triage call, a medical assistant asks the patient's parent how high the child's fever has been. The parent says "pretty high." The MA knows that "pretty high" is not a number she can chart or relay to the physician. She asks a follow-up: "Can you tell me the exact number from the thermometer?" The parent says 103.2. The MA documents: "Parent reports temperature of 103.2 degrees Fahrenheit by oral thermometer at approximately 1 p.m. today." She does not write "child has a high fever." She writes the number, the method, and the time. When she reads back to the physician, she reads those facts, not her interpretation of them. The loop closes with precision.

### Key terms

**Precise language:** specific verbs and measurable values used instead of vague approximations, in both spoken and written clinical communication.

**Specific verb:** an exact action word ("administered," "palpated," "ambulated") used in place of a general verb ("gave," "felt," "walked").

**Measurable value:** a quantified observation (BP 162/98, voided 280 mL, 2 cm laceration) used in place of qualitative approximation ("blood pressure is up," "voided," "small cut").

**Medical terminology:** the controlled clinical vocabulary used correctly and in the right context; paired with plain-language translations for patient communication.

**Verbal order:** a spoken medication or treatment order that requires read-back under Joint Commission NPSG 02.03.01 because spoken words cannot be verified without confirmation.

**Closed-loop confirmation (check-back):** the TeamSTEPPS technique in which the receiver repeats the message precisely so the sender can confirm or correct it.

**"Do Not Use" list:** the ISMP and Joint Commission list of prohibited abbreviations and dose-designation errors (including U, IU, QD, trailing zeros, and missing leading zeros) that are directly tied to patient-harm events.

**SNOMED CT:** a standardized clinical terminology system used in US electronic health records to enforce controlled vocabulary and reduce free-text precision errors.

### Chapter summary

Precision in clinical communication is a patient-safety requirement. Specific verbs name the exact action and replace vague general verbs. Measurable values replace qualitative approximations and create a record that can be compared and monitored over time. Formulating a precise response requires hearing the question accurately first and answering what was actually asked. Verbal orders carry the highest precision risk and require closed-loop read-back confirmation. The "Do Not Use" list bans abbreviations and dose-notation patterns that have caused documented patient harm, including trailing zeros, missing leading zeros, and ambiguous abbreviations for drugs and units. Controlled clinical vocabularies reduce precision errors structurally. With patients, precision means pairing technical terms with plain-language explanations, not replacing one with the other.

### Review questions

1. A shift note reads: "Patient didn't look great this morning, gave some pain meds, walked with help." Rewrite this entry using specific verbs and measurable values. Identify every change you made and state why each change improves the record.
2. A physician gives you a verbal order over the phone: "Give point five milligrams of lorazepam IV now." Write out exactly what you say during read-back, and explain what rule requires this step.
3. A medication order reads "5.0 mg warfarin po QD." Identify every "Do Not Use" violation, name the rule each violates, and rewrite the order correctly.
