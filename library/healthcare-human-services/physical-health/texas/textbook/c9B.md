# Ethical Principles and Confidentiality in Healthcare

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Name and explain the four core principles of healthcare ethics
- Describe the legal and professional framework for patient confidentiality, including HIPAA
- Classify data elements as protected health information or not
- Identify the personal, professional, and institutional consequences of a confidentiality breach

A nursing student finishes a challenging shift in the pediatric unit and texts a classmate: "Worst day. Had a 7-year-old with leukemia in room 14, mom is a mess." The message contains a patient's age, diagnosis, location, and enough detail that anyone who knows the hospital could potentially identify the child. The student did not mean to cause harm. She caused a HIPAA breach anyway. Understanding why starts with understanding the ethical principles that make confidentiality matter in the first place.

## Part 1: The Four Core Principles of Healthcare Ethics

### Where the principles come from

In 1979, philosophers Tom Beauchamp and James Childress published *Principles of Biomedical Ethics*. The four principles they described have since become the standard vocabulary of healthcare ethics in the United States. Every healthcare role, every professional code, every ethics committee, and every ethics-consultation service in the country uses these four principles as the starting framework. Students who learn them here will encounter them again in every clinical setting they enter.

The principles do not tell you what to do in every situation. They give you a vocabulary for identifying what is at stake and a framework for making decisions you can explain and defend.

### The four principles

**Autonomy** is the patient's right to make decisions about their own body and care, including decisions the clinician would choose differently. Autonomy is not conditional on the patient making a good choice. A patient who refuses a recommended surgery, declines chemotherapy, or leaves against medical advice is exercising the same right as a patient who agrees to every recommendation. The operational expressions of autonomy are informed consent, where the patient agrees to treatment after adequate disclosure, and informed refusal, where the patient declines treatment under the same conditions. Both are equally valid.

**Beneficence** is the duty to act in the patient's best interest. It sounds straightforward, but in practice it requires balancing benefit against burden, cost, side effects, and the patient's own goals. A treatment that extends life by six weeks but causes significant suffering throughout is not automatically beneficial. Beneficence means taking the patient's perspective on benefit seriously, not just the clinician's.

**Non-maleficence** is the duty to avoid harm. The phrase "first, do no harm" is often quoted as if it means clinicians should avoid any action that carries risk, but that is not what it means. Almost every treatment carries some risk. Non-maleficence means weighing the risks of action against the risks of inaction. A physician who withholds a painful treatment is not automatically acting with non-maleficence; the patient's condition may worsen without it. Harm caused by a medical intervention itself, sometimes called iatrogenic harm, is a real and recognized category that the principle asks practitioners to minimize.

**Justice** is the fair allocation of limited resources and fair access to care. When an intensive care unit has one bed and two critically ill patients, justice is the principle that governs how that decision gets made. When one community has three hospitals and another has none, justice is the lens that names that disparity as a health equity issue, not just a logistical fact. Social determinants of health, access, bias, and structural inequity are justice issues, not separate topics.

### How the principles work together (and against each other)

Most ethical decisions in healthcare involve a conflict between two or more principles. A patient who refuses a blood transfusion on religious grounds creates a direct tension between autonomy (the patient's right to refuse) and beneficence (the clinician's duty to preserve life). A family that asks clinicians to withhold a terminal diagnosis from their grandmother creates a tension between autonomy (her right to know) and beneficence (the family's intention to protect her). A hospital with limited ICU beds and two equally critical patients creates a tension between beneficence (help each patient fully) and justice (allocate fairly).

There is rarely a single correct answer to these tensions. What exists is a defensible, documented answer: one that can be explained to the patient, the family, and colleagues using the principles framework. Institutional ethics committees exist to help teams work through the hardest cases, especially when time allows for consultation.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A 78-year-old woman with stage-4 pancreatic cancer is admitted to the hospital. Her adult children ask the nursing staff not to tell her the diagnosis because "she will give up." The patient, who is alert and oriented, asks her nurse directly: "What do I have? Tell me the truth." The nurse faces a direct conflict between the family's request (beneficence: protect her from distress) and the patient's explicit request (autonomy: her right to information about her own body). The ANA Code of Ethics, Provision 1, and the AMA Principles both point the same direction: the patient who has capacity has the right to her own information. The family does not override that right.

## Part 2: Confidentiality and HIPAA

### What confidentiality is and where it comes from

Confidentiality is the obligation not to share a patient's health information with people who have no need to know. It is grounded in autonomy: the patient chose to share sensitive information in the context of a care relationship, and that act of disclosure does not give everyone in the building permission to know.

Confidentiality is both an ethical obligation and a legal one. Professionally, it is embedded in every major healthcare code of ethics. The ANA Code, Provision 3, addresses it directly. The AMA Principles, Principle IV, requires it of physicians. Legally, it is operationalized by the federal HIPAA Privacy Rule, the HIPAA Security Rule, the HIPAA Breach Notification Rule (together found at 45 CFR Parts 160 and 164, enforced by the HHS Office for Civil Rights), and by the Texas Medical Records Privacy Act (Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 181), which is stricter than HIPAA in some respects.

### What HIPAA protects

The HIPAA Privacy Rule protects **Protected Health Information (PHI)**: any identifiable health information created or held by a covered entity. The word "identifiable" is defined precisely. The HHS lists 18 identifiers that make health information protected: name, address, dates of service, phone numbers, email addresses, Social Security number, medical record number, health plan number, account number, certificate number, vehicle identifiers, device identifiers, web URLs, IP addresses, biometric identifiers, full-face photographs, geographic data smaller than a state, and any other unique identifying number or code.

**Covered entities** are the organizations directly bound by HIPAA: hospitals, individual healthcare providers, health plans, and healthcare clearinghouses. Their contracted vendors, called business associates, are also bound through required agreements.

The **minimum necessary rule** is one of HIPAA's most practical requirements. It states that covered entities must make reasonable efforts to share only the information needed to accomplish the task. This means a nurse sending a patient's chart to a specialist should not include every historical note if the specialist only needs the current medication list. It also means that looking up the chart of a celebrity patient "just to see" is a violation, even if no information is shared further, because the access itself exceeds what is needed.

HIPAA does permit certain uses and disclosures without patient authorization. The three main categories are treatment (sharing information among the care team), payment (billing the patient's insurance), and healthcare operations (quality review, accreditation activities). Public health exceptions permit disclosure to health departments for reportable diseases, to medical examiners, and in other narrow circumstances.

### What breaches actually look like

Students will encounter real breach risks in clinical settings. The most common ones are worth naming specifically.

Talking about patients by name, condition, or room number in public spaces like elevators, cafeterias, or hallways where others can hear. Posting on social media in a way that reveals details about a specific case, even without using the patient's name. The combination of a unit, a day, a diagnosis, and an age can be enough to re-identify a patient. Looking up the chart of a neighbor, a friend, or a family member without a treatment relationship, even out of concern. Sharing or using someone else's EHR login credentials. Texting PHI on a personal phone without encrypted messaging. Taking photographs in patient-care areas.

These are not obscure edge cases. They are the scenarios that appear in real HHS Office for Civil Rights enforcement actions, and they have resulted in terminations, license suspensions, and federal fines.

### Special-population protections

Some categories of information carry heightened protections beyond the baseline HIPAA framework. **42 CFR Part 2** provides stricter rules for substance-use-disorder treatment records than HIPAA provides for other health information: these records generally cannot be shared without specific patient authorization, even for treatment purposes. HIV status carries heightened protection in many states. Reproductive health information has additional protections in some jurisdictions. Minor patients have specific confidentiality rights under Texas law for certain care categories, including reproductive health, mental health, and substance use. **GINA** (the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act) protects genetic information from being used in employment decisions or health insurance underwriting.

### Consequences of a breach

Understanding the consequences of a confidentiality breach is essential. They fall into four categories.

For the patient: loss of privacy, potential reputational damage, social stigma, identity theft using health information, and in some cases physical safety risks. A patient who is a domestic violence survivor whose location becomes known, or a person whose HIV status is disclosed at work, can face immediate danger.

For the person who committed the breach: termination of employment, suspension or revocation of licensure or certification, personal civil liability, and federal criminal charges. HIPAA criminal penalties range from fines of $100 to $250,000 and up to 10 years of imprisonment, depending on whether the violation was willful and whether the information was used for personal gain.

For the institution: an HHS Office for Civil Rights investigation, a corrective action plan, and civil monetary penalties that have historically ranged from thousands to tens of millions of dollars, depending on the scope and nature of the breach. The institution's reputation with patients and the community also suffers.

For the care team: the team absorbs the workload during the investigation, loses trust in colleagues and systems, and may experience a chilling effect on the appropriate sharing of information, which itself creates patient-safety risks.

### Key terms

**Autonomy (Beauchamp and Childress):** the patient's right to make decisions about their own body and care, including unwise ones, operationalized via informed consent and refusal.

**Beneficence:** the duty to act in the patient's best interest, balancing benefit against burden, cost, and the patient's own goals.

**Non-maleficence:** the duty to avoid harm; weighs risks of action against risks of inaction.

**Justice:** fair allocation of limited resources; access, bias, and social-determinant issues are justice issues.

**HIPAA Privacy Rule:** the federal regulation (45 CFR Parts 160 and 164) defining patient confidentiality rights, enforced by HHS Office for Civil Rights.

**Protected Health Information (PHI):** identifiable health information created or held by a covered entity; 18 specific identifiers define "identifiable."

**Covered entity:** hospitals, providers, health plans, and clearinghouses bound directly by HIPAA.

**Minimum necessary rule:** HIPAA requirement to share only what is needed for the task; accessing a celebrity's chart out of curiosity violates this even if nothing is shared further.

**42 CFR Part 2:** heightened confidentiality protections for substance-use-disorder treatment records, stricter than baseline HIPAA.

**GINA:** the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (2008); federal protection against genetic-information discrimination in employment and health insurance.

**Breach:** an impermissible use or disclosure of PHI; triggers breach-notification obligations and potential OCR investigation.

### Chapter summary

The four principles of healthcare ethics, autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice, are the standard vocabulary for identifying what is at stake in any clinical ethical decision. Most ethical situations involve a tension between two or more principles, and the goal is a defensible, documented resolution, not a single right answer. Confidentiality is the ethical and legal duty not to share patient information with people who have no need to know it. HIPAA operationalizes this duty through the Privacy Rule, the Security Rule, and the Breach Notification Rule, and defines Protected Health Information precisely. Breaches have serious consequences for patients, for the person who committed the breach, for the institution, and for the care team.

### Review questions

1. A patient with a serious heart condition refuses a recommended cardiac catheterization, saying she has "seen enough doctors." Which ethical principle is the patient exercising? Which principle does the cardiologist's concern represent? How does a defensible clinical response honor both?
2. A phlebotomist draws blood on a patient and later mentions the patient's name and diagnosis to a coworker in the hospital cafeteria. Identify which element of PHI was disclosed, which HIPAA rule was violated, and two specific consequences the phlebotomist could face.
3. A neighbor who is a hospital employee calls your unit asking about a mutual friend who was just admitted. The caller says, "I'm just worried about her." Using the minimum necessary rule and the permitted-uses framework, explain why providing any information would be a HIPAA violation.
