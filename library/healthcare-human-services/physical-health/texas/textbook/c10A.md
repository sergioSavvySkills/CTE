# Patient Autonomy: Recognizing Rights in Clinical Practice

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the operational expressions of patient autonomy in clinical settings
- Distinguish capacity from competence and explain who determines each
- Recognize when autonomy is being honored and when it is at risk
- Describe how surrogate decision-making works when a patient loses capacity

A 34-year-old patient with a chronic condition is admitted to a Texas hospital. During morning rounds, his surgeon explains a recommended procedure. He listens, asks two questions, and says no. The surgeon explains the risks of refusing. The patient says no again. The nurse documents the refusal. The procedure does not happen. The patient's family, upset, asks the charge nurse why the medical team is "letting him refuse." This is what autonomy in action looks like, and recognizing it, including when it is uncomfortable, is a clinical skill.

### Autonomy as both an ethical principle and a legal right

Autonomy is one of the four core principles of healthcare ethics introduced in c9B. In that context it is a philosophical commitment: the patient has the right to make decisions about their own body and care, including decisions the clinical team would choose differently.

In clinical practice, autonomy is also a legal right. CMS Conditions of Participation (§482.13) and Joint Commission standards require certified and accredited facilities to inform patients of their rights, obtain informed consent before treatment, honor informed refusals, and support advance care planning. Professional codes, including the ANA Code of Ethics Provision 1 and the AMA Code of Medical Ethics Chapter 2, build these same requirements into the professional obligations of nurses and physicians.

Understanding autonomy means understanding its operational forms, the specific situations where the principle shows up as a documented, enforceable right.

### Informed consent

**Informed consent** is the patient's agreement to a procedure or treatment, obtained after five elements are present. Capacity: the patient has decision-making ability at the time of the consent. Disclosure: the patient received information about the diagnosis, the proposed treatment, its risks and benefits, and the alternatives including no treatment. Understanding: the patient demonstrated comprehension, not just received information. Voluntariness: the decision was made freely, without coercion or undue pressure. Documentation: the process and outcome are recorded in the patient's chart.

All five elements are required. A consent form that has been signed but obtained five minutes after IV sedation was administered does not satisfy the voluntariness element even though it looks like a signed document. A patient who nods and signs a form in a language they do not read has not given informed consent because understanding is missing. Forms are evidence that a conversation happened, they are not a substitute for the conversation.

### Informed refusal

**Informed refusal** is the patient's decision to decline a proposed treatment after the same five elements are present. It is as valid as consent, and it requires the same documentation discipline.

The clinical team's job when a patient refuses is not to change the patient's mind. It is to make sure the refusal is informed, meaning the patient understands the potential consequences of declining, and documented. A refusal that is not documented creates a legal and ethical gap.

**AMA discharge**, leaving against medical advice, is a specific and important type of informed refusal. A patient who leaves the hospital before the clinical team recommends discharge is exercising their autonomy. AMA discharge should be documented as an informed refusal of continued care, with the risks explained and the patient's decision clearly noted. It is not a punishment label. It is not grounds for withholding care. And a patient who leaves AMA may return to the same facility for care without any legal consequence to them.

### Advance directives

A patient's autonomy extends into the future through **advance directives**, documents that record the patient's wishes about care in the event they lose the ability to communicate. In Texas, the relevant documents are governed by Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 166.

The Texas Directive to Physicians (the state's version of a living will) lets a patient specify in advance whether they want life-sustaining treatment if they develop a terminal condition or an irreversible condition and can no longer communicate their wishes. A **Medical Power of Attorney** lets the patient designate a specific person to make healthcare decisions if the patient loses capacity. An out-of-hospital DNR is a Texas-specific form, signed by both the patient (or surrogate) and a physician, that instructs EMS and non-hospital providers not to attempt resuscitation.

These documents represent autonomy exercised in advance. A patient who signed a directive two years ago was making a decision about their future care at a time when they had full capacity. Honoring that document is honoring the patient's autonomy, even when family members or the clinical team might prefer a different outcome.

### Capacity and competence: two different things

Students frequently use these two terms interchangeably. They are not the same.

**Capacity** is a clinical judgment made by a clinician (usually the treating physician, sometimes a specialist) about whether this specific patient, for this specific decision, at this specific moment, has the ability to understand and make an informed choice. Capacity is decision-specific and can fluctuate. A patient with early dementia may have capacity to consent to a blood draw and not have capacity to consent to a complex surgical procedure. A patient in the middle of a delirium from a UTI may lack capacity at midnight and regain it by morning when the infection is being treated. The clinician assesses and documents.

**Competence** is a legal status determined by a court, not by a clinician at the bedside. A patient may be found incompetent by a probate court and have a legal guardian appointed. Until that happens, the patient is legally competent regardless of any clinical concerns about their decision-making. Capacity questions are common and handled at the bedside. Competence determinations are rare and require judicial involvement.

The distinction matters practically. When a clinician believes a patient lacks capacity for a high-stakes decision, the response is to conduct a formal capacity assessment, bring in a specialist if needed, and if capacity is truly absent, identify a surrogate decision-maker. The clinician does not simply substitute their own judgment for the patient's.

### Surrogate decision-making

When a patient lacks decision-making capacity and has not designated a surrogate through a Medical Power of Attorney, Texas law provides a hierarchy of people authorized to make healthcare decisions on the patient's behalf. The **Texas Consent to Medical Treatment Act** (Health and Safety Code §313) specifies the priority order: a court-appointed guardian of the person, the patient's spouse, the patient's adult children with consensus among available children, the patient's parents, the patient's nearest living relative, and finally the attending physician with ethics-committee review if no other surrogate is available.

Two standards guide surrogates in making decisions. The **substituted judgment** standard asks the surrogate to decide as the patient would have decided, based on known values, prior statements, and documented preferences. The **best-interest standard** is used when the patient's prior wishes are unknown: the surrogate and the clinical team decide what would serve the patient's interests given the available information.

### Signals that autonomy is being honored or violated

Healthcare workers who are not directly obtaining consent can still observe whether autonomy is being honored. The signals are specific.

Autonomy is being honored when: consent or refusal is documented with all five elements present; an interpreter is present for patients with limited English proficiency; the patient is answering open-ended questions and showing understanding in their own words; and the decision-maker is the patient, not a family member acting on behalf of a patient who still has capacity.

Autonomy is at risk when: a consent form is signed under sedation; consent is obtained without an interpreter despite a clear language barrier; family members answer for a patient who is sitting in the room and appears capable; there is subtle coercion ("you will die if you don't do this"); or a refusal is not documented because the team hopes the patient will change their mind.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> An 82-year-old man with mild cognitive impairment is admitted for an elective knee replacement. His daughter accompanies him. During the pre-op consent process, the surgeon directs all questions to the daughter, who answers readily. The patient sits quietly. The form is signed by the daughter. No capacity assessment has been documented. The surgery proceeds. Two days later, a patient-rights advocate reviewing the chart flags the missing capacity assessment and the surrogate signature on a consent form for a patient who the chart elsewhere describes as "alert and cooperative." This is the kind of documentation failure that creates both ethical and legal problems. A patient with mild cognitive impairment may have full capacity to consent to an elective surgery. The question should have been asked, assessed, and answered in writing before the form was signed by anyone but the patient.

### Key terms

**Informed consent:** patient agreement to treatment with capacity, disclosure of diagnosis, risks, benefits, and alternatives, understanding, voluntariness, and documentation. All five elements are required.

**Informed refusal:** the patient's decision to decline a proposed treatment after the same five elements as consent; as valid as consent and requires the same documentation.

**AMA discharge:** leaving Against Medical Advice; a documented informed refusal of continued care, not a punishment label.

**Capacity:** a clinician-determined judgment about whether this patient, for this decision, at this moment, has decision-making ability; can fluctuate with clinical status.

**Competence:** a legal status set by a court, distinct from the clinical determination of capacity.

**Advance directive:** the patient's autonomy extended into the future via directive to physicians, Medical Power of Attorney, and out-of-hospital DNR (Texas HSC Chapter 166).

**Medical Power of Attorney:** Texas statutory form designating a surrogate decision-maker if the patient loses capacity.

**Substituted judgment:** surrogate decision-making standard; choose what the patient would have wanted based on known values and prior statements.

**Best-interest standard:** surrogate decision-making standard used when the patient's prior wishes are unknown.

**Texas Consent to Medical Treatment Act:** Health and Safety Code §313; the statutory surrogate hierarchy for adults without capacity and without a designated POA.

**Mature-minor doctrine:** legal recognition that some minors have sufficient maturity to consent to specific care categories without parental involvement.

### Chapter summary

Patient autonomy is both an ethical principle and a legal right, operationalized through informed consent, informed refusal, advance directives, and surrogate decision-making. Capacity is a clinical judgment about a specific patient for a specific decision at a specific moment; it can fluctuate and is determined by a clinician. Competence is a legal status set by a court. When a patient lacks capacity, Texas law provides a specific hierarchy of surrogates, and surrogates apply either substituted judgment or the best-interest standard. Healthcare workers at every level can recognize whether autonomy is being honored or at risk by looking for the five elements of consent, the presence of an interpreter, the identity of the actual decision-maker, and the completeness of the documentation.

### Review questions

1. A 17-year-old patient presents to a Texas clinic requesting confidential STI testing. She does not want her parents notified. Does she have the legal right to consent to this care on her own? What doctrine or statute governs this, and what would you need to check?
2. A patient with a documented advance directive stating she does not want cardiopulmonary resuscitation is admitted to a hospital following a stroke. She is currently unresponsive. Her adult son arrives and tells the nurse, "Do everything." Identify what document controls in this situation, under which Texas statute, and what steps the clinical team should take.
3. A patient with a high fever and confusion refuses a recommended lumbar puncture. The clinical team believes the refusal is unsafe. What is the appropriate clinical and ethical process for evaluating whether this refusal should be honored, and who makes the determination?
