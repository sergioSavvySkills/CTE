# The Five Health Science Systems and Their Career Families

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Name and define each of the five health science career systems
- Describe the core work, typical education, required credentials, and workplace setting for representative roles in each system
- Compare two roles from different systems using a consistent six-dimension framework
- Explain how all five systems touch a single patient during a care episode

A radiologic technologist, a medical records coder, a clinical research scientist, a central-sterile technician, and a respiratory therapist walk into a hospital. They are not the setup to a joke, they are the five health science systems working on the same floor. Most students walking into a health science classroom picture one or two of those five. By the end of this chapter, you will see all of them.

## Part 1: The Five Systems and Who Works in Them

### Why five systems instead of one

Healthcare is not a single profession. It is five interconnected systems, each doing a different kind of work for the same patients. The five names come from the National Consortium for Health Science Education (NCHSE), and the Texas TEKS draws directly from that framework [NCHSE Health Science Career Clusters].

Knowing the systems by name and function matters for two reasons. First, it maps the full job market, there are roughly 300 distinct healthcare occupations in the United States, and the systems are the organizing logic that makes that number manageable. Second, it sets up your career planning: entry requirements, typical education, and day-to-day work vary dramatically across systems, and choosing without that picture means choosing blindly.

### Diagnostic services: figuring out what is wrong

The diagnostic system generates the data clinicians use to make decisions. Workers in this system run tests, capture images, and report findings, they rarely provide treatment directly.

Representative roles include the **medical laboratory scientist (MLS)** and **medical laboratory technician (MLT)**, who analyze blood, urine, and tissue samples in the clinical laboratory; the **phlebotomy technician**, who draws blood from patients and prepares it for testing; the **radiologic technologist**, who operates X-ray, CT, and fluoroscopy equipment; the **ultrasonographer**, who operates ultrasound equipment; the **nuclear medicine technologist**, who administers radioactive tracers and images their distribution; and the **EKG technician**, who records the electrical activity of the heart.

Entry into most diagnostic roles requires at minimum an associate degree and a national certification or registration, the ARRT (radiologic technology), ASCP (laboratory science), or equivalent body issues the credential. The MLS typically requires a bachelor's degree. Work settings span hospital laboratories and imaging departments, outpatient diagnostic centers, and mobile imaging units [BLS OOH Healthcare].

### Therapeutic services: treating and caring for the patient

Therapeutic is the largest and most visible system. It includes every role whose primary job is to treat, manage, or provide direct care to a patient, from the first contact to discharge and beyond.

Physician roles (MD and DO) direct care and hold prescribing authority. **Registered nurses (RNs)** and **licensed vocational nurses (LVNs)** provide most bedside care and medication administration. **Nurse practitioners (NPs)** and **physician assistants (PAs)** hold advanced practice authority and can diagnose and prescribe. **Certified nursing assistants (CNAs)** handle personal care, vital signs, and ambulation support, the most accessible entry point in the system. **Respiratory therapists (RTs)** manage breathing treatments and ventilator patients. **Physical therapists (PTs)**, **occupational therapists (OTs)**, and **speech-language pathologists (SLPs)** each rehabilitate different functional deficits. **Pharmacists** and **pharmacy technicians** verify, dispense, and counsel on medications. **Emergency medical technicians (EMTs)** and **paramedics** provide pre-hospital care.

Education requirements span the full ladder: CNA programs run 4–12 weeks; LVN programs run approximately one year; RN programs take two years (ADN) to four years (BSN); NP and PA programs require a master's degree. Most roles require state licensure [BLS OOH Healthcare].

### Health informatics: managing the data of care

Health informatics is the least visible system to most students but one of the fastest-growing. It manages the data, coding, documentation, and technology infrastructure that makes modern healthcare function.

The **health information technician** (also called a medical coder or biller) translates clinical documentation into standardized codes (ICD-10, CPT) that drive insurance reimbursement. The **health information manager** (credentialed as RHIA or RHIT through AHIMA) oversees patient records and data governance. The **EHR analyst** configures and maintains the electronic health record system. The **clinical informaticist** bridges clinical practice and information technology, often a clinician by background who now works on systems. The **medical scribe** documents the physician's clinical encounter in real time. The **healthcare data analyst** extracts and interprets population-level data for quality improvement.

Most informatics roles are accessible with a certificate or associate degree; management and analyst roles often require a bachelor's or master's. Work settings are increasingly remote, a coder in Austin may work for a hospital in El Paso [BLS OOH Healthcare].

### Support services: keeping the care environment functional

The support services system keeps the physical and logistical infrastructure of care working. Without it, clinical care stalls.

The **biomedical equipment technician (BMET)** maintains, calibrates, and repairs clinical equipment, the ventilators, infusion pumps, and monitors that therapeutic and diagnostic workers rely on. The **central sterile technician** decontaminates and sterilizes surgical instruments between procedures. **Environmental services** workers clean and disinfect patient rooms, a direct infection-control function. **Dietary services** prepares meals to therapeutic specifications (restricted diets, tube feeds). **Patient transport** moves patients between units safely. **Healthcare interpreters** bridge language gaps between clinicians and patients, their role is directly linked to patient safety.

Entry into most support services roles requires a high school diploma or a short certificate. The BMET role is an exception, typically requiring an associate degree in biomedical equipment technology or electronics [BLS OOH Healthcare].

### Biotechnology research and development: creating new knowledge

Biotechnology R&D generates the discoveries that become tomorrow's treatments. Workers in this system operate at lab benches, in clinical trial sites, and in regulatory agencies rather than at the bedside.

The **biomedical research scientist** designs and conducts experiments on disease mechanisms and potential therapies. The **clinical research coordinator (CRC)** manages the day-to-day operation of clinical trials, enrolling patients, collecting data, maintaining regulatory compliance. The **biotechnology laboratory technician** performs the bench work of experimental protocols. The **bioinformatics specialist** analyzes large genomic and proteomic datasets. The **regulatory affairs specialist** navigates FDA submission requirements to bring a new drug or device to market.

Biotech R&D typically requires a bachelor's degree at minimum; research scientist roles usually require a graduate degree. Patient contact is minimal, but every approved drug or medical device that reaches a patient at the bedside traces its path back through this system [BLS OOH Healthcare].

## Part 2: Comparing Across Systems

### A consistent comparison framework

Comparing careers across five systems is only useful if you use the same dimensions for every comparison. The six-dimension framework below makes comparisons fair and actionable.

| Dimension | What to look for |
|---|---|
| **Core duties** | What does this person actually do on a typical shift or day? |
| **Typical education** | Certificate / associate / bachelor's / graduate / doctoral |
| **Credential** | Licensure, certification, or registration required |
| **Setting** | Hospital unit, outpatient clinic, lab, imaging, remote, bench |
| **Median wage** | BLS OOH annual median, national and Texas-specific |
| **10-year outlook** | BLS growth rate category and the driver behind it |

Using this framework for every comparison prevents the most common mistake in career research: picking a number (salary) without the context (what training it takes to get there, what the growth looks like, where the jobs actually are).

### What the systems have in common

All five systems operate in the same general world: they serve patients (directly or indirectly), they are regulated by state and federal agencies, they require professional behavior from every worker, and most require some form of credential to enter. A phlebotomist and a bioinformatics specialist have more in common than their job titles suggest, both are credentialed, both work under HIPAA, and both contribute to the same patient's care even if they never meet.

### What differentiates the systems

**Patient contact** varies sharply. Therapeutic roles involve the most direct, sustained, physical contact with patients. Diagnostic roles involve contact during the test, a blood draw or a scan, but rarely ongoing care. Support services have frequent contact during specific tasks (transport, cleaning, dietary delivery) but not clinical contact. Informatics and biotech R&D have little to no patient contact.

**Entry education** also varies. The most accessible entry points are in support services (CNA, environmental services, dietary) and some therapeutic and diagnostic roles (CNA, phlebotomy technician, EKG technician), all reachable with a short certificate. The least accessible entry points are in biotech R&D and advanced practice, which require bachelor's or graduate degrees.

**Pace and predictability** differ. Diagnostic imaging and laboratory work tend to be rapid, procedural, and volume-driven. Biotech R&D is slower and experimental. Therapeutic work varies by setting, an ICU RN and a home-health RN have very different daily rhythms.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> Marcus is a 52-year-old with chest pain. He drives himself to the emergency department at 9:15 a.m. In the next 90 minutes: a triage RN (therapeutic) assesses him; an EKG technician (diagnostic) runs a 12-lead; a phlebotomist (diagnostic) draws troponin levels; a medical scribe (informatics) documents the encounter in real time; a transport aide (support services) moves Marcus from triage to a monitored bay; and a pharmacist (therapeutic) verifies the aspirin order. Marcus also happens to be enrolled in a cardiovascular registry, a clinical research coordinator (biotechnology R&D) will pull his de-identified data into a study tomorrow. All five systems touched Marcus before lunchtime.

### Key terms

**Diagnostic services**: the system that identifies what is wrong through testing and imaging (laboratory scientists, phlebotomists, radiologic technologists, EKG technicians).

**Therapeutic services**: the system that treats and cares for the patient (physicians, NPs, PAs, nurses, RTs, PTs, OTs, pharmacists, EMTs).

**Health informatics**: the system that manages the data and technology of care (coders, HIM managers, EHR analysts, scribes, data analysts).

**Support services**: the system that keeps the care environment functional (BMET, central sterile, environmental services, transport, interpreters).

**Biotechnology research and development**: the system that creates new knowledge and products (biomedical scientists, clinical research coordinators, regulatory affairs specialists).

**Core duties**: the typical day-to-day work of a role; the first of six comparison-framework dimensions.

**Cross-system collaboration**: the cooperation across all five systems that occurs during a single patient care episode.

**NCHSE career clusters**: the National Consortium for Health Science Education five-pathway framework that the Texas TEKS draws from directly.

### Chapter summary

Healthcare is organized into five systems, diagnostic, therapeutic, health informatics, support services, and biotechnology R&D, each doing distinct work in service of the same patients. Diagnostic roles generate data; therapeutic roles deliver care; informatics roles manage information; support services keep the environment functional; and biotech R&D advances what is possible. Comparing roles across systems requires a consistent six-dimension framework: duties, education, credential, setting, wage, and outlook. All five systems touch most patients during a single care episode, every worker in every system contributes to the same outcome.

### Review questions

1. A hospital patient has a bone infection. Name one specific role from each of the five systems that would contribute to this patient's care, and describe in one sentence what each contributes.
2. Two students are researching careers: one is considering becoming a radiologic technologist, the other a health information manager. Using the six-dimension comparison framework, name two dimensions on which their paths differ most significantly and explain why.
3. Why does the biotechnology R&D system count as "healthcare" even though most workers in it never meet a patient?
