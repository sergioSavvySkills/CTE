# Reading Clinical Tables, Charts, and Graphs

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify and interpret the clinical tables and graphs used in routine healthcare settings
- Read axes, scales, and reference ranges before drawing conclusions from any chart
- Spot common ways that a graph can mislead without containing a single false number
- Use data from a chart to support a specific health-related decision

A county health department releases a graph showing vaccination rates rising sharply over six months. A local news story runs the headline "Vaccination Coverage Surges 40 Percent." But the graph's y-axis starts at 85 percent, not zero. The actual change is from 87 percent to 89 percent. The graph is technically correct. The impression it creates is not. The clinician who can read the axis catches this in seconds. The one who looks only at the curve gets misled every time.

## Part 1: Clinical Tables and What They Tell You

### Tables clinicians use every day

Clinical work is organized around tables. Before you can interpret one, you need to know which table you are looking at and what question it was built to answer.

**Reference ranges** define the interval of values considered normal for a lab test in a specific population. A complete blood count panel, a CBC, comes back as a table of numbers, each paired with its reference range. A result outside that range is flagged as high or low. The range itself is not a universal truth. It represents the middle 95 percent of healthy adults, which means 5 percent of healthy people will have a flagged value anyway. A single flagged value is a signal to investigate, not an automatic diagnosis.

**Growth percentile curves** are among the most important tables in pediatric care. They plot a child's height, weight, or head circumference against the same-age peer population using CDC or WHO reference data. A child at the 25th percentile for weight is not underweight; they weigh more than 25 percent of children their age. What matters clinically is whether the child is tracking along a consistent percentile or crossing percentile lines. Crossing from the 50th to the 15th percentile over two visits is a signal that warrants attention even if the absolute number looks normal.

**BMI classification tables** organize **BMI (body mass index)**, the weight-for-height calculation in kg per square meter, into categories. The table itself is simple: a BMI below 18.5 is underweight, 18.5 to 24.9 is normal weight, 25 to 29.9 is overweight, and 30 or above is obese. The clinician's job is to apply the table to the patient's calculated number and translate the category into a conversation about health risk.

**Vital-sign normal ranges by age** remind clinicians that what is normal for an adult may not be normal for a child. A heart rate of 110 beats per minute is normal in a toddler and worrying in a resting adult. Drug dosing tables, especially those adjusted for kidney function, require the same careful reading: the table is organized around patient characteristics, and the wrong column gives the wrong dose.

### Reading a table: the questions to ask first

Before reading any number from a clinical table, orient yourself with three questions.

What is the table measuring, and in what units? A glucose value means nothing without knowing whether the unit is mg/dL or mmol/L. A lab result in the wrong units can look terrifyingly high or impossibly low.

Who does the reference range apply to? Most adult reference ranges are built on a population of healthy adults in a defined age range. Pediatric patients, pregnant patients, and patients at altitude may have different normals. The table usually specifies this in a header or footnote.

What decision does this table support? A BMI table supports a counseling conversation. A drug-dosing table supports a dose calculation. A reference range table supports a decision to flag, retest, or escalate. Knowing the decision you are trying to make tells you which part of the table to focus on.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A high school student shadowing in a pediatric clinic watches the nurse practitioner pull up a 4-year-old's growth chart. The child's weight has dropped from the 40th percentile at age 3 to the 18th percentile today. The NP explains: "The absolute weight is 35 pounds, which looks fine by itself. But the child crossed more than one major percentile line in a year, and that is what we follow up on. The table tells us where this child is relative to peers, but the trend tells us something about this child specifically." The student realizes the number and the pattern are two different pieces of information.

### The most useful tables in entry-level clinical work

Students entering clinical rotations will encounter a predictable set of tables. The normal vital-sign ranges by age group are foundational. The USPSTF recommendation table, organized by letter grades from A through D, tells a clinician how strong the evidence is for a screening or preventive service. Medication dosing tables, organized by weight, age, or kidney function, are used before nearly every pediatric or adjusted-dose prescription. Lab reference ranges, typically printed on the lab report itself, appear in every patient encounter that involves bloodwork.

Being comfortable with these tables does not mean memorizing every number. It means knowing what question each table answers, reading the units before the values, and understanding that a flagged result is a starting point, not a conclusion.

## Part 2: Charts and Graphs in Clinical Decision-Making

### The graph types you will see in healthcare

Clinical environments and public health reports use a consistent set of graph types. Each is suited to a different kind of question.

A **trend line** plots a value over time. A glucose trend over 24 hours shows when the patient's blood sugar spiked and when it fell toward target. A blood pressure series plotted over months shows whether treatment is working. The trend line answers the question: is this patient moving toward or away from the goal?

Bar charts compare quantities across categories. Infection rates per hospital unit, vaccination coverage by county, and outcomes by patient group all appear as bar charts. Each bar represents one category, and height represents the quantity. Bar charts answer: which group is highest, lowest, or most different from the others?

Scatterplots show the relationship between two continuous variables. A plot of dose versus response, or blood pressure versus age in a study population, uses dots to show whether the two variables tend to rise together, move in opposite directions, or have no clear relationship. Scatterplots do not prove that one variable causes the other. They show association.

An **epidemiology (epi) curve** is a specialized bar chart that plots case counts over time during a disease outbreak. The shape of the curve contains information about the source of the outbreak. A sharp spike followed by rapid decline suggests a single point source: everyone was exposed at the same event. A prolonged plateau suggests continuous exposure. A series of waves suggests person-to-person spread. Public health teams use the epi curve to decide where to send contact tracers and how to contain the outbreak.

Survival curves, also called Kaplan-Meier curves, plot the proportion of a study group still free of an event over time. They appear in oncology research, cardiovascular studies, and anywhere researchers are tracking whether patients survive or experience a recurrence. A steeper downward slope means the event is happening faster in that group. These charts are worth recognizing at a survey level even if you are not yet interpreting them fully.

### Reading a graph: check the axes before the curve

The most important habit in graph literacy is one that most people skip: read the axes before you look at the curve.

The x-axis and y-axis carry four pieces of information that change the meaning of any graph. The units tell you what is being measured. The scale tells you whether each equal interval on the axis represents an equal addition (linear) or an equal multiplication (**log scale**). The starting value tells you whether the y-axis begins at zero or somewhere in the middle. The time window tells you whether the x-axis covers three days or three years.

A graph can tell a completely different story depending on those choices, without containing a single false number. Two of the most common ways this happens follow directly from the axes.

A **truncated y-axis** is an axis that does not start at zero. When the y-axis starts at 85 instead of 0, a change from 87 to 89 looks like a dramatic rise that fills nearly the whole chart. The same data plotted from zero would show a nearly flat line. Truncated axes are not always dishonest, sometimes a zero-start axis hides important variation in a narrow range, but they are always worth flagging and questioning. Ask: what would this look like if the y-axis started at zero?

**Absolute vs. relative risk** is a different kind of axis issue, one that appears in the labels and titles rather than on the tick marks. A study may report that a new drug cuts the risk of a heart attack by 50 percent. That sounds large. But if the baseline risk in the study population was 2 percent, a 50 percent reduction means the risk dropped from 2 percent to 1 percent. One percentage point is the absolute difference. Headlines almost always report the relative number because it sounds more impressive. Always look for both numbers before drawing a conclusion.

### Applying data to decisions

The standard says "provide solutions," not just "read charts." Every graph-reading exercise in clinical practice ends with a decision: normal or abnormal? trending toward or away from target? needs escalation? requires counseling?

Reading the **growth percentile curve** gives a number and a position on the chart. The decision is: is this child tracking their normal curve, or are they crossing percentile lines in a direction that needs follow-up?

Reading a 24-hour glucose trend line gives a pattern. The decision is: when does this patient's blood sugar first exceed target, and does the timing suggest a meal-related spike or a background-control problem?

Reading an epi curve gives a shape. The decision is: what kind of outbreak source does this shape suggest, and where should the public health response focus?

In every case, the chart is evidence for a recommendation. Reading it well means knowing which question to bring to it before you look at a single data point.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A public health student is reviewing a county outbreak report. The epi curve shows a cluster of gastroenteritis cases concentrated over a 48-hour window two weeks ago, followed by near-zero cases since. The student recognizes this as a point-source pattern, one common exposure event rather than ongoing spread. She notes that the y-axis starts at zero, the time window is three weeks, and the x-axis is labeled in days. She recommends the investigation focus on identifying the single shared exposure event, probably food at a common gathering, rather than implementing broad community quarantine measures. The curve shape supported that recommendation before any interviews were conducted.

### Key terms

**Reference range:** the range of values considered normal for a lab test in a defined population; the standard against which a result is flagged normal or abnormal.

**Growth percentile curve:** a CDC or WHO chart that plots a child's height, weight, or head circumference against same-age peers; crossing percentiles is a clinical signal.

**Trend line:** a plot of a value (glucose, BP, weight) over time used to see whether the patient is moving toward or away from a target.

**Epidemiology (epi) curve:** a bar or line chart of case counts over time during an outbreak; the shape suggests whether a source is point, continuous, or propagated.

**Truncated y-axis:** a y-axis that does not start at zero; visually exaggerates small changes and is a common way a graph misleads without being technically wrong.

**Log scale:** an axis where equal distances represent multiplicative rather than additive changes; appropriate for data spanning many orders of magnitude but easy to misread if unlabeled.

**BMI (body mass index):** weight-for-height index (kg per square meter) used with a classification table to categorize adults and counsel accordingly.

**Absolute vs. relative risk:** absolute risk is the actual probability of an outcome; relative risk is the ratio between groups; headlines often quote the relative and hide the smaller absolute difference.

**Kaplan-Meier / survival curve:** a stepwise plot of the proportion of a group still free of an event over time; introduced here at a survey level for chart literacy.

### Chapter summary

Clinical tables and graphs are the medium through which patient data and population health data become decisions. Entry-level healthcare workers encounter reference ranges, growth percentile curves, BMI tables, and vital-sign normals in almost every patient encounter. In public health and research contexts, the same workers read trend lines, bar charts, epi curves, and survival curves. The most important habit for any of these is to read the axes before the curve: check the units, the scale, the starting value of the y-axis, and the time window. A truncated y-axis can make a trivial change look dramatic. A relative-risk headline can make a small absolute difference look clinically significant. Every data-reading task ends with a decision, and the chart is evidence, not the conclusion.

### Review questions

1. A growth chart shows a child tracked at the 60th percentile for weight at ages 1, 2, and 3, then dropped to the 25th percentile at age 4. Is the 25th-percentile weight itself a problem? What is the actual clinical signal in this data, and what would you recommend as a next step?
2. A graph shows hospital infection rates rising steeply. Before accepting that conclusion, list three features of the axes you would check and explain what each one could change about the story.
3. A news headline reads: "New drug cuts stroke risk by 33 percent." The study shows stroke rates of 3 percent in the control group and 2 percent in the treatment group. Calculate the absolute risk reduction and explain why that number matters more than the headline percentage.
