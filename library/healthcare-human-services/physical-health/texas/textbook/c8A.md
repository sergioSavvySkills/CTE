# Teaming: Coordinating Care When the Team Keeps Changing

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Explain the concept of teaming and distinguish it from working on a stable team
- Identify the four conditions that make teaming possible in a healthcare setting
- Describe specific teaming behaviors and connect each to a quality outcome
- Recognize teaming as a learnable skill rather than a fixed personality trait

A patient arrives in the emergency department at 2:00 a.m. with chest pain. In the next four hours, she will be cared for by an ED nurse who has never met her, a hospitalist covering from another unit, a radiology technician on night rotation, a phlebotomist from the lab, and a cardiologist who joins by phone. None of these people have ever worked together before. None of them will work together again after this shift. And yet they need to function as a single, coordinated team. That is not a stable team, that is **teaming (Edmondson)**.

### Teams vs. teaming, a critical distinction

When most people hear the word "team" they picture a stable group: the same nurses who work the same floor every week, the OR crew that has operated together for years. That stable group has shared history, established norms, and built-up trust. Most research on teams assumes that model.

Healthcare is different. The average hospitalized patient is cared for by dozens of people across roles, shifts, and units. Float staff cover gaps. Residents rotate through every four weeks. Consultants arrive for a single case. Patients transfer between units. In this environment, a **stable team** is the exception. Most of the coordination work happens between people who have limited shared history and may never work together again.

Amy Edmondson, a researcher at Harvard Business School, named this reality "teaming", the active, on-the-fly coordination work that makes care possible when the team is constantly changing. The skill is not maintaining a stable group. The skill is doing team-level work without one.

This distinction changes what you practice. A stable team can build trust slowly over months. A teaming environment requires that trust be established in minutes, through explicit behaviors, not accumulated history.

### The four conditions that make teaming work

Research on teaming in high-stakes industries, healthcare, aviation, nuclear power, has identified four conditions that make effective coordination possible even among strangers. All four need to be present. When one is missing, the handoff suffers, the decision slows, or the safety concern goes unspoken.

**Shared goal**, The team's aim must be explicit and aligned with care quality. In healthcare, that framework comes from the IOM/NAM quality aims: safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable. When a code-blue team assembles in 90 seconds, they do not need to discuss values, they share a goal ("keep this patient alive") that all of them already hold. When teams are less clear on the goal, coordination fragments and individuals optimize for different things.

**Role clarity**, Each person needs to know what they do and what they hand off. This is not the same as "everyone knows their job title." Role clarity in teaming means knowing, in this specific moment, who is accountable for which decision and what information needs to pass to whom. Licensure and scope-of-practice boundaries reinforce this clarity, they define what each role can and cannot do, which removes ambiguity when time is short.

**Reliable communication protocols**, Teaming requires standardized handoff and coordination tools that work even when team members have never met. SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) gives a transfer of information a predictable structure. Closed-loop communication, "I heard you say X; I will do Y by Z", confirms that the message arrived correctly. Brief, huddle, and debrief create coordination moments at predictable points in the workflow.

**Psychological safety**, This is the condition Edmondson's research identifies as the strongest single predictor of team performance: the team climate in which every member, including the person on their first day, feels safe to speak up when something is wrong. A float nurse who notices a labeling error but says nothing because "it's not my unit" is working in a low-psychological-safety environment. The cost of that silence is borne by the patient.

### Teaming behaviors you can practice right now

The four conditions do not appear automatically, they are produced by specific behaviors that team members can learn and practice. Each behavior is described below along with the condition it supports.

**Orient newcomers fast.** When a float nurse, new resident, or student on rotation joins the team, someone needs to spend 60 seconds telling them who is leading, what the current goal is, and who to go to with questions. Waiting until the newcomer asks costs time and creates the conditions for a missed handoff.

**Name who is leading this moment.** In a code blue, in a transfer, in a difficult family meeting, someone should be explicitly named as the coordinator for that task. When leadership is assumed rather than stated, two people may each assume the other is handling something critical.

**Close the loop on every handoff.** The receiving person restates what they heard and what they will do: "I heard you say the patient's potassium is 3.1 and you want a recheck in two hours. I'll draw it at 4:30 and call you." This behavior catches miscommunication before it reaches the patient.

**Stop the line when something is wrong.** Using CUS language ("I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue") or the two-challenge rule, any team member, regardless of role or seniority, is expected to halt a process when something is off. This behavior only works in a psychologically safe environment; the other three conditions make this one possible.

**Debrief after hard events.** The five-minute conversation after a difficult case, "what went well, what didn't, what one thing changes", is not administrative overhead. It converts experience into improvement and signals that the team's learning matters.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A medical student on her first day of a pediatric rotation joins a team as a patient transfers from the PICU. The charge nurse does not know her name. Three people are in the room and no one has been named as the lead. The student notices the patient's IV pump is set to the wrong rate, but she hesitates, she is not sure if speaking up is her role. She says nothing. Thirty minutes later a nurse catches the error. In the debrief, the charge nurse asks what prevented earlier identification. The student describes her hesitation. The charge nurse responds: "Your job is to speak up, always. I should have told you that when you walked in." Two conditions had failed: newcomer orientation and psychological safety. The error was caught in time. It will not always be.

### Why teaming is a quality issue

The connection between teaming and patient safety is not theoretical. The Joint Commission's Sentinel Event Alerts, analyses of cases where patients were seriously harmed, repeatedly identify communication failures and coordination breakdowns as the primary contributing cause. These are teaming failures. Poor handoffs, unclear roles, and cultures where junior staff cannot speak up are not personality problems, they are structural conditions that teaming behaviors are designed to fix.

The IOM/NAM quality aims (safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable) all improve when teaming works: fewer sentinel events, faster decisions, less duplicated effort, better patient experience, and lower clinician burnout.

### Teaming is taught, not born

The TEKS asks students to explain the concept of teaming. The deeper point is that teaming is a learnable skill, not a personality trait or an innate gift. Programs like TeamSTEPPS (from AHRQ) exist precisely because the behaviors that enable teaming, structured communication, psychological safety, loop-closing, are teachable in any clinical setting. The IPEC Core Competencies (Interprofessional Education Collaborative) extend this to every health profession: the expectation is not that teams will somehow cohere naturally, but that every practitioner will arrive trained in the behaviors that make teaming work.

### Key terms

**Teaming (Edmondson)**: the active, on-the-fly coordination work that happens when the "team" is constantly changing; distinct from stable teamwork.

**Stable team**: a group that works together repeatedly; the exception in most US healthcare settings, not the norm.

**Shared goal**: an explicit aim aligned with IOM/NAM quality aims; the first of the four conditions that make teaming possible.

**Role clarity**: each contributor knows what they do and what they hand off; reinforced by licensure and scope-of-practice boundaries.

**Psychological safety**: Edmondson's climate in which members can speak up, admit mistakes, and ask questions without fear; predicts team performance.

**Closing the loop**: the teaming behavior of confirming every handoff with "I heard you say X; I will do Y by Z."

**Stop the line**: the teaming behavior of halting work when something is wrong, using CUS or two-challenge-rule scripts.

**IPEC Core Competencies**: Interprofessional Education Collaborative framework for interdisciplinary teaming behaviors across professions.

**Newcomer orientation**: the teaming behavior of rapidly onboarding the float nurse, new resident, or rotating student so they can contribute safely.

### Chapter summary

Teaming is the coordination work that happens when the team keeps changing, the standard condition in most US healthcare settings. Edmondson's concept of teaming separates this active, on-the-fly skill from stable teamwork. For teaming to work, four conditions must be in place: a shared goal, role clarity, reliable communication protocols, and psychological safety. Each condition is produced by specific, learnable behaviors: orienting newcomers, naming who is leading, closing the loop on handoffs, stopping the line when something is wrong, and debriefing after hard events. Teaming failures are a primary contributing factor in patient-safety incidents, which makes these behaviors a quality requirement, not a soft skill.

### Review questions

1. A patient transfers from the ICU to a medical-surgical floor. The receiving nurse has never met the transferring nurse and the patient is complex. Which of the four teaming conditions is most at risk in this scenario? What single behavior would most protect that condition?
2. Explain why psychological safety is considered the most important of the four teaming conditions. What does low psychological safety look like in practice, and what is the patient-safety consequence?
3. A charge nurse says, "We're a great team on this unit, we've all worked together for five years." A float nurse who joined for a single shift just contributed a critical safety observation. Explain the difference between a stable team and teaming, and describe what the charge nurse would need to do differently to enable teaming.
