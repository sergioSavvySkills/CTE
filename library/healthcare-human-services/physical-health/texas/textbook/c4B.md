# Demonstrating Leadership Skills

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Write SMART goals for personal learning, team projects, and patient-care improvement
- Apply the brief, huddle, and debrief framework to build and maintain a team
- Delegate tasks using the Five Rights of Delegation
- Give and receive structured feedback using the SBI framework
- Demonstrate speak-up behavior under an authority gradient using CUS and the two-challenge rule

It is 6:45 a.m. and the charge nurse has 15 minutes before the day shift starts. She knows Bed 2 has a new patient with a complex history, two of her CNAs have never worked together before, and a medical student is joining for the first time. She does not panic. She runs a three-minute brief: names the team, assigns each person to a zone, identifies the one patient who needs eyes on first, and asks one question: "Anything anyone needs before we start?" Fifteen seconds of silence. One CNA says, "I've never done a post-op check." The charge nurse assigns the experienced CNA to that bed. The shift begins in order. That is leadership as a practiced skill.

## Part 1: Goal-Setting and Team Building

### Writing goals that actually work

Chapter 4A covered the traits of effective leaders. This chapter is about doing. The first skill on the list is goal-setting, not because it is the most dramatic leadership behavior, but because vague goals produce vague results. "I'll communicate better this semester" cannot be measured, cannot be evaluated, and cannot be improved. A **SMART goal** can.

SMART stands for Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Each element does distinct work. Specific defines exactly what will be done and by whom. Measurable establishes the evidence that the goal was met. Achievable tests whether the goal is realistic given available resources and time. Relevant connects the goal to a meaningful aim. Time-bound sets a deadline that creates focus.

Here is the difference in practice. Compare these two goals written by the same student:

- Vague: "I want to get better at handoffs."
- SMART: "By the end of my clinical rotation in six weeks, I will correctly complete SBAR handoffs on three supervised patient transfers, with my preceptor rating my communication as complete and organized on all three."

The second goal passes every test: it names a specific skill (SBAR handoff), a measurable standard (preceptor rating on three transfers), a realistic scope (six weeks, supervised), a relevant purpose (safe care transitions), and a deadline (end of rotation). When the six weeks are up, there is no argument about whether the goal was met.

SMART goals apply at every scale, personal skill development, team projects, and unit-level improvement targets. A team goal for a simulated patient-care improvement might look like: "In our simulation scenario this week, every team member will verbalize their role and one concern during the pre-brief, measured by observer checklist." That goal can be run, reviewed, and refined.

### Building a team before the work starts

A team is not built by assigning people to the same room. It is built through deliberate behaviors that create a shared mental model, an understanding of who is doing what, what the goal is, and what to do when something changes. The three TeamSTEPPS tools for building this model are the **brief**, the **huddle**, and the **debrief**.

The **brief** happens at the start of a shift, a procedure, or a project. It covers three things: the goal for this period, the roles of each person, and any known risks. A brief does not need to be long, three to five minutes is typical. Its job is to make sure no one starts the work with a different picture of what is happening.

The **huddle** happens mid-shift when something changes. A patient deteriorates. A team member goes on break and the assignment shifts. A result comes back that changes the plan. The huddle is not a status update, it is a regroup. It asks: given what just changed, what does the plan look like now, and does everyone know their part?

The **debrief** happens after the event. It is the most powerful of the three tools and the most often skipped. A debrief asks three questions: What went well? What did not? What will we do differently? Debrief converts experience into learning. A team that debriefs consistently improves; a team that only executes without reviewing tends to repeat the same errors.

Trust is also built through reliability, following through on what you said you would do, acknowledging others' contributions, and celebrating what the team achieved, not just what each individual accomplished.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> After a difficult shift where a patient coded unexpectedly and was transferred to the ICU, the charge nurse gathers the team for a five-minute debrief before anyone leaves. She opens with: "I want to start with what the team did well." She names three specific actions. Then she asks: "What is one thing we would do differently?" Two people speak. One identifies a 90-second delay in locating the crash cart. The team agrees to a cart-check protocol at the start of every shift. The next shift runs the check without being told.

## Part 2: Delegation, Feedback, and Accountability

### Delegating the right task to the right person

Delegation is not off-loading. It is a structured decision about who is best positioned to do a specific task right now. Delegating incorrectly, sending a task outside someone's scope, or assigning a critical task to someone already at capacity, produces errors and erodes trust. The **Five Rights of Delegation** (from NCSBN) provide a framework for getting it right every time.

The five rights are:

1. **Right task**, Is this task appropriate to delegate at all? Some tasks require a licensed clinician and cannot be reassigned.
2. **Right person**, Does this person have the scope of practice and the verified competency to do this task safely?
3. **Right circumstance**, Given the patient's current condition and the team's current workload, is this the right moment for this delegation?
4. **Right direction and communication**, Has the person been given clear instructions, including what to report back and when?
5. **Right supervision and evaluation**, Is there a follow-up plan to check that the task was completed correctly?

All five rights must be met. A delegation that is right on four of the five is still a delegation at risk.

The **accountability** that comes with delegation flows both ways. The person delegating remains responsible for the outcome, they do not hand off the task and walk away. The person receiving the delegation is responsible for completing it within their scope and reporting back. If something changes mid-task, the line of communication goes back to the delegator immediately.

### Giving feedback that lands

Most people avoid giving corrective feedback because it feels confrontational. But withholding feedback is itself a leadership failure, it allows a problem to continue, and it signals that you do not trust the person to handle honest information. The **SBI feedback** framework removes the confrontational edge by keeping feedback focused on behavior, not character.

SBI stands for Situation, Behavior, Impact.

- **Situation**, name the specific context: when and where it happened.
- **Behavior**, describe exactly what was observed, in specific and neutral terms.
- **Impact**, explain the effect the behavior had on the team, the patient, or the work.

An SBI feedback statement sounds like this: "At the 7:00 a.m. handoff on Tuesday (situation), you left before giving the night nurse your last set of vitals on Bed 4 (behavior). The night nurse had to track them down herself, which delayed her first assessment by ten minutes (impact)."

Notice what is not in that statement: "You're irresponsible" or "You always do this" or "I don't know what you were thinking." Those are character judgments. They put the receiver on the defensive and make behavior change less likely. SBI delivers the same information with none of the attack.

Feedback should be timely, given as close to the event as possible, when the details are clear and the behavior can still be changed. Corrective feedback is given privately. Positive feedback can be given publicly.

### Receiving feedback without shutting down

Receiving feedback is a skill too, and it is harder than giving it. When someone delivers a criticism, the instinct is to explain, defend, or counter-attack. None of those responses help. The effective receiving behaviors are: listen to completion without interrupting, ask one clarifying question if needed, thank the giver for the information, and then, the step that matters most, change the behavior.

Assuming feedback is for the benefit of the patient or the team, rather than a personal attack, makes this easier. The goal is not to win the conversation. The goal is to improve.

### Speaking up when it is hard

Every healthcare team has an **authority gradient**, the implicit hierarchy that makes it easier for senior people to speak and harder for junior people to push back. This gradient has killed patients. The Joint Commission and AHRQ have both documented cases where a junior nurse or technician had critical safety information but did not speak up because of the perceived power difference.

**CUS language** is a TeamSTEPPS tool designed to cut through that gradient. The script is: "I'm Concerned. I'm Uncomfortable. This is a Safety issue." Each escalating statement signals a level of urgency. If the first statement is dismissed, the second follows. If the second is dismissed, the **two-challenge rule** obligates the team member to escalate to a third party, a charge nurse, a supervisor, a rapid-response team, rather than letting the concern drop.

CUS and the two-challenge rule are not insubordination. They are documented patient-safety behaviors that the highest-performing healthcare systems train and expect of every team member regardless of role. Using them correctly, calmly, with a specific concern, not as a challenge to authority, is a demonstration of accountability to the patient.

### Leading yourself first

All of the skills above, goal-setting, brief/huddle/debrief, delegation, feedback, speak-up, scale down to one person. Self-leadership means setting a personal SMART goal and following through on it. It means briefing yourself before a clinical task: what is my goal, what do I know, what could go wrong? It means debriefing after a difficult moment: what did I handle well, what would I do differently? It means receiving feedback from a preceptor without defensiveness and using it in the next encounter.

Leadership is not a role you step into one day. It is a set of behaviors practiced in every interaction, at every level, starting now.

### Key terms

**SMART goal**: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound; the framework used for personal and team goal-setting.

**Brief**: TeamSTEPPS start-of-shift team-building tool to establish a shared mental model of goals and roles.

**Huddle**: TeamSTEPPS mid-shift regroup when something shifts; a 2–3 minute team-building behavior.

**Debrief**: TeamSTEPPS post-event review identifying what went well, what didn't, and what to change.

**Five Rights of Delegation**: NCSBN framework: right task, right person, right circumstance, right direction/communication, right supervision/evaluation.

**SBI feedback**: Situation, Behavior, Impact; a structured format for giving feedback about behavior, not character, timely and private when corrective.

**CUS language**: TeamSTEPPS safety-escalation script used to speak up under authority gradient: "I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue."

**Two-challenge rule**: TeamSTEPPS norm obligating a team member to raise a safety concern twice; if dismissed twice, escalate to a third party.

**Accountability**: owning outcomes and following up on commitments; the demonstrated leadership skill of addressing performance issues directly.

### Chapter summary

Leadership skills are practiced, not just identified. Goal-setting with the SMART framework converts intention into measurable action. The brief, huddle, and debrief create team cohesion before, during, and after the work. The Five Rights of Delegation structure accountability in both directions. SBI feedback delivers honest information about behavior without character attacks, and receiving feedback without defensiveness is a skill that must be practiced separately. When authority gradients make safety information hard to share, CUS language and the two-challenge rule provide a structured path to speaking up. All of these skills begin with leading yourself, the standard you hold for your own preparation, follow-through, and response to feedback is the foundation everything else is built on.

### Review questions

1. Write a SMART goal for improving your clinical communication skills over the next month. Then explain which of the five SMART criteria was hardest to satisfy and why.
2. A classmate delegated a wound assessment to the CNA on duty during a simulation. The CNA completed the task, but the assessment was outside her documented scope. Which of the Five Rights of Delegation was violated, and who bears accountability for the outcome?
3. A more experienced team member gives an unsafe instruction during a simulation and dismisses your first concern. Walk through the exact steps you would take using CUS and the two-challenge rule. What happens if the concern is dismissed a second time?
