# Codes of Ethics and Scope of Practice Across Healthcare Professions

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Explain what a professional code of ethics is and identify the common provisions that appear across healthcare professions
- Describe what a scope of practice defines and distinguish it from a code of ethics
- Compare the codes and scopes of two healthcare professions using a structured framework
- Identify situations where code and scope interact, conflict, or create friction

A certified medical assistant reads a physician's order to administer an IV push medication. She is competent, she has done it before in another state. But Texas scope-of-practice rules do not authorize medical assistants to push IV medications. The act would be clinically reasonable, but it would still be a scope violation. Knowing the difference between what you are capable of and what you are authorized to do is the starting point for professional practice.

## Part 1: Professional Codes of Ethics

### What a code of ethics is

Every major healthcare profession publishes a **code of ethics**, a written statement of the values and conduct expected of every member of that profession. These documents are not inspirational posters. They are operational standards, typically structured into 5 to 9 numbered provisions, each with interpretive commentary that explains how the provision applies in real clinical situations.

The most widely referenced codes in US healthcare include the American Nurses Association's *Code of Ethics for Nurses with Interpretive Statements* (9 provisions), the American Medical Association's *Principles of Medical Ethics* (9 principles, paired with a comprehensive opinion-based Code of Medical Ethics), the American Academy of Physician Associates' *Code of Ethics of the Physician Assistant Profession*, the American Association of Medical Assistants' *Code of Ethics and Creed*, and the American Physical Therapy Association's *Code of Ethics for the Physical Therapist*. Each reflects its profession's history and identity, but they share a core set of commitments.

### What the codes have in common

Across all the major codes, certain commitments appear in every document. These common provisions exist because every healthcare role, regardless of title or setting, serves the same patient.

The most consistent commitments are these. First, the primacy of patient interest: the practitioner's decisions are guided by the patient's well-being, not the practitioner's convenience, financial benefit, or employer pressure. Second, respect for autonomy, privacy, and confidentiality: patients have the right to make their own decisions and to control who knows what about their health. Third, competence and continuing education: practitioners are responsible for maintaining the skills needed to do their jobs safely, and for recognizing the edges of their own competence. Fourth, integrity and truth-telling: this includes error disclosure, which means telling a patient when something went wrong, even when that is uncomfortable. Fifth, collaboration and responsibility for delegated work: practitioners are accountable not just for what they do themselves but for what they supervise or delegate to others. Sixth, advocacy and social responsibility: healthcare professions have a collective duty to the communities they serve, not just to individual patients. Seventh, professional self-governance: each profession is responsible for holding its own members accountable through discipline, peer review, and ongoing standards-setting.

### Where the codes differ

What makes each code unique is the profession-specific language around its particular role. The ANA code addresses the nurse's duty to oneself, including self-care and limits on compromise, in a way that reflects nursing's distinct relationship to the patient. The AAPA code addresses the PA's relationship to the supervising physician. The AAMA code is notably brief and principle-based, reflecting the medical assistant's role as a support practitioner rather than an independent clinical decision-maker.

Students should read at least two codes side by side before drawing conclusions. The difference between "similar language" and "identical commitment" matters. An act that satisfies one profession's code may not satisfy another's, even when the practitioners are in the same room caring for the same patient.

### How codes get updated and enforced

Professional organizations revise their codes through member participation, typically a review committee, a comment period, and a vote. The ANA last revised its code in 2015. The AMA Code of Medical Ethics is updated more continuously through council opinion processes.

Enforcement is through the profession, not through courts. A code violation is handled by the professional organization, the employer, or the licensing board, not by a judge. A practitioner can violate a code without violating a law. They can also violate a law without violating their code's letter, which is why both documents matter.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A registered nurse discovers that a physician colleague has been documenting findings from physical exams he did not actually perform. The nurse knows this is wrong, but she is not the physician's supervisor, and she is not sure whether it is "her place" to report it. The ANA Code of Ethics, Provision 3, addresses exactly this situation: the nurse has a duty to protect patients, which extends to reporting unsafe or unethical practices. The physician's behavior also violates AMA Principles III (honesty) and IV (patient welfare). Two codes, both pointing the same direction.

## Part 2: Scope of Practice

### What scope of practice defines

A **scope of practice** is a legally or organizationally defined statement of what a practitioner is authorized to do. Unlike a code of ethics, which is developed and enforced by a professional organization, scope of practice is typically grounded in state law. In Texas, the primary sources are the **Texas Nurse Practice Act** (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 301, governing RNs, LVNs, and APRNs) and the **Texas Medical Practice Act** (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 151, governing physicians). Pharmacy, physical therapy, dental hygiene, and other licensed professions each have their own Texas practice acts.

Scope of practice answers four specific questions. What tasks is this practitioner legally authorized to perform? Which tasks require supervision or collaboration with another practitioner? Which tasks are outside scope regardless of competence or training? And what are the **delegation rules**, meaning who can assign which tasks to whom under what conditions?

### Comparing scopes across professions

The most useful way to compare scopes is to apply a consistent framework across professions. The framework has four dimensions.

The first is scope breadth: what tasks are authorized, and how much independent clinical decision-making authority does the practitioner hold? A physician has the broadest independent scope. A registered nurse has independent authority within the nursing scope. A licensed vocational nurse works under the supervision of an RN or physician. A medical assistant, in Texas, has a narrower scope still.

The second dimension is supervision requirements. Some practitioners work independently. Others work collaboratively with a physician or APRN. Others require direct supervision. These requirements are defined in the practice act and enforced by the licensing board.

The third dimension is how the scope defines tasks that are clearly prohibited. These are the hard limits. An MA performing an IV push in Texas is not a judgment call, it is a scope violation. A nurse signing off on a physician's history and physical is not within nursing scope. These limits protect patients and practitioners alike.

The fourth dimension is delegation authority: which roles can delegate tasks to which other roles, and under what conditions. The **NCSBN Scope Decision-Making Framework** is a structured tool that nurses can use to decide whether a specific task is within their scope and whether they can delegate it.

### Where code and scope interact

Here is the critical point that students most often miss: code of ethics and scope of practice are two separate documents that bind the same practitioner, and they do not always point in the same direction.

An act within legal scope can still be unethical. A physician who makes a referral that is legal under Texas law but that benefits the physician financially is acting within scope but may be violating the AMA's ethics provisions on conflicts of interest.

An act consistent with the code can be outside legal scope. A medical assistant who genuinely believes she is helping a patient by performing a task her code of ethics would approve of is still violating Texas law if that task is outside her scope. Good intentions do not create legal authorization.

The controlling principle is: the practitioner is bound by both, and the more restrictive of the two controls. If the law prohibits something the code permits, the law wins. If the code prohibits something the law permits, the code's higher standard applies.

### Points of friction between professions

**Points of friction** are areas where one profession's scope intrudes on, or competes with, another's. These are not rare edge cases. They are common and growing as scope-of-practice laws evolve.

The most visible current example is **full practice authority** for nurse practitioners. In several states, NPs may practice independently without physician supervision. In Texas, as of this writing, APRNs must have a collaborative agreement with a physician. Physicians and NPs sometimes disagree about whether expanded NP scope is safe and appropriate. This is a points-of-friction situation, not a simple right-or-wrong answer.

Other examples include pharmacist prescribing authority in some states, PA scope of practice relative to physician supervision requirements, and scope boundaries between LVNs and medical assistants for specific tasks. State laws vary, and **state variation matters**: what is within scope in California may be outside scope in Texas. Interstate compacts, such as the Nurse Licensure Compact, narrow some of this variation but do not eliminate it.

### Key terms

**Code of ethics:** a profession's published statement of values and conduct expected of every member, typically 5 to 9 provisions with interpretive commentary.

**Scope of practice:** a legally or organizationally defined statement of what a practitioner is authorized to do, derived from practice acts and professional standards.

**ANA Code of Ethics for Nurses:** the canonical US nursing ethics code, structured in 9 provisions with interpretive statements.

**AMA Principles of Medical Ethics:** the canonical US physician ethics code, paired with the opinion-based Code of Medical Ethics.

**Texas Nurse Practice Act:** Texas Occupations Code Chapter 301, the primary legal source defining RN, LVN, and APRN scope in Texas.

**Texas Medical Practice Act:** Texas Occupations Code Chapter 151, the primary legal source defining physician scope in Texas.

**Delegation rules:** scope provisions defining who can delegate which tasks to whom and under what conditions.

**Full practice authority:** a scope-expansion model allowing nurse practitioners to practice independently without physician supervision, adopted in some states but not Texas as of this writing.

**NCSBN Scope Decision-Making Framework:** a structured tool for determining whether a specific task is within a nurse's scope and whether it may be delegated.

**Points of friction:** areas where one profession's authorized scope intrudes on or competes with another's.

### Chapter summary

Every healthcare profession publishes a code of ethics that states the values and conduct expected of every member. While codes differ across professions, they share core commitments to patient primacy, confidentiality, competence, honesty, and collaboration. Scope of practice is a legally grounded document, different in origin and enforcement from the code of ethics, that defines what a practitioner is authorized to do in a given state. Comparing scopes across professions reveals differences in breadth, supervision requirements, prohibited tasks, and delegation rules. Code and scope bind the same practitioner and do not always point in the same direction. The more restrictive document controls, which means healthcare workers must know both.

### Review questions

1. A licensed vocational nurse is asked by a busy physician to co-sign a physical exam the physician did not perform. Using both a code of ethics and a scope-of-practice argument, explain why the LVN should decline.
2. A nurse practitioner moves from New Mexico, where she has full practice authority, to Texas. What changes about her legal scope of practice, and what stays the same about her ethical obligations? Which document governs each?
3. A medical assistant performs a task that the AAMA Code of Ethics would approve of, but that falls outside Texas scope for medical assistants. Identify which document controls and explain the "more restrictive controls" principle using this example.
