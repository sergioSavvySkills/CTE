# Comparing Health Practices Across Cultures and Societies

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Research health practices used by different cultures using credible ethnographic and biomedical sources
- Compare practices across cultures using a consistent framework of problem, practice, mechanism, evidence, and integration with biomedicine
- Evaluate the strength of evidence for a traditional practice without dismissing its cultural value
- Avoid common research pitfalls including Orientalism and false equivalence

A medical student on a global-health rotation in the Netherlands observes that midwives, not obstetricians, attend the majority of births in that country. Low-risk births routinely happen at home. Outcomes for mothers and newborns are excellent by every measure. She returns to her US residency, where nearly all births are attended by physicians in hospitals, and wonders: which approach is right? The answer is more complicated than "one is right." The more useful question is: what does the evidence show about each approach, for which patients, in which contexts, and what can each system learn from the other? That is the work of cross-cultural health comparison, and it is the skill this chapter develops.

## Part 1: How to Research and Frame Cross-Cultural Health Comparisons

### Two Verbs, Two Distinct Skills

The standard that drives this chapter has two verbs: research and compare. Each verb requires a different skill set.

**Research** means locating credible sources on a health practice, across both biomedical literature and the ethnographic literature that documents how cultures explain and respond to illness. The biomedical literature, found in PubMed, Cochrane Reviews, and NIH NCCIH databases, evaluates practices using clinical trial evidence. The ethnographic literature, found in journals like *Social Science and Medicine* and *Culture, Medicine, and Psychiatry*, documents what a practice means, who performs it, in what context, and with what cultural purpose. Both types of sources are necessary. A Cochrane Review can tell you whether acupuncture reduces chronic low-back pain. It cannot tell you what acupuncture means to a patient embedded in the worldview of **Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)**, or how that meaning affects treatment adherence and outcomes.

**Compare** means placing two or more practices side by side using a consistent framework so the comparison is fair. The framework this chapter uses has six dimensions: the health problem being addressed, the practice used, the proposed mechanism, the evidence base, the accessibility to patients, and the degree of integration with the biomedical healthcare system. Using the same framework for every culture prevents the common mistake of describing a Western practice in technical detail while treating a non-Western practice as a curiosity.

### The Comparison Framework in Detail

The **biomedical model** is the dominant clinical framework in the US and most high-income countries. It organizes illness into categories defined by laboratory findings, anatomy, and physiology. It has produced enormous advances in diagnosis and treatment. It is also the comparator in this chapter, the reference point against which other approaches are placed.

Using the biomedical model as a reference point does not mean treating it as the only valid framework. Every culture has developed organized practices for addressing common health problems: pain, childbirth, infection, mental illness, dying. Comparing them peer-to-peer means evaluating each on its own logic and evidence, not assuming the biomedical approach is correct simply because it is familiar.

The six-dimension comparison framework works like this:

**Problem:** What health challenge is the practice addressing? Chronic pain? Postpartum recovery? Mental distress? Infectious disease?

**Practice:** What exactly does the practitioner do? What does the patient experience? What materials, techniques, or relationships are involved?

**Mechanism:** What explanation does this cultural framework offer for why the practice works? This may be a biomedical mechanism (anti-inflammatory compounds in an herbal preparation), an energy-based explanation (qi flow in acupuncture), a social mechanism (community support in family-based mental-health care), or a spiritual one (prayer facilitating healing).

**Evidence:** What does the scientific literature say about effectiveness? The NCCIH and Cochrane Reviews are the primary sources here. Evidence can be strong, mixed, weak, or absent. Some traditional practices have strong evidence. Some biomedical practices have weaker evidence than assumed.

**Accessibility:** Who can access this practice, and at what cost? Is it available to people without insurance? Does it require travel, specialized providers, expensive materials, or cultural knowledge that most patients do not have?

**Integration with biomedicine:** Is this practice used alongside biomedical care, in place of it, or in parallel? Does the biomedical system recognize, refer to, or conflict with this practice?

### Sources and How to Use Them

Credible cross-cultural health research requires source types that may be unfamiliar. The WHO Traditional Medicine Programme documents global traditional medicine practices and the evidence base for them. The **NCCIH (National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health)** at NIH publishes the most rigorous US-based evidence reviews on traditional and complementary practices, including acupuncture, herbal preparations, and mind-body practices. **Cochrane Reviews** are systematic reviews that frequently evaluate practices from outside Western biomedicine, including midwifery care, yoga, and mindfulness interventions.

**Medical anthropology**, the scholarly field that studies how cultures explain and respond to illness, provides the ethnographic sources. Arthur Kleinman's work on the explanatory model, the patient's own understanding of what is wrong and why, provides the conceptual framework most useful for understanding what a practice means to the people who use it. Journals like *Social Science and Medicine* publish this research in peer-reviewed form.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A student is building a comparison matrix on chronic pain management. For the "evidence" row on acupuncture, she types "probably works," which the assignment software rejects. She is required to cite an actual NCCIH or Cochrane rating. She finds the 2017 Journal of Pain meta-analysis cited by NCCIH, which concludes that acupuncture shows statistically significant effects for chronic low-back, neck, shoulder, and osteoarthritis pain compared with sham controls. She enters "Strong (NCCIH 2023 review, multiple Cochrane analyses)" and the software accepts it. She then checks the evidence row for opioids for the same chronic low-back pain indication and finds that long-term opioid therapy for chronic non-cancer pain has mixed evidence for sustained efficacy and significant evidence of harms. The comparison she expected to find, "biomedical = proven, traditional = not proven," does not match what the sources actually show.

### Pitfalls to Avoid

Four research pitfalls come up regularly when students compare health practices across cultures.

**Orientalism** means treating non-Western practices as exotic, primitive, or inherently less sophisticated than Western ones. Describing acupuncture as "sticking needles in meridian points based on ancient superstition" while describing Western pain management as "evidence-based pharmacology" applies an unfair standard. Both deserve the same level of evidence scrutiny.

**False equivalence** is the opposite error: treating all practices as equally valid regardless of evidence. A practice with no evidence base is not equivalent to one with multiple randomized trials simply because both originate in cultural tradition. Honest comparison acknowledges evidence strength without dismissing cultural value.

**Appropriation** means extracting a practice from its cultural context and using it in a stripped-down form that ignores meaning. Yoga marketed as fitness without any acknowledgment of its Hindu roots is a common example. Research that treats a traditional practice as a collection of active compounds without acknowledging the relational and spiritual context that makes it work for the people who use it in its original form may be missing the mechanism.

**Ethnocentrism in research design** means applying Western research standards as the only valid standard. Some traditional practices are not amenable to randomized controlled trials because they are inherently individualized or relational. The absence of RCT evidence does not always mean a practice is ineffective, it may mean it has not been studied by a method suited to its design.

## Part 2: Comparing Practices Across Three Health Problems

### Childbirth and Postpartum Recovery

Childbirth is one of the most-compared health experiences across cultures, and the data reveal something unexpected: the US biomedical model produces worse maternal and infant outcomes than several lower-intervention models.

The US standard of care for low-risk births involves physician attendance, hospital setting, continuous electronic fetal monitoring, and a cesarean rate of approximately 32 percent. The **midwifery model of care**, the dominant approach for low-risk births in the Netherlands, United Kingdom, and much of Scandinavia, uses trained midwives rather than physicians, supports home birth as an option for low-risk patients, and relies on continuous human presence rather than technological monitoring. Cochrane Reviews show that midwife-led care for low-risk deliveries produces equivalent or better outcomes on most measures, including lower cesarean rates, lower rates of epidural use (which is not inherently better, but reflects lower rates of intervention), and higher rates of breastfeeding initiation.

**Postpartum confinement** practices, including la cuarentena in Latinx cultures and zuo yuezi in Chinese culture, involve a structured recovery period of 30 to 40 days after birth during which the new mother rests, receives support from family members, eats specific foods, and limits activity. These practices are not a single uniform thing, their specifics vary widely within and across cultures, but they share a common logic: postpartum recovery requires active support, not a rapid return to normal activity. The biomedical literature on postpartum health is increasingly validating this logic, with growing evidence that postpartum mood disorders and physical recovery outcomes are better when social support is high.

The **doula** role, a trained non-clinical support person present during labor and delivery, has expanded significantly in US communities with high maternal mortality, particularly BIPOC communities. The evidence base for doula support is strong: multiple studies show reductions in cesarean rates, preterm birth, and low birth weight when doula support is provided. The doula role exists at the intersection of cultural knowledge, social support, and the biomedical system.

### Pain Management

Pain management offers perhaps the clearest example of how the evidence for traditional practices can exceed what most students expect.

The US biomedical approach to chronic pain relies heavily on opioid analgesics, anti-inflammatory drugs, and procedural interventions. The evidence for long-term opioid therapy for chronic non-cancer pain is mixed: opioids reduce pain in the short term but show limited evidence for sustained benefit and significant evidence of harm including dependence, overdose, and hyperalgesia.

TCM's approach to pain includes acupuncture and herbal preparations. Acupuncture for chronic low-back, neck, and osteoarthritis pain now has strong evidence from multiple large trials and meta-analyses, including the 2017 systematic review published in the *Journal of Pain*. The NCCIH acknowledges acupuncture as an evidence-supported option for several chronic pain conditions. The proposed mechanisms under biomedical investigation include stimulation of endogenous opioid release, modulation of pain-processing pathways in the brain, and local tissue effects at needle sites.

**Ayurveda**, the South Asian traditional medical system, uses herbal preparations and lifestyle practices to address pain conditions. The evidence varies considerably by specific preparation and condition. Turmeric preparations containing curcumin have mixed evidence for arthritis, with some trials showing modest anti-inflammatory effects. Other Ayurvedic preparations have not been adequately studied. A culturally humble clinician evaluates each specific practice on its evidence, rather than treating the entire system as either proven or unproven.

### Mental Health Care

Across many cultures, mental distress is understood primarily through social, spiritual, or relational frameworks rather than biological ones. The US biomedical model of mental health emphasizes diagnosis using DSM categories, pharmacotherapy, and individual psychotherapy. These approaches have solid evidence for specific conditions. But the model also reflects cultural assumptions about individualism, the primacy of biological explanation, and the role of the professional clinician as the appropriate source of help.

Community-based and family-centered mental health approaches used in many African, Asian, and Indigenous cultures treat mental distress as a relational and social event, not just a neurochemical one. Family members are active participants in treatment, not waiting-room bystanders. Community elders, traditional healers, or religious figures may be central to the healing process. Research on the outcomes of psychosis in low- and middle-income countries, where biomedical resources are limited and family and community involvement is high, has repeatedly shown outcomes comparable to or better than outcomes in high-income biomedical settings, a finding that challenges simple assumptions about which model is superior.

The infectious-disease response during the West African Ebola outbreak illustrates a similar point. Initial biomedical responses focused on isolation, barrier nursing, and contact tracing, but collided directly with community burial practices central to the cultural understanding of respectful death. The response became more effective only when public-health teams worked with community and religious leaders to adapt burial practices rather than eliminate them. The lesson: effective public-health response to a disease embedded in cultural practice requires cultural knowledge, not just biomedical knowledge.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A pregnant patient in her third trimester tells her nurse that she plans to do zuo yuezi, a postpartum confinement period of 30 days, with her mother and mother-in-law providing care and cooking traditional foods. The nurse recognizes this as a Chinese postpartum recovery tradition. Rather than dismissing it or ignoring it, she uses the Kleinman explanatory-model framing: "Can you tell me what the confinement period will look like for you? What foods are you planning? Are there any restrictions, like on bathing or going outside, that I should know about?" She learns that the patient's family plans to restrict salt (good), restrict cold foods (culturally meaningful, not harmful), and restrict the patient from showering for 30 days (a practice worth discussing for hygiene and wound care after delivery). She addresses the one clinical concern directly and affirms the rest of the plan. The conversation took four minutes and prevented a conflict that would have damaged the therapeutic relationship.

### Key terms

**Biomedical model:** the dominant US clinical framework organizing illness into categories defined by laboratory findings, anatomy, and physiology; the comparator for cross-cultural analysis in this chapter.

**Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM):** a culturally grounded system including acupuncture and herbal preparations; acupuncture has strong evidence for several chronic pain conditions per NCCIH and Cochrane Reviews.

**Ayurveda:** the South Asian traditional medical system using herbal preparations and lifestyle practices; evidence varies by specific preparation and condition.

**Midwifery model of care:** a low-intervention, relationship-centered approach to childbirth used widely in the Netherlands, UK, and Scandinavia; Cochrane Reviews show equivalent or better outcomes for low-risk deliveries compared to physician-led hospital birth.

**Postpartum confinement:** structured postpartum recovery practices including la cuarentena (Latinx) and zuo yuezi (Chinese) involving family support, rest, and dietary guidance for 30 to 40 days after birth.

**Doula:** a trained non-clinical childbirth and postpartum support role expanded in BIPOC US communities; strong evidence for reducing cesarean rates and preterm birth.

**NCCIH (National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health):** the NIH center that publishes US evidence reviews on traditional and complementary practices; the primary source for evidence ratings in this chapter.

**Cochrane Review:** a systematic-review format that frequently evaluates practices from outside Western biomedicine; the highest level of evidence in this chapter's evidence ratings.

**Medical anthropology:** the scholarly field studying how cultures explain and respond to illness; the source of the Kleinman explanatory-model framework and ethnographic research in this chapter.

**Cultural appropriation:** using a traditional practice stripped of its cultural context; a research and clinical pitfall to recognize.

### Chapter summary

Cross-cultural health comparison requires two skills: research using both biomedical and ethnographic sources, and comparison using a consistent six-dimension framework. The evidence hierarchy for traditional practices is more complex than most students expect: acupuncture for chronic pain has strong Cochrane-level evidence, midwife-led care for low-risk birth has better outcomes than physician-led care on several measures, and community-based mental health approaches in low-income settings show outcomes comparable to Western psychiatry. These findings do not mean all traditional practices are equally valid. They mean that each practice deserves an honest evidence evaluation rather than dismissal by assumption. Four pitfalls, Orientalism, false equivalence, cultural appropriation, and applying only Western research methods as the valid standard, undermine fair comparison. Avoiding them is part of becoming a clinician who can actually serve the patients who walk through the door.

### Review questions

1. A classmate says that the Cochrane evidence for midwife-led care "proves midwifery is better than physician care." Using what you know about the comparison framework and evidence interpretation, explain why that claim is too simple, and restate it in a form that accurately reflects what the evidence shows.
2. You are building a comparison matrix on chronic pain management. The evidence row for long-term opioid therapy for chronic non-cancer pain should read "strong," "mixed," "weak," or "absent." Based on what you have read, what rating applies, and what does that tell you about the comparison with acupuncture?
3. A family using a traditional herbalism practice tells their clinician they are combining herbal preparations with the prescribed medication for a chronic condition. The clinician has never heard of the specific herb. Using the Kleinman explanatory-model framing and what you know about avoiding the four research pitfalls, describe how the clinician should respond. What should they ask, and what should they avoid saying?
