# Interpreting Health Science Technical Material

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Navigate a drug package insert and locate the section that answers a specific clinical question
- Extract the key findings, population, and limitations from a journal abstract using IMRAD structure
- Apply a clinical practice guideline recommendation to a specific patient and identify whether it fits
- Read a Safety Data Sheet to find the information needed in an emergency

A pharmacist is checking a new prescription for a patient who takes a blood thinner. She opens the package insert for the new drug and goes directly to Drug Interactions. Thirty seconds later she has her answer and calls the prescriber. She did not read the insert from front to back. She knew which section contained the information she needed before she opened the document. That is the skill this chapter builds.

### The landscape of health science technical material

Healthcare workers encounter four types of technical documents daily: drug package inserts, journal abstracts, clinical practice guidelines, and Safety Data Sheets. Each has a defined structure that puts specific information in a predictable location so a clinician can find it without reading everything.

The habit that makes technical reading efficient is naming the decision before opening the document. Checking whether a patient can take a drug? Go to Contraindications and Drug Interactions. Checking the dose? Go to Dosage and Administration. Evaluating whether a study applies to your patient? Go to Methods. The document is a reference, not a narrative.

### Drug package inserts: a map of the label

Every FDA-approved drug in the United States carries a **package insert**, also called the **Prescribing Information (PI)**. The format is defined by federal regulation under 21 CFR 201.56 and 201.57. That means every PI in every hospital has the same sections in the same order.

The **Highlights of Prescribing Information** is the first section and the fast-reference summary. It fits on one page and covers indications, dosing, the most important warnings, and contraindications. When a clinician needs a quick answer, this is the first stop.

Below the Highlights, the full label unfolds in this order: Indications and Usage, Dosage and Administration, Dosage Forms and Strengths, Contraindications, Warnings and Precautions, Adverse Reactions, Drug Interactions, and Use in Specific Populations. A skilled reader knows that a question about whether a drug is safe in pregnancy lives in Use in Specific Populations. A question about which other drugs interact lives in Drug Interactions. A question about the most serious risk lives in Warnings and Precautions or the **black-box warning**, which appears in a bordered box at the very top of the label and signals an FDA finding of serious or life-threatening risk.

Learning the structure of a PI is a one-time investment that pays back every time you open one. The documents are dense, small-font, and intimidating. They are also navigable, once you know the map.

### Journal abstracts: reading IMRAD under time pressure

A peer-reviewed clinical research article follows a standard structure known as **IMRAD**: Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion. Every abstract mirrors this structure in miniature.

The Introduction states the research question. Skim it quickly. The Methods section is where you spend real time: it tells you who was studied, how many, what ages, what health conditions, and what was done to them. This is where you judge whether the findings apply to your patient. A study on adults over 65 does not automatically apply to a 40-year-old.

The Results section gives you the numbers. Read the effect size, not just the direction. A statistically significant result can still be clinically trivial if the effect is small. The Discussion explains what the authors think the results mean and, critically, names the **study limitations**: sample size, study population characteristics, funding source, number of study sites, and length of follow-up. A study that followed patients for 6 weeks cannot speak to 5-year outcomes.

Go to Methods first, then Discussion. Those two sections determine whether the Results are worth acting on.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A student reviews an abstract reporting that a pain medication reduced pain scores by 2 points in a trial of 48 patients aged 18 to 35, followed for 72 hours. She checks Methods: narrow age range, short follow-up, excluded kidney impairment. She checks Discussion: authors acknowledge the small sample and short follow-up as limitations. Her conclusion: interesting finding, but underpowered, narrow population, and too short to say anything about chronic use. She would not apply this result broadly, and the analysis took four minutes.

### Clinical practice guidelines: reading the recommendation and its boundaries

A **clinical practice guideline** is a formal recommendation document produced by an expert panel from an organization such as the USPSTF, AHA/ACC, or CDC. Guidelines translate research evidence into actionable recommendations.

Every recommendation carries a strength rating. The **USPSTF grade** system uses letters A through I. Grade A means high certainty of substantial benefit. Grade B is high certainty of moderate benefit. Grade C is moderate certainty of small benefit. Grade D means the service causes more harm than good. Grade I means evidence is insufficient. These grades drive real decisions: insurers must cover Grade A and B preventive services without cost-sharing.

The other essential check is the target population. A recommendation for adults aged 50 to 75 does not automatically apply to a 45-year-old or an 80-year-old. Read the population criteria before applying any guideline, and note that applying it outside those criteria requires clinical judgment the guideline alone cannot provide.

### Safety Data Sheets and primary sources

Any clinical or laboratory setting that uses chemicals is required by OSHA to maintain a **Safety Data Sheet (SDS)** for each one. The SDS follows a 16-section format. In an emergency you do not have time to read all 16. Know the three that matter first: Section 4 (First Aid Measures) tells you what to do if someone is exposed right now; Section 8 (Exposure Controls) tells you what protective equipment is needed; Section 6 (Accidental Release Measures) covers spill response. Healthcare workers encounter chemicals on every shift, and the OSHA right-to-know standard requires that SDS documents be accessible in every workspace where chemicals are used.

The same principle of knowing the structure before the emergency applies to every technical document. Healthcare information travels through layers of summaries and secondary sources before it reaches a clinician. A news headline may cite a relative risk number without the absolute difference. A clinical summary may drop the age restriction from a guideline. When the stakes are high, go to the **primary vs. secondary source**: the actual guideline, the full package insert, or the original study, not a condensed retelling.

### Key terms

**IMRAD:** Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion; the standard structure for peer-reviewed biomedical articles and abstracts.

**Package insert / Prescribing Information (PI):** the FDA-regulated labeling document for a drug; 21 CFR 201.56 and 201.57 define the required sections.

**Highlights of Prescribing Information:** the short front-of-label summary of the PI with indications, dosing, warnings, and contraindications, designed for fast reference.

**Black-box warning:** the FDA's strongest warning on a drug label, boxed and prominently placed because of serious or life-threatening risk.

**Clinical practice guideline:** a formal recommendation document (USPSTF, AHA/ACC, CDC) with graded strength-of-recommendation and quality-of-evidence ratings.

**USPSTF grade:** letters A, B, C, D, I that rate a prevention recommendation's strength and the evidence behind it; drives whether insurers must cover a service.

**Safety Data Sheet (SDS):** the 16-section OSHA/GHS document for any workplace chemical that covers hazards, handling, storage, and emergency response.

**Primary vs. secondary source:** a primary source is the original study, statute, or label; a secondary source is a summary or news story; high-stakes decisions require the primary.

**Study limitations:** the sample size, population, funding, follow-up length, and site issues that constrain how broadly a study's findings apply.

### Chapter summary

Healthcare workers read technical documents to answer specific questions under time pressure, and the skill is navigation, not end-to-end reading. Drug package inserts follow a federal format with eight named sections; the Highlights page is the fast-reference summary, and the black-box warning signals the most serious risk. Journal abstracts follow IMRAD structure; the Methods section tells you whether the study population matches your patient, and the Discussion section tells you what limitations the authors themselves acknowledge. Clinical practice guidelines carry strength-of-recommendation grades, and those grades only apply to the population the guideline was written for. Safety Data Sheets provide emergency-response information in a predictable 16-section format. In every case, when the decision is high-stakes, verify against the primary source rather than relying on a summary.

### Review questions

1. A physician considers prescribing a medication to a patient who has severe kidney disease. Name the two sections of the drug's Prescribing Information you would check first and explain what information each section would provide.
2. A journal abstract reports that a dietary supplement reduced blood pressure by 4 mmHg in a study of 30 college students followed for 8 weeks. Identify two specific study limitations present in this description and explain how each one limits the applicability of the finding.
3. A USPSTF guideline recommends colorectal cancer screening with Grade A strength for adults aged 45 to 75. A 42-year-old patient asks if she should begin screening. Using the concept of USPSTF grades and target population, explain how you would answer her question and what additional information you would need.

### References

- **FDA 21 CFR 201.56 and 201.57, "Physician Labeling Rule":** the federal regulation that defines the mandatory structure of every US drug package insert, including the Highlights of Prescribing Information section.
- **ICMJE (International Committee of Medical Journal Editors) Recommendations:** the source for the IMRAD abstract and article structure used across peer-reviewed biomedical journals.
- **USPSTF Grade Definitions and AHA/ACC Class of Recommendation / Level of Evidence framework:** the standard schemes students will see for rating clinical-guideline recommendations.
- **OSHA Hazard Communication Standard (29 CFR 1910.1200) / GHS Safety Data Sheet format:** 16-section SDS structure used in clinical labs and on any chemical a healthcare worker handles.
- **CDC MMWR (Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report):** accessible example of public-health technical material students can practice interpreting.
