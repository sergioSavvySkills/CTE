# Reviewing Patient Rights Documents

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the key patient-rights documents used in Texas healthcare settings
- Review each document for completeness, currency, correct signer, and appropriate witnessing
- Recognize common documentation failures and describe the corrective action each requires
- Explain the practical difference between a Notice of Privacy Practices acknowledgment and an informed consent signature

A patient arrives at a Texas hospital for a planned procedure. Before going to the pre-op area, she signs four documents at the admissions desk in about three minutes. Two of them are for legal protection of her rights. One is required by HIPAA. One is required by the Joint Commission. None of the staff explain what each document is. The patient signs because that is what you do when you go to the hospital. This chapter is about what those documents actually are, what makes each legally sufficient, and why healthcare workers need to be able to review them competently, even when they are not the ones obtaining the signature.

### The difference between executing and reviewing

Chapter c10A covered the situations where patient autonomy is exercised. This chapter covers the documents that record that exercise. The standard is a records-literacy standard: the skill is reviewing, reading, spotting gaps, and routing for correction, not executing the documents or obtaining consent yourself. A student on rotation who is handed a chart and asked to check forms before a procedure is doing this work.

Reviewing a document is not passive. It requires knowing what each document should contain, what a complete form looks like, what signatures and witnesses are required by law, and what happens if something is missing. The next section covers the major documents one by one.

### The major patient-rights documents

**Informed consent form.** A procedure-specific document that records the patient's agreement to a specific treatment or procedure. A complete informed consent form includes the patient's name and medical record number, a description of the procedure in plain language, the material risks and benefits, the alternatives including no treatment, the identity of the practitioner who obtained consent, and the signatures of the patient and a witness with date and time.

What to check: Is every required field completed? Is the procedure named correctly, and does it match what is actually scheduled? Was the form signed at the right time (before sedation, not after)? Is the signer the patient, or a surrogate? If a surrogate signed, is their authority documented?

**Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP).** A HIPAA-required document (45 CFR §164.520) that every covered entity must give patients at their first encounter. The NPP describes how the facility uses and discloses protected health information and explains the patient's rights under HIPAA, including the right to request restrictions, to access their own records, and to file a complaint. The patient signs an acknowledgment of receipt, not a consent to treatment.

This distinction is one of the most commonly misunderstood points in healthcare documentation. A patient's signature on an NPP means: "I received this document." It does not mean: "I agree to treatment," "I consent to this procedure," or "I waive my privacy rights." Treating the NPP acknowledgment as equivalent to an informed consent form is both a legal error and an ethical one.

**Patient Bill of Rights.** A facility-specific document, required by the Joint Commission and CMS Conditions of Participation (§482.13), that lists the rights every patient has while in that facility. These typically include the right to receive information about care, to participate in treatment decisions, to refuse treatment, to have pain addressed, to be free from abuse and neglect, to confidentiality, and to file a grievance. The Patient Bill of Rights is typically provided in admission materials and posted in visible locations. It is not signed by the patient; it is provided to them.

**Texas Directive to Physicians (Directive).** Texas's statutory "living will" form, established under Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 166. The directive takes effect only if the patient has a terminal or irreversible condition and cannot communicate their wishes. It allows a patient to specify in advance whether they want life-sustaining treatment, whether they want comfort-focused care only, or specific instructions about particular interventions.

What to check: Is the document signed and dated by the patient? Are there two eligible witnesses or a notary? Texas law disqualifies certain witnesses: the attending physician, anyone who would benefit financially from the patient's death (such as an heir), anyone who has a claim against the patient's estate, and anyone who operates, is employed by, or has financial interest in the healthcare facility. An advance directive witnessed by the patient's adult son who stands to inherit is not legally valid in Texas.

**Medical Power of Attorney (Texas).** A Texas statutory form under HSC Chapter 166 that designates a person to make healthcare decisions if the patient loses capacity. The designated agent can be any competent adult the patient trusts. The form requires either two eligible witnesses or notarization, with the same witness restrictions that apply to the Directive to Physicians.

What to check: Is the form signed by the patient and properly witnessed or notarized? Is the designated agent named clearly? Does the agent's name on the chart match the agent on file? If a surrogate is making decisions based on a Medical Power of Attorney, the original document should be in the chart.

**Out-of-Hospital DNR.** A Texas-specific form that directs EMS providers and healthcare workers in non-hospital settings (home, nursing home, hospice) not to attempt cardiopulmonary resuscitation. Unlike a hospital DNR order, which is a physician order entered in the chart, the out-of-hospital DNR is a form signed by the patient (or the patient's legal guardian or Medical Power of Attorney agent) and a physician. It must be available in the setting where care is provided.

**In-hospital DNR order.** A physician order entered into the patient's chart directing the clinical team not to attempt resuscitation. This order is based on the patient's informed decision or, when the patient lacks capacity, on the surrogate's decision in line with the patient's advance directive or best interests. The order must be signed by a physician.

**AMA discharge form.** Documentation that a patient has chosen to leave the hospital against medical advice. A complete AMA form records the patient's understanding of the risks of leaving, the alternatives offered, and the patient's voluntary decision to leave despite that information. The form should not be used as a punitive measure, and a patient who leaves AMA retains the right to return for care.

**Release of records authorization.** A patient's written authorization to disclose PHI outside of treatment, payment, or healthcare operations. A complete authorization includes the name of the person or entity to whom disclosure is made, a description of the information to be disclosed, the purpose, and an expiration date or event.

### Common documentation failures

Certain errors appear repeatedly across clinical settings. Knowing them allows reviewers to catch problems before a procedure proceeds on incomplete paperwork.

Consent signed after sedation was given. This is the most consequential failure: the voluntariness element of informed consent is gone once a patient has received premedication. The remedy is to re-obtain consent when the patient has capacity, which may require rescheduling the procedure.

An advance directive witnessed by an **ineligible witness** under Texas HSC Chapter 166. The most common example is a family member who is also an heir. The document may look complete, but it is not legally valid. The patient, if still able to communicate, should execute a new directive with eligible witnesses.

An AMA form that records only the patient's signature without documentation of the risks explained and the alternatives offered. This is not informed refusal; it is just a signature. The form is not sufficient protection for the clinical team and does not adequately serve the patient.

A consent form with the wrong procedure name, a transposition of left and right, or missing the signature of the practitioner who had the consent conversation. These errors can indicate that the form was completed by someone other than the person who did the actual counseling.

An NPP acknowledgment used as if it were treatment consent. This is a conceptual failure as much as a documentation one. Staff who say "she already signed her HIPAA form" in response to a question about a consent-to-treat document are confusing two entirely different documents.

A Medical Power of Attorney without notarization or with a missing agent name. Either gap makes the document legally insufficient to authorize surrogate decision-making.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A health science student on a clinical rotation is handed a chart to review before a patient's morning procedure. The chart includes a consent form signed at 7:45 a.m. The anesthesia record shows the patient received IV midazolam at 7:30 a.m. The consent form has all fields completed and the patient's signature is present. The student flags the time discrepancy and asks the charge nurse before the procedure continues. The charge nurse reviews the times, confirms the sequence, and contacts the surgeon. The procedure is paused while a new consent conversation takes place with the patient, who is now alert, and a new form is signed with the correct time. The student did not obtain consent. The student reviewed a document, identified a specific failure, and routed it appropriately. That is the skill this chapter develops.

### Key terms

**Informed consent form:** the procedure-specific document capturing patient identifiers, procedure description, risks, benefits, and alternatives, with witnessed signatures and accurate timing.

**Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP):** the HIPAA-required document (45 CFR §164.520) given at first encounter; the patient acknowledges receipt, which is not the same as consenting to treatment.

**Patient Bill of Rights:** the facility-specific statement of patient rights per Joint Commission and CMS Conditions of Participation; provided to patients, not signed by them.

**Directive to Physicians:** Texas statutory "living will" form (HSC Chapter 166); takes effect if the patient has a terminal or irreversible condition and cannot communicate.

**Medical Power of Attorney (Texas):** Texas statutory form designating a surrogate decision-maker under HSC Chapter 166; requires two eligible witnesses or notarization.

**Out-of-Hospital DNR:** Texas-specific form signed by the patient (or surrogate) and physician directing EMS and non-hospital settings not to resuscitate.

**AMA discharge form:** documentation of informed refusal of continued care; requires evidence of the risks explained and the patient's voluntary decision, not just a signature.

**Ineligible witness:** a witness disqualified under Texas advance-directive statutes, including the attending physician, any heir or beneficiary, and anyone with a financial interest in the healthcare facility.

**Release of records authorization:** patient consent to disclose PHI outside treatment, payment, or operations; must name the recipient, the information, the purpose, and include an expiration.

**Voluntariness:** the requirement that consent was given freely, without sedation, coercion, or duress; one of the five elements verified on document review.

### Chapter summary

Patient-rights documents are not paperwork. They are the legal and ethical record of the most fundamental rights patients have in a healthcare setting. Each document, from the informed consent form to the advance directive to the AMA discharge form, has specific requirements for completeness, currency, correct signers, and appropriate witnessing. Reviewing these documents is a distinct and teachable skill. The most common failures include consent obtained after sedation, advance directives with ineligible witnesses, surrogates signing without documented authority, and NPP acknowledgments treated as treatment consent. Healthcare workers who can identify these failures and route them for correction protect both patients and their institutions.

### Review questions

1. A patient signs an NPP acknowledgment and an informed consent form for a procedure in the same three-minute window at admission without any explanation from the admissions clerk. Explain the legal and ethical distinction between these two documents, and describe what the admissions clerk should have done differently.
2. A Texas Directive to Physicians is submitted to the chart. It is dated and signed by the patient. The witnesses are listed as the patient's adult daughter and the attending physician. Is this document legally sufficient? Identify each specific problem and the appropriate corrective action.
3. A patient is admitted through the emergency department and is unconscious. No advance directive is on file. The patient's adult brother arrives and tells the clinical team that the patient "would never want to be on a machine." Using the Texas Consent to Medical Treatment Act (HSC §313), explain who has authority to make healthcare decisions for this patient and what standard should guide those decisions.
