# Regulatory Agencies in Healthcare

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify the six major US healthcare regulatory agencies: WHO, CDC, OSHA, FDA, The Joint Commission, and NIH
- Describe the jurisdiction and core function of each agency
- Match specific clinical practices and workplace rules to the agency that drives them
- Explain how the agencies relate to one another, including the CMS deeming mechanism

A student on a clinical rotation in a Texas hospital reaches for a sharps container after drawing blood, ties on a yellow gown before entering a contact-precaution room, and notices a poster listing the "Do Not Use" abbreviation list near the nursing station. Every one of those practices exists because a specific agency wrote a rule, a standard, or a funding condition that made it part of daily life in that building. Knowing which agency is behind which rule lets you trust the rules, question them intelligently, and apply them confidently.

## Part 1: The Six Agencies, Who They Are and What They Do

### Why six agencies, and why do they matter?

Healthcare in the United States is not governed by a single authority. Instead, a network of federal agencies, international bodies, and private organizations divides responsibility by topic, by setting, and by whether the primary customer is the patient, the worker, or the public. When a student understands this network, a rule stops being an arbitrary rule and becomes a traceable decision made by an identifiable body for a specific reason.

Six agencies form the regulatory backbone of US healthcare: the **World Health Organization (WHO)**, the **Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)**, the **Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)**, the **U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)**, **The Joint Commission (TJC)**, and the **National Institutes of Health (NIH)**. Each sits in a different part of the system.

### WHO: the global standard-setter

The **World Health Organization (WHO)** is a United Nations agency headquartered in Geneva, Switzerland. Its mission is global public health: setting international norms, coordinating pandemic response, producing evidence-based guidelines, and maintaining the International Health Regulations that govern how countries respond to cross-border health threats.

In daily US clinical practice, the WHO's fingerprints appear on two tools that most nurses and students encounter immediately: the **Five Moments for Hand Hygiene** and the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist. The Five Moments framework defines exactly when hand hygiene must happen relative to each patient contact, and it is the operational model behind hand-hygiene posters in virtually every US hospital.

One important distinction: the WHO recommends, it does not legally bind US practice. The United States is a WHO member state but is not required by law to implement WHO guidelines exactly as written. US agencies adopt or adapt WHO guidance, which is why CDC hand-hygiene guidance looks a great deal like WHO's, but with US-specific additions.

### CDC: the US disease-prevention authority

The **CDC** is a federal agency within the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). Its core functions are disease surveillance, outbreak investigation, prevention guideline development, and immunization recommendations.

The CDC's most visible output in a clinical setting is the ACIP immunization schedule, the annual schedule of recommended vaccines for children and adults maintained by the CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices. It also publishes the **Guideline for Isolation Precautions**, the foundational US document that defines standard precautions, contact precautions, droplet precautions, and airborne precautions.

CDC guidelines are not statutes. A hospital cannot be fined directly by the CDC for failing to follow an isolation guideline. However, because The Joint Commission and CMS incorporate CDC guidelines into their accreditation and payment requirements, the practical effect is the same as law for most US hospitals.

### OSHA: protecting the worker

The **Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)** is a federal agency within the Department of Labor (DOL). Its jurisdiction is workplace safety, and in healthcare, that means protecting nurses, medical assistants, technicians, and students from hazards on the job.

OSHA's most important healthcare regulation is found at **29 CFR 1910.1030**, known as the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard. This is the rule behind the sharps container on the wall, the requirement to wear gloves when touching blood or body fluids, and the protocol for what happens when a needlestick occurs. Another key regulation, 29 CFR 1910.1200, is the Hazard Communication Standard. It requires that a **Safety Data Sheet (SDS)** be accessible for every chemical in the workplace, and it sets the labeling requirements on chemical containers.

A critical conceptual point: OSHA's primary customer is the worker, not the patient. However, protecting a healthcare worker from bloodborne-pathogen exposure and protecting a patient from healthcare-associated infection often require the exact same practices, so worker safety and patient safety overlap constantly.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A medical assisting student accidentally sticks her finger with a used needle while recapping it. Within minutes, her preceptor is walking through the OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030): the wound is irrigated, the source patient is identified for possible testing, the student is referred for post-exposure evaluation and counseling on post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP). The event is recorded on the OSHA 300 log, the facility's log of work-related injuries. The student now understands that the poster on the wall about not recapping needles was not a suggestion.

## Part 2: FDA, TJC, NIH, and How the Agencies Connect

### FDA: from lab to bedside

The **FDA** is another federal agency under HHS. Its jurisdiction is products: drugs, medical devices, biologics, blood products, tobacco, and certain food safety matters. Before any medication can be prescribed or any device can be implanted, it must pass through an FDA review process that evaluates safety and efficacy.

After a product reaches the market, the FDA continues to watch it through post-market surveillance. Clinicians and patients can report unexpected adverse events through **MedWatch**, the FDA's voluntary reporting system. When a device causes a patient's death or serious injury, facilities are legally required to file a **Medical Device Report (MDR)** under 21 CFR 803. When a drug poses serious safety risks, the FDA can require a **Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS)**, a set of conditions that must be met before the drug can be dispensed.

One distinction matters here: the FDA approves products, it does not directly regulate how a clinician uses them. A physician can legally prescribe a drug for an unapproved use (off-label prescribing). The FDA's authority is over the product, not the individual clinical decision, which is governed instead by state licensure laws and the professional standard of care.

### The Joint Commission: private power with regulatory reach

**The Joint Commission (TJC)** is a private, non-profit organization that accredits hospitals, health systems, ambulatory care centers, and other healthcare settings. Students sometimes assume it is a government agency because its standards carry so much force. It is not.

TJC's authority comes from a mechanism called **CMS deeming authority**. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) sets Conditions of Participation that facilities must meet to receive Medicare and Medicaid reimbursement. CMS has determined that TJC accreditation is sufficient proof that a facility meets those Conditions. Because almost every US hospital depends on Medicare and Medicaid payments to operate, losing TJC accreditation is effectively the same as losing the ability to stay open. That is the enforcement chain that gives a private non-profit quasi-regulatory force.

The TJC's most student-visible outputs are the **National Patient Safety Goals (NPSGs)**, updated annually, which target specific, measurable safety practices. One NPSG, for example, requires two patient identifiers before any procedure or medication. Another requires a Time-Out before surgical incision. TJC also maintains the official "Do Not Use" abbreviation list, the reason certain shortcuts that look efficient can never appear in a clinical order.

### NIH: research as the foundation of all the rules

The **NIH** is a federal agency under HHS and the world's largest public funder of biomedical research. It operates 27 institutes and centers, each focused on a disease area or research discipline. NIH does not write clinical guidelines or enforce safety standards in the way CDC, OSHA, or TJC do. Its function is to generate and fund the evidence that all those other bodies use when they write guidelines and set standards.

When the CDC recommends a specific hand-hygiene technique or the FDA approves a new drug, the evidence review behind that decision traces, in large part, to NIH-funded research. NIH also runs human-subjects protections through the Office for Human Research Protections (OHRP) and maintains ClinicalTrials.gov, the public registry where active clinical studies are listed.

### How the agencies relate to each other

The six agencies do not operate in isolation. WHO and CDC collaborate on global public health and pandemic response, and CDC adapts WHO guidance for US use. CDC and OSHA frequently overlap on infection control: CDC publishes the science, OSHA writes the enforceable regulation. FDA and CDC both track adverse events, but through separate pipelines (MedWatch for products, disease surveillance for population-level signals).

TJC standards regularly incorporate CDC and WHO guidance, translating scientific recommendations into accreditation requirements. NIH produces the evidence that feeds CDC guidelines, FDA reviews, and professional-organization standards. State agencies in Texas, including the Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS) and the Texas Medical Board, implement and enforce federal rules at the state level, which is what students encounter directly during Texas clinical rotations.

### Key terms

**World Health Organization (WHO):** global public-health standards body and source of the Five Moments for Hand Hygiene, WHO Surgical Safety Checklist, and the International Classification of Diseases (ICD).

**CDC:** US federal disease-surveillance and prevention agency under HHS; publishes ACIP immunization schedules, isolation-precaution guidelines, and HAI prevention bundles.

**OSHA:** federal workplace-safety agency under DOL; enforces the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard, Hazard Communication Standard, and Respiratory Protection Standard.

**FDA:** federal agency regulating drugs, medical devices, biologics, and tobacco; oversees premarket approval and post-market surveillance including MedWatch and MDR.

**The Joint Commission (TJC):** private non-profit accreditor of US hospitals; publishes National Patient Safety Goals and the "Do Not Use" abbreviation list.

**NIH:** federal biomedical-research funder under HHS; operates 27 institutes and centers and maintains ClinicalTrials.gov and OHRP oversight of human subjects research.

**CMS deeming authority:** the mechanism by which CMS recognizes TJC accreditation as proof of meeting Medicare and Medicaid Conditions of Participation, giving TJC standards effectively regulatory force.

**National Patient Safety Goals (NPSGs):** The Joint Commission's annual set of specific, measurable bedside-safety priorities that accredited facilities must meet.

**Five Moments for Hand Hygiene:** WHO's operational framework defining exactly when hand hygiene must occur relative to each patient contact, widely adopted in US hospitals.

**29 CFR 1910.1030:** the OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard; the federal rule requiring PPE, sharps containers, and post-exposure protocols for workers with occupational blood or body-fluid exposure.

### Chapter summary

Six agencies shape the safety practices that healthcare students encounter every day on clinical rotation. WHO provides global guidance, including the hand-hygiene framework most US hospitals use. CDC issues the US disease-surveillance and infection-control guidelines that define standard and transmission-based precautions. OSHA enforces workplace-safety rules for healthcare workers, most importantly the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard. FDA regulates drugs, devices, and biologics from approval through post-market surveillance. The Joint Commission accredits hospitals and publishes the National Patient Safety Goals, wielding quasi-regulatory force through the CMS deeming mechanism. NIH funds the research that ultimately drives all of the others. These agencies overlap, reference each other, and layer their requirements, which is why a single clinical practice, such as hand hygiene, can trace back to WHO, CDC, TJC, and state agencies simultaneously.

### Review questions

1. A hospital is notified that it may lose The Joint Commission accreditation. Explain, using the CMS deeming authority mechanism, why this is effectively the same as a threat to the hospital's ability to operate. What federal agency is ultimately the source of this pressure?
2. A new drug is recalled after it causes serious liver damage in patients. Walk through the agencies involved: which one originally approved the drug for sale, which reporting system would collect adverse event reports from clinicians, and which type of mandatory report must a hospital file if the drug contributed to a patient death?
3. OSHA and CDC both issue guidance about infection control in healthcare settings. Identify one specific area where their rules overlap, and explain why a student needs to know both even though they seem to cover the same topic.
