# Reporting Equipment and Technology Malfunctions

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify when a device malfunction must be reported and at what level
- Explain the parallel tracks of internal and external reporting
- Describe what must be documented and preserved at each step in the reporting process
- Recognize the role of Just Culture in making reporting systems work

A certified nursing assistant finishes her shift and mentions in the hallway to the oncoming nurse that "the pump in room 12 was acting weird all night but the patient seemed okay." She does not file a report, does not label the device, and does not notify the charge nurse through the formal system. Two days later, the same pump malfunctions again on a different patient and delivers ten times the ordered dose of a blood thinner. Because the first event was never documented, there is no record that the pump had already been identified as a problem. This chapter explains what should have happened after that first event, and why the reporting process exists.

### Why reporting matters beyond the individual patient

When a device fails silently, it fails again. The reporting system is the mechanism that breaks that cycle. Filing a report about a device malfunction does five distinct things.

For the immediate patient, a report triggers a replacement device and medical follow-up. For other patients in the same facility, a reported malfunction can trigger an inspection of every identical device on the floor. For the institution, patterns across reports reveal preventive-maintenance gaps and training needs that no single event would surface. For the wider healthcare system, the FDA's **Medical Device Report (MDR)** database aggregates reports from facilities across the country, which is how device recalls happen and how design flaws get corrected before they injure more patients. And for the individual worker, properly reporting an event provides legal protection. A nurse who silently works around a failing pump and does not file a report has created personal liability if that pump later harms a patient.

### Not all events require the same response

Before describing the reporting process, students need to understand that different events require different levels of response. The severity of the event determines which reports must be filed.

A **device failure with no patient harm** and no close call requires an internal work order to biomed and may require an internal incident report depending on facility policy. It does not generally rise to the level of an FDA mandatory report.

A **device failure that was a near-miss**, meaning something almost went wrong but did not, requires an internal incident report. Most facility policies require escalation to risk management. Whether an FDA report is required depends on the device type and the potential severity.

A **device failure that contributed to patient harm** requires an internal incident report, risk-management escalation, and may trigger a mandatory FDA Medical Device Report under 21 CFR Part 803, depending on the seriousness of the injury.

A **device failure that contributed to patient death** requires the full set: internal incident report, risk-management escalation, a mandatory FDA MDR filed within 10 working days, and potentially a Texas DSHS notification and a Joint Commission Sentinel Event report.

### Track 1: internal reporting, every time

No matter what level of harm occurred, every device malfunction event begins with internal reporting. This track has six steps, and all six must happen.

**Step 1: Notify the clinical supervisor or charge nurse immediately.** This is verbal and direct, not a hallway mention on the way out the door. The supervisor needs to know in real time so they can ensure patient safety and activate the rest of the process.

**Step 2: Take the device out of service and label it.** This step comes from Chapter 12B. The "Do Not Use" label, with date, time, initials, and a brief description, is the physical anchor for everything that follows.

**Step 3: Document factually in the patient record.** The patient chart records what happened clinically: the device event, the patient's status before and during and after, and every action taken. The chart must not be edited retroactively to match what the device should have done. Retroactive alteration of a medical record is both an ethical violation and a legal problem.

**Step 4: File the internal incident report.** Most facilities use web-based systems such as RL6, Midas, or Datix. The incident report is separate from the patient chart and is used by risk management and quality teams to track patterns. The incident report must be factual and non-blaming. Writing "Nurse X was careless with the infusion pump" is not an incident report, it is a disciplinary accusation. Write what you observed, when, where, what the device did, and what actions were taken.

**Step 5: Submit a work order to biomedical engineering.** The biomed work order is the clinical engineering department's authorization to investigate the device. It must include enough detail, device make, model, serial number, lot number, software version, and a description of the failure, for biomed to trace the problem. The biomed team does not guess; they investigate based on what you document.

**Step 6: Preserve the device and all associated items.** Do not throw away the IV tubing, the blood glucose strip, the electrode pads, the packaging, or any other disposable associated with the event. Photographs are appropriate if relevant evidence might be disturbed before biomed arrives. Every item that gets discarded is a piece of the investigation that disappears permanently.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> During a chemotherapy infusion, a nurse notices that the smart infusion pump has been running at a rate 10 times higher than programmed. The error is caught after 200 mL of chemotherapy have been delivered instead of the intended 20 mL. The nurse stops the infusion, assesses the patient, calls the oncology physician, and activates the rapid-response team. She then labels the pump "Do Not Use," saves the IV tubing and the settings printout, and calls the charge nurse. Within the hour she has filed an internal incident report in Datix with the device serial number, the programmed versus actual rate, the time frame, the patient's current status, and the names of every clinician notified. She submits a biomed work order. Risk management is notified by the charge nurse. Two days later, the MDR process is initiated. The chemotherapy pump is found to have a software defect. Three more pumps of the same model are inspected.

### Track 2: external reporting, when required

Internal reporting is always required. External regulatory reporting depends on the severity and the type of event.

**FDA Medical Device Report (MDR), 21 CFR Part 803:** User facilities, meaning hospitals, nursing homes, and ambulatory surgical centers, must report to the FDA and to the device manufacturer any device event that caused or contributed to a patient's death (within 10 working days) or caused or contributed to a serious injury (within 10 working days). This is a mandatory legal requirement, not a recommendation. Missing the 10-working-day deadline is itself a regulatory violation.

**MedWatch (FDA Form 3500):** The FDA's voluntary reporting portal for clinicians. Any clinician can submit a MedWatch report for any device or drug adverse event, including events that do not rise to the mandatory MDR level. MedWatch reports feed the FDA's public awareness and can contribute to future safety alerts. The MAUDE database (Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience) is the publicly searchable record of all reported device events, and it is a research tool students can use to look up a device's safety history.

**Manufacturer notification:** When an MDR is filed, the manufacturer is a required recipient. Manufacturers also maintain their own reporting portals for quality feedback, and they have their own separate FDA-reporting obligations.

**Texas DSHS reporting:** Texas state law includes specific adverse-event reporting requirements for Texas healthcare facilities. The supervising clinician or risk manager handles these, but students in Texas should know the obligation exists at the state level in addition to the federal level.

**Joint Commission Sentinel Event reporting:** A Sentinel Event is a TJC-defined serious patient-harm event. Reporting to TJC is voluntary but strongly encouraged. An institution that experiences a device-related patient death will typically conduct a root-cause analysis and may report to TJC as part of its accreditation obligations.

### What to document: the factual-and-specific rule

The quality of a report is determined by its specificity. A report that says "the pump had a problem" is nearly useless. A report that says "the Baxter Sigma Spectrum infusion pump, serial number XXXXXX, software version 6.05.12, was programmed at 20 mL/hr but a rate alarm indicated the pump was running at 200 mL/hr at 09:42" is the kind of documentation that an investigation can actually use.

Every report, internal or external, must include: date, time, and exact location (unit, room, bed number); device make, model, serial number, and lot number, pulled from the device label or the EHR; software version if applicable; the user's credentials and training status on that device; a verbatim description of what was observed, including alarms displayed and error codes shown; the patient's clinical status before, during, and after the event; all immediate actions taken; the names of everyone notified and when; and the outcome, including whether the device was preserved or, if not, why not.

### Just Culture: why reporting systems work only when workers feel safe

The MDR system, the incident-report system, and MedWatch all depend on workers being willing to come forward when something goes wrong. They will not come forward if they believe that filing a report means getting fired.

**Just Culture**, developed by David Marx, is the framework that most US healthcare institutions now use to separate blame-free reporting from accountability for truly reckless behavior. Under Just Culture, a worker who makes a human error, such as not noticing a programming error on a pump, is not punished for reporting that error. A worker who engages in reckless behavior, such as deliberately bypassing a safety alert, is held accountable even within a Just Culture. The goal is a system where workers can say "something went wrong" without fear, so the system can learn and improve.

For students specifically: you are not expected to file an FDA MDR. Your supervising clinician or risk manager will route external regulatory reports. Your role is to see the event, keep the patient safe, escalate to the right person, document factually, and preserve the device and its associated items. If you do those five things correctly, the rest of the system can function.

### Key terms

**Medical Device Report (MDR):** mandatory FDA report under 21 CFR Part 803 for device events contributing to death or serious injury; due to the FDA and manufacturer within 10 working days.

**MedWatch:** the FDA's voluntary reporting portal (Form 3500) for clinicians, covering adverse events that do not rise to the mandatory MDR threshold.

**MAUDE:** the FDA Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience database; a publicly searchable record of reported device events useful for device safety research.

**Incident report:** the facility's internal report, often filed in RL6, Midas, or Datix, documenting an adverse event or near-miss; must be factual and non-blaming.

**Work order (biomed):** a clinical-engineering request to investigate or repair a device, filed alongside the incident report.

**Near-miss:** an event that could have caused harm but did not; still required in most internal reporting systems and a leading indicator of future harm.

**Sentinel Event:** a TJC-defined serious patient-harm event; voluntary but strongly encouraged reporting with device-related criteria.

**Device preservation:** keeping the device, packaging, tubing, and associated disposables intact for biomed investigation; discarding evidence is a reporting failure with legal consequences.

**Just Culture:** Marx's framework separating blame-free reporting for human error from accountability for reckless behavior; the premise underlying non-punitive reporting systems in US healthcare.

**21 CFR Part 803:** the FDA regulation defining mandatory device-event reporting by user facilities and manufacturers, including the 10-working-day deadline.

**Serial and lot number:** device identifiers required in every report; pulled from the device label or the EHR record.

### Chapter summary

Reporting a device malfunction is not optional, and its purpose extends far beyond the patient in front of you. The internal reporting track, clinical supervisor notification, patient-record documentation, incident report, biomed work order, and device preservation, is required every time. The external track, FDA MDR, MedWatch, manufacturer notification, and state reporting, kicks in based on the severity of the event, with a mandatory 10-working-day deadline for events involving death or serious injury. Documentation must be specific: date, time, location, device identifiers, verbatim observations, and clinical status before and after. Discarding evidence, writing blame into incident reports, or treating an internal report as sufficient when an MDR is required are the most common reporting failures. Just Culture creates the conditions under which workers feel safe enough to report, which is the only way the system produces the national-level knowledge needed to prevent the same device from harming patients across hundreds of facilities. Students' role is to recognize, escalate, document, and preserve.

### Review questions

1. An infusion pump in the oncology unit delivers a chemotherapy dose at 10 times the programmed rate before the error is caught. Walk through both reporting tracks, internal and external, listing every report that must be filed, who files each one, and the applicable deadline for any time-sensitive reports.
2. Explain why writing "the nurse was careless" in an incident report is a problem. What should the incident report contain instead, and what is the purpose of keeping the report factual and non-blaming?
3. A device failure with no patient harm occurs on your unit. A coworker says it is not worth filing a report since no one was hurt. Using the reasons-reporting-matters framework from this chapter, give three specific arguments for why you should file the report anyway.
