# Reporting Equipment and Technology Malfunctions

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify when a device malfunction must be reported and at what level
- Explain the parallel tracks of internal and external reporting
- Describe what must be documented and preserved at each step in the reporting process
- Recognize the role of Just Culture in making reporting systems work

A certified nursing assistant finishes her shift and mentions in the hallway that "the pump in room 12 was acting weird all night." She does not file a report, does not label the device, and does not notify the charge nurse through the formal system. Two days later, the same pump delivers ten times the ordered dose of a blood thinner to a different patient. Because the first event was never documented, there is no record the pump had already been flagged. This chapter explains what should have happened and why the reporting process exists.

### Why reporting matters beyond the individual patient

When a device fails silently, it fails again. Filing a report breaks that cycle and does five things at once: it triggers a replacement device for the immediate patient, prompts inspection of every identical device on the floor, surfaces preventive-maintenance gaps for the institution, feeds the FDA's national device-safety database so recalls can happen, and protects the individual worker. A nurse who silently works around a failing pump and does not file a report has created personal liability if that pump later harms a patient.

### Not all events require the same response

Severity determines which reports must be filed. A **device failure with no harm** requires a biomed work order and may require an internal incident report depending on facility policy. A **near-miss** requires an internal incident report and usually a risk-management escalation. A **device failure contributing to patient harm** adds a possible FDA MDR under 21 CFR Part 803 depending on the seriousness of the injury. A **device failure contributing to patient death** requires the full set: internal incident report, risk-management escalation, a mandatory FDA MDR within 10 working days, and potentially Texas DSHS notification and a Joint Commission Sentinel Event report.

### Track 1: internal reporting, every time

No matter what level of harm occurred, every device malfunction event begins with internal reporting. Six steps must happen in order.

**Step 1: Notify the clinical supervisor or charge nurse immediately.** This is verbal and direct, not a hallway comment on the way out the door.

**Step 2: Take the device out of service and label it** with date, time, initials, and a brief description. This is the physical anchor for everything that follows.

**Step 3: Document factually in the patient record.** Record the device event, the patient's status before and after, and every action taken. Do not edit the chart retroactively to match what the device should have done. Retroactive alteration of a medical record is both an ethical violation and a legal problem.

**Step 4: File the internal incident report.** Most facilities use web-based systems such as RL6, Midas, or Datix. The report must be factual and non-blaming. "Nurse X was careless" is a disciplinary accusation, not an incident report. Write what you observed, when, where, what the device did, and what actions were taken.

**Step 5: Submit a work order to biomedical engineering.** Include device make, model, serial number, lot number, software version, and a description of the failure. Biomed investigates based on what you document.

**Step 6: Preserve the device and all associated items.** Do not discard IV tubing, glucose strips, pads, or packaging. Every item thrown away is a piece of the investigation that disappears permanently.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> During a chemotherapy infusion, a nurse notices that the smart infusion pump has been running at a rate 10 times higher than programmed. The error is caught after 200 mL of chemotherapy have been delivered instead of the intended 20 mL. The nurse stops the infusion, assesses the patient, calls the oncology physician, and activates the rapid-response team. She then labels the pump "Do Not Use," saves the IV tubing and the settings printout, and calls the charge nurse. Within the hour she has filed an internal incident report in Datix with the device serial number, the programmed versus actual rate, the time frame, the patient's current status, and the names of every clinician notified. She submits a biomed work order. Risk management is notified by the charge nurse. Two days later, the MDR process is initiated. The chemotherapy pump is found to have a software defect. Three more pumps of the same model are inspected.

### Track 2: external reporting, when required

Internal reporting is always required. External regulatory reporting depends on the severity of the event.

**FDA Medical Device Report (MDR), 21 CFR Part 803:** Hospitals and other user facilities must report to the FDA and the device manufacturer any event that caused or contributed to a patient's death or serious injury within 10 working days. Missing that deadline is itself a regulatory violation.

**MedWatch (FDA Form 3500):** The FDA's voluntary reporting portal for clinicians. Any clinician can submit a MedWatch report for events that do not rise to the mandatory MDR level. The **MAUDE** database is the publicly searchable record of all reported device events and a research tool students can use to look up a device's safety history.

**Manufacturer notification:** The device manufacturer is a required MDR recipient and also accepts voluntary quality feedback through its own reporting portals.

**Texas DSHS reporting:** Texas state law imposes specific adverse-event reporting requirements. The supervising clinician or risk manager handles these, but students should know the obligation exists at the state level.

**Joint Commission Sentinel Event reporting:** Voluntary but strongly encouraged for events meeting TJC's serious-harm criteria, usually followed by a facility root-cause analysis.

### What to document: the factual-and-specific rule

"The pump had a problem" is nearly useless. "The Baxter Sigma Spectrum infusion pump, serial number XXXXXX, software version 6.05.12, programmed at 20 mL/hr, alarmed at 09:42 indicating a rate of 200 mL/hr" is the kind of documentation that drives an investigation.

Every report must include: date, time, and exact location; device make, model, serial number, and lot number from the device label or EHR; software version if applicable; user credentials and training status; a verbatim description of what was observed including alarms and error codes; the patient's clinical status before and after; all immediate actions taken; everyone notified and when; and the outcome including device-preservation status.

### Just Culture: why reporting systems work only when workers feel safe

The MDR system, incident reports, and MedWatch all depend on workers being willing to come forward. **Just Culture**, developed by David Marx, is the framework that separates blame-free reporting from accountability for reckless behavior. A worker who makes a human error is not punished for reporting it. A worker who deliberately bypasses a safety alert is still held accountable. The goal is a system where workers can say "something went wrong" without fear.

For students: you are not expected to file an FDA MDR. Your supervising clinician or risk manager routes external regulatory reports. Your role is to see the event, keep the patient safe, escalate, document factually, and preserve the device. Do those five things correctly and the rest of the system can function.

### Key terms

**Medical Device Report (MDR):** mandatory FDA report under 21 CFR Part 803 for device events contributing to death or serious injury; due to the FDA and manufacturer within 10 working days.

**MedWatch:** the FDA's voluntary reporting portal (Form 3500) for clinicians, covering adverse events that do not rise to the mandatory MDR threshold.

**MAUDE:** the FDA Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience database; a publicly searchable record of reported device events useful for device safety research.

**Incident report:** the facility's internal report, often filed in RL6, Midas, or Datix, documenting an adverse event or near-miss; must be factual and non-blaming.

**Work order (biomed):** a clinical-engineering request to investigate or repair a device, filed alongside the incident report.

**Near-miss:** an event that could have caused harm but did not; still required in most internal reporting systems and a leading indicator of future harm.

**Sentinel Event:** a TJC-defined serious patient-harm event; voluntary but strongly encouraged reporting with device-related criteria.

**Device preservation:** keeping the device, packaging, tubing, and associated disposables intact for biomed investigation; discarding evidence is a reporting failure with legal consequences.

**Just Culture:** Marx's framework separating blame-free reporting for human error from accountability for reckless behavior; the premise underlying non-punitive reporting systems in US healthcare.

**21 CFR Part 803:** the FDA regulation defining mandatory device-event reporting by user facilities and manufacturers, including the 10-working-day deadline.

**Serial and lot number:** device identifiers required in every report; pulled from the device label or the EHR record.

### Chapter summary

Reporting a device malfunction is not optional, and its purpose extends far beyond the patient in front of you. The internal reporting track, clinical supervisor notification, patient-record documentation, incident report, biomed work order, and device preservation, is required every time. The external track, FDA MDR, MedWatch, manufacturer notification, and state reporting, kicks in based on the severity of the event, with a mandatory 10-working-day deadline for events involving death or serious injury. Documentation must be specific: date, time, location, device identifiers, verbatim observations, and clinical status before and after. Discarding evidence, writing blame into incident reports, or treating an internal report as sufficient when an MDR is required are the most common reporting failures. Just Culture creates the conditions under which workers feel safe enough to report, which is the only way the system produces the national-level knowledge needed to prevent the same device from harming patients across hundreds of facilities. Students' role is to recognize, escalate, document, and preserve.

### Review questions

1. An infusion pump in the oncology unit delivers a chemotherapy dose at 10 times the programmed rate before the error is caught. Walk through both reporting tracks, internal and external, listing every report that must be filed, who files each one, and the applicable deadline for any time-sensitive reports.
2. Explain why writing "the nurse was careless" in an incident report is a problem. What should the incident report contain instead, and what is the purpose of keeping the report factual and non-blaming?
3. A device failure with no patient harm occurs on your unit. A coworker says it is not worth filing a report since no one was hurt. Using the reasons-reporting-matters framework from this chapter, give three specific arguments for why you should file the report anyway.

### References

- **FDA Medical Device Reporting regulation (21 CFR Part 803):** the federal regulation defining mandatory device-event reporting by user facilities and manufacturers.
- **FDA MedWatch (fda.gov/safety/medwatch):** voluntary reporting portal for clinicians; Form 3500.
- **FDA Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience (MAUDE) database:** public searchable database of reported device events; useful for research and for understanding how the reporting system produces public knowledge.
- **Joint Commission Sentinel Event Policy and Procedures:** accreditation-level reporting framework for device-related harm that meets sentinel-event criteria.
- **AHRQ Common Formats for Patient Safety Reporting:** standardized data elements for incident reports adopted across US healthcare.
- **ECRI Institute device-safety reports and the Health Devices journal:** independent clearinghouse for device-safety information, often drawing on aggregated MDR data.
- **Texas Department of State Health Services adverse-event reporting rules:** state-level reporting requirements applicable in Texas.
- **David Marx, Whack-a-Mole (Just Culture framework):** foundational framework for the blame-free reporting that still holds reckless behavior accountable model underlying US healthcare reporting.
- **James Reason, Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents:** Swiss cheese model of system-level failure; frames why individual reports matter to system-level safety.
