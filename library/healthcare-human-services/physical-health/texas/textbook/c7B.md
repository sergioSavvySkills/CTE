# How the Five Systems Collaborate to Deliver Quality Care

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Trace all five health science systems through a single patient care episode
- Identify the handoff points between systems where care quality is determined
- Name the quality framework used to evaluate cross-system collaboration
- Explain how a failure at one handoff point cascades through the rest of care

A patient arrives by ambulance at 2 a.m. with stroke symptoms. The paramedics called ahead. By the time the gurney clears the doors, a neurologist is ready, a CT scanner is prepped, a scribe is logged in, a phlebotomist is gloved, and a transport aide is standing by. None of those people work in the same department. None share a supervisor. But in the next 20 minutes they will hand off this patient's information and physical safety among themselves four times, and if any one of those handoffs fails, the outcome changes.

### Why collaboration is a quality requirement

The **IOM/NAM Six Aims for Improvement** define what quality healthcare looks like: safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, and equitable [NAM Crossing the Quality Chasm]. Those six aims are not achieved by any single system. A diagnosis that is accurate (effective) but communicated too slowly (not timely) produces the same harm as a wrong diagnosis. A medication that is therapeutically correct but dispensed to the wrong patient (not safe) is a failure regardless of the prescriber's intention.

Quality lives in the handoffs between systems, not inside any one system. The Joint Commission's annual sentinel-event data consistently identifies communication failures and handoff breakdowns as leading causes of serious patient harm [Joint Commission Sentinel Events]. Cross-system collaboration is not a professional courtesy, it is the mechanism by which quality is or is not achieved.

### Tracing one patient across all five systems

Follow a 52-year-old patient, call him Marcus, through a chest-pain workup in the emergency department. The episode spans 90 minutes and touches all five systems.

**Therapeutic** is the first system Marcus sees: a triage RN assesses his symptoms, vital signs, and pain level, and an ED physician evaluates him and orders a workup. A pharmacist verifies and dispenses the aspirin the physician orders. These roles carry out the treatment decisions.

**Diagnostic** generates the data those decisions are based on: a phlebotomist draws blood for troponin and BMP panels; a medical laboratory scientist runs the tests; an EKG technician captures a 12-lead; a radiologic technologist performs a portable chest X-ray. The diagnostic system's output, numbers and images, feeds directly back to the therapeutic system.

**Health informatics** runs underneath the entire episode: a medical scribe documents the physician's assessment in real time; an HIM coder will later assign the diagnosis and procedure codes that drive the insurance claim; an EHR analyst's configuration determines whether the troponin result auto-routes to the physician's alert queue or sits in a report that has to be checked manually. Every clinical decision depends on accurate, timely informatics work.

**Support services** makes the physical episode possible: a transport aide moves Marcus from triage to the monitored bay safely; central-sterile technicians ensure that the procedure tray is ready if the physician needs it; environmental services will clean the bay for the next patient. A healthcare interpreter is on call in case Marcus does not speak English.

**Biotechnology R&D** enters through a form: Marcus is asked to consent to enrollment in a cardiovascular outcomes registry. A clinical research coordinator, attached to the hospital's research office, will de-identify his data and add it to a longitudinal study. Tomorrow he is a data point. Next year, he might be part of the evidence base for a guideline that changes how his own children are treated.

### The handoff points

A **handoff point** is any cross-system transition where information or physical responsibility moves from one role to another. Marcus's episode contains at least five:

1. **Therapeutic → Diagnostic**: the physician's order for the troponin and EKG reaches the laboratory and EKG technician. If the order is incomplete or the patient is misidentified at the draw, the wrong result comes back.
2. **Diagnostic → Therapeutic**: the laboratory calls a **critical value**, a troponin level above the threshold for acute MI, directly to the ordering physician. This is a mandatory callback protocol in every accredited facility. If the call is not made, or the physician doesn't answer and the callback is not documented, the result sits idle while Marcus deteriorates.
3. **Any system → Informatics**: every clinician documents their contribution. If the scribe misses the physician's verbal assessment, the next shift's team is working from an incomplete record. If the coder misreads the physician's note, the wrong diagnosis code is submitted.
4. **Therapeutic → Support**: the physician orders transport to cardiology for an echocardiogram. If the transport aide is not notified promptly, Marcus waits in a monitored bay that another patient needs.
5. **Any system → Research (if enrolled)**: the CRC's data extraction depends entirely on the accuracy and completeness of what was documented in steps 1 through 4. Garbage in, garbage out, research built on poor documentation produces guidelines built on flawed evidence.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> Marcus's troponin comes back critically elevated at 10:02 a.m. The laboratory scientist flags it and calls the ED unit desk as required by protocol. The unit clerk takes the message but does not page the physician, she assumes the result will appear in the EHR alert. The physician's alert queue has 14 items. She does not check it until 10:47 a.m. Marcus has been waiting in a bay for 45 minutes with an evolving MI and no intervention ordered. The diagnostic-to-therapeutic handoff worked technically, the result was called, but the closed loop was never completed. One missed step in a cross-system handoff cost Marcus 45 minutes of treatment time.

### Tools that make collaboration reliable

Cross-system collaboration does not happen automatically. It is held together by specific practices that every healthcare worker is expected to know and use:

**SBAR** structures the verbal handoff so that any receiver, regardless of system, gets the same four pieces: what is happening, the relevant context, the sender's assessment, and what action is being requested. A scribe handing off documentation to a coder does not use SBAR; a nurse calling a physician about a deteriorating patient does. The format matches the stakes.

**Critical-value callback protocols** formalize the diagnostic-to-therapeutic handoff for abnormal results. They specify who calls, who must receive the call, what is documented, and what happens when the receiving clinician cannot be reached.

**Interdisciplinary rounds**, structured team meetings, often daily in inpatient settings, bring representatives from multiple systems to review each patient together. The informatics system's record is the source; the therapeutic system leads the clinical discussion; support services flags pending transport or supply needs. Everyone hears the same plan.

**Shared EHR documentation** is the informatics backbone that all other collaboration depends on. When each system documents in the same place with consistent standards, every downstream handoff has a reliable record to work from.

### Key terms

**Handoff point**: a cross-system transition where information or physical responsibility moves from one role to another; the location where care quality is determined or lost.

**IOM/NAM Six Aims**: safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable; the quality framework achieved through cross-system collaboration.

**Critical value callback**: the mandatory diagnostic-to-therapeutic communication of a seriously abnormal lab or imaging result directly to the ordering clinician.

**Interdisciplinary rounds**: structured patient-care meetings that draw representatives from multiple systems to share a common care plan.

**Sentinel event**: a serious patient-harm event as defined by the Joint Commission; most root causes involve at least one cross-system handoff failure.

**Structured handoff**: any standardized format (SBAR, callback protocol) that makes a cross-system transition reliable.

**IPEC Core Competencies**: the Interprofessional Education Collaborative framework for the collaborative behaviors expected across healthcare professions.

**Patient-episode trace**: mapping every team member across all five systems who touches one patient during a single care event.

### Chapter summary

Every patient encounter touches all five health science systems, and quality healthcare is determined by the handoffs between them, not by any system in isolation. The IOM/NAM Six Aims, safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, and equitable, can only be achieved through reliable cross-system collaboration. Handoff points are the moments of highest risk: a critical lab value not communicated, a documentation gap, a delayed transport each erode quality in measurable ways. The tools that make collaboration reliable, SBAR, callback protocols, interdisciplinary rounds, and shared documentation, exist precisely because good intentions are not enough.

### Review questions

1. In Marcus's chest-pain episode, identify two handoff points and describe specifically what each system is passing to the next, and what could go wrong.
2. A hospital's laboratory returns a critically high potassium level but the ordering physician is not reached and no backup contact is made. Which of the IOM/NAM Six Aims is violated, and why?
3. Why does the informatics system's documentation quality affect the biotechnology R&D system, even though those two systems rarely interact directly?
