# Development Across the Lifespan

---
**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Describe the major developmental stages from infancy through older adulthood
- Identify the dominant health priorities, risks, and screening needs at each stage
- Explain how Erikson's psychosocial stages and Piaget's cognitive stages inform care decisions
- Adapt communication strategies to match the developmental stage of a patient
---

A pediatric nurse greets two patients back-to-back in the same morning clinic: a 9-month-old here for a well-baby visit and an 85-year-old here for a medication review. The nurse speaks differently, assesses differently, and screens for entirely different health risks — not because these patients are simply at different points on a timeline, but because human development produces fundamentally different physical, cognitive, and social realities at every stage of life.

### Why developmental stages matter in healthcare

A **developmental stage** is an age-defined period with characteristic physical, cognitive, and psychosocial features. These features are not just background information — they determine what assessments you perform, what health risks you screen for, how you obtain consent, and what communication style will actually land.

Two theoretical frameworks anchor the study of lifespan development in healthcare. **Erikson's psychosocial stages** describe eight life-stage challenges — from trust versus mistrust in infancy to integrity versus despair in older adulthood — that shape a person's emotional and social experience of health and illness. **Piaget's cognitive stages** describe how thinking capacity develops, from sensorimotor learning in infancy through formal abstract reasoning in adolescence, and directly determine how much a patient can understand and engage with health information.

### Infancy (0–1 year)

Infancy is a period of extraordinary physical growth. Birth weight typically triples in the first year, the brain develops rapidly, and all learning is **sensorimotor** — happening through senses and movement, not language or abstract thought. Attachment — the bond between infant and caregiver — forms during this stage and has lifelong effects on emotional regulation and health.

Health priorities for infants include regular feeding and weight gain, immunizations per the ACIP schedule, safe sleep practices to reduce SIDS risk, and **developmental milestone** screening to catch delays early. A **developmental milestone** is an age-expected behavior — rolling over by 4 months, saying a first word by 12 months — used to screen for typical or delayed development. The CDC's "Learn the Signs. Act Early." program and AAP **Bright Futures** guidelines define the screening schedule.

### Early childhood (1–6 years)

In early childhood, language erupts rapidly and children begin thinking in symbols and stories — what Piaget called **preoperational thinking**. They are egocentric (unable to fully take another's perspective), engage in imaginative play, and are beginning to assert autonomy. This is also a period of high injury risk as mobility expands faster than judgment.

Health priorities include nutrition for brain development, injury prevention (car seats, supervision), continued immunizations, and vision and hearing screening. Children at this stage communicate best through simple language, visual aids, and play. Medical procedures should be explained honestly and concretely — "this will feel like a pinch for two seconds" — not minimized with vague reassurances.

### School age (6–12 years)

School-age children develop **concrete operational thinking** — they can reason logically about real, tangible situations. Peer relationships become central, and Erik Erikson characterized this stage as **industry versus inferiority**: children are building competence and comparing themselves to peers. Success and failure at school feel high-stakes.

Health priorities shift toward mental health awareness (depression and anxiety can begin here), obesity prevention, and vision and hearing screening. This is also when chronic conditions like asthma and type 1 diabetes are often diagnosed and children begin to take on self-management responsibility. Communication can include written materials and direct explanation — school-age children can understand cause and effect and appreciate being treated as capable.

### Adolescence (12–18 years)

Adolescence introduces **formal operational thinking** — the ability to reason abstractly, consider hypotheticals, and think about future consequences. At the same time, the prefrontal cortex (the brain's risk-evaluation center) is still developing, which helps explain why adolescents often act on impulse even when they can reason through risks in theory.

Erikson characterized this stage as identity versus role confusion. Adolescents are building their sense of who they are, and healthcare interactions that feel judgmental or dismissive threaten that process. Health priorities include mental health (depression, anxiety, eating disorders, suicide risk), substance use screening, sexual health, and injury prevention. Confidential communication with adolescents — separate from parents — is both a legal standard in many contexts and an evidence-based practice for honest health-seeking behavior.

### Young and middle adulthood (18–65 years)

Young and middle adulthood spans nearly five decades and covers a wide range of life contexts — forming partnerships, building careers, raising children, experiencing the chronic-disease onset that typically begins in the 40s and 50s. Erikson described early adulthood as **intimacy versus isolation** and middle adulthood as **generativity versus stagnation** — the desire to contribute to the next generation.

Health priorities during this long stage include chronic disease prevention (type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, hypertension), reproductive health, cancer screening per USPSTF guidelines, and mental health. Prevention is the primary focus in young adulthood; early detection and management increasingly take center stage through middle age.

### Older adulthood (65+ years)

Older adulthood involves real physical and cognitive changes: decreased reserve capacity in most organ systems, slower reaction times, increased risk of falls, and for some individuals, cognitive decline. Erikson described this stage as **integrity versus despair** — looking back at one's life with either satisfaction or regret. Loss and grief are common.

**Advance care planning** — documenting decisions about future medical care, including end-of-life wishes — is a key health-promotion task for this stage. **Polypharmacy** — the use of multiple medications simultaneously, common among older adults managing several chronic conditions — is a top safety risk, as drug interactions become harder to predict and side effects can mimic disease. Fall prevention programs, cognitive screening, and social-connection support are evidence-based priorities.

Communication with older adults requires attention to sensory limitations (vision, hearing), cognitive status, and the patient's own pace. Assuming confusion or incapacity before assessing for it is a form of ageism that directly harms care.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A medical assistant calls three patients back in a row at a family medicine clinic: a 4-year-old for a well-child visit, a 15-year-old for a sports physical, and an 82-year-old on six medications for a chronic-disease review. She adjusts her approach for each: she crouches to eye level and uses simple words with the 4-year-old; she speaks to the 15-year-old directly and privately for part of the visit without the parent present; and she speaks clearly, confirms understanding after each point, and runs a polypharmacy check on the 82-year-old's medication list before the physician enters the room. Same skill set, three completely different applications — all driven by developmental stage.

### Key terms

**Developmental stage** — an age-defined period of human growth with characteristic physical, cognitive, and psychosocial features that drive care priorities.

**Erikson's psychosocial stages** — eight-stage model (including industry versus inferiority in school age, identity in adolescence, integrity versus despair in older adulthood) used across nursing and allied health.

**Piaget's cognitive stages** — sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational thinking; shapes communication choices by age.

**Sensorimotor** — Piaget's infant stage (0–1 yr) of rapid physical growth and learning through senses and movement.

**Formal operational thinking** — Piaget's adolescent stage marked by abstract reasoning; pairs with identity formation and risk-taking.

**Developmental milestone** — an age-expected behavior (motor, language, social) used to screen for typical or delayed development.

**Bright Futures (AAP)** — the evidence-based pediatric health-supervision guidelines that set age-specific priorities from birth through age 21.

**Advance care planning** — the older-adulthood health focus on documenting future care preferences and end-of-life decisions.

**Polypharmacy** — the concurrent use of multiple medications in older adults; a dominant health risk requiring stage-specific screening.

### Chapter summary

Human development unfolds through predictable stages — infancy, early childhood, school age, adolescence, adulthood, and older adulthood — each with its own physical capacities, cognitive style, and psychosocial challenges. Erikson's and Piaget's frameworks give healthcare workers a map for understanding what patients at each stage are experiencing and what they are capable of understanding. The practical payoff is direct: developmental stage drives which health risks to screen for, which consent process to follow, and which communication approach will be effective. A healthcare worker who can read the developmental stage of a patient and adjust accordingly provides fundamentally better care.

### Review questions

1. A 3-year-old patient needs a blood draw. Using Piaget's cognitive stages, describe what kind of explanation will actually help her understand what is about to happen. What kind of language would not work and why?
2. Compare the dominant health priorities for a 16-year-old and an 80-year-old. Name two health risks specific to each stage and explain why the risk is tied to that particular stage of development.
3. An 82-year-old patient on eight daily medications is prescribed a ninth. Using what you know about the older-adulthood developmental stage, explain what specific health risk this creates and what the healthcare team should do before dispensing the new prescription.
