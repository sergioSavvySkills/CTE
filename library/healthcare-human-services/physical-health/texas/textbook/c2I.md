# Maslow's Hierarchy — Meeting Human Needs in Clinical Care

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Identify and describe all five levels of Maslow's Hierarchy of Human Needs
- Map physiological needs to the clinical ABCs used in emergencies
- Use the hierarchy to prioritize competing patient-care concerns
- Explain how upper-level needs — belonging, esteem, self-actualization — affect health outcomes even when they are not the immediate emergency

A nurse walks into a patient's room and finds two problems at once: the patient's oxygen saturation has dropped to 91%, and the patient is crying because her family has not visited in three days. The nurse knows exactly which problem to address first — and can explain exactly why. The framework that makes that decision clear is Maslow's Hierarchy of Human Needs.

### The five-level pyramid

**Maslow's Hierarchy of Human Needs** is a five-level model developed by psychologist Abraham Maslow in 1943. The model organizes human needs from the most basic survival requirements at the base to the highest form of personal fulfillment at the top. The key principle is that lower levels take priority when they are unmet. A person cannot focus on relationships, dignity, or personal growth when they are struggling to breathe.

The five levels, from base to top, are: **physiological needs**, **safety and security**, **love and belonging**, **self-esteem**, and **self-actualization**.

### Level 1 — physiological needs

**Physiological needs** form the base of the pyramid. These are the biological requirements for survival: air, water, food, elimination, sleep, shelter, and temperature regulation. If any of these is absent or dangerously compromised, nothing above it matters — the body will not survive long enough for higher needs to be relevant.

In clinical practice, these needs map directly to the **ABCs** — airway, breathing, and circulation — the assessment sequence used in every emergency from CPR to trauma triage. Is the patient breathing? Is there a pulse? Is blood pressure adequate? These are physiological-needs questions. A healthcare worker who pauses to offer emotional support before securing an airway has the hierarchy backwards.

### Level 2 — safety and security

**Safety and security** needs include physical safety (freedom from harm, fall prevention, infection control, a violence-free environment), emotional safety (predictability, trust in caregivers, freedom from threat), and financial security. For hospitalized patients, safety needs surface in specific forms: will I fall when I get out of bed? Will staff wash their hands before touching me? Does this environment feel safe or threatening?

Healthcare workers address safety and security needs through fall precautions, consistent hand hygiene, clear communication about procedures, and creating an environment where patients feel they can ask questions without being dismissed.

### Level 3 — love and belonging

**Love and belonging** needs are relational: meaningful connection with other people, family presence, therapeutic rapport with the care team, and a sense of belonging to a community. Research consistently shows that isolation worsens health outcomes — patients without social connection have longer hospital stays, poorer recovery, and higher readmission rates.

In clinical settings, these needs surface when a patient in a single room reports feeling invisible, when a family member is barred from visiting, or when a care team rotates so frequently that no one on the staff knows the patient's name. Addressing belonging needs might mean ensuring family can visit, using the patient's preferred name, or simply sitting down for two minutes to have a real conversation.

### Level 4 — self-esteem

**Self-esteem** needs involve respect, autonomy, dignity, and the sense that one is valued. These needs are easily eroded in a hospital: the gown that doesn't close in the back, the chart that refers to a patient by room number, the nurse who speaks about the patient to a colleague without acknowledging the patient is right there. Loss of control — having others make decisions about your own body — directly threatens self-esteem.

Healthcare workers protect self-esteem by explaining every procedure before performing it, asking patients how they prefer to be addressed, offering choices whenever choices exist, and treating patients as the experts on their own experience.

### Level 5 — self-actualization

**Self-actualization** is the top of the pyramid: realizing one's potential, finding meaning, living with purpose. It is addressed most explicitly in palliative care and rehabilitation settings. A patient recovering from a stroke may no longer grieve the loss of movement as much as the loss of identity — she can no longer play guitar, coach her daughter's soccer team, or work as a surgeon. Addressing self-actualization means acknowledging that loss and working with the patient toward a meaningful life within new constraints.

Self-actualization is not a luxury. In mental health recovery, it is often the difference between a patient who completes rehabilitation and returns to the community and one who stops trying.

### Using the hierarchy in practice

The hierarchy does not mean that lower-level needs are always unmet before upper ones arise. Multiple needs coexist. But **prioritization** — choosing what to address first when time is limited — should follow the hierarchy: unmet lower-level needs take precedence.

Don't discuss a patient's life goals during a blood pressure crisis. Don't ignore a patient's fear of a procedure (safety need) because you are focused only on the physiological readings. And don't mistake addressing the physical emergency as the whole job — a patient whose breathing is stable but who feels unseen, isolated, and stripped of dignity is still suffering, and that suffering has measurable health consequences.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A patient-care technician enters a room to check vitals. She notices the 78-year-old patient is quiet and has barely touched his breakfast. His oxygen and blood pressure are both fine. She sits down and says, "Mr. Rivera, I noticed you haven't been eating much. How are you feeling?" He says: "I was a teacher for 40 years. Now my son has to help me button my shirt." She recognizes this as a self-esteem and self-actualization concern — not a physiological problem — and notes it in her handoff to the nurse. The nurse flags it for the social worker and chaplain. The team's response is shaped by knowing which level of the hierarchy the patient just named.

### Key terms

**Maslow's Hierarchy of Human Needs** — five-level pyramid (physiological, safety, love/belonging, esteem, self-actualization) used to prioritize competing patient concerns.

**Physiological needs** — base of the pyramid: air, water, food, elimination, sleep, shelter, and temperature; map directly to clinical ABCs.

**Safety and security** — Maslow's second level: physical safety (fall prevention, infection control), emotional safety, and financial security.

**Love and belonging** — Maslow's third level: relationships, family presence, therapeutic rapport; isolation measurably worsens outcomes.

**Self-esteem** — Maslow's fourth level: autonomy, respect, and dignity — easily eroded by hospital gowns, shared rooms, and loss of control.

**Self-actualization** — top of the pyramid: realizing potential, meaning, and purpose; addressed in palliative care, rehabilitation, and recovery.

**ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation)** — clinical sequence derived from physiological-needs priority; operationalizes Maslow for emergencies.

**Prioritization** — the practical use of Maslow: lower unmet levels take priority, so urgent physiological needs come before dignity or self-actualization conversations.

### Chapter summary

Maslow's Hierarchy organizes human needs into five levels from physiological survival at the base to self-actualization at the top. In clinical settings, the hierarchy provides a decision framework for prioritization: when lower-level needs are unmet, address them first. Physiological needs map to the clinical ABCs and always take precedence in an emergency. But the upper levels — belonging, esteem, and self-actualization — are not optional afterthoughts. Unmet belonging and dignity needs have real, measurable effects on health outcomes and recovery, and skilled healthcare workers recognize and respond to all five levels.

### Review questions

1. A patient's oxygen saturation drops to 88% while she is asking a nurse about when her family can visit. Using Maslow's hierarchy, explain exactly which need takes priority and why the other must wait.
2. List one specific example of a clinical action that addresses each of the five Maslow levels. For each, name the level it addresses and explain the connection.
3. A rehabilitation patient tells his occupational therapist: "I used to build furniture. I can't even hold a screwdriver anymore." Which Maslow level is this patient expressing a threat to? Write a one-sentence response that acknowledges that specific level without deflecting or minimizing.

### References

- **Maslow, *A Theory of Human Motivation* (1943) and *Motivation and Personality* (1954)** — the original sources for the hierarchy; worth knowing the framework's origin and its limitations as a descriptive (not predictive) model.
- **American Nurses Association (ANA) *Nursing: Scope and Standards of Practice*** — Maslow is explicitly referenced in nursing care-prioritization frameworks and NCLEX-style reasoning.
- **ACLS/BLS and PALS algorithms (American Heart Association)** — operationalize physiological-needs priority via the airway/breathing/circulation (ABC) sequence.
- **Institute for Patient- and Family-Centered Care** — dignity- and belonging-level needs are codified in the patient- and family-centered-care framework adopted by most US hospitals.
