# Conflict Resolution in the Clinical Setting

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Name the five Thomas-Kilmann conflict styles and match each to the right situation
- Apply de-escalation techniques with upset patients and families
- Use CUS language and the two-challenge rule to escalate safety concerns within a team
- Evaluate whether a conflict-resolution attempt actually worked

A family member corners a new medical assistant in the hallway. She is furious about a four-hour wait time and is speaking loudly. The MA's instinct is to apologize repeatedly or to call a supervisor immediately. Both responses skip a step that would actually help: acknowledging the emotion before doing anything else. That one missed step can turn a manageable situation into a formal complaint or an escalating scene.

### Conflict is not a failure, it is a clinical reality

Every healthcare setting produces conflict. Patients and families are scared, exhausted, and dealing with information they did not want to receive. Teams are short-staffed, working under time pressure, and operating in high-stakes conditions where one disagreement about a plan can have real consequences. Conflict is not a sign that something has gone wrong with the team or the facility. It is a predictable feature of the environment, and handling it well is a clinical skill as measurable as taking a blood pressure.

The goal of conflict resolution in healthcare is specific. According to the AHRQ CANDOR framework, the question is not who wins. It is whether the immediate safety issue was resolved, whether the relationship survived, whether the underlying problem was named, and whether the documentation is clear enough to support any follow-up that becomes necessary.

### The five Thomas-Kilmann conflict styles

The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument describes five styles of engaging with conflict. Each sits at a different position on two axes: assertiveness (how much you push for your own outcome) and cooperativeness (how much you try to meet the other party's outcome). None of the five styles is always right. Each one is correct in specific situations.

**Avoid (TKI)** is low assertiveness and low cooperation. You withdraw from the conflict entirely. This is the right choice when the issue is trivial, when emotions are too high for any productive exchange, or when engaging would create a safety risk. It is the wrong choice when a real problem is being ignored repeatedly.

**Accommodate (TKI)** is low assertiveness and high cooperation. You give the other party what they want. This is appropriate when you are wrong, when maintaining the relationship outweighs the issue, or when you want to show goodwill before a harder conversation. It is the wrong choice when it means accepting a safety compromise.

**Compete (TKI)** is high assertiveness and low cooperation. You push for your outcome without seeking consensus. This sounds negative but it is the correct style in a safety emergency. When a patient is crashing and the team needs someone to direct, not consult, the competing style is the right tool. A clinical leader who seeks consensus during a code is being inappropriate.

**Compromise (TKI)** sits in the middle. Both parties give something up. This works when time is short, the stakes are moderate, and a good-enough solution is better than no solution. The risk is that both parties leave partially dissatisfied.

**Collaborate (TKI)** is high assertiveness and high cooperation. Both parties work toward a solution that fully addresses both sets of needs. This is the clinical-team default when time and stakes allow, because it builds shared understanding and produces the most durable solutions. But it is not always available. Collaboration requires time, trust, and willingness from both parties.

The most common mistake students make is treating **collaborate (TKI)** as the universally correct answer. It is not. In a safety emergency, or when one party is acting in bad faith, collaboration is the wrong tool. Choosing the right style means reading the specific situation.

### De-escalation with upset patients and families

When a patient or family member is distressed, the first job is not to solve the problem. It is to reduce the emotional intensity enough that problem-solving becomes possible. The sequence is: acknowledge, explain, offer a next step.

**Validation** comes before anything else. Name what the other person is feeling without judging it. "I can see you're really frustrated, and I understand why" is validation. "I'm sorry you feel that way" is not. The second phrase subtly shifts blame and often makes things worse. Validation signals that you have heard the person and that their emotional response makes sense. Without it, any information you offer next will be received as defensive.

The AHRQ CANDOR framework, developed for responding to patient harm events, operationalizes this sequence: acknowledge the patient's experience, apologize where legally appropriate, explain what is known about what happened and what steps are being taken, and offer a concrete next step the patient can hold onto. Legal consultation should inform how much is said and when, because certain admissions of causation carry liability consequences.

**Reframing** is a technique that moves the conversation from a contest of positions to a shared problem. Instead of "I understand you want the test done tonight, and that's not possible," try "It sounds like your priority is getting answers as quickly as possible, and mine is the same. Here's the fastest path we can take together." The underlying conflict may be identical, but the framing changes whether it feels like a standoff or a collaboration.

Behaviors that look like resolution but are not: winning the argument by volume or authority, apologizing for things that should not be admitted legally, gossiping about the conflict with uninvolved coworkers, or sending texts about the situation on unsecured channels.

### Team conflict and safety escalation

Conflict within a clinical team is a different situation from conflict with a patient or family. Team conflict that involves a safety concern requires a specific escalation path, not a de-escalation response.

**CUS language** is the TeamSTEPPS safety-escalation script: "I'm Concerned. I'm Uncomfortable. This is a Safety issue." These three phrases used in sequence signal to any trained team member that a concern is being elevated, not just expressed. The script works because it removes ambiguity. A vague "I'm not sure about this" can be ignored. A clean CUS statement cannot.

The **two-challenge rule** pairs with CUS. If you raise a safety concern and it is dismissed, raise it a second time before going up the chain. If it is dismissed a second time, the rule gives you explicit permission and professional cover to escalate to the next level: a supervisor, a charge nurse, risk management, or an ethics committee. The two-challenge rule is written into TeamSTEPPS precisely because the authority gradient in healthcare, the implicit pressure to defer to whoever is most senior, causes people to stay silent when they should not.

When to bring in help rather than continuing to resolve the conflict directly: threats of violence or physical intimidation, reasonable suspicion of clinician impairment, repeated scope-of-practice violations after a direct conversation, and patterns of disrespect that have been documented and not corrected. More communication is not the answer in those situations. Supervisor, risk management, or security is the answer.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A pharmacy technician student notices that a supervising pharmacist has initialed an order that appears to have a trailing zero, reading 10.0 mg instead of 10 mg. She says, "I'm concerned about the trailing zero on this order." The pharmacist waves it off without looking up. She says, "I'm uncomfortable with this, it could be misread as 100 mg, and I think this is a safety issue." The pharmacist looks up, rechecks the order, and confirms the technician is right. She never raised her voice and did not make it personal. She used CUS language exactly as written, twice, and both times focused on the safety concern rather than the person.

### Evaluating whether resolution actually worked

Conflict resolution is not over when the conversation ends. Evaluating effectiveness means asking: Was the immediate safety issue resolved? Did the relationship survive in a working state? Was the underlying problem named and not just papered over? Is the documentation clear enough to support any follow-up? Would the same approach work with this person or situation next time?

Documentation of a conflict, especially one involving a safety concern, should be factual and non-blaming. "At 14:30, I raised a concern about the medication order. The pharmacist reviewed and corrected the order at 14:35" is an objective record. "The pharmacist was dismissive and almost caused a serious error" is editorializing that creates legal exposure and is harder to act on.

### Key terms

**Thomas-Kilmann styles:** the five conflict modes (avoid, accommodate, compete, compromise, collaborate), each suited to specific situations.

**Avoid (TKI):** low assertiveness, low cooperation; appropriate when an issue is trivial or engagement would create a safety risk.

**Compete (TKI):** high assertiveness, low cooperation; the correct style in a safety emergency where consensus is not possible.

**Collaborate (TKI):** high assertiveness, high cooperation; the clinical-team default when time and stakes allow, but not always available or correct.

**De-escalation:** the sequence of calm tone, validation, and acknowledge-explain-next-step used to reduce emotional intensity with upset patients or families.

**Validation:** naming the other person's feeling before attempting any problem-solving; the move that makes later information land.

**Reframing:** restating a conflict as a shared goal rather than a contest of positions.

**CUS language:** the TeamSTEPPS safety-escalation script: "I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue."

**Two-challenge rule:** the TeamSTEPPS norm that allows a team member to raise a safety concern twice before escalating up the chain; provides professional cover against the authority gradient.

**CANDOR:** the AHRQ Communication and Optimal Resolution toolkit; an evidence-based process for responding to patient-harm events with acknowledgment and structured follow-up.

### Chapter summary

Conflict in healthcare is not a failure. It is a predictable feature of high-stakes environments and it requires specific, learnable techniques. The Thomas-Kilmann model provides five conflict styles, each suited to different situations, and choosing correctly means reading the situation rather than defaulting to collaboration or avoidance. With upset patients and families, validation comes before explanation, and the acknowledge-explain-next-step sequence reduces emotional intensity enough for problem-solving to work. With team conflicts that involve safety concerns, CUS language and the two-challenge rule provide a structured, non-blaming escalation path. Evaluating whether resolution worked requires checking five conditions: safety resolved, relationship intact, problem named, documentation clear, and approach transferable. That evaluation closes the loop.

### Review questions

1. A patient's family member is yelling at you about a four-hour wait time. Write out exactly what you would say in your first two sentences, and identify which conflict-resolution technique you are using.
2. A senior nurse tells you to skip the two-patient-identifier check because the unit is busy. You believe this is a safety issue. Write a clean CUS statement and describe what you do if it is dismissed the first time.
3. A conflict ended when one party stopped arguing. Using the five-condition evaluation rubric from this chapter, explain why "they stopped arguing" is not sufficient evidence that resolution occurred.
