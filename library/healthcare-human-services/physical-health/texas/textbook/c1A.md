# Expressing Ideas Clearly in the Health Workplace

---
**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Shape your message for the specific person receiving it — a patient, a peer, or a supervisor
- Write and speak in plain, precise language that leaves no room for misinterpretation
- Use structured formats (SBAR) to deliver complete information in the shortest time
- Confirm that a message was received correctly using read-back and paraphrase
---

A nurse calls the on-call physician at 2 a.m. She has thirty seconds to explain why the patient needs attention right now. If she rambles, the physician may miss the urgency. If she is too brief, he may miss the context. Getting that call right — concise, complete, and precise — is the difference between a patient who gets help in time and one who does not.

### Why communication is a clinical skill, not a soft skill

Every healthcare employer lists communication on its job-posting requirements, but that word covers something much more specific than being friendly or articulate. In healthcare, a message that is unclear, imprecise, or poorly timed can cause a medication error, delay a diagnosis, or break the chain of care between shifts. The Joint Commission, which accredits hospitals across the United States, consistently identifies communication failure as one of the leading root causes of serious patient harm events [Joint Commission NPSGs].

This means learning to express ideas clearly is not a personality trait you either have or lack. It is a set of learnable, observable techniques — and this chapter names them.

### Audience awareness: who is receiving this message?

Before you write a word or open your mouth, identify who you are communicating with and what they need from you. A shift-handoff report for an experienced RN looks nothing like a patient-education conversation, which looks nothing like an incident-report email to a supervisor. Same event, three completely different messages.

**Audience awareness** means consciously adjusting your word choice, level of detail, and tone for the specific receiver. For a peer clinician, you can use clinical abbreviations and assume shared knowledge of a diagnosis. For a patient, you strip the jargon and check understanding. For a supervisor documenting an incident, you give precise times, names, and facts without interpretation. Misjudging the audience — using medical terms with a patient who has low health literacy, or over-explaining basics to a specialist — costs time and erodes trust.

### Plain language and precise language

These two ideas sound contradictory but work together. **Plain language** means choosing everyday words over jargon whenever possible. Instead of "the patient exhibited diaphoresis," say "the patient was sweating heavily." Plain language makes your message understandable on the first read — a standard enforced by the AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit and the CDC Plain Language guidelines for patient-facing communication [AHRQ Health Literacy].

**Precise language** means choosing specific, measurable terms over vague ones. "The wound is approximately 2 cm long and 0.5 cm deep, located 3 cm superior to the right lateral malleolus" tells a clinician exactly what to expect. "There's a small cut on the ankle" does not. In clinical documentation, vagueness creates legal exposure and continuity-of-care gaps.

The two rules together: use the simplest word that is also exact. If "laceration" is the clinically precise term and your reader is a nurse, use it. If your reader is the patient's teenage child, say "cut" and describe its size.

**Active voice** supports both goals. Active sentences name who did what: "the charge nurse administered the medication" rather than "the medication was administered." Active voice is shorter, clearer, and assigns accountability — all of which matter in clinical documentation. Passive voice is appropriate only when the action matters more than the actor, for example in a procedure note where the focus is the procedure, not who performed it [ISMP Error-Prone Abbreviations].

### SBAR: a structure for high-stakes verbal communication

When you need to hand off a patient, call a provider, or escalate a concern, unstructured conversation is a liability. **SBAR** — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — is a four-part framework that forces complete information into a predictable sequence [IHI SBAR].

- **Situation:** What is happening right now? One sentence. "Mr. Torres in room 4 is having chest pain, 7 out of 10, that started 15 minutes ago."
- **Background:** What context does the receiver need? Two to three sentences covering relevant history, current medications, and what has already been done.
- **Assessment:** What do you think is going on? This is your clinical judgment, not just a list of facts. "I'm concerned this could be cardiac."
- **Recommendation:** What do you want? Be direct. "I need a 12-lead EKG order and I'd like you to come evaluate him."

SBAR is used across nearly every accredited hospital in the United States because it solves the most common communication failure: the receiver doesn't know why they're being called or what action is expected.

### Confirming the message landed: read-back and teach-back

Sending a clear message is only half the job. The other half is confirming it was received accurately. In healthcare, two techniques do this work.

**Read-back** (also called check-back) is used for verbal orders, critical lab values, and any spoken communication where a misheard word could cause harm. The receiver repeats the message back to the sender, and the sender confirms or corrects. TeamSTEPPS — the AHRQ team-training curriculum — calls this "closed-loop communication" because it closes the loop opened by the original message [AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0].

**Teach-back** is used in patient education. After explaining a medication regimen, a procedure, or discharge instructions, the clinician asks the patient to explain it back in their own words. "I want to make sure I explained that clearly — can you tell me how you'll take this medication at home?" If the patient can't, the explanation wasn't clear enough, and the clinician tries again with different language [AHRQ Health Literacy].

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> Maria is a medical assistant finishing a phone triage call. The patient reports her child has a fever of "about 103" but she isn't sure whether she measured it rectally or orally. Maria needs to document the call and relay the information to the supervising physician. She writes: "Mother reports fever, approximately 103°F, measurement method unclear. Child age 4. No other symptoms reported. Physician consultation requested." She then reads the note back to the physician over the intercom to confirm he received the right information. The physician asks one clarifying question — has the child had any febrile seizures — and Maria returns to the phone to ask. The loop closes cleanly.

### The "Do Not Use" list: precision in documentation

The Joint Commission and the Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) publish a list of abbreviations that are banned from clinical documentation because they are routinely misread in ways that cause patient harm [ISMP Error-Prone Abbreviations]. "U" written for "units" looks like the number zero and has caused ten-fold insulin overdoses. "QD" for "daily" is misread as "QID" (four times daily). Writing out the full word is not a stylistic choice — in an accredited facility, it is a mandatory safety rule.

The same logic applies to decimal points: always write a leading zero before a decimal (0.5 mg, not .5 mg) and never write a trailing zero after a whole number (5 mg, not 5.0 mg). Both errors have caused fatal medication overdoses.

### Key terms

**Audience awareness** — adjusting word choice, detail level, and tone for the specific reader or listener.

**Plain language** — using everyday words over jargon so the message is understandable on the first read.

**Precise language** — using specific, measurable terms over vague ones; essential for clinical documentation.

**Active voice** — sentence structure in which the subject performs the action; the default for healthcare communication because it names the actor and is shorter.

**SBAR** — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation; the standard structured handoff format.

**Read-back / check-back** — the receiver repeats a spoken message back to the sender to confirm accurate receipt; closed-loop communication.

**Teach-back** — asking a patient to explain instructions back in their own words to confirm understanding.

**"Do Not Use" list** — the ISMP/Joint Commission list of abbreviations banned from clinical documentation because they cause medication errors.

### Chapter summary

Clear communication in healthcare is a patient-safety skill, not a personality trait. Every message requires conscious audience awareness — the receiver determines the language, detail level, and format. Plain language and precise language work together: choose the simplest word that is also exact. For high-stakes verbal communication, SBAR provides a four-part structure that ensures the receiver knows the situation, has the context, understands your assessment, and knows what action you are requesting. Close every critical loop with read-back or teach-back. In written documentation, follow the "Do Not Use" abbreviation list and decimal safety rules — the consequences of getting them wrong are measured in patient harm.

### Review questions

1. A physician gives you a verbal order for "0.5 mg of morphine IV." Write out exactly what you would say when reading back this order to confirm it was received correctly.
2. A patient nods and says "yes, I understand" after you explain her discharge instructions. Why is that not sufficient confirmation, and what would you do instead?
3. Rewrite the following documentation entry to use active voice and precise language: *"Medication was given. Patient seemed uncomfortable, some redness was noted near the IV site."*
