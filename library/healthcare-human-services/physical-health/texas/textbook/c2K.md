# Health, Wellness, and Prevention Across the Lifespan

**By the end of this chapter you will be able to:**
- Distinguish health as a state from wellness as an ongoing pursuit
- Identify the eight dimensions of wellness and explain how they interact
- Describe the three levels of prevention and give an example of each
- Explain how wellness priorities and risks shift across the lifespan
- Identify social determinants of health and explain why they often outweigh individual behavior in shaping outcomes

Two patients at a community health fair check their blood pressure. Both readings are normal. One patient is a 24-year-old who exercises, sleeps, eats well, and feels connected to her family and work. The other is a 52-year-old with high financial stress, a job he hates, and no close friends outside his household. Both patients have "health" by the numbers — but are they equally well? The distinction matters enormously to how healthcare workers think about prevention.

### Health versus wellness

The World Health Organization defines **health (WHO definition)** as "a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity." This definition is broader than most people expect — it includes mental and social well-being, not just the absence of illness.

**Wellness** goes further still. Wellness is the active, ongoing pursuit of well-being across multiple dimensions of life. Health is a state you can measure; wellness is a direction you move in. A person can have managed chronic disease — a condition that affects health by definition — and still practice high levels of wellness by making purposeful choices across multiple dimensions of their life.

### The eight dimensions of wellness

Modern wellness frameworks identify eight dimensions that together describe what a flourishing life looks like. The **dimensions of wellness** are:

1. **Physical** — exercise, nutrition, sleep, avoiding harmful substances, preventive care
2. **Mental/emotional** — managing stress, processing emotions, maintaining psychological stability
3. **Social** — maintaining meaningful relationships, community connection, communication
4. **Intellectual** — curiosity, learning, creative engagement
5. **Occupational** — finding purpose and satisfaction in work and daily roles
6. **Spiritual** — meaning, values, connection to something larger than oneself
7. **Environmental** — safe housing, clean air and water, connection to nature
8. **Financial** — stable income, manageable debt, ability to plan for the future

These dimensions do not operate independently. Financial stress measurably increases cortisol levels and disrupts sleep — damaging physical wellness. Poor mental health reduces motivation for exercise and healthy eating. Social isolation worsens outcomes from almost every chronic disease. A student who wants to support a patient's wellness cannot focus only on the physical dimension and call the job done.

### Three levels of prevention

Disease prevention is organized into three levels based on when in the disease process an intervention acts. This framework (introduced briefly in the previous chapter) is central to how healthcare systems plan and evaluate their work.

**Primary prevention** stops disease before it starts. It acts on healthy people. Vaccines are the clearest example — a child who receives the MMR vaccine never develops measles in the first place. Other primary-prevention strategies include seatbelt use, tobacco cessation programs, sunscreen use, physical activity, clean municipal water, and hand hygiene. The target is a population or individual who does not yet have the disease.

**Secondary prevention** catches disease early, when treatment is most effective and damage is minimal. It acts on people who appear healthy but may have disease that hasn't yet caused symptoms. Screenings are secondary prevention: a mammogram for a woman with no breast lump, a colonoscopy for an average-risk 45-year-old, a blood pressure check at a pharmacy. The earlier a disease is caught, the more treatment options are available and the better the outcome.

**Tertiary prevention** limits damage after a disease is already established. It acts on people who are already sick. Cardiac rehabilitation after a heart attack, stroke rehabilitation, diabetes self-management education, and chronic-pain management programs are all tertiary-prevention examples. The goal is not to undo the disease but to prevent further deterioration and improve quality of life within the condition.

### Wellness and prevention across the lifespan

Wellness priorities are not the same at every stage of life. They shift as the body, social roles, and health risks change.

In **infancy and early childhood**, physical wellness and developmental attachment are the dominant concerns. Immunizations, nutrition, safe sleep, and developmental screening are the primary-prevention focus of this stage.

In **adolescence**, mental, social, and emotional wellness come to the front. Identity formation, peer pressure, sexual health, and the emergence of mental health conditions make this stage particularly complex. Primary prevention of substance use, injury, and mental illness is the public-health priority.

In **adulthood**, occupational and financial wellness gain importance alongside continued physical and mental health. Chronic-disease risk rises significantly in middle age, making secondary-prevention screenings — colorectal cancer screening, blood pressure checks, lipid panels — critical.

In **older adulthood**, the physical dimension requires active maintenance as physiological reserve decreases. Social and spiritual wellness also become central as people face retirement, loss of peers, and end-of-life concerns. Tertiary prevention dominates much of older-adult healthcare as chronic conditions accumulate.

### Social determinants of health

**Social determinants of health** are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age — income, housing stability, education level, food access, neighborhood safety, and transportation. These factors often predict health outcomes more powerfully than individual health behaviors. A person who knows they should eat vegetables but lives in a food desert, works two jobs, and lacks reliable transportation faces structural barriers that good advice alone cannot overcome.

The **Healthy People 2030** framework, published by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, identifies measurable national goals for wellness and health equity across the lifespan and explicitly incorporates social determinants as a primary driver of outcomes. Understanding social determinants is not optional background knowledge — it is the difference between healthcare that addresses patients as full human beings and healthcare that blames individuals for conditions driven by systems.

**DALYs (disability-adjusted life years)** combine years of life lost to early death and years lived with disability into a single measure. Comparing DALYs across communities reveals the real burden of disease — including the portion driven by social conditions, not just biology.

> **Clinical scenario**
>
> A community health nurse is reviewing data from two zip codes five miles apart in the same Texas city. One has a life expectancy of 82 years; the other, 71. The rates of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and obesity are significantly higher in the lower-life-expectancy zip code. The nurse pulls socioeconomic data and finds the gap correlates strongly with median income, access to grocery stores with fresh produce, walkability scores, and park access. She uses the Healthy People 2030 framework to identify three upstream interventions — a mobile food pantry partnership, a walking-trail safety project, and free BP screening at a neighborhood pharmacy — that address the social determinants driving the eleven-year gap, not just the diseases those determinants produce.

### Key terms

**Health (WHO definition)** — a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.

**Wellness** — the active, lifelong pursuit of well-being across multiple dimensions; distinguished from the state of health.

**Dimensions of wellness** — physical, mental/emotional, social, intellectual, occupational, spiritual, environmental, and financial; they interact and affect each other.

**Primary prevention** — stopping disease before it starts (vaccines, seatbelts, clean water, smoking cessation).

**Secondary prevention** — catching disease early through screenings (mammogram, colonoscopy, BP check, newborn screen).

**Tertiary prevention** — limiting damage after disease is established (cardiac rehab, stroke rehab, chronic-pain management).

**Social determinants of health** — income, housing, education, food access, and neighborhood conditions that often outweigh individual behavior in determining health outcomes.

**DALY (disability-adjusted life year)** — a measurable wellness indicator combining years of life lost and years lived with disability.

**Healthy People 2030** — the HHS federal framework of measurable wellness and health-equity goals across the lifespan.

### Chapter summary

Health and wellness are related but distinct concepts: health describes a state, wellness describes an active process across eight interacting dimensions. Disease prevention is organized into three levels — primary (stop it before it starts), secondary (catch it early), and tertiary (limit damage once it's established) — and effective prevention uses all three together across the lifespan. Wellness priorities shift by developmental stage, and social determinants of health often shape outcomes more powerfully than individual choices. Healthcare workers who understand these concepts can help patients and communities pursue wellness in ways that address the full picture — not just the numbers on a chart.

2. A public health department is deciding how to reduce rates of type 2 diabetes in a county. Identify one specific strategy at each level of prevention (primary, secondary, tertiary) and explain where in the disease process each intervention acts.

Using the concept of social determinants of health, explain what factors could account for this difference and what kind of intervention would address the root cause.
