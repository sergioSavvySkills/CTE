## Learning objective

Students will be able to interpret medical terms from their word parts, transcribe spoken or written clinical information accurately using electronic tools, and communicate that information using correct terminology — while avoiding the error-prone abbreviations identified by ISMP and The Joint Commission.

## Content stub

- **Three verbs, three skills, one standard:**
  - *Interpret* — decode unfamiliar terms from prefix/root/suffix
  - *Transcribe* — accurately record spoken or written clinical information in a chart, order, or documentation system
  - *Communicate* — use the correct term in the right context with the right audience (clinician vs. patient)
- **Word-part decoding (lesson 1 core content):**
  - *Root* carries meaning (cardi-, nephr-, hepat-, gastr-, oste-, derm-, neur-, my-)
  - *Combining vowel* (usually -o-) joins parts
  - *Suffix* modifies or adds action/condition (-itis inflammation, -ectomy removal, -ostomy opening, -oscopy visual exam, -algia pain, -emia blood condition, -logy study of)
  - *Prefix* modifies the front (hypo/hyper, brady/tachy, peri, sub, inter, poly, dys)
  - *Decoding order:* suffix → prefix → root — example: *pericarditis* = peri (around) + cardi (heart) + itis (inflammation)
- **Common decoding pitfalls:** similar roots that mean different things (ileum vs. ilium; myel- can mean spinal cord OR bone marrow depending on context); silent letters; abbreviations that look like word parts but aren't
- **Abbreviations, routes, and frequencies (lesson 2 core content):**
  - *Frequency and timing:* q, qd, bid, tid, qid, qh, prn, ac, pc, hs, stat
  - *Routes and sites:* PO, IV, IM, SC/SubQ, SL, PR, OD/OS/OU, AD/AS/AU
  - *Body and status:* BP, HR, RR, T, O₂ sats, WNL, NAD, NKDA, DNR, NPO
  - *Orders and diagnostics:* ABG, CBC, BMP, CXR, EKG/ECG, MRI, CT, H&P, D/C, ADM
- **Joint Commission "Do Not Use" list and ISMP Error-Prone list (enforceable in accredited facilities):**
  - U or u → spell "unit"; IU → spell "international unit"
  - QD / QOD → spell "daily" / "every other day"
  - Trailing zero (1.0 mg) and missing leading zero (.5 mg) → these misreads have killed pediatric patients
  - MS, MSO₄, MgSO₄ → spell out "morphine sulfate" or "magnesium sulfate"
  - μg (micrograms) → write "mcg"
- **Appropriate technology:**
  - *EHR documentation* — standardized terminology pick-lists, problem lists, medication reconciliation; controlled vocabularies (SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD-10, RxNorm)
  - *Dictation and voice-to-text* — benefits and failure modes (homophone errors: "hypertension" vs. "hypotension")
  - *Secure messaging and texting rules* — no patient identifiers on unsecured channels
  - *Institution-specific approved abbreviation lists* — always check the facility's list; what is safe at one hospital may be banned at another
- **Communicating with patients vs. colleagues:** clinician-to-clinician uses terminology; clinician-to-patient pairs the term with plain-language translation (crosswalks with c3B teach-back)

## Assessment stub

- Decode drill: given 20 unfamiliar medical terms, break each into prefix/root/suffix and state the meaning; no reference materials
- Build drill: given 10 plain-language definitions (e.g., "inflammation of the kidney," "surgical removal of the gallbladder"), construct the correct medical term
- Transcription practical: listen to a 90-second recorded clinical dictation and produce an accurate written transcription — using correct terminology and no "Do Not Use" abbreviations; instructor marks every error
- Error hunt: given a sample order sheet with 8 deliberate ISMP / Joint Commission violations (trailing zero, missing leading zero, banned abbreviations, ambiguous routes), identify and rewrite each correctly
- EHR documentation task: using a simulated EHR interface, document a short patient encounter selecting correct terms from controlled-vocabulary pick-lists; instructor scores accuracy of term selection and documentation completeness

## References

- **ISMP List of Error-Prone Abbreviations, Symbols, and Dose Designations** — authoritative source for the "Do Not Use" items above; directly tied to documented patient-safety events.
- **Joint Commission Official "Do Not Use" List** — accreditation-backed abbreviation rules that make the ISMP list enforceable in accredited facilities.
- **SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD-10-CM, and RxNorm** — the standardized clinical vocabularies underlying US EHR documentation.
- **21st Century Cures Act and ONC-certified EHR requirements** — federal rules on interoperability and standardized terminology use.
- **AMA Manual of Style (11th ed.)** — conventions for medical terminology, abbreviations, numerical values.
- **AHRQ resources on voice-to-text transcription safety** — failure-mode analyses for speech-recognition documentation.
- **Chabner, *The Language of Medicine*** — standard HS/undergraduate medical-terminology textbook for word-part decoding.
