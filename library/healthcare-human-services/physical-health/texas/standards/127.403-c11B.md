## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify and apply the three families of industry safety standards named in the TEKS — standard (and transmission-based) precautions; fire prevention, detection, and response; and appropriate actions to general emergency situations — to the everyday situations a healthcare student will encounter in clinical rotation.

## Content stub

- **Lesson 1 — Infection-control safety standards (standard precautions):**
  - *Chain of infection* — infectious agent → reservoir → portal of exit → mode of transmission → portal of entry → susceptible host; breaking any one link stops transmission
  - *Modes of transmission* — contact (direct/indirect), droplet (large, within ~6 ft), airborne (small, room-scale), vehicle (food, water, instruments), vector (insects)
  - *Standard precautions* — applied to *every* patient *every* time: hand hygiene, PPE as indicated, safe injection practices, respiratory etiquette, cleaning and disinfecting surfaces and equipment
  - *Hand hygiene (WHO Five Moments)* — before patient contact; before aseptic task; after body-fluid exposure risk; after patient contact; after contact with patient surroundings
  - *Alcohol-based hand rub vs. soap and water* — ABHR is faster and more effective in most situations; soap and water required when hands are visibly soiled and after caring for C. diff or norovirus patients (ABHR does not kill spores)
  - *PPE selection by precaution level* — standard (gloves when touching body fluids), contact (gown + gloves), droplet (surgical mask + eye protection), airborne (N95 + eye protection + AIIR room)
  - *Donning / doffing sequences* — donning: gown → mask/respirator → goggles/face shield → gloves; doffing: gloves → goggles/face shield → gown → mask/respirator → hand hygiene
  - *Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs)* — CLABSI, CAUTI, SSI, VAP, C. diff — standard precautions are the foundational prevention
- **Lesson 2 — Fire prevention, fire response, and emergency response:**
  - *Fire prevention in healthcare* — control of ignition sources near oxygen, enforcement of no-smoking policies, proper storage of flammable materials, inspection of electrical equipment, fire-stop maintenance in walls
  - *Fire response — RACE:*
    - *Rescue* anyone in immediate danger
    - *Alarm* — pull the pull-station, call the facility's fire code (often "Code Red")
    - *Contain* — close doors and windows to limit spread
    - *Extinguish or Evacuate* — in that order, and only if the fire is small and you are trained
  - *Fire extinguisher use — PASS:*
    - *Pull* the pin
    - *Aim* at the base of the fire
    - *Squeeze* the handle
    - *Sweep* side to side
    - Know when *not* to fight: fire is larger than a trash can, you don't know what's burning, smoke is blocking exits, or you haven't been trained
  - *Oxygen and fire* — oxygen does not burn but accelerates combustion; shut off oxygen valves during a fire, keep flame/heat away from O₂ tubing, know where zone valves are
  - *Evacuation priorities* — ambulatory first, then wheelchair, then bedbound; horizontal evacuation (to the next smoke compartment) before vertical (stairs); elevators not to be used during a fire
  - *Emergency codes (facility-specific but common patterns):*
    - *Code Red* — fire
    - *Code Blue* — cardiac/respiratory arrest
    - *Code Gray* — combative person
    - *Code Pink* — infant abduction
    - *Code Silver* — active shooter / weapon
    - *Code Black* — bomb threat (varies)
    - *Rapid Response Team (RRT)* — patient clinical deterioration before arrest
  - *Other emergency situations and appropriate actions:*
    - *Medical emergency (not yours to lead)* — call for help, stay with the patient, assist the team, don't leave until relieved
    - *Chemical spill or exposure* — evacuate and contain, consult the SDS, notify supervisor, follow OSHA Hazard Communication response
    - *Workplace violence* — de-escalate if trained; otherwise create distance, alert security, document per facility policy
    - *Severe weather / natural disaster* — follow facility shelter-in-place or evacuation plan; know your assigned role
    - *Mass-casualty event* — activate incident command; report to staging area per facility disaster plan
  - *Incident reporting — every adverse event, near-miss, or unsafe condition* — timely, factual, objective; purpose is system improvement, not individual blame

## Assessment stub

- Chain-of-infection drill: given 5 clinical scenarios (a C. diff patient with diarrhea; a TB-suspected patient in the ED; a central-line insertion; norovirus outbreak on a unit; a peripheral IV in an older adult), identify each link in the chain and propose one intervention per link
- PPE selection and sequence: given 10 precaution scenarios, select the correct PPE set; then perform a donning-and-doffing skill check on a peer observer with no self-contamination — must meet the full sequence and complete hand hygiene at the correct moments
- Fire-response tabletop: given a unit map with a fire in a specific patient room, walk the class through RACE step-by-step with peers assigned roles (primary responder, alarm activator, door-closer, patient mover, charge-nurse communication); follow with a PASS demonstration on a training extinguisher or simulator
- Code-identification quiz: given 15 overhead-announcement descriptions, match each to the correct emergency code and state the correct initial response and the student's appropriate role (lead, assist, stay out of the way, evacuate)
- Scenario analysis: respond to three mixed emergency scenarios (a needlestick injury with possible HBV exposure; a small trash-can fire in a utility room; a combative visitor in the ED waiting area); for each, walk through the appropriate actions in correct order and identify which agency's rule (CDC, OSHA, NFPA, facility-specific) governs the response

## References

- **CDC *Guideline for Isolation Precautions: Preventing Transmission of Infectious Agents in Healthcare Settings* (Siegel et al., updated)** — authoritative US guideline for standard and transmission-based precautions.
- **WHO *My 5 Moments for Hand Hygiene*** — the operational hand-hygiene framework adopted in most US healthcare settings.
- **CDC HAI prevention guidelines** — CLABSI, CAUTI, SSI, VAP, C. diff prevention bundles.
- **OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030)** — the enforceable federal rule behind PPE, sharps containers, and post-exposure protocols.
- **OSHA Respiratory Protection Standard (29 CFR 1910.134)** — N95 fit-testing requirements.
- **NFPA 101 *Life Safety Code* and NFPA 99 *Health Care Facilities Code*** — fire-safety and emergency-systems standards adopted by Joint Commission and CMS.
- **The Joint Commission Environment of Care and Emergency Management standards** — accreditation-level fire-safety, code, and emergency-response requirements.
- **CMS Emergency Preparedness Rule (42 CFR §482.15)** — federal emergency-management requirement for Medicare/Medicaid-certified facilities.
- **OSHA Hazard Communication Standard (29 CFR 1910.1200) and GHS SDS format** — chemical-spill response.
- **Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS) emergency-preparedness guidance** — Texas-specific implementation.
- **Texas Hospital Association emergency-code standardization resources** — Texas-specific efforts to standardize emergency codes across facilities.
