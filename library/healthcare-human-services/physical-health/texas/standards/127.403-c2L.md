## Learning objective

Students will be able to analyze the communication skills that support or erode healthy relationships and evaluate their effectiveness across the lifespan — peer and family relationships in childhood and adolescence, partner and workplace relationships in adulthood, caregiver and grief-centered relationships in later life.

## Content stub

- **Two verbs, two depths:** *analyze* — name the components of a communication skill and identify when each is used; *evaluate* — judge whether a given use of the skill was effective in context
- **Relationships as a health factor:** strong social ties correlate with longer life and lower cardiovascular and mental-health risk; social isolation carries mortality risk comparable to heavy smoking (HHS/NAS reports)
- **Core communication skills that maintain relationships:**
  - *Active listening* — paraphrasing, summarizing, not interrupting
  - *I-statements* — "I feel X when Y" instead of "You always Z"
  - *Boundary-setting* — clear, calm, repeatable limits; distinguishing a request from a demand
  - *Repair after conflict* — acknowledging impact, apologizing specifically, changing behavior
  - *Seeking help early* — naming when a relationship is harmful and where to get support
- **Corrosive patterns (Gottman "Four Horsemen"):** criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling — predictive of relationship failure; recognizable in peer, family, and partner contexts
- **Lifespan variation in which skills matter most:**
  - *Childhood/adolescence* — peer-skill development, handling bullying, family communication during identity formation
  - *Young adulthood* — romantic-partner communication, roommate/cohabitation, early workplace relationships
  - *Middle adulthood* — partner maintenance, parenting communication, caregiving for aging parents
  - *Older adulthood* — grief, loss, maintaining community after retirement and bereavement
- **Cultural and individual variation:** directness norms, eye contact, silence, and family-decision-making roles differ across cultures and families — a skill judged "effective" in one context may not be in another

## Vocabulary

- **Active listening** — paraphrasing, summarizing, and not interrupting; a core communication skill that maintains relationships across the lifespan. *(also in c3A)*
- **I-statement** — a message framed as "I feel X when Y" instead of "You always Z"; reduces defensiveness in relationship conflict.
- **Boundary-setting** — clear, calm, repeatable limits; distinguishing a request from a demand.
- **Repair after conflict** — acknowledging impact, apologizing specifically, and changing behavior after a relationship rupture.
- **Four Horsemen (Gottman)** — criticism, contempt, defensiveness, and stonewalling; communication patterns that predict relationship failure.
- **Contempt** — Gottman's strongest predictor of relationship failure; communication conveying superiority or disgust.
- **Stonewalling** — withdrawing from interaction during conflict; one of the Four Horsemen corrosive patterns.
- **Social isolation** — lack of meaningful social contact; carries mortality risk comparable to heavy smoking per HHS/NAS reports. *(also in c10C)*
- **ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences)** — early-life relationship harms with evidence-based long-term physical and mental-health effects.

## Assessment stub

- Behavior classification: given 12 relationship behaviors (e.g., interrupting, paraphrasing a partner's concern, stonewalling, setting a limit, contempt-laden eye-roll), sort as supporting, neutral, or harming the relationship and justify each
- Scenario evaluation: read three relationship vignettes spanning life stages (a 14-year-old friend conflict, a 35-year-old partner conflict, a 72-year-old caregiver conflict); for each, evaluate whether the communication was effective and propose a revision with the specific skill named
- Role-play: practice a repair conversation — one student delivers a specific apology for a named harm, the other evaluates it against the repair-skill components; observers score both
- Reflection: describe one relationship in your life that has supported your well-being and one that has not; name the specific communication patterns that made the difference and identify one skill you want to develop

## References

- **HHS / National Academies of Sciences *Social Isolation and Loneliness in Older Adults* (2020)** — the quantitative case that social-relationship quality is a public-health outcome, not a soft skill.
- **Gottman, *The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work* and *The Four Horsemen* research** — decades of longitudinal research on communication patterns predicting relationship outcomes; the "contempt is the strongest predictor of failure" finding is widely cited.
- **Erikson's Eight Stages** (see c2J) — maps which relationship challenges dominate at each life stage.
- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS and nonviolent-communication frameworks** — structured skill sets for I-statements, active listening, and conflict de-escalation that transfer from clinical-team contexts to personal relationships.
- **CDC *Preventing Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)* resources** — evidence base for the long-term health impact of early-life relationship quality.
