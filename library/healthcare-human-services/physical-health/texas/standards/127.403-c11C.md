## Learning objective

Students will be able to relate the safety practices a healthcare worker performs every shift — hand hygiene, PPE use, sharps handling, specimen labeling, medication double-checks, time-outs, incident reporting — to the specific regulatory agency rule or professional standard behind them, and explain the adherence barriers that make these practices fail in real settings.

## Content stub

- **Purpose of this standard — the "relate" verb:** c11A named the agencies; c11B named the safety standards; c11C asks students to *connect* the two — trace any daily practice back to the rule that requires it and the agency that enforces it; this is the integrative safety lesson
- **Everyday practices students will perform or see, and the rules behind them:**

| Daily practice | Rule or standard behind it | Agency / source |
|---|---|---|
| Hand hygiene before and after every patient contact | WHO Five Moments; CDC Guideline for Isolation Precautions | WHO / CDC (adopted by TJC and CMS) |
| Gloves and gown for contact precautions | OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard; CDC isolation guideline | OSHA (29 CFR 1910.1030); CDC |
| Annual N95 fit testing for staff who use respirators | OSHA Respiratory Protection Standard | OSHA (29 CFR 1910.134) |
| Sharps into an approved sharps container, needles not recapped | OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard | OSHA |
| Two patient identifiers before any procedure or medication | TJC National Patient Safety Goal 01.01.01 | The Joint Commission |
| Surgical Time-Out before procedure start | TJC Universal Protocol; WHO Surgical Safety Checklist | TJC / WHO |
| "Do Not Use" abbreviations avoided in orders and notes | TJC Official "Do Not Use" List; ISMP Error-Prone Abbreviations List | TJC / ISMP |
| Specimen labeling at the bedside, not at the desk | TJC NPSG and CLIA standards | TJC / CMS (CLIA) |
| Medication labeling in the procedure area (syringes, basins) | TJC NPSG 03.04.01 | TJC |
| Verbal-order read-back | TJC NPSG 02.03.01; TeamSTEPPS check-back | TJC / AHRQ |
| Incident report for adverse event or near-miss | TJC Sentinel Event Policy; state reporting laws | TJC / state DSHS |
| Immunizations per ACIP for staff (flu, MMR, Tdap, hepatitis B, varicella) | CDC ACIP recommendations, often employer-enforced | CDC (adopted by employer and TJC) |
| TB surveillance for healthcare workers | CDC TB Infection Control guidelines; OSHA | CDC / OSHA |
| SDS available for every chemical on the unit | OSHA Hazard Communication Standard | OSHA (29 CFR 1910.1200) |
| Fire drill participation and RACE/PASS training | NFPA Life Safety Code; TJC Environment of Care | NFPA / TJC |
| Equipment-failure reporting (Medical Device Reports) | FDA Medical Device Reporting regulation | FDA (21 CFR 803) |
| Patient Notice of Privacy Practices at first encounter | HIPAA Privacy Rule | HHS OCR (45 CFR 164.520) |
| Needlestick exposure — PEP evaluation, source testing, OSHA log entry | OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard; CDC PEP guidelines | OSHA / CDC |

- **Adherence barriers — why good rules fail in practice:**
  - *Time pressure* — the most common reason hand hygiene, double-checks, and documentation get shortcut
  - *Alert fatigue* — EHR warnings, alarms, and notifications lose meaning when they fire constantly
  - *Authority gradient* — junior staff hesitate to speak up when a more senior person skips a safety step; TeamSTEPPS tools (CUS, two-challenge rule) exist specifically for this
  - *Inconvenient design* — PPE stored in the wrong place, sharps containers that are full, sink without soap, dispenser without rub — system problems, not attitude problems
  - *Normalization of deviance* — small shortcuts that don't immediately cause harm become the new normal; the Columbia shuttle disaster is the canonical case study in how this happens in high-reliability settings
  - *Personal-appearance conflicts* — jewelry, artificial nails, long hair — all evidence-based infection-control issues that feel personal and create friction
- **Why students matter in safety culture:**
  - New eyes notice what has been normalized
  - Speaking up protects patients and models the behavior expected of licensed practitioners
  - The speak-up moment on a clinical rotation is what builds the muscle for licensed practice later
- **How to relate a practice back to its source when you don't know:**
  - Ask "what rule is this?" — supervisor, preceptor, or policy manual
  - Search the facility's policy library — most facilities map policies to CDC, OSHA, TJC, and CMS citations
  - Go to the agency website directly — CDC, OSHA, FDA, TJC, and CMS publish their standards openly
  - When two rules overlap, the stricter one controls

## Vocabulary

- **Two-patient-identifier rule** — TJC National Patient Safety Goal 01.01.01 requiring two identifiers before any procedure or medication.
- **Universal Protocol** — TJC's pre-procedure protocol including Time-Out to prevent wrong-site, wrong-procedure, and wrong-patient surgery.
- **Time-Out** — the surgical pause before incision when the team verbally confirms patient, procedure, and site.
- **Verbal-order read-back** — TJC NPSG 02.03.01 requirement that the receiver repeat a verbal order back to the orderer.
- **Medical Device Report (MDR)** — FDA reporting under 21 CFR 803 for device events contributing to death or serious injury. *(also in c12C)*
- **Sentinel Event** — a Joint Commission–defined serious patient-harm event triggering root-cause analysis. *(also in c3A, c7B)*
- **Normalization of deviance** — the safety-culture failure mode (Vaughan, Columbia shuttle) in which small shortcuts become accepted practice.
- **Swiss cheese model (Reason)** — framework explaining how system defenses fail when holes in layered barriers align.
- **Alert fatigue** — desensitization to EHR warnings and alarms that fire constantly; an adherence barrier.
- **Authority gradient** — hierarchy dynamic that makes junior staff hesitate to speak up; CUS and the two-challenge rule are its countermeasures. *(also in c4A)*
- **Just Culture** — Marx's accountability framework distinguishing human error from at-risk and reckless behavior to support safe reporting. *(also in c9D, c12C)*

## Assessment stub

- Practice-to-rule mapping: given 15 daily healthcare practices from the table above (or similar), for each identify (a) the specific rule or standard behind it, (b) the issuing agency, (c) the enforcement mechanism, and (d) one consequence of non-compliance for the worker or the institution; cite authoritative sources for at least 10
- Policy-to-practice trace: pick one hospital policy (students can use a real Texas hospital's publicly available policy or a course-provided sample); trace the policy back to its federal, state, or accreditation-level source; identify one specific sentence in the policy that exists because of a specific agency rule
- Adherence-barrier analysis: pick one daily safety practice (hand hygiene, PPE, double-checks, time-outs, sharps disposal, incident reporting) and identify three concrete barriers to adherence you have observed or can imagine on a rotation; for each barrier, propose a system-level fix (not a "try harder" fix)
- Scenario walk: given three rotation scenarios (a coworker skipping hand hygiene between patients; a preceptor using a banned abbreviation in an order; a colleague reusing a disposable gown), describe what specific TEKS-named regulatory source is being violated and what a respectful speak-up using CUS would sound like in each case

## References

- **All references from c11A (WHO, CDC, OSHA, FDA, TJC, NIH)** apply here; c11C is the integrative standard.
- **The Joint Commission *National Patient Safety Goals* (current year)** — updated annually; operational source for many of the bedside safety practices.
- **Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) *List of Error-Prone Abbreviations, Symbols, and Dose Designations*** — the ISMP list that TJC "Do Not Use" references.
- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0** — operational speak-up tools (CUS, two-challenge rule) for adherence barriers.
- **Diane Vaughan, *The Challenger Launch Decision*** — canonical source for "normalization of deviance" as a safety-culture failure mode.
- **James Reason, *Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents*** — "Swiss cheese model" of how system defenses fail; foundational for understanding how safety practices relate to system design.
- **AHRQ *Patient Safety Network* (PSNet)** — current case studies, commentaries, and evidence on how safety practices work in real US healthcare settings.
- **Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS)** — state-level enforcement and reporting for many of the above practices.
