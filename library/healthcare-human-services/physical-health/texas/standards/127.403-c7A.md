## Learning objective

Students will be able to compare health science careers within and across the five TEKS-named career systems — diagnostic services, therapeutic services, health informatics, support services, and biotechnology research and development — using a consistent framework (duties, education, credentials, setting, wage, outlook) so that comparisons are fair and usable for personal career planning.

## Content stub

- **The five TEKS-named systems, defined:**
  - *Diagnostic services* — identify what is wrong: medical laboratory scientist (MLS/MT), medical laboratory technician (MLT), phlebotomy technician, radiologic technologist, ultrasonographer, nuclear medicine technologist, CT/MRI technologist, EKG technician, pathology assistant
  - *Therapeutic services* — treat and care for the patient: MD/DO, PA, NP, RN/LVN/CNA, respiratory therapist, physical/occupational/speech therapist and assistants, pharmacist and pharmacy tech, dentist and dental hygienist/assistant, EMS/paramedic, medical assistant, athletic trainer, mental-health counselor
  - *Health informatics* — manage the data and technology of care: health information technician (coder/biller), medical records specialist, EHR analyst, clinical informaticist, health-information manager (RHIA/RHIT), medical scribe, healthcare data analyst
  - *Support services* — keep the care environment functional: biomedical equipment technician, central-sterile technician, environmental services, dietary services, patient transport, hospital security, facilities maintenance, healthcare interpreter
  - *Biotechnology research and development* — create new knowledge and products: biomedical research scientist, clinical research coordinator, biotechnology laboratory technician, pharmaceutical chemist, bioinformatics specialist, biostatistician, regulatory affairs specialist
- **Comparison framework (use consistently across systems):**
  - *Core duties* — what the worker actually does on a typical shift or day
  - *Typical education* — certificate / associate / bachelor's / graduate / doctoral
  - *Credential* — licensure, certification, or registration required or valued
  - *Setting* — hospital unit, outpatient clinic, lab, imaging suite, remote, lab bench, manufacturing facility
  - *Median wage (Texas-specific where available)* — cite BLS OOH
  - *10-year outlook* — fastest growing vs. stable vs. declining; with rationale
- **What the systems have in common:** all serve patients (directly or indirectly), all require professional behavior (see c1A–c1C), all operate under state and federal regulation (see c2O), most require some credential to enter
- **What differentiates the systems:**
  - *Patient contact* — therapeutic is highest; biotech R&D is often zero
  - *Entry education* — support services and some therapeutic roles (CNA, MA) open with a certificate; most biotech R&D requires a bachelor's minimum
  - *Pace and variability* — diagnostic imaging and laboratory can be rapid and procedural; biotech R&D is slower and experimental
  - *Tangible vs. intangible work product* — a phlebotomist draws a tube; a data analyst produces a report
- **Cross-system collaboration (sets up c7B):** every patient touches all five systems during a typical care episode — a chest-pain patient triggers a diagnostic workup (lab + imaging), therapeutic intervention (medication, possibly procedure), informatics documentation (EHR + billing codes), support services (transport, sterile processing), and may contribute to research (enrollment in an observational study)

## Vocabulary

- **Diagnostic services** — the system that identifies what is wrong (MLS, MLT, phlebotomy, radiologic technologist, ultrasonographer, EKG technician).
- **Therapeutic services** — the system that treats and cares for the patient (MD/DO, NP, PA, RN/LVN/CNA, RT, PT/OT/SLP, pharmacist, EMS).
- **Health informatics** — the system that manages the data and technology of care (HIM, coders, EHR analyst, clinical informaticist, scribe, data analyst).
- **Support services** — the system that keeps the care environment functional (biomed tech, central sterile, environmental services, transport, interpreter).
- **Biotechnology research and development** — the system that creates new knowledge and products (biomedical scientist, clinical research coordinator, bioinformatics, regulatory affairs).
- **NCHSE career clusters** — the National Consortium for Health Science Education five-pathway framework the Texas TEKS draws from directly.
- **Core duties** — the typical day-to-day work of a role; one of the six comparison-framework dimensions alongside education, credential, setting, wage, and outlook.
- **Setting** — the work environment (hospital unit, outpatient clinic, lab, imaging suite, bench, manufacturing facility) used to differentiate roles.
- **Cross-system collaboration** — the cooperation across all five systems that occurs during a typical patient care episode; sets up c7B.

## Assessment stub

- System classification: given 30 healthcare job titles, correctly place each in one of the five TEKS-named systems; for ambiguous cases (e.g., medical assistant who does phlebotomy — diagnostic or therapeutic?), explain the reasoning
- Comparison matrix: complete a 5 systems × 6 framework dimensions (30-cell) comparison matrix using at least one concrete role per system; cite BLS or other credible sources for each data point
- Pick-two-compare short paper: choose one role from each of two different systems (e.g., radiologic technologist from diagnostic vs. physical therapist assistant from therapeutic); produce a 1-page comparative summary across the full framework; conclude with which you could see yourself pursuing and why
- Career-fit reflection: based on the matrix you built, identify the two systems you are most drawn to and name specific features of your interests, strengths, and constraints (money, time, geography, contact preference) that support the match
