## Learning objective

Students will be able to review the real documents that operationalize patient rights and choices — informed-consent forms, advance directives, DNR orders, Notice of Privacy Practices, patient bill of rights, and AMA-discharge forms — and identify whether each is complete, appropriate, and legally sufficient.

## Content stub

- **Purpose of this standard:** c10A asked students to identify autonomy *situations*; c10D asks them to review the *documents* that record the exercise of those rights; this is a practical records-literacy standard
- **Documents students should be able to review competently:**
  - *Informed consent form* — procedure-specific; typically includes patient name/MRN, procedure description in lay language, risks/benefits/alternatives, patient and witness signatures, date/time
  - *Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP)* — HIPAA-required document given at first encounter; describes how PHI is used and the patient's rights regarding it; patient acknowledges receipt (not "consents to" — distinction matters)
  - *Patient Bill of Rights* — facility-specific statement of patient rights per Joint Commission and CMS Conditions of Participation; typically posted and also provided in admission materials
  - *Advance directive — Directive to Physicians (Texas statutory form, HSC Chapter 166)* — the "living will" under Texas law; signed by the patient with two witnesses (or notary); takes effect if the patient has a terminal or irreversible condition and cannot communicate
  - *Medical Power of Attorney (Texas statutory form, HSC Chapter 166)* — designates a surrogate decision-maker if the patient loses capacity
  - *Out-of-Hospital DNR (Texas specific form)* — signed by patient (or surrogate) and physician; directs EMS and non-hospital settings not to resuscitate
  - *In-hospital DNR order* — a physician order entered in the chart; based on patient or surrogate direction
  - *AMA (Against Medical Advice) discharge form* — documents informed refusal of continued care; should not be used as punishment
  - *Consent for release of records* — authorization to disclose PHI outside treatment/payment/operations
- **What to check when reviewing each document:**
  - *Completeness* — all required fields filled in (patient identifiers, dates, signatures, witnesses where needed)
  - *Currency* — date and time; advance directives should be reviewed periodically; stale forms may need re-confirmation
  - *Legibility and accuracy* — correct patient, correct procedure, no unauthorized corrections
  - *Appropriate signer* — patient themselves when they have capacity; the correct surrogate when they do not; verify authority for surrogates (POA document, statutory hierarchy)
  - *Witnesses and notarization* — Texas advance directives require specific witnesses (not attending physician, not an heir, not anyone who benefits) or notarization
  - *Language and accessibility* — signed in a language the patient understands; interpreter used for LEP patients; accommodations for disability
  - *Voluntariness signals* — no evidence of coercion, no sedation at the time of signing
- **Common documentation failures to catch on review:**
  - Consent signed before the physician discussion happened
  - Consent signed after pre-medication
  - Wrong procedure or side/site listed
  - Missing risks, benefits, or alternatives
  - Surrogate signing without documented authority
  - No interpreter noted when one was needed
  - Advance directive witnessed by an ineligible witness (heir, staff with conflict)
  - NPP acknowledgment used as if it were consent for treatment
- **Review is different from execution:** reviewing documents does not mean signing them — the standard is about reading, spotting gaps, and routing for correction; a student on rotation will typically be asked to check forms, not to obtain consent themselves
- **Where these documents live:** typically in the EHR's Consents/Legal/Advance Care Planning sections; some are also scanned in from paper originals; students should know where to find each

## Vocabulary

- **Informed consent form** — the procedure-specific document capturing patient identifiers, procedure description, risks/benefits/alternatives, and witnessed signatures.
- **Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP)** — the HIPAA-required (45 CFR §164.520) document given at first encounter; patient acknowledges receipt (not consent to treatment).
- **Patient Bill of Rights** — the facility-specific statement of patient rights per Joint Commission and CMS Conditions of Participation.
- **Directive to Physicians** — Texas statutory "living will" form (HSC Chapter 166); takes effect if the patient has a terminal or irreversible condition and cannot communicate.
- **Medical Power of Attorney (Texas)** — Texas statutory form designating a surrogate decision-maker under HSC Chapter 166. *(also in c10A)*
- **Out-of-Hospital DNR** — Texas-specific form signed by patient (or surrogate) and physician directing EMS and non-hospital settings not to resuscitate.
- **AMA discharge form** — documentation of informed refusal of continued care; not a punishment label.
- **Ineligible witness** — a witness (attending physician, heir, beneficiary) disqualified under Texas advance-directive statutes.
- **Release of records authorization** — consent to disclose PHI outside treatment, payment, or operations.
- **Voluntariness** — the requirement that consent was not obtained under sedation, coercion, or duress; one of the five elements checked on document review.

## Assessment stub

- Form-review practical: given a packet of 6 real (de-identified) documents — one consent, one NPP acknowledgment, one Texas Directive to Physicians, one Medical POA, one AMA-discharge form, one release-of-records authorization — review each for completeness, currency, correct signer, and appropriate witnessing; mark gaps and explain the consequence of each
- Error-catch drill: given 10 document excerpts with deliberate flaws (consent signed after sedation given; advance directive witnessed by the patient's son; AMA form without evidence of informed refusal; consent missing alternatives section; NPP treated as treatment consent; Medical POA without the notarization; consent for the wrong site; DNR without physician signature; advance directive dated but not signed; release of records missing the specific disclosure), identify each flaw and the appropriate corrective action
- Document walk-through: pick one of the Texas statutory forms (Directive to Physicians or Medical Power of Attorney) and walk through each section, explaining in plain language what the patient is agreeing to and what would make each section legally insufficient
- Short response: in 150–200 words, explain the practical difference between a Notice of Privacy Practices acknowledgment and an informed-consent signature — two documents a patient often signs back-to-back at admission — and why treating them as interchangeable is a legal and ethical problem
