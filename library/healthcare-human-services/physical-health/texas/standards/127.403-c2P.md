## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify the diverse cultural, linguistic, religious, and socioeconomic influences that shape contemporary US healthcare delivery — in how care is sought, communicated, consented to, and experienced by patients.

## Content stub

- **Why culture shapes care delivery, not just "diversity as a topic":** culture affects how illness is named and understood, who speaks for the patient, whether Western biomedicine is trusted, dietary and modesty norms, pain expression, end-of-life preferences — these map directly to daily clinical decisions
- **Influences worth identifying:**
  - *Race and ethnicity* — historical and contemporary disparities (maternal mortality, cardiac care, pain management), cultural health practices, trust gaps rooted in documented abuses (Tuskegee, forced sterilization, Henrietta Lacks)
  - *Language* — qualified medical interpreters, translated materials, limited-English-proficient (LEP) patient rights under Title VI
  - *Religion and spirituality* — dietary rules, blood-product refusal, fasting during treatment, prayer, chaplaincy, end-of-life rituals
  - *Immigration status* — access barriers, documentation fears, sanctuary policies, emergency-only Medicaid
  - *Gender identity and sexual orientation* — affirming care, pronouns, documented health disparities for LGBTQ+ patients
  - *Disability* — physical, sensory, cognitive, psychiatric; ADA requirements and communication adaptations
  - *Rural vs. urban* — access to specialists, telehealth, critical-access hospitals, transportation
  - *Socioeconomic status* — the social determinants of health: income, housing, food access, education, neighborhood environment
- **Bias in care delivery:** implicit bias measurably affects pain treatment, cardiac workup, maternal outcomes — recognized as a patient-safety issue by Joint Commission and AHRQ, not a political talking point
- **Structural responses:** Title VI language-access requirements, CLAS (Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services) Standards from HHS, TJC cultural-competence standards, NIMHD (National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities) research
- **Distinction from c2Q:** this standard asks students to *identify* the influences (knowledge); c2Q asks them to *research and compare* specific cultural practices (research skill)

## Vocabulary

- **CLAS Standards** — HHS Office of Minority Health's *National Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services* Standards; the federal framework behind interpreter services and translated materials. *(also in c10E)*
- **Title VI (Civil Rights Act of 1964)** — the legal foundation for language-access obligations in federally funded healthcare programs.
- **Limited English Proficient (LEP)** — patient population whose primary language is not English; entitled to qualified interpretation under Title VI.
- **Implicit bias** — unconscious attitudes that measurably affect clinical decisions (pain treatment, cardiac workup, maternal outcomes); recognized as a patient-safety issue.
- **Health disparity** — a documented outcome gap between population groups (e.g., Black maternal mortality, Indigenous diabetes outcomes) rooted in cultural, structural, and historical influences.
- **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** — federal law requiring communication and access accommodations for patients with disabilities in healthcare settings.
- **Social determinants of health** — income, housing, food access, education, and neighborhood environment; the socioeconomic influences shaping contemporary care delivery. *(also in c2K)*
- **Immigration status** — a demographic factor shaping access, documentation fears, sanctuary policies, and emergency-only Medicaid eligibility.
- **NIMHD** — the NIH National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities; federal research source on the influences this standard names.

## Assessment stub

- Influence map: given a list of 15 contemporary healthcare practices (interpreter services, kosher/halal meal options, same-gender-clinician requests, telehealth in rural areas, sliding-scale clinics, BIPOC doula programs), identify which cultural, linguistic, religious, or socioeconomic influence drove the practice into existence
- Case review: read three documented disparity cases (Black maternal mortality, Indigenous diabetes outcomes, LGBTQ+ primary-care access); for each, identify the specific cultural or structural influences contributing to the outcome gap
- Short research: pick one HHS CLAS Standard and explain, in 150–200 words, what contemporary healthcare practice it drove and what the law requires of providers

## Activities

- **Influence-to-Practice Mapper** — student sees 15 contemporary healthcare practices (qualified medical interpreter at intake, kosher/halal tray option, same-gender clinician request honored, doula program in a high-maternal-mortality county, sliding-scale clinic, sign-language interpreter in the ED, sanctuary-policy signage, organ-inventory approach on the intake form, telehealth in rural West Texas). For each, student drags an origin tag — language, race/ethnicity, religion, immigration, gender identity, disability, rural/urban, SES — then names the specific federal lever (Title VI, ADA, CLAS, NIMHD research). Correct origin + correct lever = full credit; origin alone = half.
  → *pattern recognition, analysis*

- **Disparity Root-Cause Card** — student opens one of three disparity cards — Black maternal mortality (2.5× white rate per CDC), Indigenous type-2 diabetes outcomes, LGBTQ+ primary-care avoidance. Each card presents a timeline, a data slice, and a patient quote. Student must select two structural influences and two immediate clinical behaviors that plausibly drive the gap, then propose one CLAS-Standard-grounded change. A reveal compares their answer to the AHRQ Disparities Report finding.
  → *analysis, empathy*

- **Implicit-Bias Pain Case** — a short scripted clinical scenario: two ED patients present with identical 8/10 back pain, same vitals, differing only in race. Student is asked, under a 20-second clock, to pick an analgesic order for each. On submit, the sim shows aggregate class patterns (where bias lives in speed, in drug class, in dose). Then a second round slows the clock, forces the student to justify the order in a sentence, and scores whether the justification names a clinical reason rather than a demographic one.
  → *metacognition, ethical reasoning*

## References

- **HHS Office of Minority Health *National CLAS Standards (Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services)*** — the federal framework for culturally responsive care; directly shaped interpreter services, translated patient materials, and staff training requirements.
- **Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (language-access obligations for federally funded programs)** — legal foundation for interpreter services in most US healthcare settings.
- **Joint Commission *Advancing Effective Communication, Cultural Competence, and Patient- and Family-Centered Care*** — accreditation-backed standards that make cultural competence a patient-safety requirement.
- **AHRQ *National Healthcare Quality and Disparities Report* (annual)** — evidence base for documented disparities by race, ethnicity, income, geography, and insurance.
- **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** — communication and access requirements for patients with disabilities in healthcare settings.
- **Washington, *Medical Apartheid*** and **Skloot, *The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks*** — historical sources for the documented abuses that shape contemporary trust gaps.
