## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify the components that make clinical communication effective — active listening, open questioning, empathy, precise language, closed-loop confirmation — and the barriers and patterns that make it non-effective.

## Content stub

- **Effective-communication components, named and operationalized:**
  - *Active listening* — eye contact, open body posture, paraphrasing, summarizing, not interrupting
  - *Open vs. closed questions, used deliberately* — "How have you been feeling?" invites a story; "Did you take your medication?" gets yes/no; each has a place
  - *Empathy statements* — naming the emotion without judging it ("That sounds really hard") — distinct from sympathy ("I feel bad for you")
  - *Productive silence* — 3–5 seconds after a hard question often surfaces the real concern
  - *Precise language* — specific verbs and measurable values (crosswalks with c1A and c2F)
  - *Closed-loop confirmation* — read-back or paraphrase-back to verify the message landed (TeamSTEPPS check-back)
- **Non-effective-communication components, named:**
  - *Interrupting, talking over* — patient's opening statement is cut off within seconds in observational studies (Beckman & Frankel; Singh Ospina et al.)
  - *Closed questions when open are needed* — the patient's full concern never surfaces
  - *Jargon without translation* — the patient can't engage with what they don't understand
  - *Minimizing emotion* — "Don't worry about that" shuts the conversation down
  - *Missing nonverbal signals* — not noticing distress, confusion, disagreement
  - *Assumptions and stereotypes* — deciding what a patient needs before asking
- **Barriers that derail even good technique:** language, hearing, vision, cognitive impairment, stress, cultural differences, time pressure, electronic-record screen distraction
- **Distinction from c3B:** c3A is about *identifying* effective vs. non-effective components (knowledge); c3B is about *demonstrating* effective communication skills (performance)
- **Patient-safety framing:** Joint Commission sentinel-event data repeatedly identifies communication failures as a leading contributor to patient harm — this is a safety standard, not a soft skill

## Vocabulary

- **Active listening** — eye contact, open posture, paraphrasing, summarizing, and not interrupting; a core component of effective clinical communication.
- **Open question** — a question that invites a story ("How have you been feeling?"); used to surface the patient's full concern.
- **Closed question** — a yes/no or short-answer question ("Did you take your medication?"); used deliberately, not as a default.
- **Empathy statement** — naming the emotion without judging it ("That sounds really hard"); distinct from sympathy.
- **Productive silence** — a 3–5 second pause after a hard question that often surfaces the real concern.
- **Closed-loop / check-back** — TeamSTEPPS read-back or paraphrase-back used to verify that a message was received accurately.
- **Jargon** — technical vocabulary used without translation; a non-effective-communication component when it blocks patient engagement.
- **Calgary-Cambridge guide** — standardized medical-education framework that codifies effective patient-communication components.
- **Sentinel event** — a Joint Commission–defined serious patient-harm event; root-cause data repeatedly names communication failure as a leading contributor.

## Assessment stub

- Identify drill: given 20 short transcript excerpts from simulated clinician-patient conversations, label each as effective or non-effective and name the specific component present or missing
- Transcript annotation: annotate a 1-page scripted patient-provider conversation — mark each therapeutic technique used, each barrier present, and each missed opportunity
- Component match: given a list of 12 communication components (active listening, interrupting, open question, empathy statement, jargon, etc.), sort into effective and non-effective columns and provide a one-sentence rationale for each placement

## References

- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0 — Communication module** — the operational definitions of check-back, call-out, and structured exchanges used across US healthcare.
- **Joint Commission sentinel-event root-cause data** — communication consistently ranks among the top root causes of patient-harm events, making communication a safety standard.
- **Beckman & Frankel (1984) *Effect of Physician Behavior on the Collection of Data* and Singh Ospina et al. (2019) *Eliciting the Patient's Agenda*** — observational studies documenting how often clinicians interrupt patients' opening statements (median 11–18 seconds).
- **Calgary-Cambridge and SEGUE frameworks** — standardized medical-education frameworks for teaching patient-communication skills that codify the effective-component list above.
- **AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit** — the "plain language, no jargon" component operationalized for everyday clinical communication.
