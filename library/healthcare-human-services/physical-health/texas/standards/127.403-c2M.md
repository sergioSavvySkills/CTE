## Learning objective

Students will be able to research and summarize one or more historically significant developments in healthcare — identifying the era, the key people or events, the change in practice, and the link to current care.

## Content stub

- **Why historical literacy matters in healthcare:** current practices (germ theory, antisepsis, evidence-based medicine) are the result of specific turning points — understanding the turning points explains why we do what we do
- **Eras and turning points worth knowing at a survey level:**
  - *Ancient and pre-modern:* Hippocratic tradition (~400 BCE, "first, do no harm"), Galen's humoral medicine, Islamic Golden Age hospitals, European guild medicine, traditional healing systems across cultures
  - *Scientific revolution (1500s–1800s):* Vesalius (anatomy), Harvey (circulation), Jenner (smallpox inoculation), Semmelweis and Lister (antisepsis), Pasteur and Koch (germ theory), Nightingale (nursing reform, sanitation)
  - *Modern medicine (1900s):* penicillin (1928), polio vaccine (1955), smallpox eradication (1980), imaging (X-ray 1895, CT 1970s, MRI 1970s), organ transplant, modern ICU
  - *Policy and access:* Medicare/Medicaid (1965), EMTALA (1986), HIPAA (1996), ACA (2010)
  - *Recent (2000s–2020s):* human genome (2003), mRNA vaccines (COVID-19), telehealth expansion, AI in diagnostics, health-equity reckoning
- **Public-health vs. clinical advances:** clean water, sanitation, vaccination, tobacco control, seatbelt laws — often saved more lives than any single clinical advance
- **Research verb, operationalized:** the TEKS verb is *research*, not *memorize* — students locate credible sources, take notes with citations, and produce a short written or oral summary
- **Credible sources for health history:** National Library of Medicine history division, CDC MMWR historical archives, Johns Hopkins and Harvard history-of-medicine archives, Smithsonian Museum of American History medicine collections, peer-reviewed history-of-medicine journals

## Vocabulary

- **Germ theory** — the scientific-revolution-era discovery (Pasteur, Koch) that microorganisms cause disease; underlies modern antisepsis and infection control.
- **Antisepsis** — sterile-technique turning point associated with Semmelweis and Lister; the practice of preventing infection during medical procedures.
- **Humoral medicine** — Galen's pre-modern theory of blood, phlegm, and bile that dominated Western medicine before the scientific revolution.
- **Hippocratic tradition** — the ~400 BCE origin of Western medical ethics, source of the "first, do no harm" formulation.
- **Nightingale reforms** — nursing and sanitation reforms in the Scientific Revolution era that transformed hospital mortality rates.
- **Flexner Report (1910)** — the ethics/education turning point that standardized US medical education and closed substandard schools.
- **Tuskegee study** — a 20th-century US research abuse (syphilis study in Black men) that shaped modern research-consent ethics and the Belmont Report.
- **Smallpox eradication** — the 1980 WHO-declared global public-health turning point; first disease eliminated by vaccination.
- **MMWR** — CDC's *Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report*; primary-source archive useful for researching public-health history.
- **Primary source** — an original document, artifact, or firsthand account; credible for historical research, distinguished from secondary summaries.

## Assessment stub

- Research product: pick one turning point from a provided list (e.g., germ theory, polio vaccine, HIV treatment, mRNA vaccines, the Tuskegee study, the Flexner Report); produce a one-page written summary citing at least three credible sources, covering the era, the key people, the change, and how current practice still reflects it
- Timeline: contribute to a class timeline of 12 healthcare milestones — each entry includes date, person or event, one-sentence impact, and a cited source
- Short essay (choice): choose one advance and explain in 200–300 words how US healthcare today would be different without it; cite at least two sources

## References

- **National Library of Medicine, History of Medicine Division** — authoritative primary-source archive for US/global health history; free online digital collections.
- **CDC *MMWR* "Ten Great Public Health Achievements"** (1999 and 2011 reports) — CDC's own list of the public-health turning points of the 20th and early 21st centuries.
- **Paul Starr, *The Social Transformation of American Medicine*** — Pulitzer-winning historical analysis of how US healthcare became what it is; standard reference in health-policy courses.
- **Rebecca Skloot, *The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks*** and **James H. Jones, *Bad Blood* (Tuskegee)** — accessible accounts of ethical turning points that shaped modern research consent and Belmont-Report-era ethics.
- **ICMJE Recommendations** — citation standards for the research output students will produce.
