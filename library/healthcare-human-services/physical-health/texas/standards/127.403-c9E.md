## Learning objective

Students will be able to research the major federal and Texas state laws governing the health science industry — locating primary sources, identifying the enforcing agency, and describing at least one concrete rule each law imposes on daily clinical practice.

## Content stub

- **Research verb, operationalized:** students locate primary legal sources (statutes, regulations, agency guidance), record citations, and summarize what each law actually requires — not memorize a list from a textbook
- **Major federal laws governing US healthcare (what to know at survey level):**
  - *HIPAA (1996)* — Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification Rules (45 CFR Parts 160 and 164); enforced by HHS Office for Civil Rights
  - *EMTALA — Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (1986)* — requires medical screening and stabilization in any hospital ED regardless of ability to pay; enforced by CMS and HHS OIG
  - *Anti-Kickback Statute (42 U.S.C. §1320a-7b) and Stark Law (42 U.S.C. §1395nn)* — prohibit certain financial relationships that could distort medical decisions; enforced by HHS OIG and DOJ
  - *Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act and subsequent amendments* — FDA authority over drugs, devices, biologics (21 CFR)
  - *CLIA — Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (1988)* — quality standards for all US clinical labs; enforced by CMS
  - *OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030)* — workplace-safety rules for exposure to blood and OPIM; enforced by OSHA
  - *Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)* — accessibility and communication requirements
  - *Affordable Care Act (2010)* — coverage expansion, value-based payment, preventive services
  - *21st Century Cures Act (2016)* — interoperability and information blocking provisions
  - *GINA — Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (2008)* — genetic-data protection
- **Major Texas state laws and regulations (what to know at survey level):**
  - *Texas Nurse Practice Act (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 301)* — scope and discipline for RNs, LVNs, APRNs
  - *Texas Medical Practice Act (Chapter 151)* — scope and discipline for physicians
  - *Texas Medical Records Privacy Act (Health and Safety Code Chapter 181)* — state-level privacy statute, in some respects stricter than HIPAA
  - *Texas Medical Liability Act (Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 74)* — 2003 tort-reform framework including damage caps
  - *Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 166 — Advance Directives* — Texas statute on medical power of attorney, directives to physicians, and out-of-hospital DNR
  - *Texas Administrative Code Title 25 (Department of State Health Services)* — the main body of Texas health regulations
- **How to find a law (research method):**
  - Start with the authoritative source — *statute* from the legislature, *regulation* from the agency
  - Federal: *Code of Federal Regulations* (ecfr.gov), *United States Code*, agency websites (CMS, HHS OCR, OSHA, FDA)
  - Texas: *Texas Occupations Code*, *Texas Health and Safety Code*, *Texas Administrative Code* (all at statutes.capitol.texas.gov)
  - Professional associations and legal-information sites (NCSBN, KFF, Justia, Cornell LII) for plain-language summaries and citations back to primaries
- **Credibility-evaluation rules for legal research:**
  - Primary (statutes, regulations, agency guidance) ranks above secondary (textbook summaries, news articles)
  - Currency matters — laws change; check effective date
  - Jurisdictional scope matters — a federal rule may be preempted by a stricter state rule (or vice versa)
- **How laws translate to daily clinical practice:**
  - Laws name the duty; institutional policies translate them into workflow; staff training turns them into behavior
  - Many "policies" students will see in rotation are the institution's implementation of a specific federal or state rule

## Assessment stub

- Primary-source navigation: demonstrate live the ability to locate four specific legal sources — (1) 45 CFR §164.502 (HIPAA Privacy Rule permitted uses), (2) Texas Occupations Code §301.002 (Nurse Practice Act definitions), (3) 29 CFR §1910.1030 (OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard), (4) 42 U.S.C. §1395dd (EMTALA); take a screenshot or copy the citation of the exact subsection cited
- Law-summary card: pick one federal and one Texas law from the lists above; produce a one-page "law card" for each that includes (a) full citation, (b) enforcing agency, (c) what the law requires in one clear sentence, (d) one concrete rule the law imposes on a daily healthcare workflow (e.g., "why every new patient signs a Notice of Privacy Practices"), (e) at least three credible sources
- Agency mapping: given 10 specific rules or requirements (mandatory reporter for suspected child abuse; disposal of sharps; prescribing a controlled substance; billing Medicare for a service; maintaining patient records for X years), identify the governing law and the enforcing agency
- Short essay: in 200–300 words, explain why the same healthcare workflow (e.g., handling a patient's medical records) is typically governed by both a federal law and a Texas law; name one case where the Texas law is stricter and explain what that means for practice in Texas

## References

- **eCFR (ecfr.gov) and United States Code (uscode.house.gov)** — authoritative federal primary sources.
- **Texas Statutes (statutes.capitol.texas.gov) and Texas Administrative Code (texreg.sos.state.tx.us)** — authoritative Texas state primary sources.
- **HHS Office for Civil Rights — HIPAA page** and **CMS — Medicare and Medicaid regulations pages** — federal-agency operational guidance.
- **Texas Board of Nursing, Texas Medical Board, Texas Department of State Health Services** — Texas-agency operational guidance and position statements.
- **Cornell Legal Information Institute (law.cornell.edu)** — widely used free legal-research resource with reliable primary-source links.
- **KFF (Kaiser Family Foundation) and NCSL (National Conference of State Legislatures) health-law summaries** — credible secondary sources that cite primaries accurately; good for context but not for citations.
- **AHLA (American Health Law Association)** and **Health Affairs** — professional and scholarly sources for deeper-dive research.
