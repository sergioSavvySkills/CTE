## Learning objective

Students will be able to evaluate the effectiveness of named conflict-resolution techniques — including the five Thomas-Kilmann styles and healthcare-specific de-escalation and escalation tools — across patient-family, team, and supervisor-conflict scenarios.

## Content stub

- **Thomas-Kilmann five conflict styles:** *avoid*, *accommodate*, *compete*, *compromise*, *collaborate* — each has a situation where it is the right choice; collaboration is the clinical-team default but not always correct (a safety emergency needs *compete*, not consensus)
- **Core techniques, named and operationalized:**
  - Calm tone and regulated body language (before any words)
  - Active listening and paraphrasing back what you heard
  - Separating the person from the problem
  - Validating feelings *before* problem-solving ("I can see you're frustrated" lands before "here's what we can do")
  - I-statements instead of you-statements
  - Reframing the conflict as a shared goal
- **De-escalation with upset patients or families (AHRQ CANDOR / acknowledge-explain-next-step pattern):** acknowledge the feeling, apologize where legally appropriate, explain what is happening and why, offer a concrete next step; avoid defensiveness, avoid arguing facts before the emotion is heard
- **Team-conflict escalation tools (AHRQ TeamSTEPPS):** CUS ("I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue"); two-challenge rule — if first safety concern is dismissed, raise it a second time before going up the chain; document objectively
- **When to bring in help:** threats of violence, clinician impairment, repeated scope violations, patterns of disrespect — supervisor, risk management, ethics committee, or security are the right next step, not more talking
- **Evaluating effectiveness — the rubric:** did the immediate safety issue get resolved? did the relationship survive? was the underlying problem named? would the same approach work with this person next time? is documentation clear enough to support any follow-up?
- **Pitfalls that look like resolution but aren't:** winning at all costs, gossiping with uninvolved coworkers, texting about the conflict, apologizing for things that shouldn't be admitted legally

## Vocabulary

- **Thomas-Kilmann styles** — five conflict modes (avoid, accommodate, compete, compromise, collaborate); each fits specific situations.
- **Avoid (TKI)** — conflict style of low assertiveness and low cooperation; appropriate for trivial or risky-to-engage disputes.
- **Compete (TKI)** — high-assertiveness, low-cooperation style appropriate in a safety emergency where consensus isn't viable.
- **Collaborate (TKI)** — high-assertiveness, high-cooperation style that is the clinical-team default when time and stakes allow.
- **De-escalation** — calm tone, validation, and acknowledge-explain-next-step used to reduce emotional intensity with upset patients or families.
- **CUS language** — TeamSTEPPS safety-escalation script: "I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue."
- **Two-challenge rule** — TeamSTEPPS norm allowing a team member to raise a safety concern twice if the first challenge is dismissed.
- **CANDOR** — AHRQ Communication and Optimal Resolution toolkit; evidence-based process for responding to patient-harm events with acknowledgment and apology.
- **Reframing** — conflict-resolution move that restates a disagreement as a shared goal rather than a contest of positions.
- **Validation** — naming the other person's feeling before problem-solving; the move that lets later content land.

## Assessment stub

- Style-matching drill: given 6 varied scenarios (a family angry about a delay, a coworker consistently late, a patient refusing treatment, a supervisor overruling a safety call, a teammate violating scope, a patient demanding an inappropriate test), select the best-fit Thomas-Kilmann style and justify why
- Role-play + evaluation: pair exercise — one student plays an upset family member in a simulated scenario, another applies the acknowledge-explain-next-step pattern; a third student evaluates effectiveness against a rubric and proposes one specific change
- Case evaluation: read a 1-page transcript of a team-conflict scenario; evaluate the communication moves used (name each technique), judge overall effectiveness, and propose how CUS or the two-challenge rule should have been applied
