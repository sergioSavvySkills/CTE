## Learning objective

Students will be able to describe the major stages of human development across the lifespan — infancy through older adulthood — and identify the dominant health needs, risks, and communication adaptations at each stage.

## Content stub

- **Infancy (0–1 yr):** rapid physical growth, sensorimotor learning, attachment formation; health focus — feeding, immunizations, safe sleep (SIDS prevention), developmental screening
- **Early childhood (1–6 yr):** language explosion, preoperational thought, autonomy struggles; health focus — nutrition, injury prevention, vaccines, early-intervention screening
- **School age (6–12 yr):** concrete operational thinking, peer relationships, industry vs. inferiority (Erikson); health focus — vision/hearing screening, mental health, obesity prevention
- **Adolescence (12–18 yr):** formal operational thinking, identity formation, risk-taking (developing prefrontal cortex); health focus — mental health, substance use, sexual health, injury, eating disorders
- **Young and middle adulthood (18–65 yr):** career and relationship formation, generativity; health focus — chronic-disease prevention, reproductive health, cancer screening, mental health
- **Older adulthood (65+ yr):** cognitive and physical changes, integrity vs. despair, grief; health focus — polypharmacy, fall prevention, cognitive screening, chronic-disease management, advance care planning
- **Using stages in clinical work:** developmental stage drives what assessments are done, how consent is obtained, what communication style lands, and what risks to screen for

## Vocabulary

- **Developmental stage** — an age-defined period of human growth with characteristic physical, cognitive, and psychosocial features that drive care priorities.
- **Erikson's psychosocial stages** — eight-stage model (including industry vs. inferiority in school age, identity in adolescence, integrity vs. despair in older adulthood) used across nursing and allied health.
- **Piaget's cognitive stages** — sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational thinking; shapes communication choices by age.
- **Sensorimotor** — Piaget's infant stage (0–1 yr) of rapid physical growth and learning through senses and movement.
- **Formal operational thinking** — Piaget's adolescent stage marked by abstract reasoning; pairs with identity formation and risk-taking.
- **Developmental milestone** — an age-expected behavior (motor, language, social) used to screen for typical or delayed development.
- **Bright Futures (AAP)** — the evidence-based pediatric health-supervision guidelines that set age-specific priorities birth through 21.
- **Advance care planning** — the older-adulthood health focus on documenting future care preferences and end-of-life decisions.
- **Polypharmacy** — the concurrent use of multiple medications in older adults; a dominant health risk requiring stage-specific screening.

## Assessment stub

- Match: given 10 developmental milestones or behaviors, match each to the correct stage
- Health-focus grid: complete a 6 stages × 3 top-health-priorities table, citing the source (AAP Bright Futures, CDC, USPSTF) for each priority
- Communication adaptation: for 4 patient ages (2-year-old, 8-year-old, 16-year-old, 82-year-old with mild dementia), describe three specific communication adaptations and justify each

## Activities

- **Stage-Sort Waiting Room** — six patients stream through a virtual clinic waiting room — a fussy infant, a 4-year-old with a scraped knee, an 8-year-old with a headache, a 15-year-old asking about a sports physical, a 45-year-old with BP concerns, an 82-year-old on six medications. Student drags each to the correct developmental stage panel and picks the top health priority from a shuffled menu (safe sleep, early-intervention screen, mental health, polypharmacy review, etc.). Mis-sorts surface the Erikson stage they missed.
  → *pattern recognition, recall*

- **Milestone vs. Myth** — rapid-fire drill: is this an expected milestone at this age, a red flag, or a parent myth? ("6-month-old not yet walking" — expected; "3-year-old with no two-word phrases" — red flag; "teething causes fever" — myth). Student has 8 seconds per card, pulling from Bright Futures and CDC "Learn the Signs" criteria. Wrong answers reveal the source guideline.
  → *recall, decision under constraint*

- **Communication Adapter** — student has 4 minutes each to script the opening line for the same clinical question ("how are you feeling?") to a 2-year-old, an 8-year-old, a 16-year-old, and an 82-year-old with mild dementia. On submit, a rubric scores vocabulary level, consent framing, and developmental fit; peer reviewers rank the four scripts blind.
  → *application, communication under pressure*

## References

- **Erikson, *Childhood and Society* (1950) — Eight Stages of Psychosocial Development** — the canonical stage model used across nursing, social work, and allied health education.
- **Piaget's Stages of Cognitive Development** — complementary cognitive-stage framework (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational).
- **AAP *Bright Futures: Guidelines for Health Supervision of Infants, Children, and Adolescents*** — evidence-based age-specific health priorities from birth through 21.
- **USPSTF Recommendations by Age Group** — age-specific screening guidance for adults.
- **CDC Developmental Milestones ("Learn the Signs. Act Early.")** — public-facing milestone checklists by age.
