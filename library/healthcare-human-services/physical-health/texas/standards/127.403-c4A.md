## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify the traits and behaviors associated with effective leaders in healthcare contexts — distinguishing durable leadership qualities from charisma or formal authority — and recognize how those traits translate to bedside and team behaviors.

## Content stub

- **Leadership is behavior, not title:** a CNA who speaks up for patient safety is leading; a manager who avoids hard conversations is not — traits describe observable behavior, not org-chart position
- **Traits consistently associated with effective clinical leaders:**
  - *Integrity and reliability* — does what they say they will; admits mistakes
  - *Clinical and professional competence* — earns respect by being credible at the work
  - *Accountability* — owns outcomes, including team failures
  - *Clear communication* (crosswalks with c1A, c3A) — short, precise, timely
  - *Emotional regulation* — stays calm in crisis; doesn't take frustration out on team
  - *Situational awareness* — reads the room, notices what is being left unsaid
  - *Courage to speak up* — raises safety concerns against authority gradient (CUS, two-challenge rule)
  - *Empathy without collapsing into it* — feels the team's or patient's experience without losing judgment
  - *Willingness to develop others* — coaches, gives honest feedback, delegates effectively
- **Traits that are often confused with leadership but aren't:** being the loudest voice, seniority, charisma, willingness to work more hours than the team, command-and-control during non-emergencies
- **Leadership at every level:** the TEKS doesn't assume a formal role — the student in a clinical rotation is expected to lead themselves first (punctuality, preparation, speaking up for patients), then support team leadership
- **Trait-based vs. situational leadership:** no single trait set works in every context; a code-blue leader uses directive command; a staff-meeting leader uses collaboration; a coaching leader uses questions and silence
- **Distinction from c4B:** c4A identifies leader traits (knowledge); c4B demonstrates leadership skills (performance)

## Assessment stub

- Trait identification: given 15 described behaviors (e.g., "the charge nurse admitted her delegation error to the team in morning huddle," "the resident repeatedly cut off the medical student"), identify which demonstrate a leadership trait, which demonstrate its absence, and name the specific trait
- Case analysis: read three short vignettes of healthcare leadership (a safety speak-up that worked, a disruptive-physician case, a new-grad stepping up during a shortage); for each, list the traits present or missing and cite one specific behavior that shows the trait
- Self-inventory: complete a structured self-assessment on 8 named leadership traits; identify the two strongest and two weakest and write one concrete action to develop each of the weakest

## References

- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0 — Leadership competency** — operationalizes leader traits into observable behaviors (brief, huddle, debrief, speak-up culture) used across US healthcare.
- **Joint Commission Sentinel Event Alerts on leadership and safety culture** — names the specific leadership traits and behaviors that correlate with lower patient-harm rates.
- **American Organization for Nursing Leadership (AONL) Nurse Leader Competencies** — consensus traits and skills for nursing leadership at every level.
- **Kouzes & Posner, *The Leadership Challenge* (Five Practices of Exemplary Leadership)** — widely adopted framework; Model the Way, Inspire a Shared Vision, Challenge the Process, Enable Others to Act, Encourage the Heart.
- **Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization* (psychological safety)** — links specific leader behaviors to team performance and patient outcomes.
- **Goleman, *Primal Leadership* (Emotional Intelligence in leaders)** — evidence base for emotional regulation and empathy as leadership traits.
