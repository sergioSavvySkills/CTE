## Learning objective

Students will be able to analyze how local, state, and national governments shape the health science industry — through coverage and payment, licensure and scope, safety and quality regulation, public-health law, and research funding — and evaluate how a specific policy change affects patients, providers, and the workforce.

## Content stub

- **Three levels, distinct roles:**
  - *Local* — county and city health departments, public hospitals, EMS systems, school health, restaurant and facility inspection, communicable-disease reporting
  - *State* — licensure of health professionals and facilities, joint administration of Medicaid with CMS, public-health laws (reporting, vaccination, isolation), state hospitals, workers' compensation
  - *National* — CMS (Medicare, Medicaid federal rules), FDA (drugs, devices, biologics), CDC (public health, surveillance), NIH (research funding), OSHA (workplace safety), HRSA (safety-net providers), SAMHSA (behavioral health), VA (veteran care)
- **How government shapes industry practice — five main levers:**
  - *Coverage and payment* — what gets paid for (and for whom) drives what gets delivered
  - *Licensure and scope* — who is allowed to deliver care, with what training, in what settings
  - *Safety and quality regulation* — how care must be delivered (CLIA for labs, CMS Conditions of Participation for hospitals, state facility regulations)
  - *Public-health law* — mandates for reporting, vaccination, quarantine, tobacco, clean water
  - *Research and workforce funding* — NIH, HRSA, state medical-school support; shapes what gets studied and who can afford training
- **Case studies worth analyzing:**
  - *Medicaid expansion (2014 onward)* — effects on coverage, rural hospital finances, workforce demand
  - *HIPAA (1996)* — effects on documentation, information sharing, patient access, IT spending
  - *ACA (2010)* — coverage expansion, value-based payment, preventive-care mandates, employer coverage rules
  - *COVID-19 response (2020–2023)* — telehealth waivers, emergency-use authorizations, public-health-authority disputes across levels of government
  - *State-level scope expansion for NPs and PAs* — variation by state, workforce access, patient outcomes
- **Where the levels conflict or overlap:** federal preemption (FDA drug approval preempts state drug rules), state-level variation within federal frameworks (Medicaid eligibility, Medicare Advantage plans), local-vs-state disputes during public-health emergencies
- **Analytical method:** for any policy, identify (1) level of government, (2) lever used, (3) intended effect, (4) observed effect on patients, (5) effect on providers and workforce, (6) unintended consequences

## Vocabulary

- **CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services)** — the federal agency that writes Medicare and Medicaid rules and drives roughly 40% of US health spending.
- **FDA (Food and Drug Administration)** — the federal agency regulating drugs, devices, and biologics; its approvals preempt state drug rules. *(also in c11A)*
- **Licensure** — the state-level lever that determines who may legally deliver care, with what training, and in what settings. *(also in c6)*
- **Scope of practice** — the state-defined tasks and decisions a licensed practitioner is authorized to perform. *(also in c1B, c8B)*
- **Medicaid expansion** — the 2014-onward ACA provision with measured effects on coverage, rural hospital finances, and workforce demand.
- **EMTALA** — the 1986 federal law requiring stabilization in any hospital ED regardless of ability to pay. *(also in c9E)*
- **CLIA (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments)** — federal quality standards applied to every US clinical laboratory. *(also in c9E)*
- **Conditions of Participation** — CMS rules that hospitals and other facilities must meet to receive Medicare or Medicaid payment.
- **Federal preemption** — the principle that a federal rule (e.g., FDA drug approval) overrides a conflicting state rule within its scope.
- **Public-health law** — mandates for reporting, vaccination, quarantine, tobacco, and clean water — the lever most visible during outbreaks.

## Assessment stub

- Level-match: given 15 policies or actions (Medicare final rule, state licensure law, county restaurant inspection, FDA drug approval, CDC guidance, school vaccination requirement), identify the level of government responsible and the lever being used
- Policy analysis: pick one named policy (Medicaid expansion, HPV vaccine school requirement, Medicare Part D, ACA preventive-services provision, a specific state scope-of-practice expansion); produce a 1-page analysis covering effects on patients, providers, workforce, and one unintended consequence — cite at least three sources
- Local inquiry: research your county or city health department's current top three priorities; for each, identify which level(s) of government set the relevant rules and the specific lever used

## Activities

- **Three-Level Lever Board** — split-screen board with three columns (Local / State / National) and five lever rows (coverage & payment, licensure & scope, safety & quality, public-health law, research & workforce). Fifteen policy cards — county restaurant inspection, FDA drug approval, Texas Nurse Practice Act, CMS Conditions of Participation, school vaccination requirement, EMTALA, municipal smoke-free ordinance, Medicaid expansion, CLIA, OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens — must be placed in the correct cell under a 4-minute timer. Cells that receive the wrong card glow amber until re-sorted; preemption conflicts (FDA vs. state drug rule) generate a bonus "who wins?" micro-prompt.
  → *pattern recognition, systems thinking*

- **Medicaid Expansion Impact Sim** — student toggles "expansion" on or off in a simulated mid-sized state and watches five dashboards move: uninsured rate, rural hospital operating margins, ED uncompensated-care volume, state Medicaid budget, and workforce demand by role. Student must hit two targets (reduce uninsured rate below 10%, keep rural hospital margins positive) using up to three supplemental levers (provider tax, waiver program, workforce subsidy). Post-run debrief overlays real KFF state data and scores the student's choices against observed outcomes.
  → *systems thinking, analysis*

- **COVID-19 Authority Conflict** — student reads a 45-second scripted news clip about a local-vs-state-vs-federal mask mandate dispute, then has 90 seconds to identify which level has authority over which piece (quarantine, workplace safety, school policy, mask distribution via FEMA). A decision tree asks: "Which preempts which?" Feedback shows the actual legal resolution and cites the specific statute. Three rounds cover telehealth waivers, EUA authority, and vaccination mandates.
  → *analysis, decision under constraint*

## References

- **CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) regulations and program data** — Medicare, Medicaid, and CHIP rules that drive roughly 40% of US health spending.
- **FDA regulations (21 CFR)** — drug, device, and biologic approval pathway that preempts state-level drug rules.
- **CDC guidance documents and *MMWR*** — federal public-health guidance that state and local agencies adapt and enforce.
- **OSHA standards (29 CFR 1910, including 1910.1030 Bloodborne Pathogens)** — federal workplace-safety rules binding on nearly all healthcare employers.
- **National Council of State Legislatures (NCSL) Health Program** — tracks state-level health legislation; useful source for cross-state comparisons of licensure and scope rules.
- **Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS) and Texas Medical Board** — the state-level examples most relevant to a Texas course; illustrate state-level public-health and licensure functions.
- **KFF *State Health Facts* and Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker** — freely available datasets for comparing policy impact across states.
