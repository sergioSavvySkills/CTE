## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify the core concepts of health and wellness — including the dimensions of wellness and the three levels of prevention — and describe how they apply at each stage of the lifespan.

## Content stub

- **Health vs. wellness, distinguished:** *health* is the state (absence of disease + functional capacity per WHO); *wellness* is the active, lifelong pursuit of well-being across multiple dimensions
- **Dimensions of wellness:** physical, mental/emotional, social, intellectual, occupational, spiritual, environmental, financial — they interact (financial stress harms mental health, which harms physical)
- **Three levels of prevention (brief introduction — revisited in c10B):**
  - *Primary* — prevent disease before it starts (vaccines, seatbelts, clean water, smoking cessation)
  - *Secondary* — catch disease early (screenings: mammogram, colonoscopy, BP check, newborn screen)
  - *Tertiary* — limit damage after disease is established (cardiac rehab, stroke rehab, chronic-pain management)
- **Wellness across the lifespan:** dominant dimensions and risks shift by stage — infancy (physical growth, attachment), adolescence (mental/social/identity), adulthood (occupational, financial, physical), older adulthood (physical maintenance, social connection, meaning)
- **Social determinants of health:** income, housing, education, food access, neighborhood — often outweigh individual behavior in determining wellness outcomes (crosswalks with c2P)
- **Measurable wellness indicators:** life expectancy at birth, healthy life expectancy, self-rated health, disability-adjusted life years (DALYs)

## Assessment stub

- Concept map: build a diagram linking the 8 wellness dimensions to one concrete example each across three life stages (adolescent, middle adult, older adult)
- Classification: given 15 public-health interventions, classify each by level of prevention (primary/secondary/tertiary) and the life stage most targeted
- Personal plan: design a one-week personal wellness plan that touches at least five dimensions, with specific measurable actions; justify the age-appropriateness for your own life stage

## References

- **WHO definition of health** (Constitution of the WHO, 1948) — "a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity" — the foundational working definition.
- **Hettler's Six Dimensions of Wellness (National Wellness Institute)** and later eight-dimension expansions (SAMHSA) — the dimension frameworks used in US health education.
- **USPSTF Recommendations by Life Stage** — age-specific screening and counseling guidance at the three prevention levels.
- **Healthy People 2030 (HHS ODPHP)** — the federal framework for measurable wellness and health-equity goals across the lifespan; an authoritative source for "what good looks like" nationally.
- **CDC Social Determinants of Health framework** — evidence base for the population-level drivers of wellness.
