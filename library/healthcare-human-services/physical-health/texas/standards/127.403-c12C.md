## Learning objective

Students will be able to recognize when an equipment or technology malfunction must be reported and explain the full reporting process — from bedside discovery through facility work order, incident report, FDA Medical Device Report (MDR), and manufacturer notification — including what must be documented and preserved at each step.

## Content stub

- **Why reporting matters:**
  - *For the individual patient* — rapid repair or replacement prevents continued harm from the same device
  - *For other patients* — the device may be one of many identical units; a single report can trigger facility-wide inspection and potentially a recall
  - *For the institution* — pattern detection reveals preventive-maintenance gaps and training needs
  - *For the industry* — FDA's MDR database aggregates reports nationally and drives recalls, label changes, and device-design improvements
  - *For the worker* — proper reporting provides legal protection; silent "workaround" of a failed device creates personal liability
- **When to report (not all events are the same):**
  - *Device failure with no patient harm or near-miss* — facility work order + internal incident report if policy requires; may not rise to MDR level
  - *Device failure with a near-miss* — internal incident report; often required by facility policy to escalate to risk management; MDR may or may not be required depending on device and severity
  - *Device failure contributing to patient harm or death* — mandatory internal incident report, risk-management escalation, regulatory reporting (FDA MDR under 21 CFR Part 803 for facilities and manufacturers; state reporting may also apply)
  - *Device failure resulting in death* — additional reporting requirements; possibly Joint Commission sentinel-event reporting; possibly state Department of State Health Services notification
- **The reporting process — parallel tracks that students must understand:**

  **Track 1 — Internal (facility) reporting, every time:**
  - Immediate notification of clinical supervisor or charge
  - Take device out of service and label (from c12B)
  - Factual documentation in the patient record (what happened, when, what was done, clinical status)
  - Separate incident report in the facility's reporting system (most use web-based systems like RL6, Midas, Datix, or similar) — factual, non-blaming, includes time, location, device identifiers, serial number, lot number, users involved, outcome
  - Work order to biomedical engineering / clinical engineering — include enough detail for them to investigate
  - Preservation of the device and all associated disposables — do not discard anything until biomed clears the evidence; take photos if appropriate

  **Track 2 — External (regulatory) reporting, when required:**
  - *FDA Medical Device Report (MDR) — 21 CFR Part 803* — facilities must report to FDA (and manufacturer) deaths caused or contributed to by devices within 10 working days; serious injuries within 10 working days; manufacturers have additional reporting obligations
  - *MedWatch — FDA voluntary program for clinicians* — any clinician can voluntarily report adverse events through MedWatch online, including events that don't rise to mandatory MDR; FDA form 3500
  - *Manufacturer notification* — the device manufacturer is a required recipient of MDRs and also a voluntary recipient of quality feedback; they often have their own reporting portals
  - *State reporting* — Texas DSHS has specific reporting requirements for some adverse events under state law
  - *Joint Commission Sentinel Event* — voluntary but strongly encouraged reporting of events meeting sentinel-event criteria; institution decides whether to report
  - *Coroner or medical examiner* — deaths in specific categories may require reporting under state law
- **What to document — the factual-and-specific rule:**
  - Date, time, exact location (unit, room, bed)
  - Device make, model, serial number, lot number — from the device label or the EHR
  - Software version if applicable
  - User credentials and training status
  - What was observed (symptoms, readings, alarms, error codes — verbatim)
  - Clinical status of the patient (before, during, after)
  - Immediate actions taken
  - Who was notified and when
  - Outcome (device replaced, patient outcome, device preserved or disposed of)
- **What NOT to do — common reporting failures:**
  - Throwing away the device, packaging, disposables, or the patient's chart before biomed sees them
  - Writing subjective blame language in the incident report ("Nurse X was careless" — incident reports are factual)
  - Editing the patient's chart retroactively to match what the device should have done
  - Informal "hallway" reporting without entering the formal system
  - Assuming someone else filed the report
  - Treating the internal report as sufficient when external MDR is required
- **The "just culture" framing:**
  - Reporting systems work only when workers feel safe reporting — the goal is system improvement, not individual punishment (Marx's Just Culture framework)
  - Mandatory reporting of device events is distinct from disciplinary action; students should feel empowered to report and let risk management sort out accountability
- **For students specifically:**
  - Students typically do not file FDA MDRs themselves — but they are expected to recognize the event, escalate correctly, and document factually
  - The supervising clinician or risk manager will route external regulatory reports
  - The student's role: see it → keep the patient safe → escalate → document factually → preserve the device → learn from the follow-up

## Vocabulary

- **Medical Device Report (MDR)** — mandatory FDA report under 21 CFR Part 803 for device events contributing to death or serious injury; due within 10 working days.
- **MedWatch** — FDA's voluntary reporting portal (form 3500) for clinicians, covering adverse events that don't rise to mandatory MDR.
- **MAUDE** — FDA Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience database; public searchable record of reported device events.
- **Incident report** — the facility's internal report (often in RL6, Midas, or Datix) documenting an adverse event or near-miss; factual and non-blaming.
- **Work order (biomed)** — a clinical-engineering request to investigate or repair a device, filed alongside the incident report.
- **Near-miss** — an event that could have caused harm but did not; still required in most internal reporting systems.
- **Sentinel Event** — a TJC-defined serious patient-harm event; voluntary but strongly encouraged reporting with device-related criteria.
- **Device preservation** — keeping the device, packaging, tubing, and disposables intact for biomed investigation; discarding evidence is a reporting failure.
- **Just Culture** — Marx's framework separating blame-free reporting from accountability for reckless behavior; the premise of non-punitive reporting.
- **21 CFR Part 803** — the FDA regulation defining mandatory device-event reporting by user facilities and manufacturers.
- **Serial / lot number** — device identifiers required in reporting; pulled from the device label or EHR record.

## Assessment stub

- Process-diagram practical: sketch the end-to-end reporting flow for a suspected device-related patient injury, from bedside discovery through internal incident report, work order to biomed, risk management escalation, FDA MDR filing, and manufacturer notification; label who does each step and the typical timeframe (hours, days, 10 working days, etc.)
- Triage decision drill: given 8 scenarios (device failure with no harm; device failure with a near-miss; device failure with minor patient harm; device failure with serious injury; device failure with death; glitchy device that was "workarounded" and never reported; device with known manufacturer recall in use; a device that was fine but the user was undertrained), identify which reports are required (internal incident, work order, MDR, MedWatch, Sentinel Event, state report, coroner) and justify each
- Documentation practical: given a short scenario (e.g., an infusion pump ran at 10× the programmed rate during chemotherapy and the error was caught after 200 mL were delivered), write the portion of the incident report and the work order that you would complete; emphasize factual, non-blaming, specific language and include all the elements listed above
- Short response: in 200–300 words, explain why the FDA MDR system relies on facilities and manufacturers self-reporting device failures; describe one consequence of under-reporting (what harm continues when devices fail silently across US healthcare) and name the ethical principle from c9B that reinforces the duty to report

## References

- **FDA Medical Device Reporting regulation (21 CFR Part 803)** — the federal regulation defining mandatory device-event reporting by user facilities and manufacturers.
- **FDA MedWatch (fda.gov/safety/medwatch)** — voluntary reporting portal for clinicians; form 3500.
- **FDA Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience (MAUDE) database** — public searchable database of reported device events; useful for research and for understanding how the reporting system produces public knowledge.
- **Joint Commission Sentinel Event Policy and Procedures** — accreditation-level reporting framework for device-related harm that meets sentinel-event criteria.
- **AHRQ Common Formats for Patient Safety Reporting** — standardized data elements for incident reports adopted across US healthcare.
- **ECRI Institute device-safety reports and the *Health Devices* journal** — independent clearinghouse for device-safety information, often drawing on aggregated MDR data.
- **Texas Department of State Health Services adverse-event reporting rules** — state-level reporting requirements applicable in Texas.
- **David Marx, *Whack-a-Mole* (Just Culture framework)** — foundational framework for the "blame-free reporting that still holds reckless behavior accountable" model underlying US healthcare reporting.
- **James Reason, *Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents*** — Swiss cheese model of system-level failure; frames why individual reports matter to system-level safety.
