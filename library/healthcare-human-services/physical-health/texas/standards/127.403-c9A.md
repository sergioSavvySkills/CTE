## Learning objective

Students will be able to compare published professional codes of ethics across at least two healthcare professions, compare the scope-of-practice definitions those professions operate under, and explain where the codes and scope definitions agree, differ, and overlap.

## Content stub

- **Two documents students must learn to read, not just know about:**
  - *Code of ethics* — a profession's published statement of the values and conduct expected of every member; typically 5–9 provisions with interpretive commentary (examples: ANA *Code of Ethics for Nurses with Interpretive Statements*; AMA *Principles of Medical Ethics*; AAMA *Medical Assistant Code of Ethics*; AAPA *Code of Ethics of the Physician Assistant Profession*; APTA *Code of Ethics for the Physical Therapist*)
  - *Scope of practice* — a legally or organizationally defined statement of what a practitioner is authorized to do, derived from state practice acts and professional-standards documents (examples: Texas Nurse Practice Act; Texas Medical Practice Act; state pharmacy acts; profession-specific scope standards from ANA, AMA, and similar bodies)
- **What codes of ethics typically cover (common provisions across professions):**
  - Primacy of patient interest
  - Preservation of autonomy, privacy, confidentiality
  - Competence and continuing education
  - Integrity, honesty, truth-telling (including error disclosure)
  - Collaboration, respect for colleagues, responsibility for delegated work
  - Advocacy and social responsibility
  - Professional self-governance and discipline
- **What scope of practice covers:**
  - Tasks the practitioner is legally authorized to perform
  - Tasks that require supervision or collaboration
  - Tasks that are *not* within scope regardless of competence
  - Delegation rules — who can delegate to whom under what conditions
- **Comparison framework (apply consistently):**
  - *Scope breadth* — what tasks are in scope? how much clinical decision-making authority?
  - *Supervision requirements* — independent, collaborative, delegated, direct supervision
  - *Ethics-code structure* — number of provisions, style (principles vs. rules), scope of interpretive guidance
  - *Common ethical commitments* — which provisions appear in every code reviewed?
  - *Profession-specific commitments* — which provisions are unique to this role?
  - *Points of friction* — where does one profession's scope intrude on another's? (e.g., NP independent practice vs. physician supervision; pharmacist prescribing authority vs. physician prescribing)
- **Where codes and scope interact:**
  - An act within legal scope can still be unethical (e.g., a legal referral that benefits the referrer financially)
  - An act consistent with the code can be outside legal scope (e.g., an MA performing a task beyond state MA scope even with good clinical intent)
  - Practitioners are bound by both — the more restrictive controls
- **State variation matters:** Texas-specific scope-of-practice laws differ from other states; interstate compacts (Nurse Licensure Compact, Interstate Medical Licensure Compact) narrow but do not eliminate the variation
- **How codes get updated:** professional organizations revise codes periodically through member vote; scope of practice changes through state legislatures, regulatory rulemaking, or court decisions — and often through sustained advocacy (e.g., recent NP full-practice-authority expansions in several states)

## Vocabulary

- **Code of ethics** — a profession's published statement of values and conduct expected of every member; typically 5–9 provisions with interpretive commentary.
- **Scope of practice** — a legally or organizationally defined statement of what a practitioner is authorized to do, derived from practice acts and professional standards. *(also in c1B, c2O)*
- **ANA Code of Ethics for Nurses** — the canonical US nursing ethics code, structured in 9 provisions with interpretive statements.
- **AMA Principles of Medical Ethics** — the canonical US physician ethics code, paired with the opinion-based *Code of Medical Ethics*.
- **Texas Nurse Practice Act** — Texas Occupations Code Chapter 301; the primary legal source defining RN/LVN/APRN scope in Texas.
- **Texas Medical Practice Act** — Texas Occupations Code Chapter 151; the primary legal source defining physician scope in Texas.
- **Delegation rules** — scope provisions defining who can delegate which tasks to whom under what conditions.
- **Full practice authority** — scope-expansion concept (recent NP advocacy) allowing independent practice without physician supervision in participating states.
- **NCSBN Scope Decision-Making Framework** — a structured tool for determining whether a specific task is within a nurse's scope.
- **Points of friction** — areas where one profession's scope intrudes on another's (e.g., NP independent practice vs. physician supervision).

## Assessment stub

- Code comparison matrix: pick two professions (e.g., registered nurse and physician assistant); build a side-by-side matrix of the ANA and AAPA codes across 6 common themes (autonomy, confidentiality, competence, error disclosure, collaboration, social responsibility); identify three points of strong agreement, two points of divergence, and one provision unique to each
- Scope comparison: for the same two professions, compare scope of practice in Texas — list three tasks that are in scope for both, three tasks that are in scope for only one, and one task that is clearly outside scope for both; cite the Texas practice acts
- Conflict analysis: given three short scenarios (a nurse asked to sign off on a task legally in MD scope only; a PA advocating for a prescribing authority that the state does not grant; a pharmacist counseling a patient in a way that exceeds pharmacy scope), identify for each whether the issue is a code violation, a scope violation, or both; explain the reasoning
- Short essay: in 250–350 words, explain why a professional code of ethics and a scope-of-practice statute are distinct documents that nonetheless bind the same practitioner, using a specific example in which they could conflict and how a reasonable practitioner would resolve it

- **American Medical Association — *Principles of Medical Ethics* and *Code of Medical Ethics*** — the canonical US physician code; paired with a comprehensive opinion-based Code of Medical Ethics.
- **American Academy of Physician Associates — *Code of Ethics of the Physician Assistant Profession*** — the PA-specific code.
- **American Association of Medical Assistants — *AAMA Medical Assistant Code of Ethics and Creed*** — the MA-specific code.
- **American Physical Therapy Association — *Code of Ethics for the Physical Therapist*** — a major allied-health example.
- **Texas Nurse Practice Act (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 301)** and **Texas Medical Practice Act (Texas Occupations Code Chapter 151)** — primary legal sources for Texas scope of practice.
- **National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN) Scope of Practice Decision-Making Framework** — structured tool for determining whether a task is within scope.
- **Texas Board of Nursing and Texas Medical Board position statements** — interpretive guidance on specific scope-of-practice questions.
