## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify the four biomechanical principles named in the standard — forces, torque, tension, and elasticity — describe their effects on the human body, and analyze how those principles govern safe lifting, transferring, and positioning of patients.

## Content stub

- **The four principles, defined on human tissue:**
  - *Forces* — gravity, applied force, normal force; every lift is a force problem
  - *Torque* — rotational force around a joint; lifting a load held away from the body multiplies spine torque (the 20-lb load at arm's length puts far more than 20 lb on the lumbar spine)
  - *Tension* — pulling force in muscles, tendons, and ligaments; where cumulative tension damage happens
  - *Elasticity* — the ability of tissues (muscles, tendons, skin, cartilage) to stretch and return; injury happens past the elastic limit
- **Base of support and center of gravity:** feet shoulder-width apart, weight centered over the base, knees bent — the posture that minimizes torque on the spine
- **Lift with the legs, not the back:** bend at hips and knees, keep the load close, never twist while lifting (torque and shear on lumbar discs is where the injury happens) — pivot with the feet instead
- **Patient-handling risk data:** musculoskeletal injuries are the top occupational hazard in healthcare; cumulative strain across hundreds of lifts per shift is the real mechanism, not any one heroic lift
- **Safe-patient-handling programs and equipment:** ceiling lifts, sit-to-stand devices, slide sheets, friction-reducing boards, gait belts — zero-lift / safe-patient-handling policies mandate equipment for lifts above defined weight thresholds
- **Before any lift — the assessment:** patient weight, cognition, ability to assist, fall risk, lines and tubes; how many staff are needed; ask for help rather than lifting alone

## Assessment stub

- Identify-and-match: given the four principles (forces, torque, tension, elasticity), match each to a labeled example on the human body (gravity on a standing patient, torque on the lumbar spine during an offset lift, tension in the Achilles tendon, elasticity of the skin during positioning)
- Video analysis: watch a short clinical-care video; label every instance of each of the four principles and identify at least five body-mechanics errors with proposed corrections
- Skill check: demonstrate a stand-pivot transfer and a proper bed-boost with a peer, scored against a checklist; then calculate the approximate torque on the lumbar spine when lifting a 20-lb load held 6 in vs. 18 in from the body to show why "keep the load close" is a physics rule
- Decision scenario: given 5 patient descriptions (weight, cognition, mobility, lines/tubes), decide which require a lift device, which can be done with two staff, and which a single worker should refuse to lift

## References

- **OSHA Safe Patient Handling guidance** — federal workplace-safety framing for why body-mechanics principles are enforced in healthcare settings (not a specific OSHA standard, but OSHA General Duty Clause plus ergonomic guidance).
- **NIOSH Lifting Equation** — the quantitative tool for evaluating manual lifts that operationalizes the force/torque principles on the lumbar spine.
- **ANA *Safe Patient Handling and Mobility: Interprofessional National Standards*** — the profession's consensus standards on when lift equipment is required and what training looks like.
- **Hall, *Basic Biomechanics*** — undergraduate biomechanics textbook that grounds the four principles (forces, torque, tension, elasticity) in human-movement contexts.
- **Marieb & Hoehn, *Human Anatomy & Physiology*** — muscle, tendon, ligament, and joint anatomy that the biomechanical principles act on.
