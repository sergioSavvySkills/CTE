## Learning objective

Students will be able to explain the four core principles of healthcare ethics, describe the legal and professional framework for patient confidentiality (including HIPAA), and identify the personal, professional, and institutional consequences of a confidentiality breach.

## Content stub

- **Lesson 1 — Ethical principles as the framework for everything else:** the four principles (Beauchamp and Childress) are the standard US healthcare-ethics vocabulary that every role in the building is expected to understand
  - *Autonomy* — the patient's right to make decisions about their own body and care, including unwise ones; informed consent and informed refusal are the operational expressions
  - *Beneficence* — the duty to act in the patient's best interest; balancing benefit against burden, cost, and the patient's own goals
  - *Non-maleficence* — "first, do no harm" — weighing risks of action against risks of inaction; iatrogenic harm is a real category
  - *Justice* — fair allocation of limited resources; access, bias, and social determinants are justice issues, not separate topics
- **How the principles get applied:**
  - Most ethical decisions involve a conflict between two or more principles (autonomy vs. beneficence in treatment refusal; beneficence vs. justice in resource allocation)
  - There is rarely a single correct answer — there is a defensible, documented one that can be explained to the patient, family, and colleagues
  - Institutional ethics committees exist to help work through hard cases
- **Lesson 2 — Confidentiality as the operational ethical duty every student will face:**
  - *What confidentiality is* — the obligation not to share protected information with people who have no need to know
  - *Grounding in ethics and law* — rooted in autonomy (the patient chose to share); operationalized by HIPAA (Privacy, Security, Breach Notification rules), state privacy statutes, and professional codes
- **What HIPAA protects:**
  - *Protected Health Information (PHI)* — any identifiable health information created or held by a covered entity; 18 identifiers define "identifiable" (name, DOB, MRN, address, phone, email, photographs, biometrics, etc.)
  - *Covered entities and business associates* — hospitals, providers, health plans, clearinghouses, and their contracted vendors
  - *Minimum necessary rule* — share only what is needed for the task; looking up a celebrity's chart "just to see" is a violation even if nothing is shared further
  - *Permitted uses* — treatment, payment, healthcare operations, and specific public-health exceptions
- **Common breaches students will actually see or be tempted by:**
  - Talking about patients in elevators, cafeterias, or on personal phones
  - Social-media posts that reveal PHI (even without a name — a specific case on a specific unit can identify a patient)
  - Looking up friends', neighbors', or family members' charts without a treatment relationship
  - Sharing EHR passwords or leaving screens unlocked
  - Texting PHI on personal phones
  - Taking photos in patient-care areas
- **Consequences of a breach (the "including" clause of the TEKS):**
  - *For the patient* — loss of privacy, reputational damage, identity theft, stigma, loss of trust in the system; in some cases physical safety (e.g., domestic-violence survivors)
  - *For the breacher* — termination from the job; revocation or suspension of license, certification, or registration; civil damages; criminal charges under HIPAA (fines from $100–$250,000, imprisonment up to 10 years for knowing misuse for personal gain); being sued personally
  - *For the institution* — HHS Office for Civil Rights investigation and settlement (penalties historically from ~$25,000 to tens of millions); corrective action plans; reputational harm; loss of patient volume
  - *For the team* — shared workload during the investigation, loss of trust within the team, chilling effect on appropriate information sharing
- **Special-population protections:** mental-health (42 CFR Part 2 for substance-use treatment), HIV status in some states, reproductive-health information, minors' health records (state law varies), genetic information (GINA)

## Assessment stub

- Principles application: given 5 short ethical scenarios (a patient refuses a life-saving transfusion citing religious belief; a family asks clinicians not to tell the patient her diagnosis; a scarce ICU bed must go to one of two patients; a colleague asks you to round on their family member; a pediatric patient asks you to keep her pregnancy from her parents), identify which principles are in tension and propose a defensible resolution; cite at least one published ethics code
- PHI classification drill: given 20 data elements (name, shoe size, MRN, license plate, genetic test result, a photograph of a patient's tattoo, a nurse's pager number, a room number combined with diagnosis), mark each PHI or not PHI and explain why
- Scenario judgment — confidentiality: given 10 workplace situations, identify which are HIPAA violations, which are permitted uses under treatment/payment/operations, and which are ambiguous and require consultation; cite the rule or exception
- Consequences matching: given 8 documented HIPAA-breach case summaries (including real enforcement actions from HHS OCR), identify the personal, professional, and institutional consequences in each and explain which could have been prevented with which specific safeguard
- Short response: in 200–300 words, explain what you would say to a coworker who asks you to pull up their neighbor's chart; name the ethical principle at stake, the HIPAA rule, and the specific consequences that could follow for you personally

## References

- **Beauchamp and Childress, *Principles of Biomedical Ethics*** — the foundational text for the four-principles framework used throughout US healthcare ethics education.
- **HIPAA Privacy Rule, Security Rule, and Breach Notification Rule (45 CFR Parts 160 and 164)** — the federal regulations that operationalize confidentiality; enforced by HHS Office for Civil Rights.
- **HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) HIPAA enforcement case summaries** — publicly available resolution agreements and corrective action plans; concrete evidence of real-world consequences.
- **Texas Medical Records Privacy Act (Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 181)** — state-level privacy statute that in some respects exceeds HIPAA; binding in Texas.
- **42 CFR Part 2** — heightened confidentiality protections for substance-use-disorder treatment records.
- **Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 2008)** — federal protection against genetic-information discrimination in employment and health insurance.
- **ANA *Code of Ethics for Nurses*, Provision 3** and **AMA *Principles of Medical Ethics*, Principle IV** — profession-specific confidentiality duties that exceed the legal minimum.
- **Joint Commission standards on information management and patient rights** — accreditation-level operational requirements.
