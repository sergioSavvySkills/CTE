## Learning objective

Students will be able to describe the economic scale of the US health services sector — its share of GDP, its workforce footprint, its major cost drivers, and its role as a local and national economic engine.

## Content stub

- **Scale of the sector:** healthcare is roughly 17–18% of US GDP (CMS National Health Expenditure data) — the largest single sector in the US economy; employs more than 20 million US workers (BLS *Occupational Outlook Handbook*) and is the top private-sector employer in many states and counties
- **Where the money goes:** hospital care (~31%), physician and clinical services (~20%), prescription drugs (~9%), long-term care, home health, dental, public health, administration
- **Where the money comes from:** private insurance (~32%), Medicare (~21%), Medicaid (~18%), out-of-pocket (~10%), employer self-insurance, VA/TRICARE, public health
- **Cost drivers:** aging population, chronic disease burden, new technology and drug prices, administrative overhead, prices (not utilization) explaining most of the US cost gap with peer nations
- **Local economic impact:** hospitals and clinics generate jobs, tax revenue, real estate demand, and supply-chain contracts; rural hospital closures have measurable downstream economic effects beyond healthcare
- **Cost-containment levers:** Medicare payment reform, value-based care, bundled payments, Accountable Care Organizations, site-of-service regulation, price transparency rules
- **Why it matters to a high-school health-science student:** the sector's size creates the job opportunities the rest of this course maps; its cost pressure drives which roles grow and which shrink

## Assessment stub

- Chart analysis: given current CMS National Health Expenditure charts (% of GDP over time, spending by category, spending by payer), answer 6 targeted questions about trends and magnitudes
- Local inquiry: identify the three largest healthcare employers in your county or metro area and summarize (1) total employment, (2) annual operating budget if public, (3) one recent expansion or closure and its economic effect
- Short response: in 150–200 words, explain why the US spends nearly twice as much per capita on healthcare as peer OECD nations without better aggregate outcomes — cite at least two specific drivers

## References

- **CMS National Health Expenditure Accounts (NHEA)** — the federal government's authoritative data source for US health spending by category, payer, and year.
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics *Occupational Outlook Handbook* and *Employment Projections*** — authoritative employment data by healthcare occupation, including projected growth rates.
- **KFF (Kaiser Family Foundation) Health Costs and Coverage briefs** — accessible summaries of US health-spending data for students and educators.
- **OECD Health Statistics** — cross-national comparisons that contextualize US spending relative to peer nations.
- **Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker** — free, regularly updated charts on US health spending, outcomes, and pricing.
