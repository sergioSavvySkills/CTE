## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify situations in healthcare where patient autonomy is central — including informed consent, informed refusal, capacity decisions, advance directives, and surrogate decision-making — and recognize the signals that autonomy is being honored or overridden.

## Content stub

- **Autonomy, restated from c9B:** the patient's right to make decisions about their own body and care, including decisions clinicians or family members would choose differently; grounded in ethics and codified in US law and professional codes
- **Operational expressions of autonomy every student will see:**
  - *Informed consent* — the patient agrees to a procedure or treatment with capacity, adequate disclosure (diagnosis, proposed treatment, risks, benefits, alternatives including no treatment), understanding, and voluntariness; documented in the record
  - *Informed refusal* — the patient declines a proposed treatment after the same elements; as valid as consent (e.g., refusing a recommended surgery, declining chemotherapy, leaving against medical advice / AMA discharge)
  - *Advance directives* — the patient's autonomy extends into the future via living will (directive to physicians), medical power of attorney, and out-of-hospital DNR in Texas (Health and Safety Code Chapter 166)
  - *Capacity assessment* — a clinician-determined judgment about whether this patient, for this decision, at this moment, has decision-making capacity; capacity can fluctuate (delirium, intoxication, sedation)
  - *Surrogate decision-making* — when the patient lacks capacity, a legally designated surrogate (POA, court-appointed guardian, or statutory hierarchy under Texas law) makes decisions using substituted judgment (what the patient would have wanted) or best-interest standard
- **Key distinctions students should recognize:**
  - *Capacity vs. competence* — capacity is a clinical judgment per decision; competence is a legal status set by a court
  - *Autonomy vs. agreement* — the patient exercising autonomy may choose against clinical advice; the clinician's job is to ensure the choice is informed and voluntary, not to get the patient to agree
  - *Patient autonomy vs. family preferences* — the patient's wishes control, even when family disagrees (with narrow exceptions like public-health threats and genuine incapacity)
- **Signals that autonomy is being honored:**
  - Consent or refusal is documented with all five elements (capacity, disclosure, understanding, voluntariness, documentation)
  - Interpreter present for limited-English-proficient patients; accessible materials for patients with disabilities
  - Patient answered open-ended questions, not just signed a form
  - Decision-maker is the patient, not the family or the clinician
- **Signals that autonomy is being violated or at risk:**
  - Consent form signed under sedation or after premedication
  - Consent obtained without an interpreter despite language barrier
  - Family members answering on behalf of a patient who has capacity
  - Coercion — subtle ("you'll die if you don't") or direct
  - Pro-forma signatures with no actual discussion
  - Refusal not documented; "AMA" used as a punishment label
- **Special-population autonomy issues:**
  - *Minors* — limited autonomy that expands with age; emancipated-minor and mature-minor doctrines; some health categories (reproductive, mental-health, substance-use) have heightened minor autonomy in most states
  - *Adolescents* — Texas and other states have specific confidentiality and consent rules for reproductive, mental-health, and substance-use care
  - *Patients with intellectual or cognitive disabilities* — supported decision-making models that preserve maximum autonomy rather than default to full surrogacy
  - *Patients with psychiatric illness* — capacity for treatment decisions may be retained even when involuntary commitment for safety is justified; the two are separable
- **When autonomy is lawfully overridden:**
  - Genuine incapacity — surrogate decides
  - Emergency without a surrogate reachable — implied consent for life-saving treatment
  - Public-health emergencies — reportable communicable disease, isolation/quarantine authority
  - Court orders — psychiatric commitment, mandated treatment in narrow categories

## Assessment stub

- Situation-identification drill: given 15 short clinical situations (a patient signs a consent form after receiving IV sedation; a JW patient declines blood products before a scheduled surgery; a 17-year-old requests confidential STI testing; a family asks staff not to tell Grandma her cancer diagnosis; a patient with mild dementia wants to refuse dialysis; an unconscious trauma patient with no known family; a patient on a 5150 hold refusing psychiatric medication), identify which element of autonomy is central, whether autonomy is being honored, and what the next appropriate step is
- Consent audit: given 5 consent-form scenarios, identify which elements (capacity, disclosure, understanding, voluntariness, documentation) are missing and describe what must happen before the procedure may proceed
- Surrogate-hierarchy application: given a Texas-specific scenario where a patient has lost capacity and has no advance directive, apply the statutory surrogate hierarchy under Texas law; identify the correct decision-maker and explain one reason a clinician might need to request ethics-committee or court consultation
- Short response: in 150–200 words, explain the difference between capacity and competence using a specific example of a patient whose capacity changes over a hospital stay (e.g., delirium from infection that resolves with treatment); identify who decides which and at what points

## References

- **Beauchamp and Childress, *Principles of Biomedical Ethics*** — the autonomy framework underlying US consent law and clinical ethics.
- **Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 166 — Advance Directives** — Texas statute on directives to physicians, medical power of attorney, and out-of-hospital DNR.
- **Texas Health and Safety Code §313 — Consent to Medical Treatment Act** — Texas statutory surrogate-decision-making hierarchy for adults without capacity.
- **Joint Commission patient-rights standards (especially the Provision of Care and Rights and Responsibilities of the Individual chapters)** — accreditation-level consent, refusal, and advance-directive requirements.
- **CMS Conditions of Participation §482.13 (Patient's Rights)** — federal Medicare/Medicaid requirements for consent and patient rights in certified facilities.
- **AMA *Code of Medical Ethics* — Chapter 2 (Consent, Communication, and Decision Making)** — profession-specific autonomy guidance.
- **ANA *Code of Ethics for Nurses*, Provision 1 (respect for human dignity)** — nursing-profession autonomy framework.
- **American Psychiatric Association — guidelines on capacity assessment** — operational guidance for the capacity-vs-competence distinction.
- **Texas POLST / Out-of-Hospital DNR forms and Texas Directive to Physicians** — operational documents students will see in practice.
