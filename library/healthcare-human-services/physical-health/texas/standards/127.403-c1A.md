## Learning objective

Students will be able to express ideas in a clear, concise, and effective manner using speaking, writing, and visual formats appropriate to a health science workplace audience.

## Content stub

- **Attributes of clear communication:** audience awareness, purpose, structure, plain language, tone
- **Conciseness:** cut filler, lead with the main point, respect the reader's or listener's time
- **Effectiveness criteria:** the message is received, understood, and acted on — confirm with read-back or paraphrase
- **Precise language:** specific verbs over vague ones (administered vs. gave), measurable values over approximations (2 cm vs. "small"), no jargon without definition
- **Active voice for clarity:** active in most workplace communication; passive only when emphasizing the action over the actor
- **Modes covered by this standard:** spoken (handoff reports, huddles), written (chart notes, emails, incident documentation), visual (signage, patient education aids)
- **Workplace stakes:** miscommunication in healthcare affects patient safety, continuity of care, and the legal record

## Vocabulary

- **Audience awareness** — shaping word choice, detail level, and tone for the specific reader or listener (peer clinician vs. patient vs. family vs. supervisor).
- **Plain language** — everyday words for everyday meanings; the health-literacy writing standard that makes a message understandable on first reading.
- **Precise language** — specific, measurable terms over vague ones ("administered 2 mg" not "gave a little"); eliminates clinical ambiguity. *(also in c2F)*
- **Active voice** — sentence structure where the subject performs the action ("the nurse gave the medication"); default for workplace communication because it names the actor and is shorter.
- **Passive voice** — sentence structure where the subject receives the action ("the medication was given"); appropriate only when the action matters more than the actor.
- **Read-back / check-back** — the listener repeats a spoken message back to the sender to confirm accurate receipt; TeamSTEPPS closed-loop communication.
- **SBAR** — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation; structured handoff format used in clinical transitions.
- **Handoff** — structured transfer of patient information and responsibility from one clinician to another, typically at shift change or care-setting transition. *(also in c1B)*
- **"Do Not Use" list** — ISMP / Joint Commission list of abbreviations (e.g., "U" for units, "QD") prohibited in clinical documentation because they cause medication errors. *(also in c2F, c3D)*
- **Teach-back** — patient-education technique where the patient explains instructions back in their own words to confirm understanding. *(also in c3B)*

## Assessment stub

- Rewrite a rambling patient-care note into a clear, concise entry; score against a rubric (audience, brevity, completeness, precision)
- Deliver a 60-second verbal handoff to a peer; peer paraphrases back to demonstrate the message was received accurately
- Given four sample workplace communications for the same scenario, identify which best fits a stated audience and purpose and justify the choice

## Activities

- **SBAR Handoff Builder** — drag 40 unsorted patient facts into Situation / Background / Assessment / Recommendation slots under a 90-second timer. End-of-round reveals the gold placement and the rubric score.
  → *application, procedural fluency*

- **Rewrite This Chart Note** — a rambling 6-sentence progress note appears; student compresses it to ≤3 clean sentences against a rubric (audience, brevity, precision). The model answer unlocks only after submit, so students have to commit before comparing.
  → *communication under pressure, metacognition*

- **Active-vs-Passive Spotter** — an incident report paragraph appears; every passive-voice sentence is a click target. Student clicks all passives in 30 seconds, then rewrites the top three in active voice. Bonus for correctly *not* clicking the two sentences where passive is justified ("the medication was administered at 14:00").
  → *pattern recognition, metacognition*

## References

- **SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation)** — structured handoff format; Institute for Healthcare Improvement and widely adopted by Joint Commission–accredited organizations. Frames the "60-second verbal handoff" assessment.
- **TeamSTEPPS 3.0 (AHRQ)** — teamwork and communication curriculum; defines "check-back" (closed-loop communication) as a standard tool for confirming a message was received and understood. Frames the read-back / paraphrase practice.
- **Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals** — NPSG.02.03.01 (critical-test-results read-back) and sentinel-event root-cause data that consistently identify communication failures as a leading contributor to patient harm.
- **AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit** and **CDC Plain Language guidelines** — plain-language writing standards for patient-facing and workplace health communication.
- **ISMP / Joint Commission "Do Not Use" list** — precision-language guidance for clinical documentation; supports the "measurable values over approximations, no jargon without definition" bullet.

**Instructions to student:** You will see a patient case with 40 facts drawn from the chart. Drag each fact into the correct SBAR slot — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — before the 90-second timer expires. Score = correct placements. 32 or more earns mastery. You can skip any case; the next one is different.

**Teacher notes:** Run 3 cases per student per class period. After the activity, spend 5 minutes on a whole-class debrief looking at the teacher-view heatmap — focus on the 2–3 facts most commonly misplaced. Ambiguous cases (a lab value often belongs in Background *or* Assessment depending on whether it triggered a decision) are the most productive to discuss; they're where the SBAR framework stops being mechanical and starts requiring clinical judgment.
