## Learning objective

Students will be able to research and compare health practices used by different cultures and societies to address a shared health problem — identifying what each practice does, the evidence for or against it, and how it relates to Western biomedical approaches.

## Content stub

- **Two verbs, two halves of the lesson:**
  - *Research* — locate credible sources on the practice, across both biomedical and ethnographic literatures; record citations; distinguish description from judgment
  - *Compare* — use a consistent framework across cultures/societies so comparisons are fair (problem addressed, practice, mechanism, evidence base, accessibility, integration with biomedicine)
- **Framing to use, not "exotic practices from elsewhere":** every culture has practices for common health problems; compare peer-to-peer, not "us vs. them"
- **Examples of comparable health problems worth comparing across cultures:**
  - *Managing childbirth and postpartum recovery* — US biomedical model vs. midwifery models used in Netherlands, UK, and many parts of the Global South; doulas in BIPOC US communities; postpartum confinement ("la cuarentena," zuo yuezi) traditions
  - *Managing pain* — opioids in US biomedicine vs. acupuncture in Traditional Chinese Medicine vs. Ayurvedic herbal preparations vs. CBT and mindfulness
  - *End-of-life care* — US hospice vs. cultural variations in disclosure, home death, family roles, spiritual rites
  - *Mental-health care* — US psychiatry and pharmacotherapy vs. community/family-based approaches in many cultures, traditional healers, religious counseling
  - *Infectious-disease response* — centralized biomedical surveillance (CDC) vs. community-based models (Ebola response with local burial practices, measles response with faith-community outreach)
- **Evidence hierarchy without dismissing tradition:** some traditional practices have strong evidence (acupuncture for certain pain conditions per NIH NCCIH; midwifery for low-risk deliveries per Cochrane reviews); others have weak or no evidence but meaningful cultural value; distinguish the two honestly
- **Credible sources for cross-cultural health research:** WHO Traditional Medicine Programme, NIH National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health (NCCIH), peer-reviewed medical-anthropology journals (e.g., *Social Science & Medicine*, *Culture, Medicine, and Psychiatry*), Cochrane Reviews, ethnographic literature
- **Common pitfalls to avoid:** orientalism (treating non-Western practices as exotic or primitive), false equivalence (treating all practices as equally evidenced), appropriation (using practices stripped of their cultural context), and judging non-Western practices by Western research methods as the only valid standard

## Vocabulary

- **Biomedical model** — the dominant US clinical framework based on laboratory science and disease categories; the comparator for cross-cultural research in this standard.
- **Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)** — culturally grounded practice including acupuncture and herbal preparations; compared with US biomedical pain management.
- **Ayurveda** — South Asian traditional medical system using herbal preparations and lifestyle practices; an example of cross-cultural comparison.
- **Midwifery model of care** — low-intervention, relationship-centered childbirth approach common in the Netherlands, UK, and many parts of the Global South.
- **Postpartum confinement** — culturally grounded recovery practices (la cuarentena, zuo yuezi) that vary meaningfully across cultures.
- **Doula** — trained non-clinical childbirth support role expanded in BIPOC US communities partly in response to maternal-mortality disparities.
- **NCCIH (National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health)** — the NIH center that publishes authoritative US evidence reviews on traditional and complementary practices.
- **Cochrane Review** — systematic-review format that frequently evaluates practices originating outside Western biomedicine.
- **Medical anthropology** — the ethnographic scholarly field (Kleinman, *Social Science & Medicine*) that frames how cultures explain and respond to illness.
- **Cultural appropriation** — using a practice stripped of its cultural context; a research pitfall this standard asks students to avoid.

## Assessment stub

- Comparative research paper: pick one health problem (childbirth, chronic pain, bereavement, depression, hypertension) and three cultures/societies with distinct approaches; produce a 2-page comparative paper with a consistent framework (problem, practice, mechanism, evidence, accessibility, integration) and at least five cited sources
- Matrix product: build a comparison matrix (3 cultures × 6 framework dimensions) for the same problem; present findings to the class in 3 minutes
- Evaluation short-answer: for one traditional practice covered in class, summarize the current biomedical evidence (strong, mixed, weak, absent) and explain how a culturally humble clinician should respond when a patient tells them they use it

## References

- **WHO Traditional Medicine Strategy and Traditional Medicine Programme** — global public-health framework for integrating traditional, complementary, and integrative medicine with biomedical systems.
- **NIH National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health (NCCIH)** — authoritative US source for evidence reviews on traditional and complementary practices (acupuncture, herbal medicine, mind-body practices, etc.).
- **Cochrane Reviews** — systematic reviews that frequently evaluate practices originating outside Western biomedicine (midwifery care, acupuncture, yoga, mindfulness).
- **Kleinman, *Patients and Healers in the Context of Culture* and *The Illness Narratives*** — foundational medical-anthropology references that frame how cultures explain and respond to illness.
- **HHS Office of Minority Health CLAS Standards** (see c2P) — structural requirements for culturally responsive care that operationalize this standard's knowledge into clinical practice.
- **Peer-reviewed journals:** *Social Science & Medicine*, *Culture, Medicine, and Psychiatry*, *Journal of Transcultural Nursing* — credible sources for comparative cross-cultural health research.
