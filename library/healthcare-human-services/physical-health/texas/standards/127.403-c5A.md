## Learning objective

Students will be able to locate credible sources of healthcare career and employment information, evaluate each source for currency and authority, and interpret occupational data — duties, education, credentials, wages, outlook — to build an informed picture of a specific role.

## Content stub

- **Three verbs as a sequence:** *locate* (find the right sources) → *evaluate* (are they current, authoritative, unbiased?) → *interpret* (read and apply what they say)
- **Primary credible sources for US healthcare career information:**
  - *US Bureau of Labor Statistics* Occupational Outlook Handbook (bls.gov/ooh) — federal, updated annually, covers every major healthcare occupation: duties, typical education, licensure, median wage, 10-year employment projection
  - *O*NET OnLine* (onetonline.org) — US Department of Labor occupational database; skills, knowledge, work activities, abilities, work context
  - *CareerOneStop* (careeronestop.org) — US Department of Labor job-search and career-exploration portal
  - *Professional-organization career pages* — ANA for nursing, AMA for medicine, AAPA for PAs, ASRT for radiologic technologists, ASCP for laboratory science
  - *State workforce data* — Texas Workforce Commission for Texas-specific wage, demand, and training-provider data
- **Sources to treat cautiously:** salary-aggregator sites (Glassdoor, Indeed, Salary.com) — user-reported data, wide variation, not authoritative but useful for ranges and recent trends; single-school or single-employer sites — may be current but incentivized
- **What to evaluate when assessing a source:**
  - *Currency* — wage and projection data drift year to year; look for the publication date
  - *Authority* — who published it and why (federal agency vs. vendor vs. employer)
  - *Scope* — national vs. state vs. local; healthcare wages vary widely by region
  - *Bias* — is the source trying to recruit you, sell you training, or inform you?
- **Interpreting wage data correctly:** median vs. mean; entry-level vs. mid-career; geographic variation (RN wages in rural Texas vs. California Bay Area differ by >50%); total compensation vs. hourly wage
- **Interpreting employment projections:** "much faster than average" is BLS language for specific growth rates; read the methodology, not just the adjective
- **Interpreting education and credential requirements:** typical entry-level degree (CNA certificate vs. BSN vs. MD); state licensure requirements (which vary); continuing education and specialty certifications

## Assessment stub

- Source location: given a list of 6 credible source categories, identify and correctly navigate to at least 4 of them live; bookmark each
- Evaluation short answer: compare two sources of wage data for the same role (e.g., BLS OOH vs. Glassdoor for RN in Texas); identify three specific differences and explain which source is more authoritative for what purpose
- Role profile: produce a one-page career profile for a specific healthcare role of your choosing, drawn from at least three credible sources — includes duties, typical education, licensure, median wage (Texas-specific if available), 10-year outlook, and one credential that unlocks advancement; cite every data point
- Interpretation check: given three short BLS excerpts with employment projections for different roles, correctly identify which is growing fastest, which is shrinking, and explain in one sentence what is driving each

## References

- **US Bureau of Labor Statistics *Occupational Outlook Handbook*** (bls.gov/ooh) — the federal source of truth for occupational duties, education, wages, and projections; updated annually.
- **O*NET OnLine** (onetonline.org) — US Department of Labor Employment and Training Administration; detailed skills, knowledge, and work-activity descriptors.
- **CareerOneStop** (careeronestop.org) — US Department of Labor career-exploration portal.
- **Texas Workforce Commission — Labor Market Information** (twc.texas.gov/lmi) — Texas-specific wage, demand, and training-provider data.
- **Professional-organization career pages** — ANA, AMA, AAPA, APTA, ASRT, ASCP, AAMA, and equivalents for each healthcare profession.
- **ACRL / CRAAP evaluation framework** (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) — standard criteria for evaluating information sources.
