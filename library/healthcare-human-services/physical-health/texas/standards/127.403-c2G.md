## Learning objective

Students will be able to describe homeostasis as the body's regulated steady state and explain the biological and chemical feedback loops — at the level of sensor, control center, and effector — that maintain it.

## Content stub

- **Homeostasis defined:** the dynamic equilibrium of internal conditions (body temperature, blood pH, blood glucose, fluid and electrolyte balance) maintained despite changing external conditions
- **Negative feedback loops — the dominant mechanism:** sensor detects change → control center compares to setpoint → effectors restore. Thermoregulation (hypothalamus, shivering vs. sweating) and blood glucose regulation (pancreas, insulin vs. glucagon) are the canonical examples
- **Positive feedback loops — rarer, amplifying:** oxytocin release during childbirth, platelet aggregation during clotting; these run until an endpoint event stops them
- **Key chemical processes:** pH buffering by the bicarbonate / carbonic-acid system, oxygen transport by hemoglobin, glucose metabolism by insulin and glucagon, water balance by ADH (antidiuretic hormone)
- **When homeostasis fails:** fever (pyrogens reset the thermoregulation setpoint), type 1 diabetes (insulin-producing cells destroyed), dehydration, metabolic acidosis or alkalosis — disease states begin where homeostasis breaks
- **Every body system contributes:** nervous system senses and signals, endocrine system adjusts chemistry, circulatory transports, respiratory and renal systems excrete — homeostasis is a system-of-systems property, not a single organ's job

## Vocabulary

- **Homeostasis** — the body's dynamic equilibrium of internal conditions (temperature, pH, glucose, fluids) maintained despite changing external conditions.
- **Negative feedback loop** — the dominant homeostatic mechanism: sensor detects change → control center compares to setpoint → effectors restore.
- **Positive feedback loop** — an amplifying mechanism (oxytocin in labor, platelet aggregation in clotting) that runs until an endpoint event stops it.
- **Setpoint** — the target value a control center (e.g., the hypothalamus for temperature) compares a sensor reading against.
- **Effector** — the structure that acts to restore homeostasis after a control center signals (shivering, sweating, insulin release).
- **Bicarbonate buffer system** — the carbonic-acid / bicarbonate chemical process that maintains blood pH.
- **Insulin and glucagon** — the pancreatic hormones that raise and lower blood glucose in the homeostatic loop regulating metabolism.
- **ADH (antidiuretic hormone)** — the hormone that regulates water balance by controlling renal water reabsorption.
- **Pyrogen** — a fever-inducing substance that resets the hypothalamic temperature setpoint upward.

## Assessment stub

- Diagram: sketch a negative feedback loop for body-temperature regulation; correctly label sensor, control center (hypothalamus), effectors (vasoconstriction, shivering, sweating), and setpoint
- Classify: given 10 biological events (e.g., sweating when hot, oxytocin release in labor, insulin release after a meal), identify each as negative feedback, positive feedback, or neither
- Failure-mode scenario: a patient has uncontrolled type 1 diabetes. Explain which homeostatic loop has failed, what is breaking down chemically (glucose, ketones, pH), and what the visible signs (polyuria, polydipsia, Kussmaul respirations) tell a clinician about the failing loop

## Activities

- **Homeostasis Gremlin** — dials on the left let the student sabotage the body: block sweating, block shivering, dump 50 g of glucose, clamp the renal artery. Live trend lines show core temp, blood pH, blood glucose, and BP as the body compensates, then fails. Score = how many dials turned before decompensation and *which* failure mode the student triggered.
  → *systems thinking, analysis*

- **Negative-Feedback Loop Debugger** — a feedback loop is broken (thermoreceptor disabled, insulin receptor blocked, hypothalamic setpoint shifted). Student runs experiments on the simulated body and must identify which signal is missing — pure black-box reasoning, the loop isn't visible, only inferable from outputs.
  → *systems thinking, metacognition*

- **Heat-Loss Race** — split-screen simulation: two bodies cool in different environments (cold room, windy beach, immersion in cold water). Student sets environmental variables and watches which body wins the cooling race, then identifies the dominant heat-loss mechanism (conduction, convection, radiation, evaporation) for each scenario.
  → *analysis, application*
