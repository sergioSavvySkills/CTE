## Learning objective

Students will be able to describe homeostasis as the body's regulated steady state and explain the biological and chemical feedback loops — at the level of sensor, control center, and effector — that maintain it.

## Content stub

- **Homeostasis defined:** the dynamic equilibrium of internal conditions (body temperature, blood pH, blood glucose, fluid and electrolyte balance) maintained despite changing external conditions
- **Negative feedback loops — the dominant mechanism:** sensor detects change → control center compares to setpoint → effectors restore. Thermoregulation (hypothalamus, shivering vs. sweating) and blood glucose regulation (pancreas, insulin vs. glucagon) are the canonical examples
- **Positive feedback loops — rarer, amplifying:** oxytocin release during childbirth, platelet aggregation during clotting; these run until an endpoint event stops them
- **Key chemical processes:** pH buffering by the bicarbonate / carbonic-acid system, oxygen transport by hemoglobin, glucose metabolism by insulin and glucagon, water balance by ADH (antidiuretic hormone)
- **When homeostasis fails:** fever (pyrogens reset the thermoregulation setpoint), type 1 diabetes (insulin-producing cells destroyed), dehydration, metabolic acidosis or alkalosis — disease states begin where homeostasis breaks
- **Every body system contributes:** nervous system senses and signals, endocrine system adjusts chemistry, circulatory transports, respiratory and renal systems excrete — homeostasis is a system-of-systems property, not a single organ's job

## Assessment stub

- Diagram: sketch a negative feedback loop for body-temperature regulation; correctly label sensor, control center (hypothalamus), effectors (vasoconstriction, shivering, sweating), and setpoint
- Classify: given 10 biological events (e.g., sweating when hot, oxytocin release in labor, insulin release after a meal), identify each as negative feedback, positive feedback, or neither
- Failure-mode scenario: a patient has uncontrolled type 1 diabetes. Explain which homeostatic loop has failed, what is breaking down chemically (glucose, ketones, pH), and what the visible signs (polyuria, polydipsia, Kussmaul respirations) tell a clinician about the failing loop

## References

- **Marieb & Hoehn, *Human Anatomy & Physiology*** — the standard HS/undergraduate A&P reference for homeostasis, feedback loops, and body-system integration.
- **Guyton & Hall, *Textbook of Medical Physiology*** — authoritative source on regulatory mechanisms for deeper instructor background.
- **NIH / National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK) educational materials on blood-glucose regulation and diabetes** — freely available patient-education material grounded in the homeostasis framework.
- **CDC educational resources on fever and thermoregulation** — public-health–facing explanations of the temperature-regulation feedback loop.
