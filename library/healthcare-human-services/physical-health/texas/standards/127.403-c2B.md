## Learning objective

Students will be able to read clinical tables, charts, and graphs — including lab reference ranges, growth percentile curves, and trend lines — and use the data to recommend or justify a health-related decision.

## Content stub

- **Clinical tables students will actually see:** lab reference-range panels (CBC, CMP), drug dosing tables (mg/kg, renal-adjusted), BMI classification tables, vital-sign normal ranges by age
- **Chart and graph types common in healthcare:**
  - Trend lines — glucose over 24 h, blood pressure series, weight over months
  - Growth percentile curves — CDC / WHO pediatric growth charts
  - Bar charts — outcomes by site, infection rates by unit
  - Scatterplots — dose vs. response, BP vs. age
  - Survival / Kaplan-Meier curves (introduce, don't master)
  - Epidemiology curves — case counts over time during an outbreak
- **Read the axes before the curve:** always check units, scale (linear vs. log), starting value (does the y-axis start at zero?), and time window — a graph can mislead without ever being wrong
- **Apply, not just read:** the standard says "provide solutions" — pair every data read with a decision: normal or abnormal? trending toward or away from target? needs escalation? counsel the patient?
- **Public-health data products:** CDC surveillance graphs, immunization coverage maps, outbreak epi curves — same reading skills, different stakes
- **Common misreads:** truncated y-axis exaggerating a change, cherry-picked date range, confusing absolute vs. relative risk, missing the units on the axis entirely

## Vocabulary

- **Reference range** — the range of values considered normal for a lab test in a defined population; the standard against which a result is flagged normal or abnormal.
- **Growth percentile curve** — a CDC or WHO chart that plots a child's height, weight, or head circumference against same-age peers; "crossing percentiles" is a clinical signal.
- **Trend line** — a plot of a value (glucose, BP, weight) over time used to see whether the patient is moving toward or away from a target.
- **Epidemiology (epi) curve** — a bar or line chart of case counts over time during an outbreak; shape suggests whether a source is point, continuous, or propagated.
- **Truncated y-axis** — a y-axis that does not start at zero; visually exaggerates small changes and is a common way a graph misleads without being technically wrong.
- **Log scale** — an axis where equal distances represent multiplicative (not additive) changes; appropriate for data spanning many orders of magnitude but easy to misread.
- **BMI (body mass index)** — weight-for-height index (kg/m²) used with a classification table to categorize adults and counsel accordingly.
- **Absolute vs. relative risk** — absolute = the actual probability of an outcome; relative = the ratio between groups; headlines often quote relative and hide small absolute differences.
- **Kaplan-Meier / survival curve** — a stepwise plot of the proportion of a group still free of an event over time; introduced at survey level for chart literacy.

## Assessment stub

- Interpretation set: given three clinical charts (a pediatric growth-percentile plot, a 24-hour glucose trend, and a medication-concentration curve), answer targeted questions — "Is this child tracking their growth curve or crossing percentiles?"; "When does this patient's glucose first exceed target?"; "When is the drug's peak concentration and when is the next dose due?"
- Spot-the-misleading-axis: two sample graphs with truncated or nonlinear axes; students explain what the graph seems to show vs. what the data actually supports, and redraw one honestly
- Scenario: given a BMI table and a patient's height and weight, classify the patient's BMI category and state two counseling implications that follow from the classification

## Activities

- **Chart Detective** — realistic published chart (infection rates, ED wait times, vaccine coverage by county). Four questions appear; student answers by clicking data points. Distractors include misleading axes, non-zero baselines, and cherry-picked date ranges. A big bonus applies for flagging the misleading chart *before* answering.
  → *analysis, pattern recognition*

- **Graph-Type Triage** — given a raw dataset, student picks the chart type (bar / line / scatter / heatmap / stacked) that best tells the clinical story. Wrong pick renders as a warped slide that "misleads the attending on rounds," visualized on screen.
  → *transfer, communication under pressure*

- **Outbreak Epi-Curve Reader** — an outbreak curve appears with labels blanked. Student clicks to label index case, exposure window, incubation period, and peak. Wrong labels distort the public-health response plan shown below — so the student sees, live, how a misread graph misdirects the intervention.
  → *analysis, systems thinking*

## References

- **CDC Growth Charts (2000 reference) and WHO Child Growth Standards** — the canonical pediatric growth percentile references used in every US pediatric visit; practical source material for chart-reading practice.
- **USPSTF recommendation statements and AHA/ACC guidelines** — examples of how tabular evidence grades (A/B/C/D/I) and summary graphs drive real clinical decisions; good source material for "apply, not just read."
- **CDC National Notifiable Diseases Surveillance System (NNDSS) weekly reports (MMWR)** — public-facing epi curves and tables that illustrate tables/charts/graphs in real public-health decision-making.
- **Clinical laboratory reference-range tables (e.g., Tietz, Mayo Clinic Laboratories)** — source for the lab-table reading practice.

## Activity specs

### Chart Detective

```yaml
name: Chart Detective
format: build_and_critique
supports:
  primary: 127.403-c2B
  secondary: [127.403-c2C]
duration_min: 15
mode: single_player
equipment: one_device_per_student
learning_goal: >
  Across 5 published-style clinical charts (infection rates, ED wait
  times, vaccine coverage), correctly answer 4 targeted data questions
  per chart AND flag the misleading-axis chart before answering at
  >= 80% overall accuracy.
vocab_exercised:
  - Truncated y-axis
  - Log scale
  - Trend line
  - Absolute vs. relative risk
  - Epidemiology (epi) curve
content_model: chart_question_bank
content:
  chart_pool_size: 15
  charts_per_session: 5
  questions_per_chart: 4
  each_chart_provides:
    - rendered_chart_svg     # bar / line / scatter / epi curve
    - axis_metadata          # zero_baseline true/false, scale type, units
    - data_points            # clickable for "click the value at..." questions
    - misleading_flag        # true for 1 of 5 charts per session
    - gold_answers           # 4 per chart
    - misleading_explanation # 1-sentence why the axis misleads
chart_misleading_categories:
    - truncated_y_axis
    - cherry_picked_date_range
    - relative_risk_no_absolute
    - log_scale_unlabeled
initial_state:
  current_chart_index: 0
  flag_misleading_button_visible: true
  flag_used_this_chart: false
  score: 0
controls:
  - click_data_point
  - click_answer_chip      # for "what does this trend show?" multiple-choice
  - click_flag_misleading  # bonus path
  - submit_chart
  - next_chart
feedback:
  moment: per_chart
  on_correct_answers:
    - green ring on each correctly clicked point or chip
  on_correct_misleading_flag_pre_answer:
    - +5 bonus banner; misleading_explanation slides in
  on_missed_misleading_flag:
    - end-of-chart reveal of the axis issue with redrawn-honest version side-by-side
scoring:
  correct_answer: +2
  wrong_answer: 0
  flag_misleading_before_answering: +5
  flag_misleading_after_answering: +1
  false_flag_on_honest_chart: -2
  mastery_threshold: 32   # 5 charts x 8 baseline + 2 bonus opportunities
win_condition:
  type: all_charts_completed
  mastery: score >= 32 / 45
reset:
  clears: [current_chart, score, flag_state, answers]
  preserves: [chart_pool, completed_charts]
student_view: >
  Chart fills the upper two-thirds of the screen; question stack on the
  right (4 questions per chart, one expanded at a time); "Flag this chart
  as misleading" button below the chart, dimmed until hovered. Score and
  chart counter at the bottom.
teacher_view: >
  Per-chart class accuracy plus flag-rate; surfaces which misleading
  charts the class is fooled by most (the high-value debrief targets).
accessibility:
  - Tab through data points; Enter to select; arrow keys to scrub trend lines
  - Numeric readout appears on focus for every data point (not color alone)
  - High-contrast chart palette; pattern fills as fallback for color-blind users
  - Screen reader reads axis units, scale type, and data-point value on focus
success_criteria: Student reaches mastery on 4 of 5 charts and correctly flags 2 of 2 misleading charts within one class period.
tech_stack:
  base: html+css+js
  libraries:
    - name: Tailwind CSS (Play CDN)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.tailwindcss.com
      purpose: Layout, question stack, chart frame styling
    - name: Chart.js (themed)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/chart.js@4.4.0/dist/chart.umd.min.js
      purpose: Polished, themable charts with native click-on-data-point support
    - name: Alpine.js
      cdn_url: https://unpkg.com/alpinejs@3.x.x/dist/cdn.min.js
      purpose: Question state, flag state, chart progression
visual_notes: >
  Editorial-chart aesthetic — slate background, single accent color per
  chart, axis labels in tabular-figures type. The "redraw honestly" reveal
  fades the misleading version to 30% opacity and slides the corrected
  version in beside it; never a modal. Flag button is small, deliberate,
  and rewards intent — it should feel like a detective's notebook, not a
  game-show buzzer.
```

**Instructions to student:** You will see 5 published-style clinical charts. For each, answer 4 targeted questions by clicking on the chart or picking from chips. One of the 5 charts is misleading — truncated axis, cherry-picked dates, or unlabeled log scale. If you flag it *before* answering, you earn a big bonus. Flagging an honest chart costs you points, so flag with intent.

**Teacher notes:** The misleading-flag rate is your most useful signal. If the class flags 0 of 2 misleading charts, they are answering questions without ever interrogating the axes — that is the entire skill the standard is asking for. Spend 5 minutes putting the misleading chart and its honest redraw side-by-side on the board and ask: *what changed about the story?* The truncated-y-axis case in particular almost always rewrites the recommendation.

### Graph-Type Triage

```yaml
name: Graph-Type Triage
format: drag_and_drop
supports:
  primary: 127.403-c2B
  secondary: [127.403-c2E]
duration_min: 12
mode: single_player
equipment: one_device_per_student
learning_goal: >
  Given 10 raw clinical datasets, drag each into the chart-type slot
  (bar, line, scatter, heatmap, stacked bar) that best tells the
  clinical story at >= 8/10 accuracy.
vocab_exercised:
  - Trend line
  - Epidemiology (epi) curve
  - Truncated y-axis
  - Log scale
content_model: dataset_to_chart_match
content:
  dataset_pool_size: 25
  datasets_per_session: 10
  chart_type_slots:
    - bar
    - line
    - scatter
    - heatmap
    - stacked_bar
  each_dataset_provides:
    - dataset_label                # "ED wait time by hour, 7 days"
    - data_table_preview           # 5 rows visible
    - gold_chart_type
    - rationale                    # 1-sentence why this type fits
    - misuse_distortion_preview    # what the chart looks like if wrong type chosen
initial_state:
  datasets_visible: 10
  slots_empty: true
  preview_pane_active: true
controls:
  - drag_dataset_card
  - drop_into_chart_type_slot
  - hover_preview      # see the dataset rendered in the chosen type before committing
  - submit_all
feedback:
  moment: end_of_round
  shows:
    - per_dataset_correct_or_warped_render
    - rationale_revealed
    - "warped" render plays a 2-second animation showing the misleading version
scoring:
  correct_match: +1
  wrong_match: 0
  mastery_threshold: 8
win_condition:
  type: submit_all
  mastery: score >= 8 / 10
reset:
  clears: [slot_assignments, submitted_state]
  preserves: [dataset_pool]
student_view: >
  10 dataset cards stacked on the left with a 5-row preview on each;
  5 chart-type slots arranged horizontally on the right, each with a
  small icon of the chart type. Hover-preview pane in the center renders
  the candidate match live as a faint sketch. Submit button bottom-right.
teacher_view: >
  Class confusion matrix — which chart type the class wrongly picks for
  each dataset. The matrix shows where the conceptual gap is (e.g., line
  vs. bar for time series; scatter vs. line for paired measurements).
accessibility:
  - Keyboard drag-and-drop (Space to pick up, arrow keys to traverse slots, Space to drop)
  - Slot icons paired with text labels (no icon-only)
  - Hover-preview also accessible via Tab + Enter
  - Screen reader announces dataset name on pick-up and slot name on focus
success_criteria: Student reaches 8/10 within one class period; weakest chart-type pair surfaced for whole-class debrief.
tech_stack:
  base: html+css+js
  libraries:
    - name: Tailwind CSS (Play CDN)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.tailwindcss.com
      purpose: Card layout, slot grid, typography
    - name: SortableJS
      cdn_url: https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/sortablejs@1.15.2/Sortable.min.js
      purpose: Polished drag-and-drop with drop-zone affordances
    - name: Chart.js (themed)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/chart.js@4.4.0/dist/chart.umd.min.js
      purpose: Live preview render and the "warped" misuse animation
visual_notes: >
  Editorial-clean palette; slot icons are simple line glyphs of each
  chart type. The "warped" render is the same data drawn in the wrong
  chart type, captioned with what the attending would misconclude — the
  pedagogy is in the visual mismatch, so make the warped version genuinely
  ugly (overlapping bars, scatter where time should be a line). No modals.
```

**Instructions to student:** You have 10 datasets and 5 chart types. Drag each dataset onto the chart type that best tells its story. Hover any pairing to see how it would render before you commit. After you submit, the wrong picks animate into "warped" charts — what an attending on rounds would actually see if you brought the wrong chart. Aim for 8/10.

**Teacher notes:** The confusion matrix usually shows two persistent patterns — *line vs. bar for time series* and *scatter vs. line for paired measurements*. Both are conceptually about whether the x-axis is a continuous variable. Spend 5 minutes on one example of each in the next session. The "warped" animation is the highest-leverage piece of the activity; replay one in the debrief to anchor the lesson visually.

### Outbreak Epi-Curve Reader

```yaml
name: Outbreak Epi-Curve Reader
format: simulation
supports:
  primary: 127.403-c2B
  secondary: [127.403-c2C]
duration_min: 18
mode: single_player
equipment: one_device_per_student
learning_goal: >
  Given an outbreak epidemiology curve with labels blanked, correctly
  place 4 labels (index case, exposure window, incubation period, peak)
  on each of 3 outbreaks at >= 10/12 placements within the session.
vocab_exercised:
  - Epidemiology (epi) curve
  - Trend line
  - Truncated y-axis
content_model: outbreak_curve_with_labels
content:
  outbreak_pool_size: 8
  outbreaks_per_session: 3
  label_set:
    - index_case
    - exposure_window
    - incubation_period
    - peak
  each_outbreak_provides:
    - rendered_epi_curve            # bar chart, case count by day
    - shape_class                   # point_source / continuous / propagated
    - gold_label_x_positions
    - linked_response_plan          # public-health response that updates live
    - response_plan_distortion_map  # which wrong label causes which distortion
initial_state:
  current_outbreak_index: 0
  labels_unplaced: true
  response_plan_panel_visible: true
  response_plan_state: baseline
controls:
  - drag_label_chip
  - drop_on_curve_x_position
  - reposition_label
  - remove_label
  - submit_outbreak
  - next_outbreak
feedback:
  moment: real_time
  behaviors:
    - on label drop, response plan panel below the curve updates live with the implied intervention
    - wrong placement visibly distorts the response plan ("contact tracing window: days 14–21" when the label puts it days 5–8)
    - on submit, gold label positions overlay in cyan; student positions remain in amber where wrong
scoring:
  correct_label_within_tolerance: +1   # ±1 day for index case, ±2 days for windows
  wrong_label_position: 0
  mastery_threshold: 10               # of 12 placements across 3 outbreaks
win_condition:
  type: all_outbreaks_complete
  mastery: total placements >= 10 / 12
reset:
  clears: [labels, response_plan_state, current_outbreak]
  preserves: [outbreak_pool]
student_view: >
  Epi curve fills the upper half — bars of case count by day, axes
  clearly labeled. Four label chips (index case, exposure window,
  incubation period, peak) sit in a tray below the curve. Response-plan
  panel beneath the tray updates live as labels move. Outbreak counter
  and shape-class hint top-right.
teacher_view: >
  Per-outbreak placement heatmap showing where the class is dropping
  each label vs. the gold position; flags students who consistently
  place the index case after the peak (a conceptual reversal worth
  catching individually).
accessibility:
  - Keyboard label placement (Tab to label, arrow keys to slide along x-axis, Enter to drop)
  - X-position numeric readout always visible on a focused label
  - Curve bars include numeric case-count labels (not color alone)
  - Screen reader announces label name on pick-up and current x-day on movement
success_criteria: Student reaches 10/12 placements with no instance of index case placed after peak within one class period.
tech_stack:
  base: html+css+js
  libraries:
    - name: Tailwind CSS (Play CDN)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.tailwindcss.com
      purpose: Tray and panel layout, label chip styling
    - name: Chart.js (themed)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/chart.js@4.4.0/dist/chart.umd.min.js
      purpose: The epi curve rendering with custom annotation plugin for label drop
    - name: Alpine.js
      cdn_url: https://unpkg.com/alpinejs@3.x.x/dist/cdn.min.js
      purpose: Label placement state, live response-plan updates, outbreak progression
visual_notes: >
  Public-health-report aesthetic — bars in muted teal, gold-position
  overlay in cyan on submit, student-misplaced labels ringed in amber
  rather than red. The response-plan panel is text-only with bullet
  points that change as labels move; the change should feel like
  consequence, not a quiz score. Avoid alarm coloring — outbreak material
  is sobering enough without dramatization.
```

**Instructions to student:** Each outbreak shows you a curve of case counts by day with no labels. Drag the four labels — index case, exposure window, incubation period, peak — to where they belong on the curve. Watch the response-plan panel below: as you place labels, the public-health response updates live. If your contact-tracing window jumps from days 5–8 to days 14–21, that is the curve telling you the placement is wrong. Submit when you are confident.

**Teacher notes:** The pedagogy is in the live response-plan panel — students see, in real time, that a misread curve sends the public-health response to the wrong week. After the activity, pull up one outbreak from the teacher heatmap where the class consistently misplaced the exposure window. Ask: *if you were the on-call epidemiologist and you sent contact tracers to the wrong days, what would the field team find?* Answer: nothing — and the outbreak keeps going. That is the stake.
