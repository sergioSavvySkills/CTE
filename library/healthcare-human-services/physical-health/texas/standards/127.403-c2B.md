## Learning objective

Students will be able to read clinical tables, charts, and graphs — including lab reference ranges, growth percentile curves, and trend lines — and use the data to recommend or justify a health-related decision.

## Content stub

- **Clinical tables students will actually see:** lab reference-range panels (CBC, CMP), drug dosing tables (mg/kg, renal-adjusted), BMI classification tables, vital-sign normal ranges by age
- **Chart and graph types common in healthcare:**
  - Trend lines — glucose over 24 h, blood pressure series, weight over months
  - Growth percentile curves — CDC / WHO pediatric growth charts
  - Bar charts — outcomes by site, infection rates by unit
  - Scatterplots — dose vs. response, BP vs. age
  - Survival / Kaplan-Meier curves (introduce, don't master)
  - Epidemiology curves — case counts over time during an outbreak
- **Read the axes before the curve:** always check units, scale (linear vs. log), starting value (does the y-axis start at zero?), and time window — a graph can mislead without ever being wrong
- **Apply, not just read:** the standard says "provide solutions" — pair every data read with a decision: normal or abnormal? trending toward or away from target? needs escalation? counsel the patient?
- **Public-health data products:** CDC surveillance graphs, immunization coverage maps, outbreak epi curves — same reading skills, different stakes
- **Common misreads:** truncated y-axis exaggerating a change, cherry-picked date range, confusing absolute vs. relative risk, missing the units on the axis entirely

## Vocabulary

- **Reference range** — the range of values considered normal for a lab test in a defined population; the standard against which a result is flagged normal or abnormal.
- **Growth percentile curve** — a CDC or WHO chart that plots a child's height, weight, or head circumference against same-age peers; "crossing percentiles" is a clinical signal.
- **Trend line** — a plot of a value (glucose, BP, weight) over time used to see whether the patient is moving toward or away from a target.
- **Epidemiology (epi) curve** — a bar or line chart of case counts over time during an outbreak; shape suggests whether a source is point, continuous, or propagated.
- **Truncated y-axis** — a y-axis that does not start at zero; visually exaggerates small changes and is a common way a graph misleads without being technically wrong.
- **Log scale** — an axis where equal distances represent multiplicative (not additive) changes; appropriate for data spanning many orders of magnitude but easy to misread.
- **BMI (body mass index)** — weight-for-height index (kg/m²) used with a classification table to categorize adults and counsel accordingly.
- **Absolute vs. relative risk** — absolute = the actual probability of an outcome; relative = the ratio between groups; headlines often quote relative and hide small absolute differences.
- **Kaplan-Meier / survival curve** — a stepwise plot of the proportion of a group still free of an event over time; introduced at survey level for chart literacy.

## Assessment stub

- Interpretation set: given three clinical charts (a pediatric growth-percentile plot, a 24-hour glucose trend, and a medication-concentration curve), answer targeted questions — "Is this child tracking their growth curve or crossing percentiles?"; "When does this patient's glucose first exceed target?"; "When is the drug's peak concentration and when is the next dose due?"
- Spot-the-misleading-axis: two sample graphs with truncated or nonlinear axes; students explain what the graph seems to show vs. what the data actually supports, and redraw one honestly
- Scenario: given a BMI table and a patient's height and weight, classify the patient's BMI category and state two counseling implications that follow from the classification

## References

- **CDC Growth Charts (2000 reference) and WHO Child Growth Standards** — the canonical pediatric growth percentile references used in every US pediatric visit; practical source material for chart-reading practice.
- **USPSTF recommendation statements and AHA/ACC guidelines** — examples of how tabular evidence grades (A/B/C/D/I) and summary graphs drive real clinical decisions; good source material for "apply, not just read."
- **CDC National Notifiable Diseases Surveillance System (NNDSS) weekly reports (MMWR)** — public-facing epi curves and tables that illustrate tables/charts/graphs in real public-health decision-making.
- **Clinical laboratory reference-range tables (e.g., Tietz, Mayo Clinic Laboratories)** — source for the lab-table reading practice.
