## Learning objective

Students will be able to evaluate how relationships — peer, family, and friendship networks — produce positive and negative effects on physical and emotional health, and explain how healthy individual relationships scale up to produce a healthier community.

## Content stub

- **Builds on c2L, applied to community:** c2L evaluated communication skills for maintaining relationships across the lifespan; c10C asks students to evaluate the *health effects* of those relationships and how the effects aggregate at the community level
- **Positive effects of strong relationships (evidence base):**
  - *Physical health* — lower cardiovascular risk, lower all-cause mortality, better immune function, better recovery from illness (multiple meta-analyses; see Holt-Lunstad et al. 2010 *PLoS Medicine* landmark review)
  - *Emotional health* — reduced depression and anxiety risk, improved resilience during stressors, meaningful life satisfaction
  - *Health behaviors* — partners and peers shape diet, exercise, sleep, substance use, and healthcare-seeking behavior
- **Negative effects of unhealthy relationships:**
  - *Physical* — chronic stress → cardiovascular and metabolic effects; domestic violence and its direct injuries; substance-enabling by peers; sleep disruption
  - *Emotional* — depression, anxiety, PTSD, suicidality in the most severe cases
  - *Specific patterns that damage health:* contempt, controlling behavior, isolation, gaslighting, financial abuse, substance-use enabling
- **Social isolation and loneliness as a public-health issue:**
  - Isolation mortality risk is comparable to heavy smoking (Holt-Lunstad)
  - HHS Surgeon General's 2023 advisory framed loneliness as a national public-health priority
  - Community-level effects: older adults, rural communities, LGBTQ+ youth, and immigrants without family networks are higher-risk groups
- **How individual relationships scale to community health:**
  - *Social capital* — trust, reciprocity, and networks in a community predict everything from vaccination rates to disaster recovery
  - *Collective efficacy* — neighborhoods where people watch out for each other have lower crime and better mental-health outcomes
  - *Community-level risk* — high-churn, low-trust communities have worse health outcomes independent of income
- **Relationships students can evaluate specifically:**
  - *Peer relationships* — friend groups, school cohorts, work teams
  - *Family relationships* — nuclear, extended, chosen family
  - *Friendship networks* — close confidants vs. wider acquaintance networks
  - *Community relationships* — neighbors, civic groups, faith communities, volunteer networks
- **Evaluating the effect — use a framework:**
  - *Direction* — positive, negative, or mixed
  - *Mechanism* — how does this relationship affect health? (direct behavior influence, stress, isolation buffering, resource access, etc.)
  - *Magnitude* — small, moderate, large effect
  - *Modifiability* — can the person change it? what community-level supports exist?
- **Crosswalks with other standards:** c2L (communication skills for relationships), c2P (cultural influences on relationships and help-seeking), c2K/c10B (wellness and prevention), c9B (confidentiality when screening for relationship concerns)

## Vocabulary

- **Social capital** — the trust, reciprocity, and networks in a community that predict outcomes from vaccination rates to disaster recovery.
- **Collective efficacy** — neighborhood-level willingness of residents to watch out for each other; correlates with lower crime and better mental-health outcomes.
- **Social isolation** — lack of meaningful social contact; mortality risk comparable to heavy smoking per Holt-Lunstad. *(also in c2L)*
- **Loneliness** — the subjective experience of isolation framed as a national public-health priority in the 2023 Surgeon General's advisory.
- **Surgeon General's advisory** — the HHS 2023 document *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation* reframing loneliness as a public-health issue rather than a personal problem.
- **Holt-Lunstad meta-analysis** — the 2010 *PLoS Medicine* review establishing that relationship quality predicts mortality.
- **Gaslighting** — a corrosive relationship pattern undermining the other person's sense of reality; damages emotional health.
- **Modifiability** — a framework dimension asking whether the person can change a relationship and what community-level supports exist.
- **Chosen family** — non-biological relationships functioning as family; a relationship category especially relevant to LGBTQ+ and immigrant populations.
- **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)** — early-life relationship harms with decades-long physical-health effects documented by the CDC.

## Assessment stub

- Relationship-effect evaluation: read three short vignettes (a high-schooler whose friend group is pressuring them to try vaping; an older adult widow living alone with limited family contact; a young adult whose partner isolates them from friends); for each, evaluate direction, mechanism, magnitude, and modifiability of the relationship's health effect, and propose one specific community-level support that could help
- Community analysis: pick a specific community (your neighborhood, school, or religious/civic group); identify two features that support healthy relationships (e.g., walkable streets, after-school programs, youth sports leagues, senior centers) and two that don't (e.g., long commutes, social-media-driven isolation, lack of gathering spaces); evaluate the aggregate effect on community health
- Short response: in 200–300 words, explain how the Surgeon General's framing of loneliness as a public-health priority (rather than a personal problem) changes what kind of interventions make sense; name one community-level intervention and one healthcare-level intervention that follow from this framing
- Reflection: identify one positive and one challenging relationship in your life; evaluate each against the framework (direction, mechanism, magnitude, modifiability); name one specific action you could take that would either strengthen the positive or reduce the harm of the challenging one

## References

- **Holt-Lunstad, Smith, Layton, "Social Relationships and Mortality Risk: A Meta-analytic Review," *PLoS Medicine* (2010)** — foundational evidence that relationship quality predicts mortality comparable to major behavioral risk factors.
- **HHS US Surgeon General's Advisory *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation* (2023)** — current federal public-health framing for the community-level dimension of this standard.
- **HHS / National Academies of Sciences *Social Isolation and Loneliness in Older Adults* (2020)** — quantitative evidence base for isolation as a health issue.
- **Kawachi and Berkman, *Social Capital and Health*** — scholarly foundation for the community-level framing (social capital, collective efficacy).
- **CDC *Preventing Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)*** — evidence for how early-life relationships produce decades-long physical-health effects.
- **HRSA / CDC Rural Health Research Programs** — evidence on relationship/community health in rural populations specifically relevant to Texas.
- **Gottman research on Four Horsemen** (from c2L) — specific relationship patterns that damage health.
- **Putnam, *Bowling Alone*** — classic analysis of US social-capital decline and its community-health implications.
