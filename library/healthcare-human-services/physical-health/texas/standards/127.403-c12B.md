## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify potential malfunctions across common healthcare equipment — recognize the warning signs (alarms, error codes, abnormal readings, physical damage, software anomalies), distinguish device issues from user errors and patient issues, and state the immediate safety action when a malfunction is suspected.

## Content stub

- **Malfunction categories — the failure modes students will actually see:**
  - *Alarms and error codes* — the device itself is signaling a problem; students should know the difference between informational, advisory, and critical alarms and never silence an alarm they don't understand
  - *Abnormal readings* — values that don't fit the clinical picture (e.g., a BP of 60/40 in an awake, alert patient; a glucose of "HI" on a finger-stick; a pulse-ox of 78% on a patient with a normal respiratory exam); first step is always "does the number match the patient?"
  - *Physical damage* — cracked housings, frayed cords, bent needles, broken connectors, leaking reservoirs; removes the device from service immediately
  - *Unresponsive device or display* — frozen screen, won't power on, won't complete a cycle, software crash
  - *Calibration drift* — device is technically working but producing inaccurate values (e.g., a glucose meter that systematically reads high); often caught by QC checks, not by the user's intuition
  - *Unexpected behavior during operation* — infusion pump running at wrong rate, ventilator not cycling as set, defibrillator failing to charge, imaging scanner producing artifacts
  - *Battery failure* — device works when plugged in, dies on battery; critical for transport equipment (AEDs, transport monitors, portable suction)
  - *Network/connectivity failure* — EHR workstation offline, barcode scanner not reading, telemetry not transmitting; has patient-safety consequences when clinical decisions depend on data flow
- **Common equipment-specific failure modes worth recognizing:**
  - *IV pumps* — wrong rate entered, air-in-line alarm, occlusion alarm, free-flow event (rare on smart pumps), dose-error alert
  - *Ventilators* — disconnection alarm, high/low pressure alarm, tidal-volume out of range, ventilator asynchrony with patient effort
  - *Defibrillators/AEDs* — pad connection error, "check patient" advisory, low battery, self-test failure on daily check
  - *Imaging equipment* — motion artifact, metal artifact on MRI, tube failure on X-ray, contrast injector jam
  - *Lab analyzers* — QC out of range, reagent expired or depleted, sample-delivery jam, calibration failure
  - *EKG machines* — lead off, wandering baseline, 60-Hz electrical interference, pacemaker-spike misidentification
  - *Sterilizers* — cycle failure, biologic-indicator positive (instruments not actually sterile — major patient-safety issue), failed vacuum pulse
  - *Patient monitors* — false alarms from poor lead contact, artifact from patient movement, cable-disconnect alarm
- **Distinguishing malfunction from user error and from patient physiology:**
  - A low pulse ox may be a bad probe placement (user), a patient who is actually hypoxic (patient), or a pulse-ox sensor issue (device); assess the patient first, then the user technique, then the device
  - Pump "occlusion" alarms are often a kinked line or patient positioning, not a pump malfunction
  - An EKG showing artifact may be electrical interference in the room, not a cardiac arrhythmia
  - This is the core clinical-reasoning skill: rule out patient emergency first, then non-device causes, then suspect device
- **Immediate safety actions when a malfunction is suspected (the common pattern):**
  - *If a patient is attached* — ensure patient safety first; use backup methods (manual BP, manual ventilation, alternate device)
  - *Take the device out of service* — remove from the patient area
  - *Label the device clearly* — "Do Not Use" with date, time, initials, and brief description of the issue
  - *Preserve the device and any disposables* — do not throw anything away that might be needed for investigation (the IV tubing, the blood-glucose strip, the settings history)
  - *Notify the right people* — clinical supervisor, biomed/clinical engineering, and — if patient harm occurred — risk management
  - *Document factually* in the patient record and in the facility incident-reporting system; separately file the biomed work order
- **Student-specific guidance:**
  - Students are not expected to troubleshoot device malfunctions — they are expected to *recognize* signs of malfunction, keep the patient safe, and escalate to the right person
  - When in doubt, take it out of service — the cost of pulling a device that turns out to be fine is far less than the cost of continued use of a malfunctioning device

## Assessment stub

- Malfunction-recognition drill: given 10 mixed scenarios (pump alarm with kinked tubing; glucose meter QC failure; AED self-test failure; ventilator high-pressure alarm on a coughing patient; EKG artifact from 60-Hz interference; frayed oxygen tubing; sterilizer biologic indicator positive; IV pump running at 999 mL/hr; pulse-ox reading 78% on a talking, comfortable patient; unresponsive EHR workstation mid-order), identify for each whether the issue is likely a patient problem, a user error, a device malfunction, or needs more information; state the immediate safety action
- Warning-sign identification: given a device description (real photographs or simulation videos) with five types of visible warning signs (error codes, physical damage, alarms, abnormal readings, unresponsive display), identify each and pair with the appropriate immediate action
- Take-out-of-service practical: given three scenarios requiring device removal from service (AED self-test failure, IV pump with erratic rate, sterilizer biologic-indicator positive), demonstrate the correct sequence (ensure patient safety → remove from patient area → label "Do Not Use" with specifics → preserve evidence → notify biomed and supervisor → document factually)
- Short response: in 150–200 words, explain why "assess the patient before assuming the device is wrong" is the correct first step when you see an abnormal reading; give a specific example of a reading that, if treated as a device problem, could have caused patient harm

## References

- **ECRI Institute *Top 10 Health Technology Hazards* (annual report)** — independent authoritative source on the most common and dangerous device failure modes in US healthcare; updated yearly.
- **FDA MedWatch Safety Alerts and Medical Device Recalls** — ongoing public record of device-malfunction events; useful teaching cases.
- **Association for the Advancement of Medical Instrumentation (AAMI) Medical Device Alarm Safety Standards** — authoritative source on alarm categorization and alarm fatigue.
- **The Joint Commission Sentinel Event Alert #50 (2013) *Medical Device Alarm Safety in Hospitals*** — foundational source on the clinical consequences of alarm mismanagement.
- **Manufacturer Instructions for Use (IFU)** — the primary source for any specific device; every healthcare facility has IFUs available for every device in use.
- **Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (CLIA) quality-control requirements** — the regulatory source for lab-analyzer QC procedures.
- **Association of periOperative Registered Nurses (AORN) and Certification Board for Sterile Processing and Distribution (CBSPD)** — standards for sterilizer monitoring, biologic-indicator interpretation, and immediate response to failures.
