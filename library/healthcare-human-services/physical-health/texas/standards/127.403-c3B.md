## Learning objective

Students will be able to demonstrate effective communication skills — active listening, open questioning, empathy, closed-loop confirmation, and appropriate adaptations — when responding to the needs of patients who vary in language, culture, age, ability, and health literacy.

## Content stub

- **From "identify" (c3A) to "demonstrate" (c3B):** this standard is a performance standard — students must do it, not just describe it; expect observed role-plays and skill checks
- **Responding to needs starts with eliciting them:** the effective opener is open-ended ("What brings you in today?") followed by silence, not a rapid checklist
- **Adaptations for linguistic diversity:**
  - Qualified medical interpreters (in-person, phone, or video); speak directly to the patient, not the interpreter; short, complete sentences; one idea at a time
  - Translated written materials at the patient's reading level; never rely on family members (especially minors) to interpret
- **Adaptations for cultural variation:** gender-concordant clinician when requested, modesty considerations, family decision-making norms, eye-contact and silence norms, dietary and religious observances during treatment
- **Adaptations for age and developmental stage (crosswalks with c2J):**
  - Pediatric — age-appropriate vocabulary, involve caregiver, play/demonstration tools, honesty about what will hurt
  - Adolescent — confidentiality framing, consent rules per state law, autonomy signals
  - Older adults — adequate time, check hearing and vision, include care-partner if patient consents, avoid "elderspeak" (infantilizing tone)
- **Adaptations for disability and cognitive status:**
  - Hearing — face the patient, don't cover mouth, written backup, sign-language interpreter for Deaf patients
  - Vision — describe the environment and what you're doing before you do it, read materials aloud
  - Cognitive impairment — short sentences, one instruction at a time, repeat back, include the care-partner
- **Health-literacy adaptations (AHRQ Universal Precautions):** assume limited health literacy by default; plain language; teach-back ("Can you tell me in your own words what we just discussed?"); avoid jargon or translate it immediately
- **Emotional adaptations:** upset, scared, or grieving patients need acknowledgment before information; the acknowledge → explain → offer-next-step pattern works in most situations

## Vocabulary

- **Qualified medical interpreter** — a trained, vetted interpreter (in-person, phone, or video) used for LEP patients; family members are not substitutes. *(also in c10E)*
- **Teach-back** — AHRQ Universal Precautions technique in which the patient explains instructions in their own words to confirm understanding. *(also in c1A)*
- **Health literacy universal precautions** — AHRQ framework that assumes limited health literacy by default and uses plain language with every patient.
- **Gender-concordant clinician** — a same-gender clinician provided when a patient requests one; a cultural adaptation for modesty-conscious encounters.
- **Elderspeak** — an infantilizing tone used with older adults; specifically avoided in age-appropriate communication.
- **Acknowledge-explain-next-step** — the emotional-adaptation pattern for upset, scared, or grieving patients: acknowledge feeling, then explain, then offer a next step.
- **Assent (pediatric)** — an age-appropriate agreement obtained from a minor alongside a parent's or guardian's consent.
- **AIDET / SBAR / CLAS** — structured communication and cultural-response frameworks operationalizing effective cross-diversity communication in US healthcare.
- **Care-partner** — a family member or designated support person included (with patient consent) for older or cognitively impaired patients.

## Assessment stub

- Observed role-play — standard encounter: conduct a 5-minute mock patient intake with a peer; observer rubric scores open questions, paraphrasing, empathy statements, closed-loop confirmation, and precise language
- Observed role-play — interpreter scenario: conduct a 5-minute intake where a peer plays a limited-English-proficient patient and a third student plays a qualified interpreter; observer scores direct address, sentence length, patient focus, and whether the interpreter was used correctly
- Adaptation portfolio: for four distinct patients (a 4-year-old with an ear infection, an 82-year-old with mild dementia, a Deaf adult, an LEP adult), produce written adaptation plans naming three specific communication adjustments each and the rationale
- Teach-back skill check: teach a peer how to use a simulated medical device (e.g., peak-flow meter) using plain language and teach-back; peer scores your ability to confirm understanding without making them feel tested

## Activities

- **Interpreter Encounter Simulator** — three-panel screen: LEP patient avatar on the left (speaks Spanish audio clips), qualified interpreter panel in the middle, student-clinician panel on the right. Student types what they want to say. The sim enforces hard rules — speak directly to the patient (not "tell her…"), one idea per sentence, no compound questions. A sentence-length meter flashes red past 20 words. A "family-member interpreter" temptation appears in round 2 when the interpreter drops off the video call; choosing to use the patient's teenage daughter instead of pausing for a re-connect loses the round.
  → *communication under pressure, ethical reasoning*

- **Teach-Back Peak-Flow Coach** — student sees a cartoon patient with a peak-flow meter and has 4 minutes to teach use via voice recording (microphone). The sim transcribes the audio and scores: plain language density (target under 6th-grade reading level per auto-readability check), no jargon without translation, and — critically — a teach-back check ("Can you tell me in your own words…"). If no teach-back appears, the score caps at 50%. A peer listener flags whether it felt like a test or a conversation.
  → *application, empathy*

- **Adaptation Match-Up** — 12 patient cards appear with one meaningful difference per card (4-year-old ear infection, 82-year-old with mild dementia, Deaf adult, LEP Spanish speaker, blind patient, adolescent seeking confidential care, non-verbal autistic adult, care-partner-accompanied elder). Student pairs each with three adaptation chips from a bank of 40 (face the patient, use pictogram, involve caregiver with consent, avoid elderspeak, request sign interpreter, obtain assent, use acknowledge-explain-next-step). Traps included: a family-as-interpreter chip, an elderspeak chip. Wrong chips get ringed with their specific policy citation.
  → *pattern recognition, application*

## References

- **AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit** — evidence-based plain-language and teach-back protocols adopted across US primary care.
- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0** — check-back, call-out, and structured-exchange skills that operationalize effective communication.
- **HHS Office of Minority Health *National CLAS Standards*** — federal framework for language-access, interpreter use, and culturally responsive care.
- **Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964** — legal basis for interpreter services in federally funded healthcare.
- **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** — communication and access requirements for patients with hearing, vision, cognitive, and mobility disabilities.
- **Calgary-Cambridge Guide to the Medical Interview** — structured framework for skill-based medical-communication education.
- **AAP communication guidelines** (pediatric) and **American Geriatrics Society communication guidelines** (older adults) — stage-specific adaptations.
