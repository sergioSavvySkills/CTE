## Learning objective

Students will be able to convert quantities between metric and US customary units of measurement using dimensional analysis, applying the conversions used in routine clinical work (weight, volume, length, temperature).

## Content stub

- **Two systems in use in US healthcare:** metric (g, kg, mL, L, cm, m, °C) — the clinical default; US customary (oz, lb, tsp, tbsp, in, ft, °F) — what patients often report in
- **Core conversions that must be automatic:**
  - Weight: lb ↔ kg (divide lb by 2.2)
  - Volume: mL ↔ tsp (5 mL = 1 tsp), mL ↔ tbsp (15 mL = 1 tbsp)
  - Length: in ↔ cm (2.54 cm = 1 in)
  - Temperature: °C ↔ °F (°F = °C × 1.8 + 32)
  - Within metric: kg ↔ mg (×1,000,000), g ↔ mg (×1,000), L ↔ mL (×1,000)
- **Dimensional analysis (unit-canceling method):** write the given quantity, multiply by the conversion factor as a fraction, cancel units — the single technique that prevents most conversion errors
- **Decimal safety rules (ISMP):** always use a leading zero for values less than 1 (0.5 mg, not .5 mg); never use a trailing zero (5 mg, not 5.0 mg) — the misread-by-10 error has killed patients, especially pediatric patients
- **Weight-based dosing as the high-stakes application:** pediatric doses are almost always mg/kg; the lb → kg conversion is the single most common point of error
- **Common error sources:** unit mixing (mg given instead of mcg), decimal misplacement, skipping dimensional analysis and "eyeballing," using patient-reported weight without unit confirmation

## Vocabulary

- **Metric system (SI)** — the clinical default system of measurement (g, kg, mL, L, cm, m, °C) used in US healthcare documentation.
- **US customary units** — oz, lb, tsp, tbsp, in, ft, °F — what US patients often report in and what clinicians must convert from.
- **Dimensional analysis** — the unit-canceling method: write the given value, multiply by conversion factors as fractions, cancel units; the single technique that prevents most conversion errors.
- **Conversion factor** — a fraction equal to 1 that relates two units (e.g., 2.54 cm / 1 in) used to change units without changing value.
- **Leading zero rule (ISMP)** — always write a zero before a decimal point for values less than 1 (0.5 mg, not .5 mg) to prevent ten-fold overdose misreads.
- **Trailing zero rule (ISMP)** — never write a zero after a whole-number decimal (5 mg, not 5.0 mg) to prevent ten-fold overdose misreads.
- **Weight-based dosing** — calculating a dose from the patient's body weight (mg/kg); pediatric dosing standard and the highest-stakes use of unit conversion.
- **mcg vs. mg** — micrograms (mcg) and milligrams (mg) differ by a factor of 1,000; confusing them has caused fatal medication errors and is explicitly addressed by ISMP.
- **ISMP (Institute for Safe Medication Practices)** — the US organization that publishes the error-prone-abbreviations and dose-designation safety rules referenced above.

## Assessment stub

- Conversion drill: 15 mixed problems covering weight, volume, length, temperature, and within-metric; no calculator; show work using dimensional analysis
- Weight-based dose calculation: given a pediatric patient weighing 44 lb and a mg/kg dose, calculate the administered dose in mg; student must convert lb→kg first and show the decimal check
- Error-spotting: given 4 physician orders containing potential decimal or unit errors (trailing zero, missing leading zero, mg/mcg confusion, lb/kg mixup), flag each unsafe order and state the specific rule it violates

## Activities

- **Drip-Rate Showdown** — patient scenarios throw mg/kg, mL/hr, and gtt/min conversions at the student; each answer feeds a virtual IV pump. Wrong math, the patient visibly crashes. Leading-zero and mcg-vs-mg traps are seeded deliberately.
  → *procedural fluency, pattern recognition*

- **Dimensional Analysis Sandbox** — student drags conversion factors onto a worksheet; units auto-cancel in real time on screen. The student either arrives at the target unit or stalls mid-chain visibly — no guessing possible.
  → *application, metacognition*

- **mcg/mg Trap Trainer** — rapid-fire drill of 20 prompts with deliberately similar-looking distractors (5 mg vs. 5 mcg vs. 0.5 mg vs. .5 mg — all ISMP-flagged). Five seconds per prompt. Builds the reflex to slow down on dangerous units rather than speed up.
  → *pattern recognition, procedural fluency*

## References

- **ISMP (Institute for Safe Medication Practices) "List of Error-Prone Abbreviations, Symbols, and Dose Designations"** — the authoritative source for leading-zero / trailing-zero rules and the mg/mcg safety guidance cited above.
- **Joint Commission "Do Not Use" list** — accreditation-backed abbreviation and decimal rules that make these conventions enforceable in accredited facilities.
- **NIST Handbook 44 and the Metric Conversion Act of 1975** — the metric standards that US healthcare aligns to, and the reason clinical documentation standardizes on SI units.
- **AAP (American Academy of Pediatrics) weight-based dosing guidance** — operational context for why pediatric dosing elevates unit-conversion accuracy from a math skill to a patient-safety skill.

## Activity specs

### Drip-Rate Showdown

```yaml
name: Drip-Rate Showdown
format: timed_drill
supports:
  primary: 127.403-c2A
  secondary: [127.403-c2F, 127.403-c11B]
duration_min: 15
mode: single_player
equipment: one_device_per_student
learning_goal: >
  Across 10 consecutive clinical scenarios (adult fluids, adult meds,
  pediatric weight-based), correctly compute the mg dose, mL/hr infusion
  rate, and gtt/min drip rate at >= 80% accuracy with zero pediatric
  decompensations.
vocab_exercised:
  - Dimensional analysis
  - Weight-based dosing
  - mcg vs. mg
  - Conversion factor
  - ISMP (Institute for Safe Medication Practices)
content_model: progressive_scenario_bank
content:
  scenario_pool_size: 30
  tiers:
    - name: adult_fluids
      count: 10
    - name: adult_meds
      count: 10
    - name: pediatric_weight_based
      count: 10
  each_scenario_provides:
    - patient              # age, weight in lb (force the lb->kg conversion)
    - order                # drug, dose, duration
    - drug_concentration   # mg/mL available on the floor
    - drop_factor          # 10 / 15 / 20 / 60 gtt/mL per tubing
    - gold_answers         # mg dose, mL/hr rate, gtt/min rate
initial_state:
  timer_seconds_per_scenario: 45
  tier_start: adult_fluids
  patient_status: stable
controls:
  - number_input_mg
  - number_input_mL_per_hr
  - number_input_gtt_per_min
  - scratch_paper_modal
  - submit_scenario
feedback:
  moment: per_scenario
  on_correct:
    - patient stays stable; gold dimensional-analysis trace shown beside student's work
  on_wrong_within_10_percent:
    - amber flag; gold trace shown; no patient crash
  on_wrong_beyond_10_percent:
    - patient decompensates on the monitor widget (BP drops, HR climbs)
    - gold trace shown; scenario marked "crash"
scoring:
  correct: +10
  wrong_within_10_percent: +2
  wrong_beyond_10_percent: 0
  pediatric_crash: counts as a hard fail regardless of accuracy score
  mastery: overall >= 80% AND zero pediatric crashes
win_condition:
  type: all_scenarios_completed_or_timer_expiry
  mastery: mastery scoring rule above
reset:
  clears: [current_scenario, patient_state, timer, student_inputs]
  preserves: [scenario_pool, tier_progress, difficulty]
student_view: >
  Patient avatar at top with a live vitals monitor (HR, BP trend line);
  order panel on the left; three numeric input cells in the middle
  (mg / mL per hr / gtt per min); scratch-paper toggle top-right;
  countdown and tier indicator at the top.
teacher_view: >
  Class-level accuracy per tier; crash heatmap per scenario (which
  scenarios trip the most students); time-per-scenario distribution
  to identify students under time pressure vs. comfortable.
accessibility:
  - Large numeric keypads on mobile; full keyboard nav on desktop
  - Vitals trend includes numeric readout and an icon, not color alone
  - Crash alert is a clear tone (toggleable) plus visual, not strobe
success_criteria: Student clears 8/10 overall with 0 crashes on the pediatric tier within one class period.
tech_stack:
  base: html+css+js
  libraries:
    - name: Tailwind CSS (Play CDN)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.tailwindcss.com
      purpose: Clinical-monitor layout, patient panel, input styling
    - name: Alpine.js
      cdn_url: https://unpkg.com/alpinejs@3.x.x/dist/cdn.min.js
      purpose: Scenario state machine, timer, per-tier progression
visual_notes: >
  Clinical-monitor aesthetic — soft-green trend line on a slate
  background, numeric readouts in tabular-figures type. Crash is a
  muted red wash that lingers 3 s, not a full-screen modal. Dimensional-
  analysis trace appears as a clean fraction chain below the input row,
  not as a dense paragraph.
```

**Instructions to student:** For each scenario, read the patient details and the order, then compute three numbers: the total dose in mg, the infusion rate in mL/hr, and the drip rate in gtt/min. You have 45 seconds per scenario. A wrong-by-more-than-10% answer on a pediatric case will visibly decompensate the patient — those are the ones that matter most. Use the scratch-paper pad if you need it.

**Teacher notes:** Run the adult tiers first to build confidence, then the pediatric tier. After the activity, pull up the crash heatmap and spend 10 minutes on the 2 or 3 scenarios the class crashed most. Almost always, the failure mode is the lb->kg conversion being skipped — *"44 lb, mg/kg order, the student dosed as if the patient were 44 kg."* Build the habit of writing the kg value as a separate line *before* computing the dose.

### Dimensional Analysis Sandbox

```yaml
name: Dimensional Analysis Sandbox
format: drag_and_drop
supports:
  primary: 127.403-c2A
  secondary: [127.403-c2B]
duration_min: 12
mode: single_player
equipment: one_device_per_student
learning_goal: >
  Using a drag-and-drop workspace, chain conversion factors so units
  cancel to reach the target unit with zero arithmetic errors across
  8 of 10 problems on first compute.
vocab_exercised:
  - Dimensional analysis
  - Conversion factor
  - Metric system (SI)
  - US customary units
content_model: conversion_chain_task
content:
  problem_pool_size: 20
  domains: [weight, volume, length, temperature, within_metric]
  each_problem_provides:
    - start_value_and_unit
    - target_unit
    - factor_bank                # 6-10 tiles, including 2-3 distractors
    - gold_chain                 # canonical sequence of factors
initial_state:
  first_problem_visible: true
  source_cell_populated: true
  chain_empty: true
  compute_disabled_until_units_cancel: true
controls:
  - drag_factor_tile
  - drop_into_chain
  - flip_factor                  # swap numerator / denominator
  - remove_tile
  - compute
  - next_problem
feedback:
  moment: real_time
  behaviors:
    - dropped factor visibly cancels matching units (soft strikethrough)
    - wrong-direction factor glows amber until flipped or removed
    - compute button enabled only when all units cancel to the target
    - after compute, gold chain shown side-by-side and edit count logged
scoring:
  correct_on_first_compute: +1
  correct_after_1_edit: +0.5
  correct_after_2_plus_edits: 0
  mastery_threshold: 8
win_condition:
  type: problem_set_complete
  mastery: score >= 8 / 10
reset:
  clears: [chain, source_cell, compute_state]
  preserves: [factor_bank, problem_pool, completed_problems]
student_view: >
  Source cell on the left with the starting value and unit; horizontal
  chain of empty slots to the right; target cell on the far right
  highlighted in cyan until achieved; factor bank as pill-shaped tiles
  below. A "unit tracker" bar above the chain shows live cancellation.
teacher_view: >
  Class success rate per problem; median edits per correct answer
  (a high median means the class is guessing and adjusting rather
  than reasoning forward from units).
accessibility:
  - Keyboard drag-and-drop (Tab to select, arrow keys to move, Space to drop)
  - Cancelled-unit strikethrough is accompanied by a font-weight change, not color alone
  - Screen reader announces factor dropped and resulting chain state
success_criteria: Student reaches 8/10 on first compute within one class period.
tech_stack:
  base: html+css+js
  libraries:
    - name: Tailwind CSS (Play CDN)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.tailwindcss.com
      purpose: Grid layout for source -> chain -> target; pill tiles for factors
    - name: Alpine.js
      cdn_url: https://unpkg.com/alpinejs@3.x.x/dist/cdn.min.js
      purpose: Chain state, unit cancellation logic, compute gate
    - name: SortableJS
      cdn_url: https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/sortablejs@1.15.2/Sortable.min.js
      purpose: Polished drag-and-drop (HTML5 native drag-and-drop looks amateurish)
visual_notes: >
  Spreadsheet-like grid for the chain; factors are pill-shaped tiles
  with numerator over denominator in tabular-figures type. Cancelled
  units fade to grey with a subtle strikethrough; uncancelled units
  stay in the accent color. Target unit pulses softly until the chain
  reaches it. No confetti or celebratory modal — the unit tracker
  turning fully cyan is the win moment.
```

**Instructions to student:** Each problem gives you a starting value with a unit and asks you to reach a target unit. Drag conversion factors from the bank below into the chain. If the unit glows amber, flip the factor — its numerator and denominator are in the wrong places. The compute button turns on only when your chain's units fully cancel to the target. Fewer edits = more points. Factor bank has distractors — don't just drag everything.

**Teacher notes:** Watch the median-edits-per-correct-answer number in the teacher view. If it's above 2, the class is still guessing. Do a 5-minute whiteboard demo of *unit-first* reasoning: pick the factor that cancels your current unit, not the factor that "looks right." Dimensional analysis only pays off as a habit — this sandbox is where that habit forms.

### mcg/mg Trap Trainer

```yaml
name: mcg/mg Trap Trainer
format: rapid_fire_quiz
supports:
  primary: 127.403-c2A
  secondary: [127.403-c1A, 127.403-c2F]
duration_min: 8
mode: single_player
equipment: one_device_per_student
learning_goal: >
  Across 20 rapid-fire medication-order prompts seeded with ISMP-flagged
  look-alike distractors (5 mg / 5 mcg / 0.5 mg / .5 mg / 5.0 mg),
  correctly classify the order as safe or unsafe and correctly identify
  the specific ISMP rule violated at >= 18/20 under 5 seconds per prompt.
vocab_exercised:
  - mcg vs. mg
  - Leading zero rule (ISMP)
  - Trailing zero rule (ISMP)
  - ISMP (Institute for Safe Medication Practices)
content_model: question_bank_with_distractors
content:
  prompt_pool_size: 50
  distractor_categories:
    - mg_mcg_confusion
    - missing_leading_zero
    - unsafe_trailing_zero
    - abbreviation_on_do_not_use_list   # "U" for units, "QD", "IU"
    - trap_safe                          # looks suspicious, is actually safe
  each_prompt_provides:
    - rendered_order_text
    - safe_or_unsafe
    - if_unsafe_reason
initial_state:
  timer_seconds_per_prompt: 5
  prompt_rotation: random_without_replacement
  score: 0
controls:
  - click_safe
  - click_unsafe
  - click_reason_chip          # appears only after click_unsafe
  - keyboard_shortcuts: [S, U, 1, 2, 3, 4]
feedback:
  moment: immediate
  on_correct:
    - green border flash (120 ms)
    - one-line ISMP citation appears for 1 s
  on_wrong:
    - red border flash (120 ms)
    - full rule statement shown for 2 s before auto-advance
scoring:
  correct_safe_or_unsafe: +1
  correct_reason_on_unsafe: +1 bonus
  wrong: -1
  timeout_no_answer: 0
  mastery_threshold: 18 / 20
win_condition:
  type: 20_prompts_completed
  mastery: score >= 18 / 20
reset:
  clears: [score, prompt_history, current_prompt]
  preserves: [prompt_pool]
student_view: >
  Medication order in very large type, centered on a flashcard-style
  surface. Two big buttons below: "Safe" and "Unsafe." On Unsafe click,
  the four reason chips fade in beneath. Timer bar slides across the top.
  Score and prompt counter at the bottom.
teacher_view: >
  Class heatmap of which distractor tripped the most students (the
  pedagogically valuable ones to debrief); per-category accuracy to
  identify whether the class is weak on mg/mcg vs. zero rules.
accessibility:
  - Keyboard shortcuts for every action (no mouse required)
  - Border-flash feedback, not full-screen strobe
  - Font size minimum 24 px for the order text
  - Screen reader announces the order and available actions
success_criteria: Student reaches 18/20 with no more than 1 misidentified reason within one class period.
tech_stack:
  base: html+css+js
  libraries:
    - name: Tailwind CSS (Play CDN)
      cdn_url: https://cdn.tailwindcss.com
      purpose: Flashcard layout, large type, button sizing
    - name: Alpine.js
      cdn_url: https://unpkg.com/alpinejs@3.x.x/dist/cdn.min.js
      purpose: Prompt rotation, timer, score, reason chip state
visual_notes: >
  Flashcard aesthetic — single large card on a neutral background, the
  order text dominates. Buttons are big and high-contrast. Border flash
  is a 2 px ring, soft and quick; never a full-screen color wash. Reason
  chips are small pills that slide up, not a modal. The ISMP citation
  on feedback is small italic type, not a banner.
```

**Instructions to student:** You will see 20 medication orders in a row, 5 seconds each. Tap **Safe** or **Unsafe**. If you tap Unsafe, you then pick which ISMP rule was broken: mg/mcg confusion, missing leading zero, trailing zero, or a Do-Not-Use abbreviation. Use keyboard shortcuts (S / U / 1 / 2 / 3 / 4) to move faster. Timeout = no points, no penalty — but no credit either.

**Teacher notes:** The heatmap tells you where the class is weakest. If the mg/mcg distractor trips >50% of students, drill that specifically in the next session — the decimal errors are faster to fix than the unit-confusion ones. This drill pairs well immediately after Drip-Rate Showdown: the calculation lives next to the formatting rule in clinical practice, and students should feel them as one skill.
