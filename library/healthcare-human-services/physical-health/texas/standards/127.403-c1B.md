## Learning objective

Students will be able to cooperate, contribute, and collaborate as a member of a healthcare team, using structured teamwork behaviors to support shared patient-care goals.

## Content stub

- **Three behaviors the TEKS names separately, distinguished:** *cooperate* (follow the shared plan, don't obstruct), *contribute* (bring your own role's work to the team), *collaborate* (build the plan together, adapt when conditions change)
- **Reliability as the foundation of team trust:** show up, follow through on commitments, flag early when you can't
- **Knowing your role and scope:** do your work; ask before acting outside your scope; hand off clearly when someone else's scope takes over
- **Sharing information up and down the team:** timely handoffs, speaking up when you notice a safety issue (CUS language — "I'm Concerned, I'm Uncomfortable, this is a Safety issue")
- **Mutual support:** step in when a teammate is overloaded; accept help without defensiveness when you are
- **Handling disagreement constructively:** separate the issue from the person; two-challenge rule when first concern is dismissed
- **Psychological safety:** the team behaviors that make it safe to raise concerns — directly tied to patient outcomes

## Vocabulary

- **Cooperate** — follow the shared team plan without obstructing; the baseline team behavior the TEKS names.
- **Contribute** — bring your own role's work to the team reliably and on time.
- **Collaborate** — build the plan together and adapt as conditions change; the most active of the three TEKS-named team behaviors.
- **Scope of practice** — the tasks a clinician is legally and institutionally authorized to perform based on role, license, and competency.
- **Handoff** — structured transfer of responsibility when another team member takes over. *(also in c1A)*
- **CUS language** — TeamSTEPPS safety-escalation script: "I'm **C**oncerned, I'm **U**ncomfortable, this is a **S**afety issue."
- **Two-challenge rule** — TeamSTEPPS norm allowing a team member to voice a safety concern twice if the first challenge is dismissed, obligating the receiver to acknowledge.
- **Psychological safety** — team climate where members can raise concerns, admit mistakes, and ask questions without fear of embarrassment or retribution; linked to patient-safety outcomes (Edmondson).
- **Mutual support** — TeamSTEPPS competency: noticing when a teammate is overloaded and stepping in; accepting help without defensiveness.

## Assessment stub

- Structured team task (e.g., triage simulation, patient-transfer drill): observer rubric scores each student separately on cooperation, contribution, and collaboration
- Scenario analysis: a teammate is consistently not pulling their weight during clinical rotations — describe the appropriate escalation path and the language to use
- Reflection: describe a team you've been on (sports, work, school, healthcare) — identify two behaviors that built trust and two that eroded it, and state how outcomes were affected

## References

- **TeamSTEPPS 3.0 (AHRQ)** — teamwork curriculum defining the four core competencies (team leadership, situation monitoring, mutual support, communication) and the CUS / two-challenge-rule scripts.
- **SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation)** — the default structured-handoff tool that operationalizes "contribute" and "collaborate" during care transitions.
- **Joint Commission sentinel-event root-cause data** — communication and teamwork failures rank among the leading contributors to serious patient harm, making team behavior a patient-safety issue rather than a soft skill.
- **Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization* (psychological safety research)** — foundational source for the link between team psychological safety and healthcare outcomes.
