## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify how team members across the five health science systems — diagnostic, therapeutic, health informatics, support services, and biotechnology R&D — collaborate during a patient's care episode and name the specific handoffs that determine care quality.

## Content stub

- **Why cross-system collaboration matters:** every patient encounter touches multiple systems; quality and safety live in the handoffs between them, not inside any single role
- **A concrete patient episode, traced across all five systems:** an adult presenting to the ED with chest pain
  - *Therapeutic* (triage RN, ED physician, paramedic) — initial assessment, treatment decisions
  - *Diagnostic* (EKG tech, lab phlebotomist, medical laboratory scientist, radiologic technologist) — data to confirm or rule out MI
  - *Health informatics* (medical scribe, HIM coder, clinical informaticist) — documentation, diagnostic coding, care-transition summary
  - *Support services* (patient transport, central sterile, environmental services, security) — move the patient safely, make the environment ready
  - *Biotechnology R&D* (potentially: clinical research coordinator enrolling the patient in a registry; pharmaceutical rep who delivered the therapy) — connection to research and innovation
- **Handoff points where collaboration happens (or fails):**
  - *Therapeutic ↔ diagnostic* — accurate order entry and correct patient ID drive correct test results
  - *Diagnostic ↔ therapeutic* — critical-value callback protocols, radiologist consultation
  - *Any system ↔ informatics* — every role documents; poor documentation fails every other handoff downstream
  - *Therapeutic ↔ support* — transport delays, equipment availability, supply-chain failures reach the bedside
- **Quality framework (IOM / NAM Six Aims):** safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable — collaboration across systems is the mechanism by which all six are achieved
- **Tools that make cross-system collaboration work (from earlier standards):**
  - SBAR and structured handoffs (c2E, c3A, c4C)
  - EHR shared documentation (c3D, c12A)
  - Team huddles and interdisciplinary rounds (c4C)
  - Clear scope-of-practice boundaries so handoffs are to the right role (c6, c9A)
- **When collaboration breaks down:** most sentinel events (Joint Commission) have a cross-system handoff failure in the root cause — lab value not communicated, imaging not acted on, discharge summary incomplete, EHR documentation ambiguous

## Vocabulary

- **Handoff point** — a cross-system transition (therapeutic ↔ diagnostic, any-system ↔ informatics, therapeutic ↔ support) where collaboration quality determines care safety.
- **Six Aims for Improvement (IOM/NAM)** — safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable; the quality framework achieved via cross-system collaboration.
- **IPEC Core Competencies** — the Interprofessional Education Collaborative consensus framework for interdisciplinary collaborative practice. *(also in c8A)*
- **Critical value callback** — the diagnostic-to-therapeutic handoff where an abnormal lab or imaging result is communicated directly to the ordering clinician.
- **Interdisciplinary rounds** — patient-focused care-team meeting where each of the five systems contributes to the day's plan. *(also in c4C)*
- **Patient-episode trace** — the mapping of every team member touched across the five systems during a single care event (e.g., chest pain workup).
- **Sentinel event** — a serious Joint Commission–defined patient-harm event; most root causes include a cross-system handoff failure. *(also in c3A, c11C)*
- **Structured handoff** — SBAR or equivalent formats that make cross-discipline transitions reliable.
- **Scope-of-practice boundary** — the delineation that ensures a handoff goes to the right role for the next step.

## Assessment stub

- Patient-episode trace: given a clinical scenario (stroke, pediatric asthma admission, outpatient surgery, home-health start-of-care), map every team member touched across all five systems; name at least two handoffs between systems and what could go wrong at each
- Role identification: given 20 specific healthcare contributions to one patient's 48-hour care episode (e.g., "the lab tech who ran the troponin," "the unit clerk who built the admission packet"), identify which system each belongs to and the downstream role that depends on that contribution
- Short answer: explain in 150–200 words how a single missed cross-system handoff (e.g., a critical lab value not phoned to the ordering provider) could cause patient harm; name the quality-framework aim (safe, effective, timely, etc.) that is violated

## References

- **NAM / IOM *Crossing the Quality Chasm* (2001) — Six Aims for Improvement** — the framework that defines "quality health care" and makes cross-system collaboration a quality requirement, not optional.
- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0** — tools and protocols specifically designed for cross-discipline and cross-unit collaboration in US healthcare.
- **Joint Commission Sentinel Event Statistics** — annual data identifying handoff and communication failures as leading root causes of serious patient-harm events; evidence base for the "collaboration across systems" framing.
- **Interprofessional Education Collaborative (IPEC) *Core Competencies for Interprofessional Collaborative Practice*** — consensus framework for the collaborative behaviors expected across healthcare professions.
- **NCHSE Health Science Career Clusters** — the five-system model the TEKS draws from, which organizes careers into the collaborative structure this standard asks students to identify.
