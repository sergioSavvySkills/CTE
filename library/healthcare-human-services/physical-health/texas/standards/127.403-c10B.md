## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify evidence-based wellness strategies for preventing disease — organized by the three levels of prevention (primary, secondary, tertiary) — and evaluate individual-level strategies against the population-level evidence for each.

## Content stub

- **Builds on c2K, which introduced the concepts:** c10B asks students to identify the *strategies* — concrete, named, evidence-based interventions — not just the levels
- **Primary prevention — stop disease before it starts:**
  - *Immunization* — childhood and adult vaccines per ACIP schedule; flu, COVID-19, HPV, Tdap, pneumococcal, shingles
  - *Behavioral strategies* — tobacco cessation, moderate alcohol use, physical activity, balanced nutrition, sleep
  - *Environmental strategies* — seatbelt use, helmet use, sunscreen, home fire-alarm maintenance, poison-control
  - *Hand hygiene* — the single most effective infection-prevention strategy in healthcare and community settings (WHO)
  - *Occupational safety* — PPE, ergonomics, bloodborne-pathogen training (OSHA)
  - *Environmental public-health measures* — clean water, sanitation, air quality, vector control
  - *Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) for HIV* — pharmacologic primary prevention for high-risk populations
- **Secondary prevention — catch disease early, when treatment is most effective:**
  - *Screenings per USPSTF grade A/B recommendations* — mammography, cervical screening, colorectal screening, lung cancer screening for eligible smokers, AAA screening, diabetes screening, BP screening, HIV and hepatitis C screening
  - *Newborn screenings* — Texas panel of state-mandated newborn screens
  - *Developmental screening* — AAP Bright Futures recommendations
  - *Vision and hearing screening* — school-age
  - *Contact tracing and testing* — a secondary-prevention strategy during outbreaks
- **Tertiary prevention — limit damage after disease is established:**
  - *Cardiac rehabilitation* after MI
  - *Stroke rehabilitation* — PT, OT, speech therapy
  - *Diabetes self-management education* to prevent complications
  - *Chronic-pain management programs*
  - *Mental-health relapse prevention* — medication adherence, therapy, peer support
  - *Falls-prevention programs* for older adults at risk
- **Individual vs. population level:**
  - Some strategies act at the individual level (e.g., personal PrEP adherence)
  - Some require population-level infrastructure (e.g., safe water, herd immunity, clean-air regulations)
  - Effective wellness strategy typically requires both
- **Evidence strength matters:** USPSTF grades (A, B, C, D, I) indicate how strong the evidence is and how clinicians should counsel; students should recognize that "everyone should get X" is rarely the right framing — recommendations are population- and risk-specific
- **Cost, access, and justice dimensions (crosswalks with c2P, c9B):** effective wellness strategies are only useful if reachable; access barriers (insurance, geography, language, cost) are themselves a disease-prevention issue
- **Distinction from c2K:** c2K asked students to identify the *concepts* of health and wellness; c10B asks for the *strategies* — specific named interventions with an evidence base

## Vocabulary

- **Primary prevention** — interventions that stop disease before it starts (vaccines, seatbelts, hand hygiene, PrEP, smoking cessation).
- **Secondary prevention** — interventions that catch disease early through screening (mammography, colonoscopy, BP check, newborn screen).
- **Tertiary prevention** — interventions that limit damage after disease is established (cardiac rehab, stroke rehab, diabetes self-management).
- **USPSTF grade** — letters A, B, C, D, I that rate the strength of a prevention recommendation and the evidence behind it; drives insurer coverage.
- **ACIP schedule** — the CDC Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices schedule of recommended childhood and adult vaccines.
- **Newborn screening panel** — Texas-mandated state newborn screens; a secondary-prevention infrastructure example.
- **PrEP** — pre-exposure prophylaxis for HIV; pharmacologic primary prevention for high-risk populations.
- **Cardiac rehabilitation** — a tertiary-prevention program of supervised exercise, education, and counseling after a cardiac event.
- **Herd immunity** — population-level primary prevention achieved when enough individuals are immune to break transmission chains.
- **The Community Guide** — evidence reviews from the Community Preventive Services Task Force on community-level strategies (e.g., tobacco taxation).

## Assessment stub

- Strategy classification: given 25 specific interventions (childhood MMR vaccine, fluoride in municipal water, colonoscopy at age 45, medication-assisted treatment for opioid-use disorder, stroke rehab, annual flu shot, seatbelt law, mammography for average-risk women aged 50–74, diabetes self-management class, daily baby aspirin for post-MI patient, smoke-alarm ordinance, HPV vaccine, tobacco-tax increase, falls-prevention Tai Chi program for older adults, PrEP), classify each as primary, secondary, or tertiary prevention and explain the reasoning
- USPSTF research: pick one Grade A or B recommendation; summarize (1) the population it applies to, (2) the intervention, (3) the evidence behind the grade, (4) one access barrier that would prevent the intervention from reaching everyone eligible; cite the USPSTF recommendation statement
- Personal wellness plan: design a four-week personal wellness plan naming at least two strategies at the primary level (that apply to you now) and one secondary-prevention action (appropriate to your age and risk profile per USPSTF); justify each strategy with an evidence source
- Short response: in 150–200 words, identify one disease (e.g., type 2 diabetes, colorectal cancer, cardiovascular disease, cervical cancer) and name one concrete strategy at each of the three prevention levels that reduces its burden; explain why the most effective overall approach typically uses all three together

## References

- **US Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) recommendation statements (uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org)** — the authoritative US source for secondary-prevention screening recommendations, with evidence grades.
- **CDC Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) schedules** — current US childhood and adult immunization schedules.
- **Healthy People 2030 (HHS ODPHP)** — federal framework of measurable wellness and prevention objectives; useful for strategy identification.
- **AAP *Bright Futures* Guidelines** — pediatric primary-care and preventive-services schedule.
- **WHO *Five Moments for Hand Hygiene*** — evidence-based hand-hygiene framework (crosswalks with c11B).
- **AHA / ACC Secondary Prevention Guidelines (post-MI)** and **ADA Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes** — disease-specific tertiary-prevention references.
- **CDC MMWR (*Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report*)** — primary source for US surveillance data and prevention-program effectiveness.
- **Texas DSHS Newborn Screening Program** — Texas-specific secondary-prevention example relevant to the course.
- **Community Preventive Services Task Force (The Community Guide)** — evidence reviews for community-level prevention strategies (e.g., tobacco taxation, seatbelt laws).
