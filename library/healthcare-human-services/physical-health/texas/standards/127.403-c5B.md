## Learning objective

Students will be able to recognize how healthcare career decisions — choosing a role, choosing a setting, changing jobs, changing specialties, taking time out of the workforce — affect income, benefits, credential portability, and long-term career trajectory.

## Content stub

- **Why career decisions compound:** the role and setting a student chooses at age 18 or 22 shapes the credentials earned, the specialties available, the pay trajectory, and the options 10 years later — decisions are reversible but with cost
- **Decision points students will face:**
  - *Entry role* — CNA vs. MA vs. phlebotomy vs. direct-to-college paths (each opens different next steps)
  - *Setting* — hospital vs. clinic vs. long-term care vs. home health vs. public health (each has different pay, schedule, and skill exposure)
  - *Specialty* — ED vs. OR vs. med-surg vs. peds vs. mental health (each changes day-to-day work and future options)
  - *Further education* — continuing at full-time work or pausing for school
  - *Changing jobs* — switching employer, switching role, switching state
- **Causes of changing employment situations:**
  - *Pull causes* — better pay, better schedule, growth opportunity, geographic move, specialty interest
  - *Push causes* — burnout, workplace safety, toxic team, layoff, scope or ethics conflict, life event (caregiving, illness)
  - *Structural causes* — hospital closure (especially rural), merger and acquisition, Medicare payment changes, pandemic-driven shifts
- **Effects of changing employment situations on the worker:**
  - *Income* — hourly rate, shift differentials, overtime access, benefits (health insurance, retirement match, tuition reimbursement)
  - *Benefits continuity* — health insurance gaps, retirement-account rollovers, PTO and accrued leave
  - *Credential portability* — state-by-state licensure variation; Nurse Licensure Compact states vs. non-compact; re-credentialing costs and time
  - *Seniority* — starting over at new employer resets PTO accrual, scheduling priority, tuition benefits
  - *Resume trajectory* — frequent moves can look like flight risk; strategic moves can accelerate pay
- **Effects on patients and teams:** turnover reduces continuity of care; high-churn units correlate with higher patient-safety events (AHRQ research); each departure requires onboarding time and institutional knowledge rebuild
- **Macroeconomic context (crosswalks with c2N/c2O):** healthcare labor market is sector-wide tight; some roles (RN, surgical tech, respiratory therapist, medical assistant) face sustained shortages; others grow faster than training pipelines; burnout and aging workforce drive current vacancies

## Vocabulary

- **Push cause** — a factor that drives a worker away from a job: burnout, workplace safety, toxic team, layoff, scope conflict, life event.
- **Pull cause** — a factor that attracts a worker to a new job: better pay, schedule, growth opportunity, geographic move, specialty interest.
- **Structural cause** — a system-level driver of employment change: hospital closure, merger, Medicare payment change, pandemic-driven shift.
- **Credential portability** — the ability to carry a license or certification across state lines; varies by profession and compact status.
- **Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC)** — the NCSBN-administered interstate compact that lets RNs and LVNs practice across compact states without re-licensing; Texas is a member. *(also in c6)*
- **Benefits continuity** — preserving health insurance coverage, retirement-account rollover, and accrued PTO when changing jobs.
- **Seniority** — tenure-based priority for scheduling, PTO accrual, and tuition benefits that resets at a new employer.
- **Turnover** — the rate at which workers leave a unit or employer; AHRQ research links high turnover to worse patient-safety outcomes.
- **Shift differential** — added hourly pay for evening, night, or weekend shifts; a compensation component that varies by employer and setting.

## Assessment stub

- Decision-tree exercise: starting from "current HS senior," map three distinct 10-year career paths leading to the same end role (e.g., BSN RN); at each branching decision, name one cause that could redirect the path and the effect on credentials, income, and time to target
- Cause-effect analysis: read three short case studies of healthcare workers changing jobs (a new grad leaving their first hospital after 18 months; an RN leaving bedside for case management; a CNA using tuition reimbursement to complete an ADN); for each, identify the push and pull causes and list three downstream effects on the worker and the employer
- Personal reflection: write 200–300 words on one potential career decision you might face in the next 5–10 years; name two causes that could drive you to make a change, two effects you would need to plan for, and one mitigation (savings cushion, credential portability research, networking, etc.)

## References

- **US Bureau of Labor Statistics *Occupational Outlook Handbook* — "Job Outlook" and "Projections" sections** — federal data on healthcare labor-market trends.
- **AHRQ research on nurse turnover and patient outcomes** — evidence base for the team/patient effects of employment changes.
- **KFF and Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker** — accessible data on healthcare workforce shortages, wage trends, and geographic variation.
- **Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC) — National Council of State Boards of Nursing** — the primary US mechanism for interstate nursing license portability; Texas is a compact state.
- **American Hospital Association *Workforce reports*** — industry-side analyses of turnover causes and costs.
- **BLS *Employment Situation* monthly release** — the primary data source for healthcare employment trends over time.
