## Learning objective

Students will be able to explain "teaming" — the intentional practice of coordinating across disciplines, shifts, and settings to deliver quality care — distinguish it from simply working in a group, and describe the conditions that make teaming possible in a healthcare workplace.

## Content stub

- **Teaming vs. a team, distinguished (Edmondson):** a *team* is a stable group that works together repeatedly; *teaming* is the active, on-the-fly coordination work that happens when the "team" is constantly changing — exactly the condition most US healthcare settings operate in (rotating shifts, float staff, consultants, cross-unit transfers)
- **Why the concept matters in healthcare:** the average hospitalized patient is cared for by dozens of people across roles, shifts, and units; a stable "team" is the exception; the skill is doing team-level work without a stable team
- **What teaming requires — four conditions (from implementation-science research):**
  - *Shared goal, explicit and aligned with quality aims* — safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, equitable (IOM/NAM)
  - *Clarity about roles and scope* — each contributor knows what they do and what they hand off; reinforced by licensure and scope-of-practice boundaries
  - *Reliable communication protocols* — SBAR, check-back, brief/huddle/debrief, standardized handoffs (from c2E, c3A, c4C)
  - *Psychological safety* — the team member on their first day feels safe to speak up when something is off; Edmondson's research shows this single factor predicts team performance more than any other
- **Teaming behaviors, named:**
  - Orient newcomers fast — the float nurse, the new resident, the student on rotation
  - Explicitly name who is leading *this* moment (the code? the rounds? the transfer?)
  - Close the loop on every handoff — "I heard you say X; I will do Y by Z"
  - Stop the line when something is wrong — CUS language, two-challenge rule
  - Debrief after hard events — what went well, what didn't, what changes
- **Quality outcomes teaming is meant to produce (crosswalks c7B):** fewer sentinel events, better patient experience, faster decisions, less duplicated work, less clinician burnout
- **Teaming as a learnable skill, not a personality trait:** the TEKS frames it as a concept to *explain*, but the underlying idea is that teaming is taught and practiced — it is the core content of programs like TeamSTEPPS

## Assessment stub

- Define-and-distinguish: in 150–200 words, explain what teaming is and how it differs from either "working in a team" or "working independently"; give one healthcare example for each of the three modes
- Conditions match: given a list of 10 workplace behaviors (e.g., "the charge nurse names who is leading the code before it starts," "the resident dismisses the med student's speak-up"), sort as supporting or undermining teaming and name which of the four conditions is at play
- Short case analysis: read a short sentinel-event summary (or TeamSTEPPS case study); identify which teaming condition failed and what single change would most likely have prevented the harm
- Reflection: describe a time you were part of a fluid, cross-role group effort (clinical rotation, group project, team sport, job); identify which teaming behaviors were present and which were missing, and explain how the outcome was affected

## References

- **Edmondson, *Teaming: How Organizations Learn, Innovate, and Compete in the Knowledge Economy*** — the foundational source for the "teaming" concept this standard asks students to explain; distinguishes teaming from stable teamwork.
- **Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization*** — evidence base for psychological safety as the core enabler of teaming.
- **AHRQ TeamSTEPPS 3.0** — the operational US healthcare curriculum for teaming behaviors (brief/huddle/debrief, CUS, two-challenge rule, closed-loop communication).
- **NAM / IOM *Crossing the Quality Chasm*** — the quality framework that makes teaming a quality requirement rather than a soft skill.
- **Interprofessional Education Collaborative (IPEC) Core Competencies** — consensus framework for the interdisciplinary teaming behaviors expected across US healthcare professions.
- **Joint Commission Sentinel Event Alert #40 (2008) and Sentinel Event Alert #58 (2017)** — document how teaming failures produce patient harm and what institutional conditions support teaming.
