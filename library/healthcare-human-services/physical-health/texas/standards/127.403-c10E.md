## Learning objective

Students will be able to demonstrate an understanding of how diversity and cultural practices shape contemporary healthcare — by explaining specific effects, identifying appropriate adaptations, and describing how they themselves would adjust practice when caring for patients who differ from them in language, culture, religion, ability, gender identity, immigration status, or socioeconomic circumstance.

## Content stub

- **Builds on c2P, which identified the influences:** c10E asks students to *demonstrate understanding* — apply the knowledge, explain in their own words, propose specific adaptations, not just recognize that diversity matters
- **Performance expectations at this standard's depth:**
  - Explain, in plain language, how a specific cultural or diversity factor shapes a healthcare encounter
  - Propose concrete behavioral adaptations ("I would ask the patient which gender clinician they prefer," not just "I would be culturally competent")
  - Recognize the limits of one's own knowledge and name the appropriate next step (interpreter request, chaplain consult, community-health worker referral)
  - Distinguish cultural *humility* (ongoing posture of learning) from cultural *competence* (finite set of facts to memorize)
- **Diversity and cultural factors to apply:**
  - *Language* — qualified medical interpreters, translated materials, patient-chosen communication preferences; never family as interpreter for substantive clinical conversations
  - *Culture of origin* — communication norms, decision-making authority (individual vs. family), concepts of illness and healing, modesty expectations, food practices
  - *Religion and spirituality* — dietary rules (halal, kosher, fasting), blood-product refusal (Jehovah's Witnesses), prayer and ritual during treatment, end-of-life practices, chaplaincy and spiritual-care referral
  - *Gender identity and sexual orientation* — affirming language, correct pronouns and names, sensitivity to past healthcare-system harms, organ-inventory approach for clinical care rather than assumed anatomy
  - *Disability* — communication adaptations (sign interpretation, plain language, written materials), accessible facilities, supported decision-making, avoiding "medical model" condescension
  - *Immigration status* — information-sharing concerns, fear of enforcement affecting healthcare-seeking, sanctuary-policy awareness, emergency-only Medicaid realities
  - *Race and ethnicity* — trust gaps rooted in documented history (Tuskegee, sterilization abuses, ongoing disparities); contemporary disparity patterns (Black maternal mortality, Indigenous chronic-disease burden)
  - *Socioeconomic status* — inability to afford recommended care, transportation, time-off from work, housing instability as a health issue
  - *Age and life stage* — pediatric assent, adolescent confidentiality, adult autonomy, geriatric communication norms
- **Healthcare practices that exist because of these influences (contemporary-practice examples):**
  - Qualified-interpreter services (Title VI compliance)
  - Chaplain and spiritual-care departments in hospitals
  - Doula programs in high-maternal-mortality communities
  - Sliding-scale and FQHC clinics for the uninsured
  - Sanctuary-policy clinics
  - LGBTQ+-affirming care programs and clinics
  - Culturally tailored education materials at appropriate reading levels
  - Community-health-worker (promotores de salud) programs
- **Self-awareness is part of demonstrating understanding:**
  - Recognize your own cultural defaults (US healthcare has a dominant biomedical culture that feels neutral to insiders)
  - Name biases you may hold and the behaviors you will use to counter them
  - Commit to continued learning rather than claiming arrival
- **When to escalate rather than improvise:** communication failures, suspected coercion, family-vs-patient conflicts about care decisions, religious directives about treatment, cultural concerns about disclosure — these are ethics-committee, social-work, or chaplaincy consults, not solo-student decisions

## Vocabulary

- **Cultural humility** — an ongoing posture of learning and self-examination; distinguished by Tervalon & Murray-García (1998) from cultural competence.
- **Cultural competence** — a finite set of facts about specific cultures; a weaker framing than humility because cultures are too varied to fully memorize.
- **CLAS Standards** — HHS Office of Minority Health's *National Culturally and Linguistically Appropriate Services* Standards; the operational source for adaptation behaviors.
- **Qualified medical interpreter** — a trained, vetted interpreter (never a family member for substantive clinical conversations) required under Title VI.
- **Organ inventory** — an affirming-care approach for transgender and gender-diverse patients that documents anatomy present rather than assuming anatomy by gender.
- **Affirming care** — use of correct pronouns, chosen name, and responsive language for LGBTQ+ patients; grounded in National LGBTQIA+ Health Education Center guidance.
- **Chaplaincy / spiritual care** — the hospital-based department that provides culturally and religiously responsive support, including end-of-life rituals.
- **Promotor/a de salud** — a community-health-worker role in Latinx communities; a culturally tailored support structure relevant to Texas.
- **Explanatory model (Kleinman)** — the patient's own understanding of what is wrong, why it happened, and what should be done; a tool of cultural humility.
- **Sanctuary clinic** — a healthcare setting with policies limiting immigration-enforcement cooperation so patients can seek care without fear.

## Assessment stub

- Adaptation portfolio: for four named patient scenarios (an LEP patient presenting with chest pain; a Deaf adult in the ED; a Jehovah's Witness patient refusing transfusion for a scheduled surgery; a transgender adolescent coming for routine well-care), produce a written adaptation plan naming three specific behaviors you would use, one resource you would involve, and one thing you would check about yourself; justify each choice with a source
- Role-play demonstration: in a 5-minute simulated encounter with a peer playing a patient who differs from you in one meaningful way (cultural, linguistic, religious, or ability), demonstrate appropriate adaptations; observer rubric scores direct address, sentence length, respectful language, awareness of what you do not know, and appropriate escalation
- Explain-in-plain-language task: pick one CLAS Standard or one ADA communication requirement; in a 3-minute oral explanation to a peer, explain what the requirement is, why it exists, and what contemporary healthcare practice it drove into existence
- Reflection: identify one aspect of your own background (language, culture, religion, ability, experience) that makes you a better fit for certain patients and one aspect that may create distance; name two concrete behaviors you will use to narrow the distance

## References

- **HHS Office of Minority Health *National CLAS Standards*** — the federal framework for culturally and linguistically appropriate services; operational source for adaptation behaviors.
- **Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964** and **Americans with Disabilities Act** — legal bases for language access and communication adaptations.
- **Joint Commission *Advancing Effective Communication, Cultural Competence, and Patient- and Family-Centered Care*** — accreditation-level operational standards.
- **AHRQ *National Healthcare Quality and Disparities Report* (annual)** — evidence base for the contemporary disparities this standard addresses.
- **National LGBTQIA+ Health Education Center (Fenway Institute)** — authoritative source for affirming-care practices for LGBTQ+ patients.
- **National Association of the Deaf (NAD) guidance on healthcare interpretation** — operational source for Deaf-patient care.
- **Kleinman, *The Illness Narratives*** — foundational framework for cultural humility and the patient's explanatory model of illness.
- **Tervalon & Murray-García (1998) *Cultural Humility versus Cultural Competence*** — seminal article distinguishing the two framings.
- **Texas DSHS Office of Border Public Health** — Texas-specific cultural and linguistic context relevant to the course state.
