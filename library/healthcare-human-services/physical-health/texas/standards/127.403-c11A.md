## Learning objective

Students will be able to identify the six regulatory and standards-setting agencies named in the TEKS — WHO, CDC, OSHA, FDA, The Joint Commission, and NIH — describe the jurisdiction and function of each, and match specific healthcare practices to the agency that governs them.

## Content stub

- **Why this standard matters:** every daily clinical safety practice — from hand hygiene to PPE to incident reporting to device recalls to vaccine scheduling — traces back to one or more of these agencies; knowing which agency sets which rule lets students trust (and question) the rules appropriately
- **The six named agencies — jurisdiction, function, and the daily practices they drive:**

  **1. World Health Organization (WHO)** — global, not a US regulator
  - *Function:* global public-health standards, pandemic coordination, International Health Regulations (IHR), guideline development
  - *Daily-practice touchpoints:* *Five Moments for Hand Hygiene* (the operational framework used in most US hospitals); WHO Surgical Safety Checklist; WHO Essential Medicines List; WHO International Classification of Diseases (ICD) — the basis for US ICD-10-CM
  - *Key distinction:* WHO recommends; it does not legally bind US practice. US agencies adopt or adapt WHO guidance.

  **2. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)** — US federal, under HHS
  - *Function:* US disease surveillance, outbreak response, prevention guidelines, immunization recommendations through ACIP, standard and transmission-based precautions
  - *Daily-practice touchpoints:* ACIP immunization schedule (what vaccines go in every patient), CDC Guideline for Isolation Precautions (standard + contact + droplet + airborne), HAI prevention bundles (CLABSI, CAUTI, VAP, SSI), outbreak investigation, MMWR surveillance reports
  - *Key distinction:* CDC guidelines are not statutes but are widely adopted by states, facilities, and accreditors — so they effectively regulate practice.

  **3. Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)** — US federal, under DOL, with state OSHA plans
  - *Function:* workplace safety for employees — including healthcare workers
  - *Daily-practice touchpoints:* Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) — the rule behind PPE, sharps containers, post-exposure protocols; Hazard Communication Standard (29 CFR 1910.1200) — the rule behind SDS and chemical labeling; Respiratory Protection Standard (29 CFR 1910.134) — the rule behind annual N95 fit testing; workplace-violence prevention guidance
  - *Key distinction:* OSHA's primary customer is the worker, not the patient — but worker safety and patient safety overlap extensively.

  **4. U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)** — US federal, under HHS
  - *Function:* regulates drugs, medical devices, biologics, blood products, tobacco, food safety; premarket approval and post-market surveillance
  - *Daily-practice touchpoints:* FDA-approved prescribing information (Prescribing Information format, 21 CFR 201.56/57), drug recalls, MedWatch adverse-event reporting, Medical Device Reporting (MDR, 21 CFR 803), Emergency Use Authorizations, Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies (REMS)
  - *Key distinction:* FDA approves products; it does not directly regulate *how* a clinician uses them (that is state licensure + standard of care).

  **5. The Joint Commission (TJC)** — private, non-profit accreditor (often confused with a government agency)
  - *Function:* voluntary accreditation for US hospitals, health systems, and some other settings; sets standards that accredited facilities must meet; accreditation is typically required for Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement (through CMS "deeming" authority)
  - *Daily-practice touchpoints:* National Patient Safety Goals (annual), Sentinel Event reporting, "Do Not Use" abbreviation list, universal protocol for wrong-site surgery, tracer-methodology surveys
  - *Key distinction:* TJC is *private*, but operates with quasi-regulatory force because of CMS deeming; not the only accreditor (DNV, HFAP are others) but the dominant one for hospitals.

  **6. National Institutes of Health (NIH)** — US federal, under HHS
  - *Function:* US federal biomedical research agency — the world's largest public funder of biomedical research; runs 27 institutes and centers
  - *Daily-practice touchpoints:* research funding that produces the evidence base behind most guidelines; human-subjects protections (via OHRP for IRB oversight), ClinicalTrials.gov registration, data-sharing requirements; NIH NCCIH evidence on complementary/integrative medicine
  - *Key distinction:* NIH is primarily a *research* funder, not a *practice* regulator — but the evidence it produces ultimately shapes what CDC, FDA, and professional organizations recommend.

- **How the agencies relate to each other:**
  - WHO and CDC collaborate on global public health; CDC adapts WHO guidance for the US
  - CDC and OSHA often overlap on infection control; OSHA incorporates CDC recommendations into enforceable standards
  - FDA and CDC both track adverse events but through different pipelines
  - Joint Commission standards often reference or incorporate CDC and WHO guidance, giving them accreditation-level force
  - State agencies (Texas DSHS, Texas Medical Board) translate federal rules into state enforcement; students in Texas will see state-level implementation of these federal rules in their clinical rotations
- **Other agencies worth naming in passing (not TEKS-required but students will encounter):** CMS (Medicare/Medicaid payment and Conditions of Participation), HRSA (safety-net funding), SAMHSA (behavioral health), EPA (environmental health), Texas DSHS, Texas Medical Board, Texas Board of Nursing

## Vocabulary

- **World Health Organization (WHO)** — global public-health standards body; source of the Five Moments for Hand Hygiene, Surgical Safety Checklist, and ICD.
- **CDC** — US federal disease-surveillance agency under HHS; publishes ACIP schedules, isolation-precaution guidelines, and HAI prevention bundles.
- **OSHA** — federal workplace-safety agency under DOL; enforces the Bloodborne Pathogens, Hazard Communication, and Respiratory Protection Standards.
- **FDA** — federal agency regulating drugs, devices, biologics, and tobacco; runs premarket approval and post-market surveillance (MedWatch, MDR).
- **The Joint Commission (TJC)** — private non-profit accreditor of US hospitals; publishes National Patient Safety Goals and "Do Not Use" list.
- **NIH** — federal biomedical-research funder and world's largest public biomedical funder; 27 institutes and centers, plus OHRP and ClinicalTrials.gov.
- **CMS deeming authority** — the mechanism that gives Joint Commission accreditation effectively regulatory force by linking it to Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement.
- **National Patient Safety Goals (NPSGs)** — Joint Commission's annual bedside-safety priorities that accredited facilities must meet.
- **Five Moments for Hand Hygiene** — WHO's operational hand-hygiene framework adopted in most US hospitals.
- **29 CFR 1910.1030** — OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard; the federal rule behind PPE, sharps containers, and post-exposure protocols.

## Assessment stub

- Agency-to-rule matching: given 25 specific healthcare practices or requirements (annual N95 fit testing; hand-hygiene policy posted in every patient room; the childhood MMR vaccine schedule; "Do Not Use" abbreviation list in the EHR; an FDA drug recall notice; the CLABSI prevention bundle; sharps-container disposal rules; OSHA 300 log on workplace injuries; MedWatch adverse-event reporting; sepsis-bundle compliance; IRB oversight of a clinical study; post-surgery surgical-site infection surveillance; clinical-trials registry filing; hazard communication SDS binders; pandemic quarantine authority; pharmacy REMS-controlled drug tracking; bloodborne-pathogen training; Time-Out before procedures; ICD-10 diagnostic coding; WHO Surgical Safety Checklist implementation; MMWR disease surveillance; medical-device reporting after patient harm; flu-vaccine requirement for staff; tuberculosis screening for healthcare workers; accreditation survey), identify which of the six agencies primarily drives each (or which pair/combination, for overlapping cases)
- Research short-answer: pick one agency; in 200–300 words, describe (a) the agency's jurisdiction, (b) two specific, citable rules or guidelines it issues that a student on clinical rotation would see in practice, (c) the enforcement mechanism (statute, accreditation, voluntary adoption); cite at least three authoritative sources
- Scenario walk: a needlestick injury occurs on a hospital floor during a clinical rotation. Walk through which agency's rule governs each step of the response (exposure evaluation, source-patient testing, post-exposure prophylaxis, OSHA log entry, workers'-comp claim, facility incident report, any MedWatch relevance)
- Short response: in 150–200 words, explain why the Joint Commission — a private non-profit — has effectively regulatory force in US hospitals despite not being a government agency; use the CMS deeming-authority mechanism in your explanation
