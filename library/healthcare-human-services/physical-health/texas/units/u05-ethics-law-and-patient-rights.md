# u05 — Ethics, Law, and Patient Rights

## Purpose and description

u05 is the **ethics and law unit** — where students learn the principles that guide healthcare decisions, the rules that bind practice, and the rights that patients hold. Six atoms cover ethical principles, HIPAA and confidentiality, patient rights and informed consent, scope of practice and licensure, malpractice and healthcare law, and the emerging ethics of healthcare technology.

This is one of the **heaviest cognitive-load units** in the course — 4 weeks, 6 atoms, all knowledge-type, discussion-based pedagogy (u04 competes at 5 atoms with all-skill practice). The unit anchors the **Ethics Case Analysis** performance task, which integrates content from u01-u05 into a multi-part case response.

## Why this unit exists

Healthcare carries obligations: to do good, to avoid harm, to respect choice, to act justly, to keep secrets, to practice within limits. Students need a working vocabulary for these obligations before they can reason about any specific clinical situation. Placing ethics and law in the middle of the course — after students have met careers, teams, bodies, and communication — means they have concrete context for what the principles apply to.

§127.403 gives this content explicit weight: KS area (c)(9) has 5 standards and (c)(10) has 5 more. Many are assessed in practicum and credentialing exams downstream. Students who understand the framework now will make safer decisions later.

## Placement in the course (why u05 and not later)

Middle placement (after u01-u04, before u06-u08) is deliberate:

- **Needs:** u03 Maslow for prioritization reasoning, u03 lifespan for capacity considerations, u04 therapeutic communication for informed-consent conversations
- **Feeds:** u06 autonomy in wellness/cultural choices, u07 scope violations and reportable safety incidents, u08 EHR privacy and continuing tech ethics

Four weeks is the right length: six atoms — all knowledge-heavy with discussion-based classwork — plus the Ethics Case Analysis performance task. This is the single longest unit by weeks.

## Essential questions

1. What ethical principles guide healthcare decisions?
2. How do ethical principles conflict with each other in real clinical situations?
3. What does HIPAA require, and what are the consequences of breaching confidentiality?
4. What rights do patients hold, and how is informed consent supposed to work?
5. What is scope of practice, and why does it matter for every healthcare worker — not just physicians and nurses?
6. What distinguishes malpractice from ordinary mistakes, and how do liability and negligence shape daily practice?
7. How does emerging technology (AI, genomics, telehealth, wearables) create new ethical questions without clear precedent?

## Unit learning outcomes

Students will be able to:

1. Apply the four principles of biomedical ethics — autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice — to analyze a healthcare scenario.
2. Identify HIPAA-protected information, permitted vs. prohibited disclosures, and consequences of breach.
3. Describe the elements of informed consent and recognize when consent is valid, invalid, or not required (emergency exceptions).
4. Determine whether a given task falls within a specific role's scope of practice using state practice acts and facility policy as the frame.
5. Analyze a scenario for the four elements of malpractice — duty, breach, causation, damages — and explain when a claim would or would not stand.
6. Evaluate an ethical dilemma raised by emerging healthcare technology using the four principles as a framework.

## Content scope — in / out

**In scope for this unit**

- Four principles of biomedical ethics: autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice — with conflict cases
- Professional codes of ethics: American Medical Association (AMA) code for physicians, ANA code for nurses, allied-health codes — what they share and what they don't
- HIPAA basics: Privacy Rule, Security Rule, 18 identifiers (Safe Harbor), minimum-necessary standard, treatment/payment/operations (TPO) exception, breach consequences (civil + criminal penalties, job loss, license loss)
- Informed consent: elements (capacity, information, understanding, voluntariness, consent), emergency exception, implied consent, **capacity** (clinical assessment) vs. **competence** (legal determination), assent for minors, advance directives
- Patient rights: the Patient's Bill of Rights, the right to refuse treatment, the right to documentation access, the right to leave against medical advice (AMA discharge)
- Scope of practice: state practice acts, facility policy, nurse delegation rules under state practice acts, independent vs. supervised practice, the "license protects the public, not the practitioner" principle
- Licensure vs. certification vs. registration: what each actually guarantees
- Malpractice four-element test: duty of care, breach of standard, causation, damages
- Liability types: personal, vicarious (employer for employee), institutional
- Major U.S. healthcare laws: HIPAA, EMTALA, Stark Law, Anti-Kickback Statute, ACA, the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA), CLIA — one-line each
- Ethics of emerging technology: AI and diagnostic algorithms (bias, accountability), genomic data (privacy, GINA, family implications), telehealth (access, state-licensure, quality), wearables (data ownership, consent), end-of-life technology (when to stop)

## Unit narrative / throughline

**"What does care owe patients — and what does the law say when care fails?"** — the unit moves from principle (what should guide us?) through rule (what does law require?) through patient perspective (what are they owed?) through provider perspective (what must we do, what can't we do?) through consequence (what happens when the promises break?) and closes with the emerging frontier (what does the framework say about tools that didn't exist when the framework was written?).

Every atom in this unit asks students to **reason from a principle to a situation**. Pedagogy is discussion-based because the answers are rarely single-valued; the professional skill is showing your work.

## Assessment strategy

**Atom-level (embedded, auto-graded)**

- 3-5 formative checks per atom
- 8-15 item summative quiz per atom

**Unit summative quiz** (30-35 items)

Mix: four-principle application to short cases, HIPAA violation identification (what kind of violation, what consequence), informed-consent element matching, scope-of-practice classification (which role can do this?), four-element malpractice analysis, tech-ethics scenario response.

**Unit game** (template `game: true`)

Jeopardy or Bingo covering ethical principles, HIPAA rules, legal vocabulary (negligence, liability, tort, duty of care, etc.), and codes-of-ethics identification. Vocabulary is dense in this unit; memory aid pays off.

**Performance task — Ethics Case Analysis** (template `performance_task: true`)

Multi-part case spanning ethical principles + HIPAA + informed consent + scope of practice + malpractice exposure. Students receive an unfolding scenario (typically 3-4 scenes) and must:

- Identify the ethical principles at stake in each scene
- Flag HIPAA or confidentiality issues
- Assess informed-consent validity
- Determine whether any action violates scope of practice
- Analyze malpractice exposure using the four-element test
- Recommend a defensible course of action with reasoning

Rubric dimensions: principle application, legal identification, reasoning quality, writing clarity (c1A and c2F from upstream units), and recognition of uncertainty — students shouldn't pretend ethics has easy answers.

**Career Pathway Portfolio revisit after u05:** students add a note to each of their two careers covering (1) the primary scope-of-practice limits for the role, (2) the most common liability exposures, and (3) one or two ethical flashpoints the role regularly navigates.

## Common misconceptions

- "Ethics and law are the same thing" — related but distinct. Many things are legal and yet ethically contested (certain billing or conflict-of-interest practices), and ethics sometimes demands more than the law requires (voluntary disclosure of errors when no law compels it). Students need both vocabularies.
- "HIPAA means you can't talk about patients at all" — HIPAA permits disclosure for **t**reatment, **p**ayment, and health-care **o**perations (TPO) with minimum-necessary standard. It restricts gossip, disclosure to unauthorized parties, and many post-care conversations. It does not prevent teamwork.
- "Informed consent is just a signature on a form" — consent is a **process**: assessing capacity, providing information in understandable language, checking understanding, confirming voluntariness, documenting. The form is a receipt, not the event.
- "If a patient asks for it, autonomy means you have to do it" — autonomy is the right to accept or refuse; it does **not** obligate providers to act beyond their scope, judgment, or the law. Providers retain professional judgment.
- "Malpractice is just making a mistake" — the four-element test requires duty + breach + causation + damages. Missing any element, there is no claim. Most errors are not malpractice.
- "Tech ethics is a future problem" — AI diagnostic tools, consumer genomics, telehealth, wearables, and EHR-driven decisions are deployed now. The framework applies today.
- "You have to practice defensively to avoid liability" — defensive medicine has real costs (unnecessary tests, patient anxiety, expense). The standard is competent care, not fear-driven care.

## Equity and sensitivity notes

- **Historical context.** Tuskegee and Henrietta Lacks — introduced in u01 history — come back here as documented bases for modern **research-ethics law and the Common Rule** (IRB review, informed consent for research, GINA). Informed-consent law for clinical care has a separate legal lineage (Salgo 1957, Canterbury v. Spence 1972); Tuskegee shaped research ethics specifically. Frame as history tied to current practice, not contemporary commentary. J. Marion Sims and similar cases remain district-discretion per u01.
- **Consent across language.** Informed consent cannot be valid if the patient does not understand it. Professional medical interpreters (from u04) are not optional for limited-English-proficiency patients and Deaf patients — consent without language access is void.
- **Consent and cognitive disability.** Capacity is decision-specific, not global. A patient with cognitive disability may have capacity for some decisions and not others. Supported decision-making is the modern framework; guardianship is a last resort.
- **Medical racism still present.** Pain-assessment disparities (Black patients consistently under-treated for pain), maternal mortality disparities (Black maternal mortality 2-3× white), and algorithmic bias (risk scores that under-weight Black patients) are documented current realities. Teach as data, not advocacy.
- **LGBTQ+ patients.** Consent-for-care applies equally. Provider conscience exceptions exist in some states but have ethical and legal limits; emergency care cannot be denied.
- **Advance directives and cultural views.** Different cultures approach end-of-life decisions differently (family-centered vs. patient-centered decision making, how much information is shared with the patient). Honor patient and family preference within legal requirements.
- **Minors and consent.** Minors have some rights (e.g., mature-minor doctrine for specific care); parents/guardians have others. State law varies widely. Texas specifics apply and should be taught to the Texas context.
- **Financial barriers as an autonomy limitation.** Choice requires options. A patient who can't afford a recommended treatment has a constrained choice, not a free one. Naming this is a justice consideration.
- **Reproductive healthcare in Texas.** Texas law constrains some reproductive-care options; the ethical framework (autonomy, beneficence, justice) applies regardless of what a given state permits. Present the framework and the current legal landscape; do not advocate for a specific outcome. **District policy may vary on depth.**
