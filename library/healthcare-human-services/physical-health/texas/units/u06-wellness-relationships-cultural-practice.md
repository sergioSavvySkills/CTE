# u06 — Wellness, Relationships, and Cultural Practice

## Purpose and description

u06 is the **humanistic unit** — where students move from the legal and ethical frameworks of u05 to the patient-centered dimensions of care: wellness and disease prevention across the lifespan, the role of relationships in health, and cultural practice as a daily consideration in clinical work. Three atoms, two weeks, no performance task — a shorter unit by atoms and weeks, but substantive in KS coverage (7 primary standards, tied with u05 for third-heaviest coverage count).

## Why this unit exists

Patients don't show up as disembodied cases. They arrive with lifestyles, relationships, families, and cultural frames that shape what "good care" means for them. Clinicians who ignore these dimensions practice worse medicine — lower adherence, worse outcomes, lower patient satisfaction, and more ethical conflicts.

u06 owns 4 of the 17 (c)(2) academic-integration standards (c2K wellness, c2L relationships, c2P and c2Q cultural influences) and 3 of the 5 (c)(10) rights-and-choices standards (c10B wellness for prevention, c10C relationships on health, c10E diversity and cultural practices). The unit gives the course a coherent place to teach these as patient-facing skills, not abstractions.

## Placement in the course (why u06 and not later)

Middle-to-late placement after u05 (ethics) and before u07 (safety) is deliberate:

- **Needs:** u03 homeostasis and body systems (wellness operates at the system level); u03 lifespan (relationships evolve across stages); u04 therapeutic communication (the delivery vehicle for wellness teaching and cross-cultural conversations); u05 autonomy (patients choose their own wellness path within legal and clinical constraints).
- **Feeds:** u07 safety practices (wellness informs occupational safety; cultural practice informs how safety protocols land across populations); u08 capstone (integration of humanistic dimensions into the final synthesis).

Two weeks is the right length: three atoms of "both" skill-type with applied-task simulations. Shorter than u05 because the content is less legally dense — students apply u05's autonomy framework to specific wellness and cultural contexts, rather than learning new legal frameworks.

## Essential questions

1. What do "health" and "wellness" actually mean across a lifetime?
2. How are health and wellness different from each other?
3. What wellness strategies prevent disease?
4. How are strong-evidence recommendations distinguished from weak ones?
5. How do relationships — family, peer, community — affect physical and emotional health for better and for worse?
6. How do culture and social context shape what patients consider "healthy" and how they engage with care?
7. What does culturally responsive care look like in practice?
8. What's the difference between cultural competence and cultural humility — and why did the field shift?

## Unit learning outcomes

Students will be able to:

1. Distinguish health, wellness, and the multiple dimensions of well-being across the lifespan.
2. Identify evidence-based wellness strategies across primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention.
3. Evaluate how specific relationship patterns (family, peer, romantic, community) affect physical and emotional health.
4. Describe how social determinants of health (SDOH) — including culture, language, socioeconomic status, neighborhood, and access factors — shape health outcomes.
5. Apply cultural humility and responsive-care principles to a clinical scenario, including recognizing and accounting for one's own cultural frame as part of the encounter.

## Content scope — in / out

**In scope for this unit**

- Definitions: health, wellness, well-being; the eight dimensions of wellness (physical, emotional, social, intellectual, occupational, spiritual, environmental, financial) — with a teaching-convention note that some sources teach 6 or 7, similar to the u03 organ-systems convention variation
- Levels of prevention: primary (immunization, nutrition, exercise, sleep), secondary (screening, early detection), tertiary (chronic-disease management, rehab) — with U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) evidence-grading (Grades A, B, C, D, and I for Insufficient evidence) as the basis for separating strong from weak recommendations
- Lifestyle factors with strongest outcome evidence: tobacco cessation, physical activity, diet quality, sleep adequacy, stress management, social connection
- Relationships and health: social ties as a health determinant; positive patterns (respect, reciprocity, repair after conflict); harmful patterns (control, isolation, contempt, abuse); relationship dynamics across the lifespan — peer-centered in adolescence, partner in young adulthood, caregiving in middle age, and in older adulthood a mix of long-standing partnership, grandparenting and intergenerational connection, community and volunteer ties, and experience of loss that becomes part of relational life
- Social determinants of health (SDOH): housing, food access, income, education, transportation, discrimination experienced — how each shapes outcomes
- Health disparities: documented outcome gaps by race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, rural/urban geography, insurance status
- Cultural frameworks in healthcare: cultural competence (mastery model) vs. cultural humility (ongoing-learning model) — the shift from the former to the latter in modern practice
- Implicit bias: what it is, how it affects decisions (pain, prescribing, diagnostic), how to mitigate (bias training, structured decision tools, team review)
- Language access in depth (building on u04): medical interpreters, translation of written materials, reading-level-appropriate patient education
- Culturally influenced healthcare practices: dietary restrictions, end-of-life views, traditional healing, gender preferences in providers, decision-making styles (family-centered vs. individual), modesty norms

## Unit narrative / throughline

**"Patients don't show up as cases — they show up as lives."** The unit moves from wellness (how patients take care of themselves or don't) to relationships (who patients live their lives alongside) to culture (the frames within which those lives make sense). Every atom asks students to see the patient before the diagnosis.

Pedagogy blends knowledge (evidence-based prevention, SDOH data) with applied scenarios (responding to a patient whose wellness plan conflicts with their cultural practice, or whose family dynamics complicate adherence). Discussion is lighter than u05 because the immediate stakes shift — the primary risks here are rapport and clinical outcomes, with legal-liability exposure less direct than in u05 (though still real in ADA, Title VI, and refusal-of-care contexts).

## Common misconceptions

- "Wellness is just diet and exercise" — wellness has multiple dimensions. Sleep, stress management, social connection, financial stability, environmental quality, and purpose all have measurable health outcomes. Reducing wellness to diet+exercise misses most of what keeps people healthy.
- "Prevention is optional / nice-to-have" — the US Preventive Services Task Force grades preventive services by evidence strength; the Grade-A recommendations (immunizations, certain screenings) are among the highest-value interventions in healthcare. Missing them means preventable illness and death.
- "Stronger-willed people don't need social connection" — social connection is a measurable health factor. Loneliness correlates with mortality comparable to smoking. It's a biological need, not a weakness to require it.
- "Culture only matters for patients 'from other cultures'" — every patient has a cultural frame, including patients who share the clinician's culture. Unexamined shared culture still shapes care, often in ways the clinician doesn't notice.
- "Cultural competence is something you achieve" — the modern framework is cultural humility: an ongoing stance of curiosity and self-reflection, not a credential. No one is fully "competent" across all the cultures they will encounter.
- "Patient non-adherence means the patient doesn't care about their health" — non-adherence often reflects unaddressed social determinants (no transportation to appointments, no refrigeration for insulin, rotating work schedules) or cultural mismatch with the plan. Labeling it as patient failure misses the real cause.
- "Implicit bias isn't real or doesn't affect me" — implicit bias is documented in pain management, diagnostic decisions, and prescribing across thousands of studies. Most clinicians hold some bias; the goal is mitigating its effect, not claiming freedom from it.

## Equity and sensitivity notes

- **SDOH as baseline, not add-on.** Social determinants of health explain more of the variation in outcomes than clinical care does. Frame SDOH as foundational, not as a sidebar or "extra" topic.
- **Documented disparities.** Black maternal mortality 2-3× white (CDC), Black-white pain-treatment gap, LGBTQ+ health disparities, rural-urban gaps in access, disability-related discrimination in healthcare. Present as data, not advocacy (same approach as u05).
- **Cultural humility over cultural competence.** Teach humility as the modern framework; note that some older materials still use "competence" without the learning-stance shift. Both terms appear in practice; students should recognize both.
- **Religion and spirituality.** Many patients make healthcare decisions based on religious or spiritual beliefs (blood-product refusal, prayer as adjunct to treatment, end-of-life practices, dietary laws). Respect is the starting point; safety concerns may require additional conversation, documented in u05's consent framework.
- **LGBTQ+ patients.** Respectful care — appropriate language, relevant medical history without pathologizing, chosen family recognized as family — is the standard. **District policy may vary on depth**; default to the clinical-communication practice already established in u04 (names and pronouns as given).
- **Immigrant and refugee patients.** Language access, concerns about documentation status being shared, cultural frames from countries of origin, and trauma histories all affect care. Trauma-informed defaults from u04 apply.
- **Weight and body-image bias.** Weight stigma is documented in healthcare and worsens outcomes for heavier patients (avoided care, missed diagnoses). Weight-neutral clinical practice is the emerging standard; teach explicitly.

## Open questions / decisions pending

- **Wellness dimensions count.** Some sources teach 6 dimensions, others 7 or 8 (adding environmental and/or financial). Current lean: teach 8 with explicit note that conventions vary — similar to the u03 organ-systems teaching-convention note.
- **USPSTF grading depth.** Grades A, B, C, D, I exist with specific meanings. Current lean: introduce Grades A-D-I at the wellness atom; deeper treatment in later courses.
- **SDOH screening tools.** Several validated tools exist (PRAPARE, AHC-HRSN). Current lean: mention as category; specific tool demonstration is district/facility discretion.
- **Weight-neutral clinical practice** is newer than cultural humility as a taught framework. Current lean: introduce with one slide in the cultural-competence atom; treat as evolving standard.
