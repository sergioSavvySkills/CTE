# u08 — Healthcare Technology and Data (capstone)

## Purpose and description

u08 is the **capstone unit** — where students close the course by surveying healthcare technology and data across the five health science systems, learning the mechanics of electronic health records, encountering vital signs as a data stream, and building quantitative fluency with conversions and data interpretation. Four atoms, three weeks, anchored by the **Technology Systems Map** capstone performance task which requires explicit integration of content from u01-u07.

One atom (`vital-signs-overview`) is **cross-scope to §127.403** — it primary-belongs to Health Science Theory (§127.422) but is taught here because modern vital signs are a data phenomenon: continuous monitor feeds, EHR documentation, and clinical decision rules all sit at the intersection of technology and data this unit covers.

## Why this unit exists

Healthcare has become a data industry. Every encounter generates measurements, codes, documentation, and device outputs; every role touches technology daily; and many safety events (u07) involve equipment malfunction or data misinterpretation as a root or contributing cause. Students who leave PoHS without literacy in healthcare technology and basic clinical data will be unprepared for modern practice, regardless of which career they pursue.

§127.403 gives this explicit weight: KS area (c)(12) has three standards (c12A technological equipment across systems, c12B potential malfunctions, c12C reporting process) — all owned here. (c)(2) also gives this unit c2A (unit conversions) and c2B (data interpretation from tables, charts, graphs), which sit naturally alongside EHR and vital-signs content.

The unit is the course **capstone** because it loops back to u01: equipment and data exist to serve the five health science systems (therapeutic, diagnostic, informatics, support services, biotechnology research and development) that students first met as career families. The Technology Systems Map PT asks them to complete that loop explicitly.

## Placement in the course (why u08 and not earlier)

Final placement is deliberate:

- **Needs:** everything. u01 five systems (equipment is organized by them); u02 team collaboration (who uses which equipment); u03 homeostasis and body systems (vitals measure homeostatic variables); u04 medical vocabulary and technical writing (documentation); u05 HIPAA and tech ethics (EHR privacy, AI in diagnostics); u06 SDOH and cultural humility (EHR captures these fields); u07 regulatory agencies (FDA for devices, Joint Commission for safety) and safety/reporting culture (malfunction reporting extends safety reporting)
- **Feeds:** the end of the course; no later units in PoHS

Three weeks is the right length: four atoms, with an integrative capstone performance task that expands beyond the slot count. The PT is the single largest assessment in the course.

## Essential questions

1. What kinds of equipment do healthcare workers use across the five health science systems?
2. How do I recognize a potential equipment malfunction before it causes harm?
3. What is the process for reporting a malfunctioning device — within the facility and to the FDA?
4. What is an electronic health record, and what does safe EHR use look like?
5. What are vital signs measuring, and what values should prompt escalation?
6. How do I convert between measurement systems (metric ↔ US customary) for clinical calculations?
7. How do I interpret the tables, charts, and graphs that healthcare data is presented in?
8. How does everything we've learned this year fit together — careers, teams, body, communication, ethics, wellness, safety, and now technology and data?

## Unit learning outcomes

Students will be able to:

1. Identify representative technological equipment used in each of the five health science systems (diagnostic, therapeutic, health informatics, support services, biotechnology research and development).
2. Recognize common signs of equipment malfunction and select the appropriate safety response (take out of service, label, report).
3. Describe the reporting process for device malfunctions, including the FDA Medical Device Reporting (MDR) pathway for device-related patient harm.
4. Navigate a basic electronic health record safely — identify key sections, protect credentials, and document accurately.
5. Identify and interpret the five standard vital signs with age-appropriate normal ranges and escalation thresholds.
6. Convert fluently between measurement systems commonly used in healthcare (weight, volume, length, temperature).
7. Interpret clinical tables, charts, and graphs to support decisions.
8. Synthesize content from across the entire course to complete the Technology Systems Map capstone.

## Content scope — in / out

**In scope for this unit**

- Equipment across the five health science systems: diagnostic (imaging devices, lab analyzers, EKG, point-of-care testing), therapeutic (IV pumps, ventilators, defibrillators including AEDs, beds, dialysis), informatics (EHR workstations, barcode scanners, telemetry monitors, patient portals), support services (sterilizers, HVAC with HEPA filtration, fire and call systems), biotechnology R&D (centrifuges, PCR thermocyclers, flow cytometers, microscopes, biosafety cabinets)
- Malfunction recognition: alarms, error codes, abnormal readings, physical damage, lockout tags — with the safety response (stop use, label "Do Not Use," notify biomed)
- Reporting process: internal (biomed / clinical engineering ticket; risk management notification when patient harm is suspected; EHR incident report) and external (FDA Medical Device Reporting — MDR — under 21 CFR Part 803). The user-facility reporting rule is specific: device-related **deaths** must be reported to both the FDA and the manufacturer within 10 work days; device-related **serious injuries** must be reported to the manufacturer (and to the FDA if the manufacturer is unknown) within 10 work days; other malfunctions and near-misses are captured through internal reporting rather than FDA MDR.
- Electronic health records: EHR vs. EMR distinction, chart sections (demographics, problem list, medications, allergies, progress notes, orders, labs, imaging, flow sheets), safe documentation (accuracy, timeliness, objective language, late-entry rules), credential hygiene (individual logins, no sharing, secure logout), HIE (health information exchange) overview
- Vital signs: temperature (normal ranges, methods, fever and hypothermia thresholds), pulse / heart rate (ranges by age, sites, rhythm and strength), respiratory rate (ranges, effort and pattern), blood pressure (ranges, cuff sizing, position), SpO2 (range, false-reading sources). Pain briefly as an essential assessment, with the nuance that pain was historically called the "fifth vital sign" (a framing coined by the American Pain Society in 1995, adopted by the VA in 1998-1999, and amplified by Joint Commission's 2001 pain-management standards; since retired in many settings because the framing contributed to opioid over-prescribing).
- Measurement-system conversions: weight (lb ↔ kg, oz ↔ g), volume (mL ↔ tsp, L ↔ oz), length (cm ↔ in, m ↔ ft), temperature (°C ↔ °F); healthcare standardizes on metric
- Clinical data interpretation: reference ranges in lab results, drug dosing tables, growth-chart percentiles, trend lines, infographics from public-health data; common error sources (unit mixing, decimal placement, mis-read axes)

**Capstone performance task — Technology Systems Map** (template `performance_task: true`)

The single largest assessment in the course. Students pick one career from their Career Pathway Portfolio and produce an integrated technology-and-data map for that role. Required components:

- Equipment: list 5-8 pieces of equipment the role uses, identifying which of the five systems each belongs to (drawing on u01 career content and u08 atom 1). Most roles span 2-3 of the five systems; the list should reflect the role's actual scope rather than artificially covering all five.
- Data flow: show how information moves between the role, the EHR, and the care team (u02 teams + u04 communication + u08 atom 2)
- Safety: describe one plausible equipment-related safety event, the role's immediate response, and the reporting pathway (u07 safety and reporting + u08 atom 1)
- Vital signs or other clinical data: identify one type of clinical data central to the role, the normal range, and an escalation trigger (u03 homeostasis + u08 atom 3)
- Quantitative step: show a worked example of a unit conversion or chart interpretation that the role routinely performs (u08 atom 4)
- Ethics check: flag one tech-ethics or privacy consideration relevant to the role (u05 ethics-of-tech and HIPAA)
- Humanistic check: identify one SDOH or cultural consideration the role regularly navigates (u06 cultural humility and SDOH)
- Synthesis reflection: 2-3 paragraphs describing how this role sits within the health care system (pulling from u01 through u07)

Rubric dimensions: equipment accuracy, data-flow clarity, safety-response correctness (including correct PPE doffing order from u07 if relevant), vital-signs or data accuracy, quantitative work, cross-unit integration evidence, writing clarity (c1A + c2D + c2F from upstream units), and thoughtfulness of the synthesis reflection.

**Career Pathway Portfolio — final revisit after u08:** students add the **technology/equipment note** (equipment the role uses, data the role handles, and one reflection on how the role fits across the year's course content). This is the closing entry — the Portfolio is now complete.

## Common misconceptions

- "The EHR is just a digital filing cabinet" — modern EHRs are clinical decision-support systems, order-entry platforms, billing engines, and quality-measurement tools all at once. Framing them as paper-plus-computer misses most of their functional weight.
- "If the machine says so, it's true" — equipment produces measurements, not truths. A pulse oximeter reads 85% when a patient is cold; an automatic BP cuff reads high when the cuff is too small; a thermometer reads low when placed wrong. Clinical judgment interprets data; it does not defer to data.
- "Medical math is just for nurses and pharmacists" — every clinical role encounters unit conversions, dose calculations, or chart interpretation at some point. A CNA weighing a patient, an EMT estimating transport time, a medical-assistant documenting vitals — all do quantitative work daily.
- "Metric vs. US customary doesn't really matter" — it matters enough that decimal and unit errors kill patients. The FDA publishes medication-error data; unit confusion is a recurring theme.
- "Equipment malfunctions are the biomed team's problem, not mine" — recognizing malfunction and taking the device out of service is every user's responsibility. Biomed fixes it; clinical users are the first line of safe response.
- "Near-miss device events don't need to be reported because nothing broke" — same logic as u07 near-miss reporting. A near-miss device event reveals a latent failure that could harm the next patient. FDA MDR captures device-related patient harm specifically, but internal reporting covers the rest.
- "Healthcare technology is about computers and AI" — most healthcare technology is mechanical and physical (beds, pumps, imaging, sterilizers, HVAC). Computer and AI tools are growing but not the majority of what breaks, hurts, or helps.

## Equity and sensitivity notes

- **Algorithmic and device bias in healthcare technology.** Risk-prediction algorithms, pulse oximeters (which **overestimate SpO2** in patients with darker skin — reading falsely high and hiding hypoxemia), and diagnostic AI can carry bias from training data or physical design. Mentioned in u05 as a tech-ethics issue; reinforced here as a practical data-quality issue that clinicians should know about and account for in interpretation.
- **EHR access and the digital divide.** Patient portals assume internet access, device literacy, and English reading ability. Patients without those still deserve equivalent information; phone-based, printed, and caregiver-mediated alternatives are standard accommodations.
- **Equipment fit and vital-signs measurement.** Blood-pressure cuffs that don't fit larger arms produce wrong readings; pulse oximeters on darker skin read falsely high (consistent with the algorithmic/device-bias note above); pediatric equipment on adults and vice versa produce errors. Equipment should fit the patient, not the other way around.
- **Data equity in documentation.** EHR fields for race, ethnicity, sexual orientation, gender identity, preferred language, and SDOH only work if they're used consistently and accurately. Patient self-report is the gold standard; provider assumptions are the failure mode.
- **Accessibility of vital-signs technology.** Patients with disabilities may have difficulty with standard vital-signs measurement (tremor affects BP, cognitive differences affect cooperation for temperature). Adapt technique and document; do not skip.
