**Definitions:**

- **A.** Added hourly pay for evening, night, or weekend shifts; part of total compensation that varies by employer and resets when changing jobs
- **B.** Tenure-based priority for scheduling, PTO accrual, and tuition benefits that resets at a new employer
- **C.** A system-level driver of employment change such as hospital closure, merger, or payment-policy change
- **D.** A state of chronic emotional and physical exhaustion caused by sustained work demands; a leading push cause of healthcare turnover
- **E.** A factor that attracts a worker to a new job such as better pay, schedule, advancement, or culture
- **F.** The NCSBN-administered interstate compact that lets RNs and LVNs practice across compact member states without re-licensing; Texas is a member
- **G.** The ability to carry a license or certification across state lines; varies by profession and compact status
- **H.** A factor that drives a worker away from a current job such as burnout, unsafe conditions, toxic team, layoff, or scope conflict
- **I.** The rate at which workers leave a unit or employer; linked by AHRQ research to worse patient-safety outcomes

## Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

> Delilah has been an RN in a med-surg unit for three years. She is experiencing burnout, she feels emotionally exhausted, she has stopped caring about whether her documentation is thorough, and she calls in sick more frequently than she used to. A case management position opens in the same hospital: no nights, no weekends, less acute patient contact, and a $4,000 salary increase. Delilah applies. The push cause (burnout, schedule) and the pull cause (better hours, more money) are both operating. She takes the job. Her transition requires two weeks of bridge training, she loses her night-shift differential ($6,000 annually), her new health insurance costs $40 more per month, and her PTO accrual rate resets. Six months later she reports her burnout is resolved. 3. The textbook says Delilah's burnout caused her to stop caring about thorough documentation and to call in sick more frequently. Explain how each of these behavioral changes could affect patient safety on her unit.

4. Delilah moves to a new role within the same hospital rather than a new employer. Which of the financial effects described in the scenario (bridge training, shift differential loss, health insurance cost, PTO accrual reset) would likely be different if she had moved to a competing hospital instead? 5. The scenario ends by noting that Delilah's departure leaves one less vacancy on a unit that was already understaffed. Using the chapter's explanation of turnover and patient outcomes, describe one specific patient-safety risk that the unit faces during the gap before her position is filled.

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers, accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. "Compound" means each decision shapes the options available at the next decision point. The textbook example: a CNA who takes a long-term care job instead of a hospital job builds a different skill set, and when she applies to an LVN bridge program three years later, the skills she did or did not build at that first job matter for her application and her competency.

2. A push cause drives a worker away from a current position (burnout, unsafe conditions, scope conflicts). A pull cause attracts a worker toward a new one (better pay, schedule, advancement). Yes, both can operate at the same time; a worker who is dissatisfied at her current job and has found an appealing alternative is experiencing both simultaneously. The textbook says many job changes involve exactly this combination.

3. A structural cause operates at the system level rather than the individual level, meaning it is driven by forces outside the worker's control. Example: a rural Texas hospital closes because of a failing payer mix, forcing all clinical staff to find new positions through no fault of either the workers or the hospital. Neither push (personal dissatisfaction) nor pull (a better option found) describes this situation.

4. Three financial effects to investigate: (a) Shift differentials: if the new hospital pays a lower night-shift differential or the role is day-shift only, the apparent salary increase may disappear. (b) Health insurance premiums: different employers charge different amounts for comparable coverage; a $200/month difference is $2,400 per year. (c) Retirement matching: if the current employer matches 6% and the new employer matches 3%, the salary increase may be partially or fully offset. Accept any three clearly explained from the chapter.

5. Credential portability is the ability to carry a license or certification from one state to another without re-applying. Compact membership matters because Texas is a member of the Nurse Licensure Compact, which makes a Texas RN license valid in approximately 40 other compact states. If the travel contract is in a non-compact state (the textbook notes California as an example), the RN must apply separately, pay additional fees, and wait for approval before beginning work.

6. The textbook treats burnout as a push cause rather than a personal weakness because it has observable, measurable effects on work behavior: Delilah stops caring about thorough documentation and calls in sick more frequently. These are clinical and operational consequences that affect the team and the patients she cares for, not merely a personal feeling. The textbook frames burnout as a predictable response to sustained high-intensity work, particularly prevalent in acute care, rather than a character flaw.

7. Seniority affects: (a) PTO accrual rate, a senior nurse earns more paid time off per pay period; (b) scheduling priority, a senior nurse can choose preferred shifts before junior colleagues; (c) tuition-reimbursement eligibility, which typically requires a tenure threshold. Resetting to zero means starting at the bottom of the scheduling queue, earning PTO at the slowest rate, and waiting out the probationary period before tuition benefits begin, all real day-to-day disadvantages.

8. Individual turnover decisions aggregate into unit-level outcomes. AHRQ research documents that high-turnover units have higher rates of medication errors, hospital-acquired infections, and adverse events. The textbook includes this to show that career decisions have consequences that extend beyond the individual worker's paycheck. The point is not to pressure workers to stay in harmful situations but to give them context: their choices participate in a system, and that system affects patient safety.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Push causes: burnout (emotional exhaustion), the med-surg schedule (nights and weekends). Pull causes: no night or weekend shifts in the new role, a $4,000 salary increase. None of her causes are structural; the position change is driven entirely by her personal experience of the current job and the attractiveness of the new one, not by hospital closure, merger, or payment-policy change.

2. Net financial effect: +$4,000 salary minus $6,000 shift differential = net loss of $2,000 in annual compensation. The health insurance cost increase ($40/month x 12 = $480/year) adds to the net loss, bringing the total financial cost to approximately $2,480 per year, despite the salary increase. Accept calculations that correctly identify both the differential loss and the insurance cost.

3. Incomplete documentation: if Delilah is not thorough in charting assessments, medication administration, or care notes, the next nurse inherits an incomplete record, which can lead to missed interventions, duplicate medications, or failure to recognize a deteriorating patient. Frequent sick calls: when Delilah calls in, the unit is short-staffed, increasing the patient load on remaining nurses and raising the risk of missed observations or delayed responses to patient deterioration.

4. If Delilah moved to a competing hospital: (a) Bridge training would likely be longer and not employer-funded; she would be starting from scratch in an unfamiliar system. (b) Shift differential loss would still apply if the new role does not include night/weekend shifts. (c) Health insurance costs could be higher, lower, or the same; she would need to compare plans. (d) PTO accrual would definitely reset to zero at a new employer, whereas within the same hospital there may be continuity of service credit depending on policy. The within-hospital move likely preserves some seniority elements that a cross-employer move would not.

5. During the gap before Delilah's position is filled, the unit carries one fewer experienced nurse per shift. This increases the patient load for remaining nurses, which research links to higher rates of medication errors, missed care, and delayed response to clinical deterioration. Specifically, an understaffed unit may experience delayed detection of a patient's worsening vital signs if the remaining nurses have more patients per nurse than is safely manageable.
