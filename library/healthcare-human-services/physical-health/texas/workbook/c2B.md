**Definitions:**

- **A.** A y-axis that does not start at zero; visually exaggerates small changes and is a common way a graph misleads without being technically wrong
- **B.** A CDC or WHO chart that plots a child's height, weight, or head circumference against same-age peers; crossing percentiles is a clinical signal
- **C.** The range of values considered normal for a lab test in a defined population; a result outside this range is a signal to investigate, not an automatic diagnosis
- **D.** A bar or line chart of case counts over time during an outbreak; the shape suggests whether the source is point, continuous, or person-to-person
- **E.** Weight-for-height index (kg per square meter) used with a classification table to categorize adults and counsel accordingly
- **F.** Absolute risk is the actual probability of an outcome; relative risk is the ratio between groups; headlines usually quote the relative and hide the smaller absolute difference
- **G.** An axis where equal distances represent multiplicative rather than additive changes; appropriate for data spanning many orders of magnitude but easy to misread if unlabeled
- **H.** A stepwise plot of the proportion of a group still free of an event over time; introduced at a survey level for chart literacy
- **I.** A plot of a value (glucose, blood pressure, weight) over time used to see whether a patient is moving toward or away from a target

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. A reference range represents the middle 95 percent of healthy adults, which means 5 percent of healthy people will have a flagged value without having any disease. A single flagged result is a signal to investigate, not an automatic diagnosis. The clinician still needs to consider the patient's full picture before drawing a conclusion.

2. A child at the 25th percentile simply weighs more than 25 percent of children their age, which may be perfectly normal for that child. Crossing from the 50th to the 15th percentile over two visits means the child is gaining weight more slowly than peers and moving in a direction that warrants clinical attention, even if the absolute weight still looks acceptable by itself. The trend, not just the position, is the clinical signal.

3. The three questions are: What is the table measuring and in what units? (Getting this wrong could mean working with mg/dL values as if they were mmol/L, making a normal glucose look dangerously high or low.) Who does the reference range apply to? (Applying an adult range to a pediatric patient could cause a normal value to be flagged or an abnormal one to be missed.) What decision does this table support? (Knowing the decision tells you which column or row to focus on, and using the wrong part of the table can produce the wrong dose or the wrong counseling.)

4. An epi curve is a specialized bar chart that plots case counts over time during a disease outbreak; its shape reveals the likely source. A point-source pattern shows a sharp spike over a short window (typically 48 to 72 hours) followed by rapid decline, suggesting everyone was exposed at the same event, such as a shared meal. A prolonged plateau would suggest continuous exposure from an ongoing source. Waves suggest person-to-person spread.

5. First, check where the y-axis starts. If it starts at 72, calculate the actual numerical change to see how small it is relative to the full range (72 to 100). Then ask: what would this look like if the y-axis started at zero? A rise that looks dramatic on a truncated axis may be nearly flat on a zero-start axis. The absolute numerical change (rather than the visual impression) is what matters clinically.

6. Absolute risk reduction: 5% minus 3% = 2 percentage points. The headline reports the relative risk reduction (2/5 = 40%). The absolute number matters because it tells you how many additional patients actually benefit from the drug. A 2-percentage-point reduction means you would need to treat 50 patients to prevent one cancer event, which is critical information for deciding whether the drug's cost and side effects are worthwhile.

7. Every chart is evidence for a decision: whether a patient's trend is moving toward or away from a target, whether an outbreak needs broad quarantine or a focused investigation, whether a treatment's benefit justifies its risk. Reading a chart without the decision in mind means you may focus on the wrong element or mistake pattern for conclusion. The chart is evidence, not the answer.

8. Survival curves appear in oncology and cardiovascular research that entry-level workers will encounter. The chapter introduces them at a survey level so students can recognize the chart type and understand that a steeper downward slope means events are happening faster in that group. Full interpretation is not expected yet, but recognizing the chart and knowing what its slope means prevents a student from being completely lost when the chart appears in a clinical context.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. She checks (1) that the y-axis starts at zero, which confirms the case counts are not visually inflated by a truncated axis; (2) the time window of three weeks, which gives adequate context for the 48-hour cluster; and (3) that the x-axis is labeled in days, confirming the time unit matches the outbreak's time scale. Checking these features confirms the graph is not distorting the data before she acts on its shape.

2. A prolonged plateau would suggest a continuous exposure source, such as a contaminated water supply or an ongoing food source, and the recommendation would shift to identifying and removing that source rather than tracing a single event. A series of waves would suggest person-to-person transmission, pointing the investigation toward contact tracing and potentially broader quarantine. Each shape leads to a different public health response.

3. The scenario shows that a chart shape can generate an actionable recommendation before any interviews or additional data are collected. Data literacy translates directly into decision-making; the student does not just describe what the curve looks like but uses it to prioritize the investigation. Every chart-reading task ends with a decision, and reading the chart well makes the decision faster and more accurate.

4. A y-axis starting at 5 with counts of 6, 8, and 7 would make the peak bar (8) look nearly 3 times taller than the baseline bar (6), creating a visual impression of a dramatic spike. On a zero-start axis, those same bars would show counts that are nearly identical. Checking the y-axis starting point immediately reveals that the visual impression is exaggerated by the truncated scale, and the actual variation is small.

5. The example shows that quantitative pattern recognition can precede and guide qualitative investigation. A student who can read an epi curve does not need to wait for interview data to form a working hypothesis. Data literacy compresses the timeline of public health decision-making and allows limited investigative resources to be pointed in the most productive direction from the start.
