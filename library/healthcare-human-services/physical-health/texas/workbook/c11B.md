- **C.** The six-link sequence an infectious agent must travel to reach and infect a new host
- **D.** The clinical team activated when a patient shows signs of deterioration before reaching full cardiac arrest
- **E.** Contact, droplet, and airborne precautions layered on top of standard precautions for specific pathogens
- **F.** The CDC's minimum set of infection-prevention practices applied to every patient at every contact

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The chain of infection framework reveals that transmission requires all six links to be intact, which means any single intervention that breaks one link stops spread entirely. Example: hand hygiene between patient contacts breaks the mode-of-transmission link because contaminated hands can no longer carry the pathogen to a new host. Because the chain is universal, the same framework applies to every pathogen and every care setting.

2. ABHR is faster, more effective at killing most pathogens, and less damaging to skin, making it the preferred choice for routine clinical use. However, C. difficile and norovirus produce spores that alcohol does not kill; for those pathogens, the physical scrubbing action of soap and water is required to remove spores mechanically from the hands. Recommending one method universally would either expose patients to spore-forming organisms or damage workers' skin through excessive soap use.

3. Donning for airborne precautions: gown, N95 respirator with seal check, eye protection, gloves. The gown is donned first because tying it at the back of the neck involves touching an area close to the face; doing this after putting on a mask would contaminate the mask ties. The mask is removed last during doffing because contaminated particles may drift during earlier removal steps, and keeping the mask in place protects the face and mucous membranes throughout the rest of the doffing sequence.

4. All four conditions must be met: the fire is no larger than a trash can, you know what is burning (so the correct extinguisher class can be used), there is a clear exit behind you, and a trained responder is present. If any single condition is absent, the correct action is to contain (close doors) and evacuate. Attempting extinguishment without a clear exit, for example, risks trapping the responder.

5. The RRT is a clinical team called when a patient shows signs of deterioration before reaching cardiac arrest; it is not a code response but an early-intervention mechanism. A Code Blue response is triggered after cardiac or respiratory arrest has occurred. Activating the RRT at the first signs of deterioration allows the team to intervene while the patient still has a functioning airway and circulation, which dramatically improves outcomes compared to waiting for a full arrest.

6. The Contain step calls for closing all doors and windows, which limits the spread of smoke through the facility. In healthcare facilities, fire-rated doors are designed to hold back smoke for a period of time. Because smoke inhalation causes more deaths in healthcare fires than flames, slowing smoke spread through closed fire doors saves lives even when the fire itself cannot be immediately controlled.

7. Elevators may lose power during a fire, trapping occupants inside. Ambulatory patients are moved first because they can self-evacuate with minimal staff time. Wheelchair patients go next. Bedbound patients require the most staff resources. Horizontal evacuation to another smoke compartment on the same floor is preferred because it is faster, requires less staff per patient, and avoids the risks of stairwell congestion that vertical evacuation creates.

8. The textbook states the purpose of incident reporting is system improvement, not individual blame. A student who observes an unsafe condition and does not report it is described as "not protecting anyone," implying that silent observation allows hazards to persist and recur. Developing the reporting habit early is framed as a professional responsibility and as a direct contribution to patient safety.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. An AIIR maintains negative air pressure relative to the hallway, meaning air flows into the room from the hallway rather than out. This prevents contaminated airborne particles from escaping into the corridor and reaching other patients. If the room were not at negative pressure, exhaled aerosols containing Mycobacterium tuberculosis could flow out each time the door opened, potentially exposing patients and staff in the hallway.

2. An N95 respirator is a tight-fitting device designed to filter at least 95% of airborne particles when properly sealed to the face. A surgical mask is loose-fitting and blocks large droplets but does not create a seal, so small aerosolized particles can enter around the edges. Tuberculosis is transmitted by airborne particles small enough to pass around a loose surgical mask, making a proper N95 seal essential.

3. Hand hygiene is performed immediately after removing gloves, because gloves are the most contaminated item and hand hygiene at this point removes any contamination that may have transferred when the gloves were pulled off. Hand hygiene is performed again after removing the mask, because the final mask removal step involves touching the face area, and any residual contamination on hands from earlier steps could be introduced to mucous membranes. Each moment corresponds to a transition from a more contaminated item or action to a cleaner state.

4. Rolling the gown inward keeps the contaminated outer surface from contacting the student's skin, clothing, or the surrounding environment as the gown is removed. This applies the principle that contaminated surfaces should be folded inward on themselves so that clean surfaces face outward throughout the removal process.

5. First: removing gloves and the gown at the same time would require touching the outer (contaminated) surface of the gown with bare hands after the gloves are already off, transferring contamination directly to the skin. Second: removing the mask at the same time as other items, rather than last, would expose the face and mucous membranes to contaminated particles stirred up during gown and glove removal, because the mask would no longer be in place to protect the airway.
