### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Local government handles the functions closest to individual communities: county and city health departments, EMS, school health programs, and facility inspection. State government controls licensure, scope of practice, and co-administers Medicaid. National government sets payment rules through CMS, approves drugs through the FDA, and funds research through NIH and workforce development through HRSA. Example functions: local, restaurant inspection; state, issuing nursing licenses; national, approving a new drug.

2. Five levers: (1) Coverage and payment, example: CMS deciding to cover a new procedure. (2) Licensure and scope, example: Texas setting the Texas Nurse Practice Act requirements. (3) Safety and quality regulation, example: CLIA standards applying to all clinical labs. (4) Public-health law, example: states requiring disease reporting or school vaccination. (5) Research and workforce funding, example: HRSA loan-repayment programs incentivizing providers to work in underserved areas.

3. Federal preemption means a valid federal rule overrides a conflicting state rule within the scope of federal authority. Its limits lie in the Tenth Amendment, which reserves authority over health, safety, and welfare to the states. Example where it applies: FDA drug approval preempts any state law prohibiting that drug's sale. Example where it does not: Medicaid expansion is voluntary for states, meaning Texas can choose not to expand without violating federal law.

4. EMTALA requires hospital emergency departments to screen and stabilize any patient who arrives, regardless of ability to pay. It was designed to eliminate the practice of "patient dumping," transferring uninsured patients to public hospitals before they were stable. One unintended consequence identified in the textbook: emergency departments became the primary-care provider of last resort for many uninsured patients, contributing to ED crowding and a role the ED was not designed or funded to fill efficiently.

5. Scope of practice refers to the state-defined tasks and decisions a licensed practitioner is legally authorized to perform. It varies significantly across states for nurse practitioners and physician assistants. State-level variation matters because NPs who can practice independently in one state may be required to have a physician supervision agreement in another, which limits where they can practice and creates access gaps in underserved areas.

6. A hospital that fails its CMS Conditions of Participation survey faces the loss of Medicare payment eligibility. Because most hospitals cannot survive financially without Medicare reimbursement, this effectively forces closure within weeks. The threat of this consequence makes the Conditions of Participation the primary federal quality lever over hospitals.

7. The three levels disagreed because each has different authority and different accountability: the local school board answers to parents and the local community, the state governor has statewide authority over public-health policy, and the CDC issues guidance but does not have direct enforcement authority over state or local school policies. In Texas, enforcement authority over school mask policies ultimately resided with the state government, as the governor's executive orders were treated as binding over local school-board decisions under Texas law.

8. Research and workforce funding works more slowly because it shapes the composition and size of the healthcare workforce over years or decades rather than days. Example: NIH funding priorities determine which diseases get studied and what treatments emerge over the next generation. HRSA loan-repayment programs shape where physicians and nurses choose to practice over the course of their early careers, gradually filling access gaps in underserved communities.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Sample answers for each step:

   Step 1 (Level): The state government of Texas is making the decision within the framework of a federal law.

   Step 2 (Lever): Coverage and payment.

   Step 3 (Intended effect): To limit growth in the state Medicaid budget.

   Step 4 (Observed effect on patients): Hundreds of thousands of Texans in the coverage gap remain uninsured and cannot access preventive or routine care.

   Step 5 (Effect on workforce): Rural hospitals generate less revenue, limiting their ability to hire staff and compete on salaries, which creates workforce shortages in rural areas.

   Step 6 (Unintended consequence): Rural hospital closures reduce local emergency access, shift uncompensated care costs to remaining providers, and increase the distance patients must travel for emergency care.

2. The distinction matters because a historical effect might be considered resolved over time, while an ongoing effect requires current policy action. If the student frames the effects as historical, her analysis might suggest no action is needed. If she identifies them as ongoing, her analysis must address what is happening now and what interventions remain available.

3. The ACA is a federal law, but the Supreme Court ruled that the Medicaid expansion provision was optional for states rather than mandatory. This means states can choose to accept or decline the expansion without losing other federal Medicaid funding. The legal principle at work is the limits of federal spending power: Congress can offer states funding with conditions, but it cannot coerce states by threatening to withhold unrelated existing funding.

4. The coverage gap refers to people who earn too much to qualify for traditional Medicaid in a non-expansion state but too little to afford marketplace insurance plans (which receive subsidies starting at the federal poverty level). In Texas, adults without dependent children often do not qualify for traditional Medicaid regardless of income, leaving them ineligible for either program.

5. Student answers will vary. A complete answer should identify: Level (national and/or state, depending on the hypothetical framing), Lever (coverage and payment), Intended effect (improve hospital financial viability by increasing reimbursement revenue), Observed/likely effect on patients (hospital remains open, maintaining local emergency access and healthcare employment), Effect on workforce (higher revenue supports hiring and competitive salaries, reducing workforce shortages), and Unintended consequence (accept any reasoned possibility, such as increased federal spending, other hospitals lobbying for similar designations, or over-reliance on a single reimbursement lever without addressing uncompensated care from uninsured patients).
