- **A.** The system that manages the data and technology of care (coders, HIM managers, EHR analysts, scribes, data analysts)
- **B.** The typical day-to-day work of a role; the first of six comparison-framework dimensions
- **C.** The system that identifies what is wrong through testing and imaging (laboratory scientists, phlebotomists, radiologic technologists, EKG technicians)
- **D.** The National Consortium for Health Science Education five-pathway framework that the Texas TEKS draws from directly
- **E.** The system that creates new knowledge and products (biomedical scientists, clinical research coordinators, regulatory affairs specialists)
- **F.** The system that keeps the care environment functional (BMET, central sterile, environmental services, transport, interpreters)
- **G.** The cooperation across all five systems that occurs during a single patient care episode

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers, accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Healthcare encompasses approximately 300 distinct occupations doing fundamentally different kinds of work, and grouping them into five systems provides organizing logic that makes the full job market manageable. For a student, the framework maps which roles do what, what education each requires, and what the day-to-day work looks like, enabling comparisons across the full market rather than tunnel vision on one or two familiar roles.

2. The diagnostic system generates the data clinicians use to make decisions through testing and imaging, without providing treatment directly. The therapeutic system uses that data and provides treatment, management, and direct care. Example diagnostic role: phlebotomy technician (draws blood for testing). Example therapeutic role: registered nurse (provides bedside care and medication administration).

3. Health informatics manages the data, documentation, coding, and technology infrastructure that makes modern healthcare function. It is least visible because informatics workers are rarely at the bedside; they work behind the clinical encounter in coding systems, EHR configurations, and data analysis. Yet every clinical decision depends on accurate documentation and correctly coded records to drive insurance reimbursement and coordinate the care team.

4. Support services keeps the physical and logistical infrastructure of care working; without it, clinical care stalls. Example: environmental services workers clean and disinfect patient rooms, which is a direct infection-control function. A contaminated room can transmit pathogens to the next patient regardless of how competent the clinical team is, making environmental services directly linked to patient safety even though its workers are not clinicians.

5. Every approved drug or medical device used at the bedside traces its path back through the biotechnology R&D system. Biomedical research scientists design experiments; clinical research coordinators manage the trials that test whether treatments work; regulatory affairs specialists navigate FDA approval. The patient who benefits from a new medication never meets the research team, but without that team's work, the medication would not exist.

6. The six dimensions and their information:
   - Core duties: what the worker actually does on a typical shift or day.
   - Typical education: whether the role requires a certificate, associate degree, bachelor's, graduate degree, or doctoral degree.
   - Credential: whether entry requires licensure, certification, or registration and who issues it.
   - Setting: where the work takes place (hospital, outpatient clinic, laboratory, remote, research bench).
   - Median wage: the BLS annual median wage nationally and for Texas specifically.
   - 10-year outlook: the BLS growth-rate category for the occupation and the driver behind it.

7. Most to least patient contact: Therapeutic (sustained direct physical contact throughout care); Diagnostic (contact during the test or draw, then the patient moves on); Support services (contact during specific tasks such as transport, cleaning, or meal delivery, but not clinical contact); Health informatics (minimal to no direct patient contact; mostly data and documentation); Biotechnology R&D (little to no patient contact; work is at lab benches, trial sites, or regulatory agencies).

8. Two systems with minimal patient contact: Health informatics (example: healthcare data analyst extracts population-level data for quality improvement; EHR analyst configures the electronic health record system) and Biotechnology R&D (example: bioinformatics specialist analyzes large genomic datasets; regulatory affairs specialist prepares FDA submissions). Both systems contribute meaningfully to healthcare outcomes without direct patient care.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Workers and contributions:
   - Triage RN (therapeutic): assesses Marcus's symptoms, vital signs, and pain level to determine the urgency and direction of his care.
   - EKG technician (diagnostic): runs a 12-lead EKG to capture the electrical activity of Marcus's heart and provide data to the physician.
   - Phlebotomist (diagnostic): draws blood for troponin testing, the key laboratory marker for cardiac muscle damage.
   - Medical scribe (informatics): documents the physician's assessment and clinical encounter in real time, creating the medical record.
   - Transport aide (support services): moves Marcus safely from the triage area to a monitored bay where he can receive continuous observation.
   - Pharmacist (therapeutic): verifies the aspirin order to ensure it is appropriate and correctly dosed before dispensing.
   - Clinical research coordinator (biotechnology R&D): will de-identify Marcus's data and add it to a cardiovascular outcomes registry for longitudinal research.

2. The triage RN assesses Marcus's clinical status, decides urgency, and directs the care episode. The pharmacist verifies a specific medication order before it is dispensed. Both are therapeutic, but the RN provides sustained assessment and coordination while the pharmacist performs a verification function at a specific decision point. This illustrates that the therapeutic system is the largest and most diverse, spanning roles that provide continuous care alongside roles that perform a defined technical function at one moment in the episode.

3. The medical scribe creates the authoritative clinical record. That record is the source that the next shift's team uses to understand what happened; it feeds the EHR system that routes the troponin result to the physician's alert queue; and it is the document the HIM coder will use to assign the diagnosis codes that drive the insurance claim. If the scribe misses or misrecords the physician's verbal assessment, every downstream system, including the coder, the billing team, and the next provider, works from an incomplete or inaccurate foundation.

4. Marcus's de-identified data enters a cardiovascular registry. Pooled with hundreds or thousands of similar patients over time, it becomes the evidence base used to evaluate whether current treatment protocols are effective, to identify which patient populations are at higher risk, and to develop or refine clinical guidelines. The guidelines that result may eventually change how Marcus's own family members are treated if they present with similar symptoms. Biotechnology R&D translates individual patient data into generalizable knowledge that improves future care at scale.

5. Accept any two roles from different systems. Example: EKG technician (diagnostic) vs. medical scribe (informatics).
   - Core duties: EKG technician operates equipment to capture heart rhythm data during a procedure; scribe documents the physician's clinical encounter continuously throughout the visit.
   - Typical education: EKG technician typically requires a short certificate; HIM or scribe roles range from on-the-job training to associate degree.
   - Setting: EKG technician is at the patient's bedside; scribe may be in the room or working remotely via telehealth documentation.
   - Patient contact: EKG technician has direct physical contact during the procedure; scribe has incidental contact (in the room) or none (if remote).
   A student choosing between them needs to understand whether they prefer procedural hands-on work with patients versus documentation and record-keeping, and what education investment each path requires.
