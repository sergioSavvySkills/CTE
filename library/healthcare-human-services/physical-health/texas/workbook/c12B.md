### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The seven categories are: alarms and error codes, abnormal readings, physical damage, unresponsive devices, calibration drift, unexpected behavior during operation, and battery or connectivity failures. Physical damage is a malfunction category even when the device is still running because damage visible on the outside often signals an invisible safety risk. A frayed cord or cracked housing can create an electrical hazard or structural failure that does not affect the device's readings until the moment of actual failure, and the chapter states the device must come out of service immediately when physical damage is observed.

2. An abnormal reading is more dangerous than an alarm because there is no alarm: the device produces numbers without alerting anyone that those numbers are wrong. An alarm at least signals that something needs attention. Calibration drift requires quality control checks to detect because the device consistently produces inaccurate values without generating a fault signal. Clinical intuition fails to detect it because the values may be plausible in isolation; only comparison with a known reference standard during a QC check reveals the systematic error.

3. Step one: assess whether the patient is in immediate danger (airway, breathing, responsiveness). Step two: check for a non-device explanation such as a probe that slipped off, nail polish interfering with a pulse-ox sensor, or a kinked IV tube. Step three: if the patient is stable and user error is ruled out, suspect the device and take it out of service. Skipping step one is the most dangerous error because a reading that appears to be a device error may be a true clinical emergency. A pulse-ox of 78% treated as a sensor problem when it represents real hypoxia can result in the patient's death.

4. An IV pump occlusion alarm most often traces to a kinked tube or a patient who repositioned their arm. Before suspecting the pump, check the tubing along its entire length and check the patient's arm for kinking, compression, or infiltration. The chapter says to rule out these user and environment factors before pulling the device. You would suspect the pump itself only after the tubing and patient site check out and the alarm persists, at which point the three-step heuristic moves to step three.

5. The most likely explanation is artifact: device-produced signal distortion caused by 60-Hz electrical interference, patient movement, or a loose lead. An alert, comfortable patient with a normal pulse is inconsistent with a life-threatening arrhythmia. The correct next step is to reattach the leads and repeat the tracing. The clinical picture, a patient who is not in distress, must be weighed against the device output, and in this case the patient clearly does not match the tracing.

6. Alarm fatigue is desensitization to alarms caused by excessive or frequent false alarms. It becomes a patient-safety problem because a clinician who has silenced many false alarms develops the habit of silencing alarms quickly or ignoring them, and a critical alarm that signals a real event gets the same response as the false ones. The chapter notes that this is why the Joint Commission issued a Sentinel Event Alert specifically about medical device alarm safety: the habitual silencing of alarms can mean a true emergency goes unrecognized.

7. An AED with a failed daily self-test cannot be relied upon in a cardiac emergency. The failure matters because the AED is needed only once, in an acute emergency, and that is precisely the moment when the battery or self-test failure would become apparent. Daily self-test failures are caught during safety rounds when a trained person checks the AED log. The chapter identifies checking the AED log during safety rounds as the specific practice that catches failures before an emergency.

8. According to the immediate safety action sequence, students are expected to: ensure patient safety first (switch to a backup method if available, call for clinical backup), take the device out of service, label it "Do Not Use" with date, time, initials, and a factual description, preserve all associated items (tubing, strips, pads), notify the clinical supervisor and biomed department, and document factually in the patient record.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Step one: the student assesses whether the patient is in immediate danger. She checks whether the patient shows signs of extreme hyperglycemia. He appears comfortable, is speaking normally, and reports eating a large breakfast. She concludes he is not in immediate danger. Step two: she checks for a non-device explanation. She examines the test strip lot number and determines the QC check was not done. She concludes there is a plausible non-device process failure (no QC check) that could explain the unreliable reading. Step three: she suspects the device and takes it out of service. The subsequent QC check confirms the meter is reading significantly high.

2. The student is asking whether the patient matches the reading, the chapter's core clinical-reasoning question for abnormal readings. This step must come before suspecting the device because an abnormal reading may represent a true clinical condition requiring immediate response. A glucose of "HI" in a patient showing signs of severe hyperglycemia is an emergency, not a device problem. The patient assessment determines whether step one of the heuristic requires calling for clinical backup; only a stable patient allows progression to steps two and three.

3. The QC check had not been done, which falls under calibration drift: a device that produces numbers without alarming but whose values may be systematically wrong. This differs from alarm-based malfunctions because there is no signal that anything is wrong. The device produced a number ("HI") and the only way to know whether that number was trustworthy was to check whether the QC process had validated the meter that morning. Without the QC check, the meter's output could not be trusted.

4. Additional steps from the immediate safety action sequence: preserve all associated items (the glucose test strips, any packaging) for biomed investigation; notify the supervisor and the biomed/clinical engineering department; document factually in the patient record, including what the device displayed, when the reading was taken, the patient's clinical status, and every action taken. The "Do Not Use" tag must contain: date, time, the student's initials, and a brief factual description of what was observed (accept: "meter returned HI reading; QC check not completed; meter may be reading high").

5. Items to preserve for biomed: the glucose meter, the test strip lot being used, any remaining strips from the same lot, and the QC log documentation. The chapter states that discarding associated items makes investigation impossible because biomed needs those items to determine whether the failure was a meter malfunction, a strip lot problem, or a storage or handling error. Without the strips and the QC documentation, the investigation loses key evidence and the finding cannot be traced to a correctable cause.
