**Definitions:**

- **A.** The top of Maslow's pyramid: realizing potential, finding meaning, and living with purpose; addressed in palliative care and rehabilitation
- **B.** Maslow's second level: physical safety from harm, emotional safety and predictability, and financial security
- **C.** The clinical assessment sequence derived from physiological-needs priority; used in every emergency from CPR to trauma triage
- **D.** The five-level pyramid organizing human needs from physiological survival at the base to self-actualization at the top
- **E.** Maslow's fourth level: respect, autonomy, and dignity; easily eroded by loss of control in a healthcare setting
- **F.** The base of Maslow's pyramid: air, water, food, elimination, sleep, shelter, and temperature regulation
- **G.** The practical application of Maslow: when a lower-level need is unmet, address it before moving to higher-level needs
- **H.** Maslow's third level: meaningful connection, family presence, therapeutic rapport; isolation measurably worsens health outcomes

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers -- accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The key organizing principle is that lower-level needs take priority when they are unmet: a person cannot engage with higher-level needs until the levels below are adequately met. For healthcare workers under time pressure, this provides a clear decision rule: if a patient's physiological or safety needs are threatened, address those first before engaging with belonging, esteem, or self-actualization concerns. This prevents the error of offering comfort while a crisis goes unaddressed.

2. Level 1, physiological: a patient's oxygen saturation drops and needs supplemental oxygen. Level 2, safety: a patient asks for a bedside safety rail because she is afraid of falling. Level 3, love and belonging: a patient in a single room reports feeling isolated because no family has visited. Level 4, self-esteem: a patient asks to be addressed by her first name and to be told what each medication is before receiving it. Level 5, self-actualization: a stroke rehabilitation patient says he wants to return to woodworking and asks if that is still possible.

3. The drop in oxygen saturation is a physiological need (Level 1); the family visit question is a belonging need (Level 3). The physiological need takes priority because unmet physiological needs are immediately life-threatening, while the belonging need, though real and important, does not carry immediate biological risk. Maslow's principle is that lower unmet levels take precedence over higher ones.

4. Measurable consequences means the effects can be observed and quantified, not just described. The textbook states that patients without social connection have longer hospital stays, poorer recovery, and higher readmission rates. A patient who feels invisible and stripped of dignity may refuse care, fail to disclose important symptoms, or lose the motivation to participate in recovery, all of which produce worse clinical outcomes that show up in measurable data.

5. A physiological need is a biological requirement for immediate survival: air, water, food, elimination, sleep. A safety need is protection from harm: fall prevention, infection control, emotional predictability. In clinical practice, the distinction matters because they require different interventions. A drop in oxygen saturation requires immediate physiological intervention (oxygen delivery); a patient who is afraid of falling requires safety-level interventions (bed alarm, gait training, clear call-light access). Treating them as the same level would produce mismatched responses.

6. Healthcare workers protect self-esteem by: (a) explaining every procedure before performing it, which preserves the patient's sense of control and autonomy; (b) asking how the patient prefers to be addressed, which honors the patient's identity; (c) offering choices whenever choices exist, which restores agency in a setting where most decisions are made for the patient; (d) treating patients as the experts on their own experience, which communicates respect for their knowledge of themselves.

7. In acute emergencies, the patient's immediate physiological and safety needs are dominant and consuming, making it clinically inappropriate to shift attention to self-actualization. In palliative care and rehabilitation, the patient's physiological status is managed or stable, so the patient's focus naturally shifts to questions of meaning, identity, and what their life will look like within new constraints. Self-actualization is not a luxury in those settings; it is often the factor that determines whether the patient engages with rehabilitation at all.

8. A nurse who only addresses physiological and safety needs might miss a patient who is physiologically stable but is experiencing deep isolation, loss of dignity, or despair about their prognosis. The textbook's example is the patient with stable vitals who hasn't eaten and is silently grieving the loss of his independence. Without someone asking a follow-up question, that patient's unmet belonging and esteem needs go unaddressed, potentially leading to depression, reduced motivation to participate in care, inadequate nutrition, and longer recovery.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Mr. Rivera's statement addresses Level 4, self-esteem, and Level 5, self-actualization. Self-esteem involves autonomy, dignity, and the sense that one is valued; he has lost the ability to perform a basic self-care task independently, which directly erodes his sense of competence and autonomy. Self-actualization involves identity and purpose; his 40-year career as a teacher was central to who he is, and his inability to button his own shirt signals a broader loss of the capable, purposeful self he built over a lifetime.

2. The interaction itself addresses Level 3, love and belonging. The PCT creates belonging by sitting down (physically committing attention), using Mr. Rivera's name (acknowledging him as a person), and genuinely asking how he is feeling (inviting honest communication). These behaviors communicate that he is seen and that his experience matters to the people caring for him, which directly addresses his need for therapeutic rapport and human connection.

3. The social worker referral is best positioned to address Level 4 (self-esteem) and Level 3 (belonging) by connecting Mr. Rivera to resources that can help restore some sense of agency and community connection. The chaplain referral is best positioned to address Level 5 (self-actualization) by engaging with his search for meaning and purpose within his changed circumstances. These professionals have the training to work specifically within those domains in ways that general clinical care does not.

4. Maslow's framework makes clear that normal physiological readings do not mean all patient needs are met. The hierarchy has five levels; checking off the bottom two does not address the top three. The PCT records and communicates her observation because clinical care is responsible for the whole patient, and an observation about behavior and mood at a higher Maslow level is clinically meaningful information that should reach the people best positioned to act on it.

5. Without the PCT's intervention, Mr. Rivera's Level 3 (belonging), Level 4 (esteem), and Level 5 (self-actualization) needs remain unaddressed. Likely clinical consequences include: continued or worsening depression, reduced appetite and nutritional intake, decreased motivation to participate in care or rehabilitation, and social withdrawal that prolongs recovery. Research cited in the chapter establishes that unmet belonging needs are associated with longer hospital stays and higher readmission rates, making this not merely a compassion issue but a quality-of-care issue with measurable downstream effects.
