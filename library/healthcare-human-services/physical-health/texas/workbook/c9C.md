### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. It means ethics reasoning in technology contexts is not about finding one correct answer but about identifying which principles are at stake and articulating why different priorities lead to different conclusions. It requires the ability to hold two legitimate perspectives in tension, explain each using the same framework, and arrive at a defensible position without dismissing the other side. This is harder than answering right-or-wrong questions and is exactly what the chapter is designed to develop.

2. Algorithmic bias is when a clinical algorithm produces systematically different (and less accurate) outputs for some groups than others because of biases in the data it was trained on. In the 2019 study, the algorithm was trained on historical healthcare spending data. Because Black patients historically received less care due to systemic inequities, their spending data was lower. The algorithm interpreted lower spending as lower illness severity. It then reproduced this error at scale, underestimating the needs of Black patients across the millions of cases it processed.

3. Automation bias is the tendency to over-trust a machine output, especially under time pressure. It is dangerous for clinicians specifically because clinical decisions carry life-or-death consequences. In any other industry, over-trusting a machine recommendation might result in a bad product recommendation or a scheduling error. In a clinical setting, over-trusting a sepsis alert can result in unnecessary aggressive intervention; dismissing it can result in failure to treat a deteriorating patient. The clinician remains legally responsible for the outcome regardless of what the algorithm said.

4. Telehealth is a beneficence and justice benefit because it allows patients in rural areas who previously had to travel hours for specialist care to access that care by video, removing a significant access barrier. It is also a justice problem because the digital divide means the populations most likely to benefit from this access, rural communities, elderly patients, and lower-income households, are also the least likely to have the broadband, devices, or digital literacy required to use it. Expanding telehealth without addressing the digital divide can widen health equity gaps rather than narrow them.

5. Consumer health wearables are made by companies that are not covered entities under HIPAA, so the federal health privacy law does not apply to the data they collect. The ethical principle threatened is autonomy: the patient's right to make informed decisions about their own information. The mechanism of the threat is that terms of service, which users technically agree to but rarely read carefully, may permit sharing data with employers, advertisers, or researchers. Consent that exists on paper but is not genuinely informed is not meaningful autonomy.

6. Non-maleficence means weighing the risks of action against the risks of inaction. In the sepsis scenario, the algorithm flagged a patient as high risk. The risk of not acting is that a true sepsis case goes untreated. The risk of acting is that aggressive fluid resuscitation in a patient who does not have sepsis can cause fluid overload. In this case the algorithm's sensitivity for Black patients was 51%, meaning it was wrong about half the time, significantly increasing the risk of unnecessary intervention and the harm that follows.

7. An incidental finding is information a genetic test reveals that the patient did not ask about. For example, a panel ordered to look for hereditary cancer syndromes might reveal a variant associated with heart disease. The ethical problem is that the patient consented to learn about one thing and now has information about another that they may not want or be prepared for. The principles most directly in tension are autonomy (the patient's right to choose what to know about their own body, including the right not to know) and beneficence (the obligation to share clinically significant information that could save the patient's life).

8. An advance directive is a legal document that records a patient's wishes about end-of-life care for situations when they can no longer communicate those wishes. End-of-life technology makes it urgent because mechanical ventilation, ECMO, artificial nutrition, and dialysis can now sustain organ function even when the underlying condition is not recoverable. Without a directive, those decisions fall to surrogates or clinicians who may not know what the patient would have wanted. A patient without a directive leaves the hardest decisions to others.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Beneficence: the physician ordered fluid resuscitation intending to treat a potentially life-threatening infection, which is exactly what the principle of acting in the patient's best interest looks like under time pressure. Non-maleficence: the intervention caused a fluid overload complication, a direct harm caused by the clinical action itself. Justice: the algorithm is known to perform significantly worse for Black patients, and this patient is Black; she received care shaped by a tool that encoded racial inequity. Autonomy: the patient was not told that an algorithm with a known racial performance gap shaped the clinical decision made about her body.

2. Automation bias explains the physician's behavior as the natural human tendency to accept a machine recommendation, especially when under time pressure and when the recommendation aligns with a worst-case-scenario concern (sepsis). The physician should have evaluated the patient's clinical presentation independently before accepting the alert, noting whether the presentation was consistent with sepsis beyond the algorithmic flag. Specific information that would have helped: knowing that the algorithm's sensitivity for Black patients is 51%, meaning it produces a false positive in approximately half of cases for this demographic.

3. The justice principle requires fair allocation of resources and care, and names structural inequity as a justice issue rather than a logistical fact. The hospital knows the tool performs unequally for Black patients and has not corrected it. This means the hospital is knowingly deploying a tool that produces less accurate results for a specific demographic, which is a justice violation. Addressing it in practice would require either correcting the algorithm (retraining on less biased data or adjusting for the disparity), suspending its use for affected patients, or at minimum disclosing the known limitation to clinicians at the point of the alert so they can calibrate their response.

4. Accept any well-reasoned argument grounded in the autonomy principle. A strong response would argue that autonomy requires informed decision-making, that the patient has a right to know when her care is being shaped by an algorithm with a known racial performance gap, and that consent that does not include this information is structurally incomplete. A student may also note the regulatory dimension: current standards do not require this disclosure, which the textbook calls an "active ethical and regulatory debate," so a strong answer acknowledges what the law currently requires versus what the principle demands.

5. Accept any response that connects a specific nursing action to a chapter concept. Strong responses might include: reviewing the algorithm's alert against the patient's clinical presentation before the physician acted, asking whether the alert had been cross-checked against clinical assessment, documenting the clinical reasoning and timeline, following up after the fluid resuscitation to monitor for fluid overload, or ensuring the patient understood what had happened and why. The key is that the nurse's role is to apply independent clinical judgment alongside, not instead of, the algorithm.
