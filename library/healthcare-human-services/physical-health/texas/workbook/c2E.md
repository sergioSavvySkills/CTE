**Definitions:**

- **A.** A 2 to 3 minute mid-shift regroup used when conditions change and the team needs to realign
- **B.** A formal 20 to 45 minute hospital teaching presentation for a clinical audience using precise terminology and evidence citations
- **C.** A paper or index-card sketch of the presentation's message flow, built before any slide is created, used to confirm logical order and identify what to cut
- **D.** Speaking the presentation out loud before delivery to surface timing, stumble points, filler words, and pacing problems that silent review does not catch
- **E.** A 5 to 10 minute post-event structured review covering what went well, what did not, and what to change next time
- **F.** A 3 to 5 minute start-of-shift presentation naming goals, roles, and anticipated issues
- **G.** The practice of one idea per slide, minimal text, and honest visuals, so the audience can listen without reading
- **H.** A brief staff-education presentation delivered to colleagues on a unit, focused on one practical skill or policy change

## Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

> A health science student has 5 minutes at the next staff meeting to present new hand-hygiene compliance data to the nursing staff on her unit. She builds 4 slides: the compliance number, the comparison to last month, the two most common failure points from the audit, and the one change the team is being asked to make. She rehearses aloud twice, cutting her time from 7 minutes to 4 minutes 45 seconds on the second run. She also catches that she said "kind of concerning" to describe a 12-point drop and replaces it with "a 12-point drop that puts us below the national benchmark." 4. The student has 5 minutes but arrives with a presentation clocking in at 4 minutes 45 seconds on her second rehearsal. Why is finishing under the time limit better than going over, and what does this preparation choice communicate to the audience?

### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. A deck of slides is an output, and outputs built without a plan reflect the plan's absence. What should exist first is an answer to four questions in writing: who the audience is and what they know, the single message they should carry out, the time limit, and the desired action or decision after the presentation. Only after these are answered is the presenter ready to build a storyboard and eventually slides.

2. A storyboard lets the presenter ask whether every idea card earns its place, whether any card is doing two jobs and should be split, and whether any card could be cut without breaking the argument. Fixing logical connection problems or cutting unnecessary cards on index cards takes 30 seconds. Fixing those same problems after a 10-slide deck is built takes much longer, and the natural reluctance to delete slides that took time to build makes the problem worse.

3. The "wall of bullets" slide, with eight or ten full-sentence bullet points, causes the audience to read the slide faster than the speaker speaks, so they finish before the speaker does and then wait. The solution is to put only the key term or phrase on the slide and speak the explanation aloud. This keeps the audience listening to the speaker rather than reading independently, and it enforces slide discipline: one idea per slide with minimal text.

4. A brief is a 3 to 5 minute start-of-shift presentation naming goals, anticipated issues, and team roles; it is used before the shift or case begins to align the team on what is ahead. A debrief is a 5 to 10 minute post-event review used after an event or shift ends; it covers what went well, what did not, and what to change. The brief looks forward; the debrief looks backward.

5. Grand rounds is a formal 20 to 45 minute presentation for a clinical audience expecting precise terminology, evidence citations, and a clear clinical argument. An in-service is a 10 to 20 minute staff-education presentation for colleagues on a unit who want to know what to do differently on their next shift. Grand rounds requires deep technical preparation and formal citation; an in-service is more conversational and focused on a single practical skill, which changes how detailed the slides should be and how much background context is appropriate.

6. She skipped rehearsing aloud. Aloud rehearsal would have revealed the timing problem (she ran over) and the filler words, both of which silent review does not surface. Silent review feels complete because the speaker is supplying the missing words mentally; aloud rehearsal requires actually saying every word, which shows exactly where the timing breaks and where filler words fill hesitation gaps.

7. If the presentation falls apart without the slides, the slides are serving as the speaker's script, which means the speaker does not actually know the material well enough to deliver it. The standard is that the speaker should know the message cold enough that the slides are supporting evidence, not a crutch. A presenter who can deliver the core message without the slides and then use slides to reinforce it is in control of the material.

8. Four things to listen for: (1) filler words ("um," "uh," "like," "so") that appear when the speaker is unsure what comes next, signaling a content gap or lack of rehearsal; (2) pauses that are too short, signaling rushing, or too long, signaling lost momentum; (3) hedging phrases ("kind of," "sort of," "I think maybe") that undermine the speaker's authority and make the content sound uncertain; (4) transitions between sections that are not smooth, signaling a logical gap between ideas that the storyboard should have caught.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The four storyboard cards likely said: (1) the number, 72% compliance last month; (2) the comparison, down from last month's rate; (3) the two most common failure points from the audit; and (4) the one change the team is being asked to make. The storyboard keeps the presentation organized around what the audience (nursing staff) needs to know and do, rather than around how the data was collected or what the student found most interesting. Each card maps to an audience need: knowing the number, understanding the trend, knowing the causes, knowing what to do next.

2. The first rehearsal revealed that the presentation was 7 minutes long, which is 2 minutes over the 5-minute time limit. The second rehearsal confirmed she had trimmed it to 4 minutes 45 seconds. Aloud rehearsal, not silent review, surfaced the timing problem.

3. The student caught a hedging phrase and an approximation. "Kind of concerning" is vague and undermines the authority of the message. "A 12-point drop that puts us below the national benchmark" is specific, measurable, and frames the data in a clinically meaningful context (the benchmark comparison). The revision applies slide discipline's principle that the audience needs to hear concrete, authoritative information, not the speaker's hedged impressions.

4. Finishing under time shows respect for the audience's schedule and demonstrates that the speaker has prepared and edited rather than cramming in everything she knows. Going over time in a 5-minute slot in a staff meeting takes time from other agenda items or from the staff's patient care responsibilities. It also communicates to the audience that the speaker has not rehearsed. Finishing under time signals competence and preparation.

5. Scripting the first 30 seconds gives the speaker's body and voice something specific to do at the moment anxiety is highest. The anxiety spike occurs when the speaker stands up and the room goes quiet; having the opening memorized means the speaker does not have to generate the first sentence from scratch under pressure. The chapter says knowing the opening sentence "cold" gives the body something to do besides panic, which allows the speaker to settle into the rest of the presentation once the opening is complete.

### Part E: Reflection

No single correct answer. Award full credit for responses that: (1) use at least three chapter vocabulary terms accurately, (2) engage specifically with at least one of the preparation steps (storyboarding, slide discipline, aloud rehearsal, or the four planning questions), and (3) demonstrate understanding that preparation is a clinical skill with audience-specific standards, not just practice for its own sake. Deduct credit for responses that treat presentation quality as a matter of confidence or personality rather than preparation.
