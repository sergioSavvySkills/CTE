### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Prescription drugs account for only about 9 percent of all US health spending, according to the NHEA. The largest single category is hospital care, which accounts for roughly 31 percent of total health spending. The news coverage of drug prices is disproportionate to drug spending's actual share of the total.

2. The textbook's primary explanation for the cost gap is prices, not utilization. The US pays more for the same procedures, the same drugs, and the same devices than comparable nations do. An MRI, a hip replacement, a brand-name drug, and a hospital day all cost more in the US than in Canada, Germany, France, or the UK, even though Americans do not use dramatically more of these services.

3. ACOs are groups of providers who are jointly accountable for both the cost and quality of care for a defined patient population. Unlike fee-for-service, which pays providers for each service delivered regardless of outcome, ACOs share in savings when they keep their patients healthier and out of the hospital. This creates a financial incentive to invest in prevention and coordination rather than volume.

4. Administrative overhead refers to the cost of billing, insurance processing, prior authorization, and compliance documentation across hundreds of different insurance plans with different rules. The textbook says it does not buy better care because these costs are generated by the complexity of the payment system rather than by the delivery of clinical services. Simplifying the payment system would reduce overhead without removing any clinical service.

5. The textbook describes three direct ways hospitals anchor local economies: employing diverse workers across clinical and non-clinical roles, generating local tax revenue, and anchoring the surrounding business district. Every hospital job supports local spending in restaurants, housing, and retail. The hospital also provides the emergency access that makes a community viable for families with children.

6. Because Medicare and Medicaid set the payment rates and rules that govern roughly 40 percent of all US health spending, their decisions affect what services are economically viable for providers to offer. A hospital that cannot cover its costs for Medicare patients may eliminate a service line, affecting all patients regardless of insurance. And because CMS Conditions of Participation apply to any hospital receiving Medicare or Medicaid, the quality and operational standards those programs set become universal in practice.

7. The US is not dramatically older than Germany or Japan, both of which spend far less per capita on healthcare. If aging demographics were the primary driver, the US would need to be significantly older than peer nations to justify the cost gap, and it is not. The textbook points to price levels as a stronger explanation.

8. The textbook identifies at least three measurable effects: increases in cardiovascular-disease mortality, delayed cancer diagnosis, and worsening maternal outcomes. These outcomes worsen because travel distance to care increases significantly when the local hospital closes, and distance is a barrier to timely emergency and preventive care.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. (a) Low Medicare reimbursement rates is a cost-driver problem: the payment rate set by a government payer does not cover the hospital's cost of providing the service. (b) Uncompensated care from uninsured patients is a revenue problem tied to lack of coverage: the hospital provides services it cannot bill for, eroding operating margin. (c) Inability to recruit specialist physicians reflects the workforce dimension of rural hospital viability: lower revenue limits the salaries and resources that attract providers.

2. Administrative overhead is a recognized cost driver: billing complexity across multiple insurance plans requires significant staff time and IT infrastructure. Joining a regional ACO can pool administrative resources, sharing the cost of billing systems, compliance staff, and health information technology across multiple facilities rather than each facility bearing the full cost alone. This directly reduces overhead without cutting clinical services.

3. The officer is correct because the textbook establishes that hospitals are local economic anchors. Beyond healthcare access, the county would lose its largest private employer (eliminating dozens of jobs across clinical and non-clinical roles), its largest generator of local tax revenue, and the economic activity generated by those employees spending in local businesses. Real estate values would likely fall, and families might relocate to communities with closer hospital access, further shrinking the tax base.

4. Medicaid expansion would extend Medicaid coverage to adults up to 138 percent of the federal poverty level, converting many currently uninsured patients into Medicaid-insured patients. This would reduce the volume of uncompensated care the hospital provides while generating Medicaid reimbursement for those services. Texas's choice not to expand Medicaid means the rural hospital continues to absorb uncompensated care costs that expansion states' hospitals do not face at the same rate.

5. Clinical roles exist inside a system shaped by economic forces. When a hospital closes or cuts services because of financial pressures, clinical workers lose jobs, patients lose access, and the scope of practice shrinks for everyone. Understanding that Medicare reimbursement rates, Medicaid policy, and administrative overhead directly affect hiring, salaries, and service availability helps a student make informed career decisions and advocate for policy changes that affect their work.
