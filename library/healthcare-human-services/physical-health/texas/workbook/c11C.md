### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Merely naming the six agencies satisfies a memorization goal. The TEKS verb "relate" requires tracing the line from a specific daily clinical practice to the exact rule behind it and the body that enforces it. This is a higher-order skill: a student who can name OSHA but cannot connect it to the sharps container on the wall has not yet met the standard's intent.

2. The two-patient-identifier rule is TJC NPSG 01.01.01, which requires two independent pieces of patient-identification information to be confirmed before any procedure or medication. A valid second identifier: date of birth confirmed against the armband. An example that does not count: the patient's room number, because room assignments change and the room number is not tied to the individual patient.

3. Time pressure: The fix is system redesign, such as placing hand-hygiene dispensers at the point of care to eliminate the distance cost of compliance. Simply telling a nurse to "try harder" during an understaffed shift does not change the time available and therefore does not change the behavior. Inconvenient design: A sharps container that is full or a soap dispenser that is empty forces a choice between the safe behavior and getting the task done. Restocking and repositioning supplies removes the structural obstacle rather than relying on individual willpower.

4. Normalization of deviance is the process by which small departures from safe procedure that do not immediately cause harm gradually become accepted as normal. Example: A nurse skips a medication double-check once during a busy shift. No harm occurs. Over several weeks, she skips it routinely, and colleagues who observe her begin doing the same. After months, the double-check is functionally absent from that unit's practice. When a wrong-dose error finally occurs, it was enabled by dozens of normalized shortcuts, not one single decision.

5. The Swiss cheese model represents each layer of a safety system as a slice of cheese with holes. No single layer is perfect; each has gaps. A single layer's hole rarely aligns with the patient to cause harm. However, when multiple layers fail simultaneously and their holes align, an unobstructed path opens from hazard to harm. This explains why a single skipped step usually has no immediate consequence (other layers catch it), but the same step normalized across a team eventually produces harm (there are no longer enough intact layers to fill the aligned gaps).

6. CUS is a speak-up tool from TeamSTEPPS: Concerned (state your concern factually), Uncomfortable (state your discomfort with the situation), Safety issue (name it explicitly as a safety issue requiring action). The two-challenge rule requires that if a concern is raised and ignored, it must be raised a second time before the action proceeds. The textbook says to use these tools when a more senior person is skipping a safety step. The CUS sequence is ordered from observation to personal discomfort to explicit safety claim because it escalates progressively, giving the senior person an opportunity to reconsider at each step before the student is forced to invoke the safety-issue label.

7. The four paths are: ask the preceptor or supervisor what rule a specific practice traces to; search the facility's policy library, which usually cites CDC, OSHA, TJC, and CMS in policy footers; go to the agency website directly, since all those agencies publish their standards freely; and apply the rule that the stricter standard controls when two rules seem to overlap or conflict.

8. Fresh eyes means that a student entering a clinical site has not yet been conditioned to treat normalized shortcuts as normal. Practices that have been deviated from for months or years are invisible to the staff performing them because the deviation has become their baseline. A student who just read the standard in a textbook and then sees a gap between the standard and observed practice is positioned to notice what experienced staff cannot, which is a genuine patient-safety contribution rather than a deficit of experience.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The safety practice being skipped is the two-patient-identifier check before medication administration, required by TJC NPSG 01.01.01. The practice is designed to prevent wrong-patient medication errors, which can cause serious harm or death when a patient receives a drug intended for a different person.

2. During shifts one through four, a single practitioner's shortcut (which had no immediate harmful consequence) was spreading to other staff who observed it without challenge. This is normalization of deviance in real time: the deviation was not harming anyone visibly, so it was being accepted as an alternative norm. The student correctly recognized it as systemic at the point when two other nurses had adopted the same shortcut, distinguishing a single lapse (one person, one shift) from a pattern (multiple people, multiple shifts).

3. "I'm Concerned that we haven't been checking two identifiers before meds." This communicates the factual observation. "I'm Uncomfortable because I know this is a TJC requirement from NPSG 01.01.01." This adds the student's personal discomfort and names the regulatory source, shifting the conversation from preference to compliance. "I think this is a Safety issue we need to address." This explicitly names the stakes. The order is effective because it moves from observation to personal response to safety declaration, giving the preceptor escalating opportunities to engage before the student invokes the safety label.

4. The authority gradient explains why the student hesitated through four shifts before speaking up: the preceptor's greater experience and status created an implicit hierarchy in which questioning the preceptor felt presumptuous. The initial defensive response confirms the gradient was operating. The two-challenge rule would have required the student, if the preceptor had remained defensive after the first CUS statement, to raise the concern a second, explicit time before the medication administration proceeded, ensuring the concern was on record even if the preceptor did not immediately respond to it.

5. A Sentinel Event is a Joint Commission-defined serious patient-safety event resulting in death, permanent harm, or severe temporary harm, which triggers a required root-cause analysis. A wrong-patient medication error, depending on the drug, could easily meet this threshold. The highest-value outcome of the student's speak-up is that a preventable patient-harm event never occurred, which means no root-cause analysis was needed, no patient suffered, and the system was corrected while still in the prevention phase rather than the harm-response phase.
