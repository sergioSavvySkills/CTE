### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. The continuous glucose monitor generates glucose readings continuously, making its primary function diagnostic. However, the alerts it delivers to the patient warning of dangerous levels have a therapeutic effect by prompting corrective action. The data it sends to the EHR platform places it within the Health Informatics system because it is actively populating a health record. One device thus participates in three systems based on the function being considered at any moment.

2. Standard radiography uses a single beam of ionizing radiation to produce a flat, two-dimensional image that shows bone and dense tissue clearly. CT uses a series of X-ray slices acquired from multiple angles and reconstructs them computationally into a three-dimensional picture. CT provides far more anatomical detail and can image soft tissue, blood vessels, and complex structures in ways that a flat X-ray cannot, making it appropriate for evaluating chest anatomy and blood vessels when ruling out a pulmonary embolism.

3. The EKG machine records the heart's electrical activity at a single point in time through skin-surface leads and is appropriate when a snapshot of rhythm is needed in a clinical or emergency setting. The Holter monitor records a continuous EKG over 24 to 48 hours as the patient goes about normal life, making it appropriate when symptoms such as palpitations or presyncope occur intermittently and are unlikely to be captured during a brief clinic visit.

4. A smart infusion pump delivers IV medications at a programmed rate and uses a built-in drug library to alert the nurse if a dose falls outside a defined safe range, addressing the risk of programming errors and catching overdoses before delivery. A PCA pump lets patients self-administer a preset dose of pain medication within locked limits, giving patients control over their pain management while hardware limits prevent any single administration from exceeding a safe dose. The smart pump protects the patient from clinician programming errors; the PCA pump protects the patient from receiving too much medication by accident or patient misuse.

5. A biological indicator is a quality control test for sterilizers that uses live bacterial spores, the organisms most resistant to sterilization, to confirm that the autoclave cycle actually killed all microorganisms. A positive result means the spores survived, which indicates sterilization did not occur. Instruments from that cycle may already have been delivered to operating rooms and used on patients in a non-sterile state, creating a potential for surgical site infections and requiring a recall of all affected instruments and patient notification.

6. FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is the modern API standard that allows different EHR systems to share structured clinical data with each other. Without a common standard, an EHR from one vendor cannot exchange data in a readable format with an EHR from a different vendor. The result would be that patient data stays siloed within individual institutions, making coordinated care across facilities, remote monitoring, and population health analytics functionally impossible.

7. The five sources are: FDA 510(k) database (what the device does and what it is cleared for); manufacturer documentation and IFU (specific operational and safety details for the exact device model); ECRI Institute reports and Top 10 hazards list (independent safety performance and failure-mode data); professional organization references such as ASRT, AARC, ASCP, and APTA (which credential operates the device and in what clinical context); Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook (which profession operates the device and its workforce context). Accept other valid distinctions between sources.

8. PCR thermocyclers were originally used in research settings to amplify specific DNA sequences for laboratory study. They are now widely deployed in clinical diagnostic labs for pathogen detection, including COVID-19 testing. The chapter uses PCR as an example of technology that originated in biotechnology R&D and crossed into the Diagnostic Services system as the technique became sufficiently reliable and fast for clinical use. NGS platforms show the same trajectory, moving from genome-research settings into cancer mutation profiling and clinical microbiology.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Five devices: (1) Pulse oximeter: Diagnostic Services; not individually credentialed in scenario, typically operated by any nurse or assistant at the bedside. (2) EKG machine: Diagnostic Services; ECG technician (named in scenario). (3) Chest X-ray: Diagnostic Services; radiologic technologist (named in scenario). (4) CT scanner (CT angiogram): Diagnostic Services; radiologic technologist (named in scenario; CT is a radiography subspecialty). (5) Automated chemistry analyzer: Diagnostic Services; lab scientist (named in scenario as "lab scientist"). Accept slight variations in credentialing descriptions tied to chapter text.

2. A chest X-ray produces a flat, two-dimensional image that shows bony structures and gross lung density but cannot reliably visualize blood vessels or soft-tissue detail within the chest. CT angiography acquires cross-sectional slices and reconstructs them in three dimensions, allowing visualization of the pulmonary vasculature with contrast to detect a clot (embolism) directly. The three-dimensional vessel detail that CT provides is the feature that makes it the correct tool for ruling out pulmonary embolism.

3. CLIA (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments), enforced by CMS, governs all lab equipment quality control. CLIA requires that facilities perform and document QC procedures to confirm that analyzers are producing accurate results before patient specimens are run. This means that when a troponin or D-dimer value is reported, the facility has verified the analyzer was within its accuracy range for that shift, making the reported number trustworthy as the basis for a clinical decision.

4. Accept any two of the following (sample answers): Pulse oximeter: answered the question "is this patient hypoxic right now?" before the team knew the cause of the chest pain. Without knowing the oxygen saturation, the team could not determine urgency or whether supplemental oxygen was needed immediately. Automated chemistry analyzer (troponin): answered the question "is there cardiac muscle damage?" because elevated troponin indicates myocardial infarction. Without this, the team could not distinguish cardiac from pulmonary causes. CT angiogram: answered the question "is there a pulmonary embolism?" a diagnosis that requires anticoagulation and would be missed by X-ray alone. EKG: answered the question "is there an arrhythmia or ST-segment change indicating an MI pattern?" needed before any cardiac intervention decision.

5. Pulse oximeter research profile using chapter content: What it does: estimates oxygen saturation through the skin using infrared light (from chapter). Clinical use case: monitoring oxygenation in emergency and routine patient assessment (from chapter scenario). Manufacturer: not specified in chapter; would consult FDA 510(k) database for cleared manufacturers. Approximate cost range: not in chapter; would consult ECRI Institute or manufacturer. Credentialed role: used by nurses and medical assistants at the bedside; chapter does not name a dedicated credential for this device. Authoritative sources: FDA 510(k) database (to identify cleared devices), ECRI Institute (for safety alerts), Bureau of Labor Statistics (for credentialing context). Accept reasonable answers tied to chapter text.
