### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers. Accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Treating conflict as a clinical reality rather than a failure means teams should anticipate it, train for it, and have named protocols for managing it rather than treating each conflict as a surprise or a sign that something has gone wrong. The chapter notes that patients and families are scared, exhausted, and dealing with unwanted information, while teams are short-staffed and working under time pressure. These conditions reliably produce conflict, and the question is not whether it will occur but whether the team has the tools to handle it well.

2. Compete (TKI) is correct in a safety emergency, such as a cardiac arrest, where a team leader must direct the resuscitation without stopping to build consensus. In that situation, Collaborate (TKI) would be wrong because it requires time, trust, and willingness from both parties, none of which are available during a code. The chapter states explicitly that a clinical leader who seeks consensus during a code is using the wrong tool. The stakes require a high-assertiveness, low-cooperation approach so that decisions are made and executed quickly.

3. Validation names the other person's feeling without judging it: "I can see you're really frustrated, and I understand why." Sympathy, and the phrase "I'm sorry you feel that way," shifts blame and centers the speaker's emotional response rather than the patient's. The chapter says this phrase makes things worse because it contains a subtle implication that the patient's feelings are the problem, rather than the situation. Without validation, any information offered next will be received as defensive.

4. CANDOR (Communication and Optimal Resolution) operationalizes the sequence of acknowledging the event, apologizing where legally appropriate, explaining what is known, and offering a concrete next step for patient-harm events. The textbook mentions legal consultation because certain admissions made during this process can carry liability consequences; what a healthcare worker says about a harm event can affect the legal exposure of the facility and the individual, so the amount disclosed must be informed by legal advice.

5. Reframing restates the conflict from a contest of positions to a shared goal. The textbook's two versions: without reframing: "I understand you want the test done tonight, and that's not possible." With reframing: "It sounds like your priority is getting answers as quickly as possible, and mine is the same. Here's the fastest path we can take together." The underlying conflict is identical: the test cannot be done tonight. But the framing changes whether the interaction feels like a standoff or a collaboration toward a shared objective.

6. CUS language is a specific, trainable script: "I'm Concerned. I'm Uncomfortable. This is a Safety issue." A vague "I'm not sure about this" can be interpreted as uncertainty rather than a safety escalation, and it gives the senior person permission to dismiss it as a matter of preference. A CUS statement, by contrast, uses words that every trained team member recognizes as a safety escalation, removing the ambiguity that allows the concern to be waved off.

7. The two-challenge rule requires raising a safety concern twice before escalating. After the second dismissal, the rule gives the team member explicit permission to escalate to the next level: a supervisor, charge nurse, risk management, or an ethics committee. The rule provides professional cover because the authority gradient in healthcare makes people feel they should defer to more senior personnel even when they have a legitimate safety concern.

8. The five conditions are: (1) Was the immediate safety issue resolved? (2) Did the relationship survive in a working state? (3) Was the underlying problem named and not just papered over? (4) Is the documentation clear enough to support any follow-up? (5) Would the same approach work with this person or situation next time? "They stopped arguing" does not address any of these conditions. The conflict may have ended because one party was silenced rather than heard, the underlying problem may still be present, and the documentation may be empty, meaning the same event could recur with no prevention.


### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. The first application of the two-challenge rule was the student's initial statement: "I'm concerned about the trailing zero on this order." The pharmacist dismissed it without looking up. That was the first challenge and the first dismissal. The second application was the student's follow-up: "I'm uncomfortable with this, it could be misread as 100 mg, and I think this is a safety issue." This was the second challenge. At that point, the pharmacist looked up, rechecked, and confirmed the error. The rule was complete at that moment.

2. First statement: "I'm concerned about the trailing zero on this order" contains the first CUS phrase, "I'm Concerned," but does not yet include Uncomfortable or Safety. Second statement: "I'm uncomfortable with this, it could be misread as 100 mg, and I think this is a safety issue" contains both "I'm Uncomfortable" and "this is a Safety issue," completing the CUS script. Across the two statements, all three CUS phrases were used in the correct order.

3. The authority gradient is the implicit pressure in healthcare to defer to the most senior person in the room. A student or junior team member who has a concern dismissed by a pharmacist or physician may interpret the dismissal as evidence that their concern was wrong, rather than as a safety event requiring escalation. The two-challenge rule is designed specifically to counter this tendency: it names the authority gradient as a known problem and provides a script and a rule that gives the junior person permission to push back a second time before escalating.

4. By keeping both statements focused on the order ("the trailing zero," "it could be misread as 100 mg") rather than on the pharmacist's behavior, the student used reframing: she positioned the issue as a shared safety concern rather than a personal disagreement. She also avoided the behaviors the chapter identifies as false resolution: winning by volume or authority, or making the conflict personal. The lack of escalating tone and the focus on the observable document rather than the pharmacist's judgment kept the interaction in the de-escalation zone.

5. After a second dismissal, the two-challenge rule gives the student explicit permission to escalate to a supervisor, charge nurse, risk management, or an ethics committee. Documentation of the conflict should be factual and non-blaming: the date and time, the specific order in question (the trailing zero notation), what the student observed, each statement made and each response received, and the ultimate outcome. "The pharmacist was dismissive and almost caused a serious error" is editorializing; "At 10:15 I identified a trailing zero on the order for patient X. I notified the pharmacist twice; after the second notification, the pharmacist reviewed and corrected the order at 10:18" is factual documentation that supports follow-up.
