### Part C: Comprehension Questions

**Sample answers -- accept well-reasoned responses that cite textbook content.**

1. Primary prevention targets healthy people who have not yet developed the disease: vaccines, PrEP, seatbelt use, tobacco cessation. Secondary prevention targets people who appear healthy but may have subclinical disease: colorectal cancer screening at 45, blood pressure checks, mammography. Tertiary prevention targets people with an established diagnosis: cardiac rehabilitation after a heart attack, diabetes self-management education to prevent complications. Each level acts at a different point in the disease process.

2. "Everyone should get this" is almost never correct because screening recommendations are population-specific and risk-specific. The USPSTF assigns grades based on who benefits from a given intervention given their age, sex, risk factors, and other characteristics. A Grade A recommendation for mammography applies to women aged 40-74, not to all people. A recommendation for abdominal aortic aneurysm screening applies specifically to men aged 65-75 who have ever smoked. Applying population-specific recommendations universally would mean screening people for whom the harms (false positives, unnecessary procedures, anxiety) outweigh the benefits.

3. For secondary prevention screening to be worthwhile: (1) there must be a reliable test for the condition; without reliability, false positives and false negatives undermine the entire enterprise. (2) The disease must have a meaningful preclinical window; if disease progresses so quickly that catching it early provides no treatment advantage, screening is not useful. (3) Earlier detection must actually improve outcomes; catching a disease early only helps if treatment during that early window is more effective than treatment later. (4) The benefits must outweigh the harms, including false-positive anxiety, unnecessary follow-up procedures, and overdiagnosis.

4. Herd immunity is the population-level protection that occurs when enough individuals in a community are immune to a disease (through vaccination or prior infection) that transmission chains cannot sustain themselves. People who cannot be vaccinated, including newborns, immunocompromised patients, and those with allergies to vaccine components, are protected because the disease cannot spread through the population even when it is introduced. Herd immunity is a primary-prevention strategy; it prevents disease before it starts, at the population level rather than the individual level.

5. The USPSTF is the US Preventive Services Task Force, an independent panel that reviews research on preventive interventions and assigns grades to its recommendations. The grades reflect the strength of evidence: Grade A (high certainty of substantial benefit) and Grade B (high certainty of moderate benefit or moderate certainty of substantial benefit) are the two levels that trigger mandatory cost-free coverage under the Affordable Care Act for insured patients. Grade D indicates evidence of net harm or no benefit. Grade I means evidence is insufficient to make a recommendation.

6. PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) is a daily medication that prevents HIV infection in people at high risk who have not yet been infected. The textbook uses it to illustrate that primary prevention can use pharmacologic tools, not just behavioral interventions. This is an important point because primary prevention is often described as if it only includes vaccines and lifestyle advice. PrEP demonstrates that medication can be used before disease begins, as part of a prevention strategy rather than treatment.

7. Cardiac rehabilitation is a supervised exercise, education, and counseling program delivered after a cardiac event such as a heart attack or cardiac surgery. It is tertiary prevention because it acts on people who already have an established cardiovascular condition, aiming to prevent further deterioration rather than to cure the disease or catch it early. The textbook cites USPSTF Grade A support and evidence that patients who complete cardiac rehab have lower mortality rates and fewer rehospitalizations than those who do not.

8. Using only individual-level prevention produces smaller effects than combining individual and population-level strategies because individual behavior change is constrained by the environments people live in. The textbook uses tobacco cessation as the example: individual counseling programs help people who participate, but without tobacco taxation, marketing restrictions, and smoke-free air laws, the structural pressures promoting tobacco use remain in place. Population-level strategies change the environment for everyone, including people who never accessed individual counseling. The combination produces larger, more durable effects.

### Part D: Clinical Scenario Analysis

1. Grade A means the USPSTF has high certainty that the net benefit is substantial, and the intervention is covered without cost-sharing under the ACA for insured patients. Colorectal cancer screening begins at age 45 for average-risk adults. It is secondary prevention because the target population appears healthy and asymptomatic; the screening detects cancer or precancerous polyps in the preclinical window, before the disease has produced symptoms the patient would notice. The patient in the scenario has no reported symptoms; catching the cancer or a polyp now, before it progresses, is exactly what secondary prevention is designed to do.

2. Primary prevention in this context means acting before type 2 diabetes has started. This patient has risk factors (BMI of 34 and family history) but has not been diagnosed. The population being acted on is people at elevated risk who are still disease-free. The intervention would include formal counseling on weight management and diabetes risk reduction, potentially referral to a lifestyle-change program such as the CDC-recognized National Diabetes Prevention Program, and discussion of how weight reduction and physical activity reduce the probability of progression to diabetes.

3. Hypertension management is tertiary prevention because the patient already has a diagnosed condition (poorly controlled hypertension) and the goal is to prevent the complications hypertension causes, specifically organ damage such as stroke, heart attack, and kidney failure, not to prevent hypertension from developing. Secondary prevention would have been a blood pressure screening for this patient before hypertension was diagnosed. Now that the diagnosis exists and control is poor, the goal is to prevent further damage and slow progression.

4. The overdue colorectal cancer screening is the most urgent because there is an active, undetected potential disease process: a polyp or early cancer may already be present and growing. Every month of delay is a month during which a treatable precancerous polyp could progress, or an early-stage tumor could advance to a later stage with worse outcomes. The primary-prevention counseling and hypertension management, while important, address ongoing risk management rather than an acute window of opportunity for disease detection that is already three years past its scheduled date.

5. If the NP only addressed the overdue screening, she would miss the opportunity to reduce the patient's diabetes risk through primary prevention and to improve hypertension management through tertiary prevention. The patient would leave the visit with a screening referral but without the counseling or medication-adherence support that address her other risks. Effective prevention requires all three levels because each acts at a different point in the disease process: stopping disease before it starts (primary), catching it early (secondary), and limiting damage from disease that is already present (tertiary). Addressing only one level leaves the other two disease processes unchecked.
