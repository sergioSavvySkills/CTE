# u02 — The Healthcare Team

## Purpose and description

u02 introduces how healthcare actually gets delivered — not as individual
practitioners but as coordinated teams. Students meet the roles on a
typical care team across the five health science systems, learn how
those roles collaborate, and discover the professional organizations
that credential and govern each role behind the scenes. The unit closes
with the leadership and teamwork skills that make teams functional:
goal-setting, team-building, running meetings, and contributing
constructively.

This is a lighter unit than u01 — three atoms, two weeks, no performance
task. Its job is to establish **team literacy** that every downstream
unit relies on.

## Why this unit exists

Quality of healthcare depends on team function, not individual heroics.
Grade 9-10 students usually imagine healthcare as solo work (the doctor
diagnoses, the nurse treats), and that mental model breaks down the
moment they encounter real clinical contexts. Establishing team literacy
early means every downstream unit can reference "the multidisciplinary
team" without re-teaching.

Professional organizations and credentialing bodies together form the
**invisible scaffolding** behind every licensed role — professional
organizations (AMA, ANA, and others) set practice standards, publish
guidelines, and run continuing-education programs, while separate
credentialing bodies (NBME, NCSBN, ARRT, and others) govern the exams
and hours required to earn and maintain the credential. Students need
to know both exist, and that they are not the same entity, so that
u01's career research makes sense ("who decides an RN is an RN?") and
so u05's scope-of-practice content has a foundation.

## Placement in the course (why u02 and not later)

Right after u01 careers is the natural move: students have met
individual roles; now they learn how roles work together. The
individual → team progression is pedagogically standard.

Team knowledge is also **relatively low cognitive load** — mostly
concept application and role identification, not dense science or legal
rules. That makes u02 a breather between the motivation-heavy u01 and
the science-spine u03.

## Essential questions

1. Why does healthcare require a team rather than individual experts?
2. What happens when teaming breaks down — and what are the known failure modes?
3. Who's on a typical healthcare team, and what does each role contribute?
4. How do professional organizations credential and govern who practices in healthcare?
5. What makes someone a leader in healthcare, and how are leadership skills different from clinical skills?

## Unit learning outcomes

Students will be able to:

1. Identify the roles on a multidisciplinary healthcare team and describe what each contributes to patient care.
2. Explain how team members collaborate across the five health science systems to deliver quality care.
3. Describe the role of professional organizations in credentialing, certifying, and setting practice standards for healthcare workers.
4. Identify the traits of an effective healthcare leader.
5. Demonstrate leadership and teamwork skills — goal-setting, team-building, and conducting meetings — in applied tasks and simulations.

## Content scope — in / out

**In scope for this unit**

- Multidisciplinary team roles across the 5 health science systems: physician, nurse (RN, LVN, CNA, NP), PA, allied health (RT, PT, OT, SLP, lab/imaging techs), pharmacist, social worker, chaplain, care coordinator, behavioral health, support services
- Collaboration patterns: how roles hand off, coordinate, and depend on each other
- "Teaming" as an active practice (per c8A — the concept, not just the noun)
- Professional organizations by role — AMA, ANA, AACN, AAMN, ASRT, AARC, APTA, AOTA, ASHA, ASCLS, AAMA, and others as illustrative examples
- Credentialing bodies distinct from professional organizations — NBME/USMLE, NCSBN/NCLEX, ARRT, CCI, NREMT
- Traits of effective healthcare leaders: situational awareness, emotional intelligence, accountability, psychological safety, decision-making under uncertainty
- Goal-setting frameworks at an introductory level (SMART goals)
- Team-building concepts: huddles, debriefs, closed-loop communication, speaking-up behaviors
- Conducting and participating in meetings: agenda, handoffs, rounds, huddles, and debriefs
- **SBAR** (Situation / Background / Assessment / Recommendation) — the default handoff and phone-call-a-provider structure; fully taught here because the leadership-and-teamwork atom owns it
- **Closed-loop communication** — order → readback → confirmation; fully taught here
- **CUS and the two-challenge rule** — "I'm Concerned / Uncomfortable / this is a Safety issue"; speak-up behaviors against the authority gradient
- **TeamSTEPPS essentials** — leadership, situation monitoring (shared mental model), mutual support, communication — fully taught here at an introductory depth
- HOSA as a student-organization connection where PoHS students practice these skills outside class

## Unit narrative / throughline

**"No one works alone in healthcare."** — each atom adds a layer to a
widening picture of how care happens:

- Slot 1 shows students **who** is in the room
- Slot 2 shows them **who credentialed them to be there**
- Slot 3 shows them **how the people in the room actually function together**

The unit uses increasingly complex scenario prompts — a routine clinic
visit → a scheduled surgery → a multi-trauma response — to illustrate
how the same roles collaborate differently depending on stakes and
urgency.

## Assessment strategy

**Atom-level (embedded, auto-graded)**

- 3-5 formative checks per atom
- 8-15 item summative quiz per atom

**Unit summative quiz** (20-25 items)

Mix: MCQ (role × system assignment, credentialing body match,
leadership trait identification), ordering (components of an SBAR
handoff), scenario-based (given a patient scenario, identify which
team members are involved and what each contributes), and a
short-answer item on "what is teaming and how is it different from
just having a team."

**Performance task: none** (this is intentionally a breather unit).

**Career Pathway Portfolio revisit (from u01)**

After u02, students add a **team note** to each of the two careers in
their Portfolio:

- Who are the two-to-four roles this person most commonly works with?
- What professional organization and credentialing body govern this role?
- One traits-of-a-good-leader reflection: if your chosen role became a
  team lead, which two leadership traits would matter most for them?

The revisit feeds c1B (cooperate as team member) and preserves the
portfolio as a living artifact.

## Common misconceptions

- "The doctor is always the leader" — leadership in healthcare is
  situational: a charge nurse runs the unit, a senior nurse runs a
  cardiac arrest, a rapid-response physician leads a code blue. The
  role with authority changes with the situation.
- "Nurses just carry out doctor orders" — nurses have their own
  licensure, scope, and practice authority; many interventions are
  nurse-initiated.
- "Allied health is support staff" — PTs, OTs, PAs, NPs, RTs, SLPs,
  pharmacists, social workers, and chaplains are licensed professionals
  with specialized graduate training.
- "Leaders are born, not made" — leadership in healthcare is a set of
  learnable behaviors (situational awareness, closing the loop, inviting
  dissent, debriefing). c4A "identify traits of a leader" is built
  around the assumption that these traits are teachable and observable,
  not innate.
- "Credentialing is one test" — most credentials require education +
  clinical hours + exam + ongoing continuing education to maintain.
- "A good leader has all the answers" — in healthcare, a good leader
  builds **psychological safety**, asks questions, and explicitly invites
  people to speak up when something looks wrong. Over-confident leaders
  are a known patient-safety risk.

## Equity and sensitivity notes

- **Representation in examples.** Healthcare leadership and specialty
  roles have historically skewed white and male. Default images and
  cases should show diverse practitioners across race, gender, age, and
  ability. Avoid the default "female nurse / male doctor" stereotype.
- **Speaking up across cultures.** The "speak-up" behavior that
  healthcare teams require (challenge orders, raise concerns, say "I'm
  not sure") is culturally mediated. Some students come from cultures
  or households where questioning authority is discouraged. Frame
  speak-up explicitly as a **professional expectation that overrides
  deference norms for patient-safety reasons**, not as a personality
  trait. This is a real transition and worth naming.
- **Foreign credentials.** Some team members (and some students'
  relatives) have international healthcare credentials that don't
  transfer to U.S. practice. When discussing credentialing, acknowledge
  this without making it the central story.
- **Accessibility.** Team roles span a wide range of physical demands —
  from bedside-physical to entirely computer-based (informatics,
  telehealth coordination, billing, public health). Frame team
  participation as accessible to students with varied abilities.
