# u03 — How the Body Works

## Purpose and description

u03 is the **science spine** of the course. Students learn the language
healthcare workers use to describe the body (anatomical position,
planes, cavities, directional terms), survey the eleven major organ
systems, then build the unifying principle that explains why systems
matter (homeostasis), pivot from biology to human needs (Maslow), and
close by tracing how the body and its needs change across the lifespan.

This is the densest content unit so far — five atoms, three weeks. Two
of the atoms (body-organization, body-systems) are **cross-scope to
§127.403** and primarily belong to Anatomy and Physiology
(§127.423), but they are taught here because the §127.403 content
this unit owns (homeostasis, Maslow, lifespan) does not stand alone
without an anatomical foundation, and many PoHS students will never
take A&P.

## Why this unit exists

Every later unit assumes basic body literacy. Wellness (u06) presumes
homeostasis. Ethics (u05) presumes Maslow as the framework for thinking
about patient autonomy and prioritization. Safety (u07) presumes
muscular/skeletal anatomy for body mechanics, and immune/lymphatic
function for infection precautions. Technology (u08) presumes that
equipment exists to measure and modulate homeostatic variables.

Maslow specifically anchors u05's autonomy and rights content;
lifespan stages anchor u04's lifespan-communication content (c2L) and
u06's relationships-and-wellbeing content. Without u03 first, those
later atoms have no scaffolding.

## Placement in the course (why u03 and not later)

After motivation (u01) and team literacy (u02), the course needs its
science foundation before tackling ethics, wellness, safety, or
technology. Putting science third also benefits from the team and
career context students already have — when we say "the cardiovascular
system supports the work of cardiologists, EKG techs, perfusionists,
and ICU nurses," students recognize those roles from u01-u02.

Three weeks is the right length: five atoms — two of them dense
cross-scope A&P content compressed to 45-minute survey form — plus a
unit game for retention of the high-volume factual material.

## Essential questions

1. How is the human body organized, and what language do healthcare workers use to describe it precisely?
2. What are the eleven major organ systems, and how do they depend on each other?
3. What is homeostasis, and why is it the single concept that explains both health and most diseases?
4. How does Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs help healthcare workers prioritize patient care?
5. How does the body — and what it needs — change across the lifespan?

## Unit learning outcomes

Students will be able to:

1. Use anatomical position, planes, cavities, quadrants, and directional terms to describe locations and movements on the human body.
2. Identify the eleven major organ systems and describe each system's primary function and at least one common disorder.
3. Explain homeostasis using a negative-feedback example, and predict the consequences when a feedback loop fails.
4. Apply Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs to prioritize a patient's needs in a clinical scenario.
5. Describe the major stages of human development from infancy through older adulthood and identify the leading health priorities for each stage.

## Content scope — in / out

**In scope for this unit**

- Anatomical position, planes (sagittal, frontal/coronal, transverse), body cavities, abdominal quadrants and regions
- Directional terms: superior/inferior, anterior/posterior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal, superficial/deep
- Eleven organ systems with primary functions and one or two common disorders each: integumentary, skeletal, muscular, cardiovascular, lymphatic (including immune function), respiratory, digestive, urinary, endocrine, nervous (including sensory organs), reproductive
- The principle that no system works alone — system interactions exemplified through blood-pressure regulation (cardio + renal + endocrine + nervous)
- Homeostasis as dynamic equilibrium; negative feedback loops as the dominant mechanism; positive feedback as a rarer amplifying mechanism (childbirth, clotting)
- Key chemical processes maintaining homeostasis: pH buffering (bicarbonate / carbonic-acid), oxygen transport (hemoglobin), glucose regulation (insulin / glucagon), water balance (ADH)
- Common homeostasis failures as examples: fever, type 1 and type 2 diabetes, dehydration, acidosis / alkalosis
- Maslow's five levels: physiological → safety/security → love/belonging → self-esteem → self-actualization
- Prioritizing in practice: ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation) first, then Maslow ascending; explanation that lower levels generally precede higher but the framework is heuristic, not rigid
- Lifespan stages with health priorities and communication adaptations: infancy, early childhood, school age, adolescence, young and middle adulthood, older adulthood

**Explicitly out of scope (covered elsewhere)**

| topic | moved to |
|---|---|
| Detailed individual anatomy (every bone named, muscle origin/insertion, full nerve pathways) | §127.423 Anatomy and Physiology |
| Pathophysiology depth — mechanisms of how diseases unfold | Pathophysiology course |
| Clinical assessment and physical-exam techniques | Health Science Theory (§127.422) |
| Specific disease treatment, drug therapy, surgical interventions | Health Science Theory and beyond |

## Unit narrative / throughline

**"What is a body, what does it need, and how does that change?"** —
the unit moves outward in concentric rings: anatomy (what the body
*is*) → physiology and homeostasis (how it *works*) → human needs (what
it *requires*) → development (how all of that *changes across a
lifetime*).

The framework students walk out with: every patient is **a body, in a
particular state of development, with a particular set of needs**.
That framing — body × stage × needs — is the lens the rest of the
course uses to think about ethics (whose needs?), safety (which body?),
wellness (which stage?), and technology (which needs?).

## Common misconceptions

- "Body systems work independently" — they are deeply interconnected.
  Blood-pressure regulation alone involves cardiovascular, renal,
  endocrine, and nervous systems simultaneously.
- "Maslow's pyramid is rigid: lower needs must be fully satisfied
  before higher needs can emerge" — Maslow himself later softened this;
  the framework is heuristic, not a strict ladder. People pursue
  multiple levels at once. Use it to prioritize, not to dismiss.
- "Homeostasis means everything stays the same" — it means *dynamic
  equilibrium*: constant adjustment to keep variables in range, not
  zero change.
- "Older = sicker" — chronological age and physiological age are
  different. Healthy older adults exist; chronic illness happens at
  every stage.
- "Childhood is just smaller adulthood" — children have specific
  developmental needs, anatomical proportions (larger heads, smaller
  airways), and disease patterns that are not adult-medicine scaled
  down.
- "Pain is just a nervous-system signal" — pain is biopsychosocial:
  nervous-system signal *plus* endocrine response (stress hormones)
  *plus* psychological appraisal *plus* cultural framing. Treating it
  as nerves-only mis-handles it.

> **Teaching-convention note (not a misconception):** what counts as a
> distinct "organ system" varies by textbook — some teach 10 systems,
> some 11, some 12 (typically by handling the immune system either as
> a standalone system, as a function within the lymphatic system, or
> as a distributed function across multiple systems). This unit uses
> the **11-system** convention with immune function noted within the
> lymphatic system, matching the body-systems-overview atom. Students
> who continue to A&P or Health Science Theory may see different
> conventions there; flag this proactively so they don't think one
> source is wrong.

## Equity and sensitivity notes

- **Body images and representation.** Visual content should show body
  types, abilities, skin tones, and ages across the full range of human
  variation. Default illustrations have historically been thin, white,
  young, and male — push back deliberately.
- **Disability and bodily variation.** Body-systems content should not
  assume "typical function" as the only valid state. Many people live
  full lives with one or more systems functioning differently from the
  textbook description (congenital differences, injuries, chronic
  conditions). Frame variation as a baseline, not as deficit.
- **Sex, gender, and reproductive content.** Use accurate scientific
  language for biological sex while acknowledging that gender identity
  is distinct from biological sex; intersex bodies exist and warrant
  brief, accurate inclusion. Avoid framing reproductive anatomy as
  defining gender. **District policy may vary on the depth of
  gender-vs-sex framing**; the safe default is anatomical-systems
  language ("structures involved in reproduction") which is rigorous
  and district-defensible regardless of policy.
- **Mental health throughout the lifespan.** Lifespan stages should
  treat mental health as integral, not as a separate sidebar. Adolescent
  mental-health risks and older-adult cognitive changes belong in the
  main content.
- **Chronic illness at every stage.** Lifespan content should include
  chronic conditions across all stages, not concentrate them in older
  adulthood. Pediatric diabetes, adolescent autoimmune conditions, and
  young-adult chronic pain are common.
- **Cultural views of aging and the body.** Different cultures
  conceptualize aging, the body, and health differently. Acknowledge
  variation with respect — neither dismissing nor romanticizing.
- **Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs).** Lifespan content for
  school-age and adolescence should briefly note that not all
  childhoods are stable; some students may have lived through ACEs.
  Frame this as factual context, not as a derail.

## Open questions / decisions pending

- Body-systems-overview is a single 45-minute atom covering eleven
  systems. Should it split into 2-3 atoms (more time per system, more
  total slots)? Current decision: keep single atom; the unit game and
  spaced review handle retention.
- Maslow's later work and other theorists have proposed a sixth level
  (self-transcendence). For PoHS L1, stick with the canonical five
  levels per the standard's wording (c2I); note the 5-vs-6 debate in
  one slide as a "you'll see this in later study" footnote.
- Sex/gender terminology in reproductive-system content — exact wording
  needs a sensitivity-review pass when atom content is built out.
  Default to anatomical-system language ("structures involved in
  reproduction") rather than gendered shorthand.
