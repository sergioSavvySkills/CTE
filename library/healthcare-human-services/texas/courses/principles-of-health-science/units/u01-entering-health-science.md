# u01 — Entering Health Science

## Purpose and description

This unit opens the course. It is **not** where students learn how to do
healthcare — it is where they figure out **why they might want to**.
Students meet the five health science systems (therapeutic, diagnostic,
health informatics, support services, biotechnology research and
development), explore career families within each, and test their own
interest against real-world evidence (BLS data, education pathways, the
economic and government forces shaping the industry). They also
encounter the non-negotiable professional standards every healthcare
employer expects, and anchor all of it in a short historical arc.

The unit's artifact is the **Career Pathway Portfolio** — a student
product that starts here and grows across the year.

## Why this unit exists

Motivation-first pedagogy. Ninth and tenth graders have not committed
to healthcare; leading with careers and "why this matters" lets them
self-select into deeper engagement before they memorize a single body
system or infection-control protocol. Satisfying §127.403(c)(1)
employability up front also means every downstream unit can thread those
standards through rubrics and applied tasks without re-teaching.

This unit also establishes a **shared vocabulary** — the five systems,
career-family names, credential categories (certificate / license /
degree), payer types, agency roles — that every later unit assumes is
known.

## Placement in the course (why u01 and not later)

Motivation and context come before clinical content and before any
cognitively heavy work. Placing the unit first also means the Career
Pathway Portfolio has maximum time to develop: students seed it here,
return to it implicitly as they meet more roles in later units, and
formalize it into the capstone synthesis in u08.

Nothing in this unit depends on prior course content. Everything in
later units depends on at least one concept introduced here (team
structure in u02, scope-of-practice in u05, regulatory detail in u07,
equipment-by-system in u08).

## Essential questions

1. What kinds of people work in healthcare — beyond the doctors and nurses most of us picture — and what do they actually do?
2. What does it take, in education and credentials and day-to-day professionalism, to join them?
3. How does healthcare sit inside the U.S. economy and government, and why does that shape the jobs available to me?

## Unit learning outcomes

Students will be able to:

1. Identify and compare careers across the five health science systems, naming at least three representative roles per system.
2. Evaluate career options using current employment data, education pathways, and credential requirements.
3. Describe the employability skills every healthcare employer expects and self-assess against them.
4. Explain how healthcare functions as a major U.S. economic sector and how federal, state, and local government shape the industry.
5. Place current healthcare practice within a short historical arc of advances, public-health wins, and policy changes.

## Content scope — in / out

**In scope for this unit**

- The five health science systems, with representative careers in each
- Education ladders: certificate, associate, bachelor's, graduate/doctoral
- BLS *Occupational Outlook Handbook* as the research tool for current salary and outlook
- Credential types: certification, license, registration, continuing education, advanced degrees (c6)
- Employability expectations: punctuality, attendance, communication, cooperation, time management, productive work habits (c1A-C)
- Economic scale of the U.S. healthcare sector (c2N)
- Federal / state / local roles in regulation and funding (c2O)
- Historical milestones that shape current practice (c2M)
- Macro view: care settings, payer basics, continuum of care, access barriers (via healthcare-delivery-system-overview as integrator)

## Unit narrative / throughline

**"Why healthcare, why me, why now?"** — the unit is built as a staged
self-discovery anchored in real-world evidence. Every lesson asks
students to imagine themselves in a specific role or setting, then test
that imagined self against data (BLS, education-pathway realities,
historical context, economic and policy forces). The Career Pathway
Portfolio is where that exploration lives and evolves.

No single case study runs through the unit; instead, each atom introduces
a mini-profile (a person in a role, a milestone, a policy moment) that
students react to and add to their portfolio.

## Prior knowledge expected

- Middle-school science: body systems named at a high level (fully taught in u03)
- General social-studies exposure to federal/state/local government structure
- Reading at ~8th-9th grade Lexile; technical reading is introduced in u04
- Basic digital literacy (this is a digital-first course)

## Assessment strategy

**Atom-level (embedded, auto-graded)**

- 3-5 formative checks per atom during instruction
- 8-15 item summative quiz per atom at the end of each lesson

**Unit summative quiz** (25-30 items)

Mix: MCQ (careers × systems matching, credential-type classification,
economic and government examples, historical milestone identification),
short-answer (employability scenario response), case-based (given a
career goal, identify plausible education pathways).

**Performance task — Career Pathway Portfolio (cornerstone)**

- Students select **two** healthcare careers from different systems
- For each, research and document: role description, education pathway,
  required credentials (certificate / license / degree, whichever apply),
  current BLS salary range and outlook, three employability skills most
  critical to the role
- Written reflection: why this career aligns with their interests and
  constraints — or why it doesn't, which is equally valid
- Add one historical advance that shaped the role and one current
  policy or economic factor affecting it today
- Rubric dimensions: research depth, source quality, clarity of writing
  (c1A), self-assessment honesty, evidence-based reasoning
- The portfolio is seeded here and **re-visited** in u08 capstone;
  students add new roles as they encounter them in later units

## Common misconceptions

Students often arrive with these — teach against them explicitly:

- "Healthcare = doctors and nurses" — students don't know informatics,
  support services, or biotech R&D careers
- "You have to pick your career now" — confusing career exploration with
  commitment; reassure that iteration is expected
- "More education is always better" — education has real cost and
  opportunity cost; pathway choice is strategic, not linear
- "The U.S. healthcare system is the best in the world" — benchmark
  against peer countries briefly; outcomes data matters
- "Private insurance covers everyone" — Medicaid, Medicare, uninsured and
  underinsured populations exist and shape access
- "Professionalism is just being polite" — underestimates the legal,
  clinical, and safety weight of punctuality, documentation, and time
  management in healthcare specifically

## Equity and sensitivity notes

- **Pay gaps**: healthcare salaries vary by race and gender; acknowledge
  without derailing the unit. BLS data include wage-gap reports worth a
  single-slide reference.
- **Access to healthcare careers** is uneven — rural-vs-urban education
  access, socioeconomic barriers to higher ed, student-debt realities.
  Portfolio research should accommodate students considering certificate-
  first pathways for cost reasons.
- **Historical content** — do not sanitize. Brief, factual inclusion of
  medical racism (e.g., Tuskegee, Henrietta Lacks, J. Marion Sims) belongs
  in the history atom at age-appropriate depth. Frame as context for
  modern informed-consent law (forward reference to u05).
- **Immigrant and first-generation students** — foreign credentials often
  do not transfer to U.S. practice; first-gen students may have no family
  context for credential / degree distinctions. Make these explicit in
  examples rather than assuming shared background.
- **Ability and accommodation** — healthcare careers include roles
  accessible to students with a wide range of physical abilities; avoid
  framing all roles as requiring bedside physicality.
