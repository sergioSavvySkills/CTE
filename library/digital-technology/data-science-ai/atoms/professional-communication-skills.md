## Learning objective

Students will be able to demonstrate effective written, verbal, and listening communication skills appropriate for the technology workplace.

## Content stub

- **Written communication:** email etiquette, ticket and incident documentation, technical memos, and chat/Slack tone; matching formality to audience (peer, manager, client); clarity, brevity, and correct grammar as professionalism markers
- **Verbal communication:** structuring a status update, explaining technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders, giving and receiving instructions, handling phone and video calls with clients
- **Active listening:** paraphrasing to confirm understanding, taking notes during user interviews, avoiding interruption, asking clarifying questions before responding
- **Nonverbal cues:** eye contact, posture, camera-on expectations on video calls, and reading body language to gauge whether a message is landing
- **Audience and purpose:** adjusting vocabulary and depth for executives vs. engineers vs. end-users; deciding when an email, a meeting, or a quick message is the right channel
- **Common pitfalls:** jargon overload, burying the lede, reply-all misuse, defensive tone in written responses, and the difference between urgency and importance

## Assessment stub

- Students rewrite a poorly worded support email into a professional version and justify each edit
- Role-play: one student explains a technical issue verbally while another paraphrases back what they heard; partner scores accuracy
- Short-response: list three clarifying questions to ask when a user reports 'the system is broken' and explain what each reveals
